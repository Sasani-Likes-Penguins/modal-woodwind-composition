# Glossary

Definitions for all technical terms, non-English words, and music theory concepts used throughout this repository. Terms are listed alphabetically. Where a term appears in a non-English language, its language of origin is noted.

## A

**Accidental**—A symbol that raises or lowers a note by a half-step or more. The most common accidentals are the sharp (♯, raises by a half-step), the flat (♭, lowers by a half-step), and the natural (♮, cancels a previous sharp or flat). See also: *koron*, *sori*, *quarter-tone*.

**Affect / Affekt** (Latin/German)—The specific emotional state that a piece of music is intended to evoke. The Baroque *Doctrine of Affections* held that every piece should express one clearly defined affect. See also: *Affektenlehre*, *rasa*.

**Affektenlehre** (German: "doctrine of affections")—The Baroque theory that music should express specific, identifiable emotional states with the same precision a speaker uses to move an audience. Derived from ancient Greek rhetoric. See also: *affect*, *rasa*, *nusach*.

**Agréments** (French: "ornaments")—The system of ornamental symbols used in French Baroque music. Each symbol indicated a specific ornament that the performer was expected to execute precisely. See also: *ornamentation*.

**Ajnas**—Plural of *jins*. See *jins*.

**Alap** (Hindi/Sanskrit)—The slow, unmetered opening section of a Hindustani classical performance, in which the musician introduces the notes of the *raga* one by one without rhythmic accompaniment. The most directly applicable structure for solo wind improvisation in the Indian tradition. See also: *jor*, *jhala*, *raga*.

**Aeolian mode**—The sixth of the seven diatonic modes. Equivalent to the natural minor scale. See [Aeolian](reference/mode-aeolian.md).

**Alap**—See above.

**Andolan** (Sanskrit/Hindi)—A gentle, slow oscillation between a main note and a neighboring note, used in Indian classical music. Wider and slower than Western vibrato. Part of the *gamaka* ornament family. See also: *gamaka*, [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Anchihoye** (Amharic)—One of the four primary *kiñit* (modes) of Ethiopian traditional music. Associated with sacred and solemn occasions. See [Ethiopian Kiñit](reference/world-ethiopian-kinit.md).

**Appoggiatura** (Italian: "leaning note")—An ornament in which the melody approaches the main note by landing first on the note above or below it (usually a step away), holding that preparatory note on a stressed beat, then resolving to the main note. One of the most emotionally charged ornaments in Western music. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Arohana** (Sanskrit)—The ascending scale pattern of a Hindustani *raga*. Often differs from the descending pattern (*avarohana*). See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Asma Karar** (Turkish)—A "suspended cadence" in Turkish *makam*—a temporary resting point on a note other than the tonic, creating a sense of incomplete arrival. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

**Augmented interval**—An interval that is one half-step wider than a perfect or major interval of the same numerical name. An *augmented second* is one-and-a-half steps wide (e.g., C to D♯). An *augmented fourth* is three whole steps (a *tritone*). See also: *tritone*, *augmented second*.

**Augmented second**—An interval of three half-steps (one and a half steps). Wider than a major second (two half-steps) and narrower than a minor third (three half-steps). The defining sound of scales including Double Harmonic, Hungarian Minor, Harmonic Minor, and Hijaz maqam. See also: [Double Harmonic Scale](reference/scale-double-harmonic.md), [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Avarohana** (Sanskrit)—The descending scale pattern of a Hindustani *raga*. Often differs from the ascending pattern (*arohana*). See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Avaz** (Persian)—One of five secondary modal systems derived from the seven primary *dastgah* in Persian classical music. Also refers to the vocal improvisation section within a dastgah performance. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

## B

**Balungan** (Javanese)—The core melodic skeleton of a gamelan piece, which all instruments elaborate around. See [Gamelan](reference/world-gamelan.md).

**Bandish** (Hindi/Urdu)—A composed song or melodic piece within a Hindustani classical performance, performed after the *alap* within a *tala* (rhythmic cycle). See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Bati** (Amharic)—One of the four primary *kiñit* (modes) of Ethiopian traditional music. Associated with celebration and intensity. See [Ethiopian Kiñit](reference/world-ethiopian-kinit.md).

**Bayati** (Arabic)—One of the most important Arabic *maqamat*. Characterized by a soulful, yearning quality. Contains a neutral second degree (between flat and natural). See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md).

**Bebop**—A style of jazz developed in the early 1940s by musicians including Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie, characterized by fast tempos, complex harmonies, and virtuosic improvisation. The *bebop scales* are scale collections developed for improvisation in this style. See [Bebop Scales](reference/scale-bebop-dominant.md).

**Binary form**—A musical structure with two sections (A and B), each often repeated. One of the most common forms in Baroque dance music and Celtic traditional music.

**Blue note**—A note that is slightly lowered in pitch compared to its standard Western position—most commonly the third, fifth, or seventh degree of the scale—creating a characteristically expressive, "bent" quality. Originated in African American musical traditions and is central to blues, jazz, and gospel. See [Minor Blues Scale](reference/scale-minor-blues.md).

**Bol** (Hindi/Urdu)—A syllable used in Indian rhythmic and melodic practice to represent a specific stroke or note pattern. In *qawwali*, melodic patterns are sometimes sung on bols.

## C

**Cadence**—A melodic or harmonic gesture that creates a sense of arrival or conclusion, like a musical punctuation mark. A *perfect cadence* arrives firmly at the home note; a *deceptive cadence* arrives somewhere unexpected.

**Cante Jondo** (Spanish: "deep song")—The most serious and emotionally profound style of flamenco singing, associated with *siguiriyas*, *soleá*, and related *palos*. Characterized by intense, raw emotional expression. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Carnatic music**—The classical music of South India, one of the oldest continuously practiced musical traditions in the world. More composition-oriented than North Indian Hindustani classical music. See [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Cent**—A unit of musical measurement equal to one hundredth of a half-step (semitone). Used to describe microtonal differences in pitch. 100 cents = 1 half-step. 1200 cents = 1 octave. See also: *microtone*, *comma*, [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Chahargah** (Persian)—One of the seven primary *dastgah* (modal systems) of Persian classical music. Associated with power, drama, and heroic feeling. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Chakra** (Sanskrit: "wheel")—One of twelve groups of six *melakarta* ragas in the Carnatic classification system. See [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Characteristic phrase**—A short melodic gesture that immediately identifies a mode, scale, or raga to a trained listener. Called *pakad* in Hindustani music, *jins* in Arabic music, *gusheh* (in part) in Persian music. See also: *seed phrase*, *motive*.

**Chromatic**—Relating to notes outside the standard seven-note diatonic scale. A *chromatic scale* uses all twelve notes within an octave (all the white and black keys on a piano). *Chromatic passing notes* are notes between the standard scale degrees used in passing.

**Circular breathing**—A technique used on wind instruments in which the player continuously inhales through the nose while simultaneously pushing air out through the mouth using cheek pressure, allowing an uninterrupted sound with no audible breath break. Used in didgeridoo, Middle Eastern *ney*, *kaval*, *bawu*, recorder, flute, clarinet, and virtually all wind instruments. Requires significant practice. See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

**Comma**—A very small unit of musical pitch measurement. In Turkish *makam* theory, the octave is divided into 53 equal *komas* (commas). One comma equals approximately 22.6 cents. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md), [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Compás** (Spanish)—The rhythmic cycle or metric pattern that defines a flamenco *palo*. Each palo has its own specific compás, typically 12 beats with characteristic accent patterns. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Consonance**—A combination of notes that sounds stable and pleasant. Opposite of *dissonance*. Perfect fifths (7 half-steps) and perfect fourths (5 half-steps) are considered strongly consonant.

**Cran**—An Irish traditional music ornament specific to the lowest note of a whistle or flute, where a tap is physically impossible. Consists of a series of cuts from different holes above the note. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Cross-fingering**—A fingering on a wind instrument that uses non-adjacent holes to produce notes not part of the instrument's natural scale. Often produces a slightly different tone color from standard fingerings. See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

**Cut**—An Irish traditional music ornament consisting of a very brief lift of a finger above the note being played, creating a momentary upward pitch spike that articulates the note without stopping the airflow. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

## D

**Da capo** (Italian: "from the head")—An instruction to repeat from the beginning of a piece. *Da capo form* (ABA) consists of an opening section, a contrasting middle section, and a return to the opening. See also: *binary form*.

**Daramad** (Persian)—The opening *gusheh* of a *dastgah* performance. Establishes the tonal center and emotional mood of the dastgah. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Dastgah** (Persian: "system" or "method")—One of the seven primary modal systems of Persian classical music. Each dastgah contains a family of related *gusheh* (melodic fragments) organized into the *radif*. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Descending seyir**—See *seyir*.

**Diatonic**—Using only the seven notes of a standard major or minor scale without additional sharps or flats. A *diatonic mode* is one of the seven modes (Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Aeolian, Locrian) built from those seven notes.

**Dissonance**—A combination of notes that sounds tense, unstable, or clashing. Opposite of *consonance*. Dissonance creates tension that typically "wants" to resolve to consonance. The *tritone* is the most dissonant interval in Western music.

**Doctrine of Affections**—See *Affektenlehre*.

**Dominant**—The fifth degree of a scale. In Western harmony, the chord built on the dominant has a strong tendency to resolve back to the tonic. In Turkish *makam*, the *güçlü* serves a similar function.

**Drone**—A sustained note (or notes) held continuously while a melody plays above it. Common in Indian classical music (the *tanpura* drone), Persian classical music, Celtic bagpipe music, Byzantine *ison*, and many other traditions. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Duende** (Spanish)—A concept in flamenco and wider Spanish artistic culture describing a quality of authentic, raw emotional power and presence. Closely associated with the *cante jondo* tradition. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

## E

**Echos** (Greek: "sound" or "mode")—One of the eight modes of the Byzantine *Octoechos* system. See [Byzantine Modes](reference/world-byzantine-modes.md).

**Equal temperament**—The standard Western tuning system in which the octave is divided into 12 equal half-steps of exactly 100 cents each. This system makes all keys sound equally (slightly) out of tune compared to pure harmonic relationships, but allows instruments to play in any key without retuning. Not used in most world music traditions. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**ERAN**—Early Right Anterior Negativity. A measurable electrical response in the brain that occurs within 100–250 milliseconds after a note that violates musical expectation. Detectable via EEG. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

---

## F

**Falseta** (Spanish)—A short melodic phrase unit in flamenco guitar playing. A flamenco performance consists of many falsetas strung together, separated by rhythmic strumming patterns. Equivalent in function to a *motive* or *seed phrase*. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Figured bass**—A Baroque notation system in which a bass line is written with numbers indicating which chords should be played above it. Performers improvised the inner voices from these figures. See [Baroque Techniques](reference/baroque-techniques.md).

**Flattement** (French)—A French Baroque ornament consisting of a gentle, undulating vibrato produced by a slight wavering of finger pressure on a wind instrument hole, or by breath modulation. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Forud** (Persian)—The descending return toward the tonic in a Persian *dastgah* performance, after the middle *gushes* have risen progressively higher in register. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Free reed**—A type of sound-producing mechanism in which a thin metal or cane tongue (the reed) vibrates freely within a close-fitting frame when air is blown past it. Used in the *bawu*, harmonica, concertina, and accordion. The reed vibrates continuously as long as air flows—unlike woodwind reeds which strike against a surface. See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

**Frisson**—A shiver or wave of goosebumps produced in response to emotionally intense music. A measurable physiological response associated with dopamine release. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Fipple**—The mouthpiece mechanism of a duct flute (such as a recorder or *kaval*) in which a narrow channel directs the player's breath across a sharp edge to produce sound. The player does not need to form an embouchure as on a transverse flute or clarinet—the fipple does the work of directing the air. See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

## G

**Gamaka** (Sanskrit/Kannada)—In Indian classical music, an ornamental gesture—a slide, oscillation, trill, or bend—that is inseparable from a note's identity within a *raga*. A raga played without its characteristic gamakas is not considered that raga. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md), [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md), [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Gat** (Hindi)—A composed melody in a Hindustani classical performance, performed within a *tala* (rhythmic cycle) with tabla accompaniment. Follows the *alap* section. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Gending** (Javanese)—A piece of gamelan music. See [Gamelan](reference/world-gamelan.md).

**Ghammaz** (Arabic)—The pivot note at the end of a *jins* (melodic cell), from which a melody can modulate to a new *jins* in Arabic *maqam*. See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md).

**Glissando** (Italian/French, from Italian *glissare*: "to slide")—A continuous slide between two notes, passing through all intermediate pitches. Distinct from a *portamento* (a voice-like slide) in that a glissando often passes through every pitch chromatically. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Goltraighe** (Old Irish)—In ancient Irish musical tradition, music for weeping—laments and grief songs. One of the three fundamental modes of musical expression in early Irish culture, alongside *Geantraighe* (joy) and *Suantraighe* (sleep). See [Celtic Modes](reference/world-celtic-modes.md).

**Gong Ageng** (Javanese)—The large gong in a Javanese gamelan ensemble, marking the end of each musical cycle. See [Gamelan](reference/world-gamelan.md).

**Grace note**—A very short ornamental note played just before or after a main note. In Western notation, written as a small note. In Celtic music, the *cut* and *tap* are types of grace note. In Indian music, *kan* is a grace note approach. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Groove**—The quality of music that creates a pleasurable physical impulse to move. Associated with medium-complexity rhythmic patterns and reward-circuit activity in the brain. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Griot**—A West African professional musician, oral historian, and storyteller (*jeli* in Mande languages). The griot tradition preserves historical and cultural knowledge through music and spoken word. See [West African Music](reference/world-west-african.md).

**Gruppetto** (Italian: "little group")—A four-note ornament that turns around the main note: the note above, the main note, the note below, the main note. Also called a *turn*. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Güçlü** (Turkish)—The dominant or secondary resting point in a Turkish *makam*—a structurally important note, often a fourth or fifth above the tonic, that serves as a temporary point of arrival during a performance. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

**Gusheh** (Persian)—A short melodic figure or fragment that is part of a *dastgah*'s *radif* (the complete body of Persian classical melody). Each gusheh has its own character and position within the performance journey. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

## H

**Half-step**—The smallest interval in standard Western music—the distance between two immediately adjacent notes on a piano keyboard (one white key to the nearest black key, or vice versa). Also called a *semitone*. Equal to 100 cents in equal temperament.

**Harmonic minor**—A variant of the natural minor scale with a raised seventh degree, creating a half-step approach to the octave. See [Harmonic Minor](reference/scale-harmonic-minor.md).

**Hemiola**—A rhythmic device in which two bars of triple meter (3+3) are articulated as three bars of duple meter (2+2+2), or vice versa. Creates a sense of rhythmic disorientation or forward rush.

**Hicaz / Hijaz**—An Arabic *maqam* (and Turkish *makam*) characterized by an augmented second in its lower *jins*, giving it a dramatic, exotic quality. See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md), [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

**Hindustani music**—The classical music of North India and Pakistan. One of the two main branches of Indian classical music (the other being *Carnatic* music). See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Homayoun** (Persian)—One of the seven primary *dastgah* of Persian classical music. Associated with solemnity and grandeur. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

## I

**Imperfect cadence**—A cadence that ends on the dominant (fifth degree) rather than the tonic, creating a sense of unresolved continuation. Like ending a spoken sentence with a rising intonation.

**Improvisation**—Creating music in the moment, without a written score. Central to jazz, blues, Indian classical music, Arabic maqam, Persian dastgah, flamenco, and many other traditions.

**Interval**—The distance between two pitches, measured in half-steps. A *major second* is 2 half-steps; a *minor third* is 3 half-steps; a *perfect fifth* is 7 half-steps; an *octave* is 12 half-steps. See [Emotional Affect](reference/emotional-affect.md).

**Ison** (Greek)—The sustained drone note sung or played beneath a Byzantine chant melody. Provides tonal grounding and acoustic resonance. See [Byzantine Modes](reference/world-byzantine-modes.md).

---

## J

**Jali / Jeli**—See *griot*.

**Janya raga** (Sanskrit)—In Carnatic music, a derived *raga* that uses a subset of the notes from its parent *melakarta* raga, or uses different notes ascending and descending. See [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Jhala** (Hindi)—The third and final section of a Hindustani *alap*, characterized by fast, rhythmically driven playing before the composed *gat* begins. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Jins** (Arabic: singular; plural *ajnas*)—A melodic cell of 3–5 consecutive notes that is the fundamental building block of Arabic *maqam*. Each maqam scale is built by combining two or more ajnas. Each jins has its own emotional character and characteristic phrases. See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md).

**Jor** (Hindi)—The second section of a Hindustani *alap*, in which a rhythmic pulse emerges without percussion accompaniment. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Just intonation**—A tuning system based on pure mathematical ratios between note frequencies, derived from the natural harmonic series. Sounds "purer" than equal temperament but makes it difficult to play in multiple keys without retuning. Used as a reference point for understanding why microtonal systems sound the way they do. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

---

## K

**Kaba**—A technique on the Bulgarian *kaval* (open-rimblown type, distinct from the Moldavian fipple kaval) in which the player produces both the fundamental note and its octave harmonic simultaneously. Not to be confused with the dual-voice technique of the Moldavian kaval. See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

**Kampita** (Sanskrit)—A sustained oscillation ornament in Carnatic music—similar to a trill but smoother, with a rocking quality. Part of the *gamaka* family. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Kan** (Hindi)—A grace note approach in Hindustani music—a quick touch of a neighboring note before or after the main note. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Karar** (Turkish/Arabic)—The tonic or home note of a *makam* or *maqam*. The note on which every piece must end.

**Kiñit** (Amharic: ቅኝት)—The Ethiopian modal system. The four primary kiñit are Tizita, Bati, Ambassel, and Anchihoye. See [Ethiopian Kiñit](reference/world-ethiopian-kinit.md).

**Koma** (Turkish)—A very small unit of pitch measurement in Turkish *makam* theory. The octave is divided into 53 komas. One koma equals approximately 22.6 cents. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md), [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Koron** (Persian: ♭)—A Persian microtonal accidental that lowers a note by approximately a quarter-tone (roughly 50 cents). Used in Persian *dastgah* notation. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Kumbengo** (Mande)—A short repeating melodic ostinato figure played on the *kora* in West African griot music, over which the singer improvises. Structurally similar to a drone-and-melody relationship. See [West African Music](reference/world-west-african.md).

---

## L

**Leading tone**—The seventh degree of a major or harmonic minor scale, a half-step below the octave. Has a strong tendency to resolve upward to the tonic. Its presence or absence significantly affects a scale's emotional character. See also: *Mixolydian* (which lacks a leading tone).

**Legato** (Italian: "tied" or "smooth")—A style of playing in which notes are connected smoothly without gaps. The opposite of *staccato*.

**Locrian mode**—The seventh of the seven diatonic modes. The only diatonic mode with a diminished (unstable) tonic triad. See [Locrian Mode](reference/mode-locrian.md).

**Luthier**—A maker of stringed instruments. By extension, a maker of musical instruments generally.

---

## M

**Mahur** (Persian)—One of the seven primary *dastgah* of Persian classical music. Associated with brightness and joy; the closest Persian dastgah to Western major. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Makam** (Turkish; plural *makamlar*)—The Turkish modal system, related to but distinct from Arabic *maqam*. Defined by specific notes, the *seyir* (required melodic direction), and characteristic phrases. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

**Maqam** (Arabic; plural *maqamat*)—The Arabic modal system. Each maqam is a complete melodic framework including scale, emotional character, characteristic phrases (*jins*), and modulation pathways. See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md).

**Meend** (Hindi)—A smooth, continuous slide between notes in Hindustani classical music. The most fundamental ornamental technique in the tradition—without it, raga melody is barely possible. Equivalent in function to a *glissando* or *portamento* but with specific expressive rules governing its use. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Melakarta** (Sanskrit/Tamil)—One of the 72 parent scales in Carnatic music theory, from which all *janya* (derived) ragas are obtained. Each melakarta contains all seven notes in both ascending and descending form. See [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Melodic minor**—A variant of the natural minor scale with raised sixth and seventh degrees when ascending, reverting to natural minor when descending (in classical usage). See [Melodic Minor](reference/scale-melodic-minor.md).

**Messa di voce** (Italian: "placing the voice")—A technique in which a sustained note begins softly, swells to a louder dynamic in the middle, then fades back to soft—all on one breath. One of the most expressive sustained-note techniques in Western vocal and wind playing. See [Expressive Playing](reference/expressive-playing.md).

**Microtone / microtonal interval**—Any musical interval smaller than a half-step (semitone). Used extensively in Arabic, Turkish, Persian, Indian, and many other world music traditions. Equal to less than 100 cents. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**MMN**—Mismatch Negativity. A brain electrical response triggered by any deviation from an established auditory pattern, detectable via EEG. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Mode**—A scale with a particular ordering of intervals and a specific emotional character. The seven diatonic modes (Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Aeolian, Locrian) are the standard Western modes. Many non-Western traditions have their own modal systems (maqam, makam, dastgah, raga, kiñit). See [Modal Theory Introduction](reference/modal-theory-introduction.md).

**Modulation**—Moving from one tonal center (key or mode) to another within a single piece. In *maqam*, modulation occurs through *ghammaz* (pivot notes). In *dastgah*, through borrowing from related systems. In Western music, through shared notes between keys.

**Mordent**—An ornament consisting of a rapid lower-neighbor figure: the main note, quickly the note below, back to the main note. Creates a brief flash of downward direction without sustained tension. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Motive**—See *seed phrase*.

**Mujarrad**—A simple, unornamented melodic line in Arabic music.

## N

**Navarasa** (Sanskrit: "nine essences")—The nine fundamental emotional essences (*rasas*) in Indian aesthetic theory that art can evoke: Shringara (love/beauty), Hasya (joy/humor), Karuna (sorrow/compassion), Raudra (anger/fury), Veera (heroism/courage), Bhayanaka (fear/terror), Bibhatsa (disgust), Adbhuta (wonder/awe), Shanta (peace/serenity). See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Nava** (Persian)—One of the seven primary *dastgah* of Persian classical music. Associated with longing and melancholy. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Neume**—A symbol in Byzantine and medieval Western musical notation, indicating a melodic gesture rather than a specific pitch. Byzantine neumes are a separate and complex notation system. See [Byzantine Modes](reference/world-byzantine-modes.md).

**Neutral interval**—An interval that falls between the standard major and minor versions—for example, a neutral second (between a minor and major second) or neutral third (between a minor and major third). Common in Arabic, Turkish, and Persian music. Approximately 150 cents for a neutral second; approximately 350 cents for a neutral third. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Nucleus accumbens**—A brain structure involved in reward, motivation, and pleasure. Activated during peak emotional responses to music, associated with dopamine release. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Nusach** (Hebrew: נוסח, "manner" or "custom")—The system of musical modes used in Jewish liturgical chant, specifically the cantorial tradition. Each mode (*shtayger*) is associated with a specific occasion and emotional register. See [Jewish Nusach](reference/world-jewish-nusach.md).

---

## O

**Octoechos** (Greek: Ὀκτώηχος, "of the eight sounds/modes")—The eight-mode system of Eastern Orthodox Christian liturgical music, codified by Saint John of Damascus in the 8th century. See [Byzantine Modes](reference/world-byzantine-modes.md).

**Ornament / ornamentation**—A note or gesture that decorates a melody without being structurally essential to it. In many traditions, ornamentation is inseparable from the melody itself. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Ostinato** (Italian: "obstinate")—A short melodic or rhythmic pattern that is continuously repeated, typically in the same pitch. See also: *kumbengo*, *balungan*.

---

## P

**Pakad** (Hindi)—The characteristic phrase of a Hindustani *raga*—the short melodic gesture that immediately identifies the raga to a trained listener, even without hearing the full scale. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Pallavi** (Tamil/Sanskrit)—The main theme or refrain of a Carnatic *kriti* (composed piece). The *pallavi* is stated at the beginning and returns at the end. See [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Palo** (Spanish; plural *palos*)—One of the traditional forms or styles of flamenco, each with its own *compás*, emotional character, and modal associations. Examples: *soleá*, *bulerías*, *siguiriyas*, *farruca*. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Parasympathetic nervous system**—The division of the autonomic nervous system responsible for rest and recovery responses—slowed heart rate, deeper breathing, relaxed muscles. Engaged by slow music around 60 beats per minute. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Passus duriusculus** (Latin: "hard step")—A Baroque compositional device consisting of a descending chromatic line (moving by half-steps). Used as the standard "grief figure" or "lament bass" in Baroque music. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md), [Baroque Techniques](reference/baroque-techniques.md).

**Pathet** (Javanese)—One of the three modal sub-categories of Javanese *slendro* (nem, sanga, manyura), appearing in order during a traditional *wayang* performance. See [Gamelan](reference/world-gamelan.md).

**Patter**—A technique on the Moldavian *kaval* (and related instruments) in which a held note is given a gentle rhythmic pulsing without breaking the tone—creating subtle internal movement beneath a sustained sound. See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

**Pentatonic scale**—A scale of five notes per octave. The most universal scale type in human music, appearing independently across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Celtic, West African, Native American, and many other traditions. See [World Pentatonic Systems](reference/world-pentatonic-systems.md).

**Perfect cadence**—A cadence that moves from the dominant (fifth) chord to the tonic (home) chord—the most conclusive harmonic ending in Western music.

**Perfect fifth**—An interval of 7 half-steps—for example, C to G. The most acoustically pure interval after the octave, and the most structurally stable interval in most world music traditions.

**Perfect fourth**—An interval of 5 half-steps—for example, C to F. Acoustically pure and stable. The one stable consonance in the *Locrian* mode.

**Phrase**—A musical sentence—a melodic idea that begins, develops, and arrives at a resting point (however temporary).

**Phyrigian mode**—The third of the seven diatonic modes. Characterized by a half-step between the tonic and its second degree. See [Phrygian Mode](reference/mode-phrygian.md).

**Pitch bend**—A continuous smooth change in pitch above or below a target note, then returning to the target. Central to blues guitar, *bawu* playing, *sitar* playing, and many non-Western instrument traditions. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Port de voix** (French: "carrying the voice")—A French Baroque ornament consisting of an upward approach to the main note by step, with the approach note taking weight on the beat. Equivalent to the *appoggiatura*. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

---

## Q

**Quarter-tone**—An interval of approximately 50 cents—half of a half-step. The fundamental microtonal unit in Arabic and Turkish music theory. Quarter-tones cannot be played on standard Western fixed-pitch instruments (piano, standard guitar). They are central to *maqam*, *makam*, and many other non-Western traditions. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Qawwali** (Arabic/Urdu)—A form of devotional Sufi music from Pakistan and North India, associated with the *Chishti* order of Sufism. Characterized by ecstatic repetition, building intensity, and call-and-response between a lead singer and a group. Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan is the most internationally known qawwali artist.

---

## R

**Radif** (Persian: ردیف, "order")—The complete body of Persian classical melody—approximately 300–400 *gusheh* (melodic fragments) organized into the seven *dastgah* and five *avaz*. Learning the radif is the foundation of Persian classical music education. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Raga** (Sanskrit: "color" or "passion")—In Indian classical music, a complete melodic framework with specific ascending and descending patterns, characteristic phrases (*pakad*), most important notes (*vadi* and *samvadi*), ornamentation (*gamaka*), emotional essence (*rasa*), and time of day association (*samay*). Much more than a scale. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md), [Carnatic Melakarta](reference/world-carnatic-melakarta.md).

**Rasgueado** (Spanish)—A flamenco guitar strumming technique using the back of the fingernails. Provides rhythmic accompaniment between *falseta* (melodic) sections.

**Rasa** (Sanskrit: "essence" or "juice")—In Indian aesthetic theory, one of the nine fundamental emotional essences (*navarasa*) that art can evoke. Each *raga* has a primary rasa. See *navarasa*, [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Register**—A portion of an instrument's pitch range with a characteristic timbre. Most wind instruments have a low register (darker, warmer), a middle register (most characteristic), and a high register (brighter, more intense). See [Instrument Capabilities](reference/instrument-capabilities.md).

**Remate** (Spanish)—A closing figure in a flamenco *palo* that signals the end of a section. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Roll**—An Irish traditional music ornament consisting of a cut followed immediately by a tap on the same note, producing a three-note embellishment wrapping around the main note. The most versatile ornament in Celtic wind playing. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).

**Rubato** (Italian: "stolen")—Expressive flexibility in tempo—intentional stretching of some note values and compressing of others in response to emotional content. The listener should feel the emotion, not notice the timing. See [Expressive Playing](reference/expressive-playing.md).

---

## S

**Saba** (Arabic)—One of the most emotionally intense *maqamat*, associated with deep grief and pain. Has an unusual scale structure that doesn't complete a standard fourth. See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md).

**Samay** (Hindi/Sanskrit)—The time of day (and sometimes season) associated with a Hindustani *raga*. Ragas are traditionally performed at their assigned times to maximize emotional resonance. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Samvadi** (Sanskrit)—The second most important note in a Hindustani or Carnatic *raga*, in balance with the *vadi*. Always a fourth or fifth away from the vadi. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Scale**—See *mode*.

**Seed phrase**—A short melodic idea (2–4 notes) that serves as the foundation for development throughout a composition. Called *motive* in Western classical music, *pakad* in Hindustani music, *jins* in Arabic music, *gusheh* in Persian music, *falseta* in flamenco. See [Structure and Surprise](reference/structure-and-surprise.md).

**Segah** (Persian/Turkish)—A *dastgah* in Persian music, and a *makam* in Turkish music. Both are associated with a plaintive, meditative, quarter-tonal character. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md), [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

**Semitone**—See *half-step*.

**Seyir** (Turkish: "route" or "path")—The prescribed melodic direction of a Turkish *makam*—ascending, descending, or ascending-descending. Not just which notes to use, but in which direction the melody must travel through them. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

**Shur** (Persian)—The most important *dastgah* in Persian classical music. Considered the "mother of modes." Closest in character to Phrygian. Associated with fire, meditation, and deep emotion. See [Persian Dastgah](reference/world-persian-dastgah.md).

**Shtayger** (Yiddish: "way" or "manner"; plural *shtaygers*)—One of the Jewish liturgical modes used in *nusach*. The three primary shtaygers are Ahavah Rabbah, Magen Avot, and Adonai Malakh. See [Jewish Nusach](reference/world-jewish-nusach.md).

**Sikah** (Arabic)—An Arabic *maqam* associated with intimate love. Unusual in that it begins on a neutral (microtonal) note. See [Arabic Maqam](reference/world-arabic-maqam.md).

**Siguiriyas** (Spanish)—One of the most serious *palos* (forms) of flamenco, associated with the deepest grief in *cante jondo*. Typically in A Phrygian. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Slendro** (Javanese)—A five-tone gamelan tuning system with roughly equal intervals, used in Javanese and Balinese music. No direct Western equivalent. See [Gamelan](reference/world-gamelan.md).

**Soleá** (Spanish)—One of the foundational *palos* of flamenco, considered the "mother form." Typically in E Phrygian. See [Flamenco](reference/world-flamenco.md).

**Sori** (Persian: ♯)—A Persian microtonal accidental that raises a note by approximately a quarter-tone (roughly 50 cents). Used in Persian *dastgah* notation. See [Microtonal Notation and Theory](reference/microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

**Staccato** (Italian: "detached")—A style of playing in which notes are made short and separated. The opposite of *legato*.

**Suantraighe** (Old Irish)—In ancient Irish musical tradition, music for sleeping—lullabies and meditative music. One of the three fundamental modes of musical expression in early Irish culture. See [Celtic Modes](reference/world-celtic-modes.md).

**Suspiratio** (Latin: "sigh")—A Baroque compositional figure consisting of a short note followed by a rest, imitating a sigh. Used to express grief or emotional restraint. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md), [Baroque Techniques](reference/baroque-techniques.md).

**Sympathetic nervous system**—The division of the autonomic nervous system responsible for arousal responses—increased heart rate, faster breathing, heightened alertness. Engaged by fast, energetic music. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](reference/brain-biology-psychology.md).

---

## T

**Taksim** (Turkish/Arabic)—An unmetered, free improvisation for a solo instrument in Turkish and Arabic music. Demonstrates the *makam* or *maqam* through characteristic phrases and modulations. Structurally similar to the Indian *alap*.

**Tala** (Sanskrit)—A rhythmic cycle in Indian classical music, consisting of a fixed number of beats with a characteristic pattern of accents. The foundation of metered Hindustani and Carnatic music. Examples: Teentaal (16 beats), Ektaal (12 beats), Rupak (7 beats).

**Tanpura** (Hindi)—A long-necked plucked string instrument used to provide a continuous drone in Hindustani classical music performances. Tuned to the tonic and fifth of the raga being performed.

**Tintinnabuli** (Latin: "little bells")—A compositional technique developed by Estonian composer Arvo Pärt in 1976. Music is composed from two voices: a melody voice (*m-voice*) moving stepwise, and a tintinnabuli voice (*t-voice*) restricted to the notes of the tonic triad. See [Composing in Imitation](reference/composing-in-imitation.md).

**Tizita** (Amharic: ትዝታ, "remembrance")—One of the four primary *kiñit* (modes) of Ethiopian traditional music. Associated with nostalgia and longing. Exists in major and minor forms. See [Ethiopian Kiñit](reference/world-ethiopian-kinit.md).

**Tonic**—The home note of a scale or mode—the note the melody feels most at rest on. Every scale has one.

**Tritone**—An interval of six half-steps—exactly halfway between two octaves. The most dissonant interval in Western equal temperament. Historically called *Diabolus in musica* ("the devil in music"). The defining interval of the *Locrian* mode. See [Locrian Mode](reference/mode-locrian.md).

**Turn**—See *gruppetto*.

---

## U

**Uşşak** (Turkish)—A Turkish *makam* associated with introspection and intimate feeling. Often described as the "Turkish minor." The word means "lovers" in Turkish. See [Turkish Makam](reference/world-turkish-makam.md).

---

## V

**Vadi** (Sanskrit)—The most important note in a Hindustani or Carnatic *raga*—the note the melody returns to most frequently and on which it dwells longest. See [Hindustani Raga](reference/world-hindustani-raga.md).

**Vibrato**—A regular, pulsing variation in pitch around a central note, used to add warmth and expressiveness to sustained tones. On wind instruments, produced by breath pressure variation (diaphragm vibrato) or, in Celtic whistle tradition, by rapid finger movement on a hole below the note.

---

## W

**Wayang** (Javanese)—Traditional Javanese shadow puppet theater, performed to live gamelan accompaniment. Different *pathet* (modal categories) are used in the three phases of a wayang performance. See [Gamelan](reference/world-gamelan.md).

**Whole step**—An interval of two half-steps—the distance skipping one note on a piano keyboard (e.g., C to D, skipping C♯). Also called a *major second* or *tone*.

**Whole-tone scale**—A six-note scale in which every interval is exactly a whole step. Has no half-steps, no leading tone, and no stable tonal center. Creates a floating, directionless quality. See [Whole-Tone Scale](reference/scale-whole-tone.md).

---

## Z

**Zamzama** (Arabic)—A trill ornament in Arabic maqam—rapid alternation between two notes. See [Ornamentation Guide](reference/ornamentation-guide.md).
