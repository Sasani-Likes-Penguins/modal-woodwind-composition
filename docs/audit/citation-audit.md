## Section 1: Dorian Mode Symmetry Claim—VERIFIED WITH CONTEXT

**CLAIM**: "Dorian is the only diatonic mode that is perfectly symmetrical—its step pattern reads identically ascending and descending."

**STEP PATTERN VERIFICATION**:
- Ascending: W-H-W-W-W-H-W
- Descending: W-H-W-W-W-H-W
- **Palindromic confirmation**: ✅ YES

**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED—ACCURATE WITH IMPORTANT QUALIFIER

**SOURCES VERIFIED**:
1. **Wikipedia—Dorian mode**: "Thus, the Dorian mode is a symmetric scale, since the pattern of whole and half steps is the same ascending or descending."
2. **flutetunes.com**: "The Dorian mode is the only mode that is symmetric, meaning that the pattern of tones and semitones (T-s-T-T-T-s-T) is the same ascending or descending."
3. **Ian Ring scale database (Scale1709)**: "The inverse of a scale is a reflection using the root as its axis. The inverse of 1709 is itself, because it is a palindromic scale!"
4. **Offtonic Theory**: "It's also symmetric: the pattern of Whole and Half steps is WHWWWHW, so if you play exactly the same intervals going down instead of up you get the same scale."
5. **mynewmicrophone.com**: "Dorian is a palindromic scale! This means that its intervallic series is the same forwards as it is backward."

**IMPORTANT CONTEXT**: The claim is **accurate as stated** because it specifies "diatonic mode." However, other symmetric scales exist outside the diatonic family:
- **Whole-tone scale** (hexatonic, all whole steps)
- **Diminished scales** (octatonic, W-H-W-H-W-H-W-H or H-W alternating)
- **Double harmonic scale** (S-M-S-T-S-M-S, heptatonic palindrome per Wikipedia)
- **Chromatic scale** (12 equal semitones)
- **Augmented/hexatonic scales**

**CONCLUSION**: The repository's claim is accurate. Among the seven diatonic modes, Dorian is indeed the only symmetric one. The distinction is important and preserved correctly in documentation.

## Section 3: Neuroscience and Psychology Claims

**CLAIM**: Minor second intervals appear disproportionately in human infant crying.

**SOURCE**: [10] PMC/NIH—Zeloni & Pavani (2022) https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9019334/
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: PMC article confirms: "minor 2nd outnumbered all other intervals in cry vocalizations, but not in neutral babbling." The claim that the ♭2 "may be biologically rather than culturally determined" is consistent with the paper's findings.

**CLAIM**: Dopamine releases in two distinct phases during emotionally powerful music—during anticipation and at peak emotional response.

**SOURCE**: [102] Salimpoor, V.N., et al.—*Anatomically distinct dopamine release during anticipation and experience of peak emotion to music*, Nature Neuroscience 14 (2011)
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: Salimpoor et al. used PET and fMRI to show dopamine release in the caudate nucleus during anticipation and in the nucleus accumbens during peak emotional response. The repository accurately represents this finding.

**CLAIM**: The brain generates measurable ERAN/MMN responses within 100–250ms after a note violating musical expectation.

**SOURCE**: [104] Koelsch, Stefan—*Brain and Music*, Wiley-Blackwell, 2012
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: ERAN (Early Right Anterior Negativity) and MMN (Mismatch Negativity) are well-documented EEG responses in music cognition research. The timescale (100–250ms) is consistent with published neuroscience literature.

**CLAIM**: Slow music near 60 BPM can induce alpha brainwave states (8–12 Hz).

**SOURCE**: [105] Thaut, Michael H.—*Rhythm, Music, and the Brain*, Routledge, 2005
**STATUS**: ✅ PLAUSIBLE—supported by general music neuroscience literature
**NOTE**: The relationship between slow tempo, resting heart rate (~60 BPM), and alpha brainwave entrainment is widely reported in music therapy and neuroscience. The repository appropriately frames this as "can induce" rather than "always induces," acknowledging individual variation.

## Section 4: Modal Theory Claims

**CLAIM**: Dorian is mathematically symmetrical (confirmed above).
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED

**CLAIM**: Locrian's tonic triad is diminished; the perfect fourth is the only stable interval.

**SOURCE**: [2] Wikipedia—Locrian mode; [4] PDMusic
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: B-D-F (B Locrian tonic) is diminished. The fourth (E above B) is a perfect fourth and the most stable available interval. Consistent across all theory sources.

**CLAIM**: Medieval Dorian was Mode I (Primus tonus) in the Catholic church system.

**SOURCE**: [1] Wikipedia—Mode (music); [18] Louis Pressbooks—Exploring Arts
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: Mode I (authentic Dorian, final on D) is correctly identified as the first and most important of the eight medieval modes. The Dies irae's Dorian character is a widely cited example in liturgical music scholarship.

**CLAIM**: Miles Davis's *Kind of Blue* (1959) uses D Dorian ("So What") and E♭ Dorian ("Freddie Freeloader").

**SOURCE**: [8] Musical-U; verified by multiple theory sources
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: "So What" in D Dorian and "Freddie Freeloader" are correctly identified. This is one of the most cited examples in modal jazz theory.

**CLAIM**: *Scarborough Fair* (Simon and Garfunkel) is in Dorian mode.

**SOURCE**: [8] Musical-U; verified by Hooktheory, multiple theory sources
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED—KEY VARIATION NOTED
**NOTE**: Multiple independent sources confirm Dorian modal character. **Key discrepancy**: Sources identify the song as either "A Dorian" (capo position on guitar) or "E Dorian" (concert pitch). Both descriptions are defensible depending on frame of reference. The repository should note: "A Dorian (E Dorian in concert pitch depending on arrangement)."

**CLAIM**: Beethoven's String Quartet Op. 132, 3rd movement is inscribed "in der lydischen Tonart" (in the Lydian mode).

**SOURCE**: [8] Musical-U; general classical music scholarship
**STATUS**: ✅ VERIFIED
**NOTE**: Beethoven's inscription is historically documented. The piece is F Lydian and inscribed "Heiliger Dankgesang eines Genesenen an die Gottheit, in der lydischen Tonart." This is one of the most significant classical uses of Lydian mode.

## Section 10: Symmetric Scales—User Correction and Verification

**User Correction**: The claim "Dorian is the only diatonic mode that is perfectly symmetrical" was challenged with: "There's Javanese Celendro, chromatic scale, whole tone scale, and double harmonic scale."

**VERIFICATION PERFORMED**: All user-cited symmetric scales verified.

**Symmetric scales confirmed to exist** (outside diatonic modes):
1. **Whole-tone scale** (hexatonic): W–W–W–W–W–W (Wikipedia—Whole-tone scale; confirmed)
2. **Chromatic scale** (12-tone): all semitones (confirmed)
3. **Diminished scale/Octatonic** (8-tone): W–H–W–H–W–H–W–H or H–W–H–W–H–W–H–W (Wikipedia—Octatonic scale; confirmed)
4. **Double harmonic scale** (heptatonic): S–M–S–T–S–M–S (palindromic) (Wikipedia—Double harmonic scale; confirmed as "its steps form a palindrome")
5. **Augmented/hexatonic scales**: multiple symmetric hexatonic patterns exist
6. **Javanese Celendro**: five-note symmetric tuning (confirmed in gamelan scholarship)

**RESOLUTION**: The repository's claim is **ACCURATE AS WRITTEN** because it specifies "**diatonic mode**," which is the correct qualifier. Among the seven diatonic modes (rotations of the major scale), Dorian is indeed the only symmetric one. The distinction is important and should be preserved. Other symmetric scales exist outside the diatonic family, but the repository does not claim Dorian is the only symmetric scale in all of music—only among diatonic modes.
