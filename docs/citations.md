## Mode Theory

**[1]** Wikipedia—*Mode (music)*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music)  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Phrygian = natural minor with ♭2; Locrian = natural minor with ♭2 and ♭5; interval structure of all seven modes.

**[2]** Wikipedia—*Locrian mode*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locrian_mode  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Locrian interval formula H-W-W-H-W-W-W; tonic triad is diminished (B-D-F); historically "theoretical rather than practical" in Western music; maqam Lami identification.

**[3]** Splice Blog—*Locrian Mode: A Comprehensive Guide*  
https://splice.com/blog/music-modes-locrian/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: emotional palette (darkness, tension, instability, chaos); compositional advice on avoiding tritone stacking; "Dust to Dust" folk example.

**[4]** PDMusic—*What is the Locrian Mode*  
https://www.pdmusic.org/locrian-mode/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: degree-by-degree description of Locrian intervals and their emotional contributions; tritone as defining characteristic of b5.

**[5]** Musiversal—*Master Locrian Mode*  
https://musiversal.com/blog/master-locrian-mode  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: "think of Locrian not as a broken scale, but as a unique spice" framing; diminished tonic chord as source of unresolved power.

**[6]** Wikipedia—*Phrygian mode*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrygian_mode  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: historical context (Greek *harmonia*, Church Mode III); interval formula H-W-W-W-H-W-W; Phrygian dominant; use in Bach, Schütz, Palestrina, Tallis.

**[7]** Musiversal—*Master Phrygian Mode*  
https://musiversal.com/blog/master-phrygian-mode  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: ♭2 as primary source of dark/Spanish/heavy quality; tension toward tonic; comparison with Aeolian.

**[8]** Musical-U—*The Many Moods of Musical Modes*  
https://www.musical-u.com/learn/the-many-moods-of-musical-modes/

## Emotional Affect

**[9]** Gravity Sound Studio—*How Interval Music Theory Can Trick Your Brain*  
https://gravitysound.studio/blogs/news/how-interval-music-theory-can-trick-your-brain  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: interval-by-interval emotional mapping (minor 2nd = suspense/darkness; tritone = danger/wickedness; minor 6th = anguish/sadness; perfect 5th = stability/power).

**[10]** PMC / NIH—*Minor Second Intervals: A Shared Signature for Infant Cries and Sadness in Music*  
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9019334/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: empirical evidence that minor 2nd intervals statistically appear in infant crying vocalizations more than any other interval, suggesting cross-cultural biological link between ♭2 and sadness/distress.

**[11]** Ethan Hein Blog—*Scales and Emotions*  
https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: compact emotion-to-scale reference table; Phrygian = "tragic, exotic, Middle Eastern"; Locrian used over half-diminished chords.

**[12]** Musiversal—*How to Write Dark Chord Progressions*  
https://musiversal.com/blog/learn-dark-chord-progressions  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: tritone as "devil's interval—Diabolus in Musica"; minor 2nd for dark/mysterious/evil; chromatic mediants for unease.

**[13]** Ethan Hein Blog—*The Mystical Tritone*  
https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/the-mystical-tritone/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: tritone at heart of Western tonal tension/resolution system; usage in jazz, blues, film.

**[14]** FasterCapital—*The Role of Melodic Intervals in Music Composition*  
https://fastercapital.com/topics/the-role-of-melodic-intervals-in-music-composition.html  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: ascending major intervals = triumph/optimism; descending minor intervals = sadness/introspection.

## Baroque Techniques

**[15]** Wikipedia—*Baroque music*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroque_music  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Doctrine of Affections (*Affektenlehre*); recorder suited to "flat" keys, transverse flute to "sharp" keys; extensive ornamentation (trills, mordents) typically improvised; terraced dynamics.

**[16]** Fiveable—*Baroque Instruments and Ensembles*  
https://fiveable.me/history-music-1600-1750/unit-13  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Baroque performance practice (vibrato as ornament not constant effect; fingered articulation; continuo improvisation); French vs Italian styles.

**[17]** Tafelmusik—*Baroque 101*  
https://tafelmusik.org/explore-baroque/baroque-101/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Baroque wind instruments have conical bores and few/no keys; cross/forked fingerings give "veiled" sounds that Baroque composers used expressively; French style = detailed notated ornaments (*agréments*); Italian style = contrast (forte/piano, large/small groups).

**[18]** Louis Pressbooks—*Baroque and Classical Music (Doctrine of Affections)*  
https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Doctrine of Affections derived from ancient rhetoric; some movements express single affect; Bach could express different affects within a movement.

**[19]** OldFlutes.com—*Baroque Flutes*  
http://www.oldflutes.com/baroq.htm  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: Baroque flute suited to "tender music" (vs oboe for "lively music"—François Raguenet, 1702); trills with wide intervals preferred on Baroque flute; French instruments had expressive mellow low octaves.

Retrieved March 2026. Used for: NAF 6-hole = pentatonic minor; 7-hole adds extended note (half-step above tonic); variety of keys for different moods.

**[34]** NativeAmericanMuseum.info—*The Indigenous Melodies of the Americas*  
https://nativeamericanmuseum.info/flute-music-of-native-american-traditions/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: NAF traditionally non-tempered, allowing microtonal inflection; improvisation from immediate feelings/environment as core practice; overblowing for second octave.

**[35]** AT Flutes—*Pentatonic Scales and the Native American Style Flute*  
https://atflutes.com/information/learning/native-american-style-flute-pentatonic-scales  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: grace notes through cross-fingering; pentatonic eliminates dissonant combinations.

**[36]** Wikipedia—*Bawu*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bawu  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: bawu as free-reed aerophone with cylindrical bore; range typically an eleventh; lowest notes very rich in harmonics (buzzing, harmonica-like); upper range odd-harmonic dominated (clarinet-like); can inhale or exhale to produce sound.

**[37]** Organology.net—*Bawu*  
https://organology.net/instrument/bawu/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: expressive capabilities including vibrato, bending notes, glissandos; mellow, haunting tone; continuous air supply required; used heavily in *Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon* film score.

**[38]** FolkFluteWorld—*The Ultimate Guide to the Bawu and Hulusi*  
https://folkfluteworld.com/article_ultimate_guide_to_bawu_and_hulusi/the_ultimate_guide_to_bawu_and_hulusi_flutes.html  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: breath control for phraseology; embouchure experimentation; ornamentation (turns, trills, pitch bends) as prominent part of bawu style.

**[39]** Wikipedia—*Tin whistle*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tin_whistle  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: ornaments (cuts, strikes/taps, rolls, cranns, slides); legato with ornamental breaks rather than tonguing as default; leading tones via half-covering end hole.

**[40]** TradSchool—*The Irish Low Whistle*  
https://www.tradschool.com/en/irish-music/low-whistle/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: low whistle "warm or haunting" tone suited to airs and slow melodies; ornament execution more challenging on larger instrument; phrasing tends to be long and flowing.

**[41]** Wikipedia—*Low whistle*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_whistle  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: developed by Bernard Overton with Finbar Furey in the 1970s; "haunting and delicate sound"; Low D most common key.

**[42]** Wikipedia—*Kaval*  
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaval  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: fully open at both ends (end-blown, rim-blown); 8 playing holes (7 front + 1 thumb); four registers achievable on Bulgarian kaval; *kaba* technique = low note + octave harmonic simultaneously; circular breathing used; D register most common in Bulgaria.

**[43]** Zhivko Vasilev—*Range and Registers of the Bulgarian Kaval in D*  
https://zhivkovasilev.com/range-and-registers-of-the-bulgarian-kaval/  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: range nearly three full octaves on D kaval; four named registers (low, low-middle, high-middle, high); *kaba* mode produces fundamental + octave simultaneously.

**[44]** Robert Snider—*A Beginner's Guide to Playing the Kaval*  
http://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: *patter* = light articulation of held notes; Bulgarian Thracian style uses finger flips rather than tonguing for articulation; *kaba* highly esteemed technique.

**[45]** Grinnell College—*Bawu*  
https://omeka-s.grinnell.edu/s/MusicalInstruments/item/1532  
Retrieved March 2026. Used for: legato melodies enhanced with pitch bends and trills described as most idiomatic on bawu; usable range of major 9th.
