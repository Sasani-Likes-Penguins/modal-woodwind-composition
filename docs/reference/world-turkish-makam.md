## What Makes Turkish Makam Different from Arabic Maqam?

Turkish makam and Arabic maqam share the same word and many of the same scales, but they are distinct systems with different theoretical foundations and different expressive sensibilities. The key differences:

- **Microtone system**: Turkish makam uses the *koma*—a very small unit of pitch (about 1/9 of a whole tone)—divided into a 53-step octave. This is different from the Arabic quarter-tone system [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)
- **Seyir (melodic direction)**: Turkish makam theory explicitly defines the *direction* in which a melody must move—not just which notes to use, but how to travel through them [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)[20](https://musictales.club/article/phrygian-mode-classic-examples-phrygian-diatonic-scale-through-music-eras)
- **More formalized structure**: A Turkish makam, more than simply a selection of notes and intervals, is essentially a guide to compositional structure [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)

## The Seyir: The Melody's Required Journey

The most important concept in Turkish makam composition that has no parallel in Western music theory is the *seyir*—pronounced "say-ear"—meaning "route" or "path."

Every Turkish makam has a prescribed melodic direction. There are three types of seyir [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)[19](https://www.oldflutes.com/baroq.htm):

**Ascending seyir** (*çıkıcı*): the melody begins near the tonic, rises through the scale, explores the upper register, and cadences—comes home—from above. Uşşak makam has an ascending character.

**Descending seyir** (*inici*): the melody begins in the upper register, descends through the scale, and comes home from above. It enters from height and settles down. Muhayyer makam has a descending character.

**Ascending-descending seyir** (*inici-çıkıcı*): the melody begins in the middle register, rises to explore the upper register, then descends to the tonic. Hicaz makam follows this pattern.

**Why this matters for composition**: in Turkish classical music, a melody that uses the right notes but moves in the wrong direction is considered wrong—not merely unusual, but actually incorrect. The seyir is as essential as the notes themselves. The scale is no more than a frozen structure, a skeleton. The skeleton may gain life only through the seyir.

For a solo wind player: before composing in a Turkish makam, decide: does this melody begin low and climb? Begin high and descend? Begin in the middle and explore both directions before coming home? That decision shapes everything.

## Key Turkish Makam Notes

Each makam has three structurally important notes [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)[12](https://musiversal.com/blog/learn-dark-chord-progressions):

- **Karar (durak)**: the tonic—the home note, on which every piece must end
- **Güçlü**: the dominant—a secondary resting point, often a fourth or fifth above the tonic
- **Giriş**: the entry note—the note on which the melody characteristically begins

These three notes define the makam's "personality" even more than its scale does.

## The Most Important Turkish Makamlar

### Hicaz (Hijaz)
The most distinctively "Turkish" sound to Western ears. Contains the augmented second—the one-and-a-half step leap—in its lower tetrachord.

Scale (from A): A – B♭ – C♯ – D – E – F – G♯ – A

**Seyir**: ascending-descending—begins in the middle, rises, then descends home.

**Emotional world**: Hicaz is that stereotypic Arabic sound—but in Turkish context it sounds slightly different, more formal and classical, less folk-like. Dramatic, yearning, ceremonially intense.

**Kaval connection**: The Moldavian kaval's Hungarian-influenced scale includes intervals very similar to Hicaz. Playing Hicaz on the kaval feels natural rather than exotic.

### Uşşak
Often described as the "Turkish minor"—but warmer, with the characteristic microtonal second degree.

Scale (from A): A – B-half-flat – C – D – E – F – G – A (with B slightly flat—a koma below Western B♭)

**Seyir**: ascending—begins near the tonic and climbs.

**Emotional world**: introspective, like minor but with a warmth. Uşşak in Turkish is introspective, like minor but with a warmth. It is the makam of lovers (the word "Uşşak" literally means "lovers" in Turkish [16](https://fiveable.me/history-music-1600-1750/unit-13)).

### Rast
The Turkish Rast is similar to Arabic Rast but with a key difference: the third degree is a pure major third rather than a neutral third [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/). This makes Turkish Rast feel slightly more "major" than its Arabic counterpart.

**Seyir**: ascending.

**Emotional world**: happiness, tranquility; described as bringing happiness and tranquility to the hearer [18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/). Used for the afternoon and final calls to prayer [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/).

### Segâh
Contains microtonal intervals throughout, giving it a distinctively "floating" quality.

**Emotional world**: peaceful, meditative, almost otherworldly. To me, when it resolves by returning to the E microtonal flat, it is extremely peaceful, in both Turkish or Arabic [13](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/the-mystical-tritone/).

### Nihavend
Essentially the same as natural minor in Western terms—the Turkish classical tradition's equivalent of a standard minor scale, used as a point of familiar emotional reference.

**Emotional world**: familiar sadness; the most Western-adjacent of Turkish makamlar.

## How Turkish Makam Modulation Works

Turkish makam uses modulation—moving between makamlar—in a specific way. The most common technique:

1. Establish the home makam clearly
2. At the *güçlü* (dominant), borrow the characteristic tetrachord of a different makam
3. Use this borrowed sound briefly—a few measures—as an expressive color (*çeşni*, meaning "flavor")
4. Return to the home makam

Such typical modulations cannot be excluded from the broader understanding of makams although they are not included in the general, standard structure of the same makam, because they are modulations that are "appealing" since they add a certain loveable "flavour" to it.

For solo wind players: think of modulation as visiting a neighboring emotional world briefly—feeling its color—then returning home transformed by the visit.

## The Asma Karar: Suspended Cadence

One of Turkish makam's most expressive devices is the *asma karar*—a "suspended cadence" or temporary stopping point that is NOT the home note [12](https://musiversal.com/blog/learn-dark-chord-progressions).

Instead of resolving to the tonic at the end of a phrase, the melody pauses on a different note—creating a kind of "false landing" that feels momentarily complete but leaves the deeper resolution still to come. This is the musical equivalent of finishing a sentence with a comma instead of a period. The listener is held in expectation.

For solo wind: use the güçlü (dominant) or another designated stopping point from the makam's theory as your asma karar. End a phrase there instead of the tonic. Let silence follow. Then continue.

## The 13 Basic Makamlar

The 13 basic modes in Turkish music are: rast, çargah, hüseyni, neva, buselik, uşşak, kürdi, suzinak, hicaz, hümayun, uzzal, karçığar, zirgüle [19](https://www.oldflutes.com/baroq.htm). Beyond these, compound makamlar number in the hundreds—created by combining tetrachords from different basic makamlar.

## Composition Exercise: Hicaz on Kaval

**Setup**: D Hicaz on kaval = D – E♭ – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C♯ – D. Moderate tempo.

**Rules following seyir (ascending-descending)**:
1. Begin on A (the güçlü—middle of the scale)—not at the bottom.
2. From A, rise: A – B♭ – C♯ – D. Explore the upper register briefly.
3. Descend through the full scale: D – C♯ – B♭ – A – G – F♯ – E♭ – D.
4. The leap from E♭ to F♯ (ascending: the augmented 2nd) and from F♯ down to E♭ (descending) are the signature Hicaz gesture—play them with expressive weight.
5. The asma karar: at some point, end a phrase on G (the fourth degree, the güçlü of Hicaz) and hold silence there before continuing.
6. Final cadence: E♭ – D (the half-step approach from above to home).

**Goal**: a melody that begins with a sense of arrival in the middle of something already in progress, explores upward, then descends home with authority.

## Composing in Turkish Makam for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### The Seyir: Your Compositional Roadmap

The single most important concept in Turkish makam composition is the *seyir* (pronounced roughly "SAY-ir")—the prescribed melodic direction that every makam requires. Unlike Arabic maqam (where melodic direction is characteristic but not strictly required), Turkish makam theory mandates that each makam follow a specific ascending or descending path through its notes.

There are three seyir types:
- **Ascending seyir**: melody begins on or near the tonic, rises to the higher notes, and develops from there. Opens outward.
- **Descending seyir**: melody begins high (often on the fifth or higher) and settles progressively downward toward the tonic. Settles inward.
- **Ascending-descending seyir**: melody begins in the middle range, rises to the upper notes, then settles back toward the tonic. The most common and most structurally complete.

Before writing a note in any Turkish makam, determine its seyir and use it as your structural roadmap.

### Composing in Makam Hicaz: Full Step-by-Step

Hicaz is the most immediately recognizable Turkish makam to Western ears—the augmented second in the lower tetrachord creates the characteristic "Middle Eastern" leap. Seyir: ascending-descending.

**D Hicaz**: D – E♭ – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C – D

Key structural notes:
- **D** (karar—tonic): every composition ends here
- **G** (dominant/*güçlü*): the temporary resting point in the middle of the seyir; the "home away from home"
- **E♭** (the pressed note): the Phrygian-like half-step above D
- **F♯** (the leap note): the augmented second from E♭; the drama

**Step-by-step**:

1. **Begin in the middle range** (ascending-descending seyir starts middle): play G (the *güçlü*). Hold. Play A. Return to G. This middle-range opening establishes the upper anchor before the melody descends toward the tonic.

2. **Introduce the characteristic gesture**: descend from G: G – F♯ – E♭ – D. The F♯ → E♭ is the augmented second descending—a drop from the raised note into the pressed note. Then E♭ → D is the Phrygian half-step resolution home. These two consecutive emotionally charged intervals are the heart of Hicaz.

3. **Ascending phrase through the augmented second**: from D, ascend: D – E♭ – F♯. The upward leap (E♭ → F♯: one and a half steps upward) is fierce and dramatic. Continue: F♯ – G – A. The G arrival is the *asma karar* (suspended cadence)—a temporary rest on the dominant that feels incomplete.

4. **The *asma karar***: from A, use an ornamental figure around G: A – G – F♯ – G. This oscillation around G (approaching from above, settling briefly) is the *asma karar* in action—a cadence that arrives somewhere but does not close.

5. **Second phrase, upper range**: ascend to B♭ (the sixth). In Hicaz, B♭ is a characteristic upper note. From B♭: B♭ – A – G – F♯ – E♭ – D. The full descending scale in the upper range, settling back through the augmented second and Phrygian half-step to D.

6. **The final *karar***: the last phrase must end on D (the tonic) with unmistakable finality. The standard approach: G – F♯ – E♭ – D, played slowly and with full weight on each note, especially E♭ before its final resolution to D.

### Five Key Makamlar: Compositional Personalities

**Hicaz** (seyir: ascending-descending): the characteristic augmented second E♭ → F♯; fierce, dramatic, exotic; the most immediately recognizable Turkish makam.

**Uşşak** (seyir: descending): begins high (on the fifth A in D Uşşak), settles toward D through the characteristic neutral second (E♭ neutral); intimate, introspective, the "Turkish minor"; ends on D but the journey is a slow settling rather than a dramatic arrival.

**Rast** (seyir: ascending-descending): warm, almost major quality from the neutral third; noble and expansive; associated with formal ceremonial occasions; the "Turkish major" despite containing a neutral third.

**Nihavend** (seyir: ascending-descending): closest to Western natural minor among the common Turkish makamlar; emotionally accessible for beginners familiar with Western minor; a good entry point for learning makam structure without microtonal complexity.

**Hüzzam** (seyir: ascending-descending): uses the neutral second of Bayati maqam but with a different ascending-descending path; emotional register: profound, meditative, deeply sorrowful; the most emotionally serious of the commonly performed makamlar.

### The *Asma Karar*: Suspended Cadence as Compositional Tool

The *asma karar* (suspended cadence) is a distinctively Turkish compositional device: a temporary arrival on a note other than the tonic that feels like a comma in a sentence—a pause that signals continuation rather than conclusion.

In Hicaz (D tonic): the *asma karar* most commonly occurs on G (the fourth/güçlü). A phrase that arrives on G—especially with an ornamental figure like A – G – F♯ – G—sounds like it has completed a thought without finishing the sentence. The listener knows the music will continue.

**Using the asma karar compositionally**: plan one or two asma karar points in each composition. These are not endings—they are breathing points, moments where the energy pauses before continuing. Place them at the middle of the compositional arc (before the upper-range climax) and again before the final descent to the tonic.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Fierce dramatic tension (Hicaz)**: the augmented second E♭ → F♯ as an upward leap on accented beats; the descending G – F♯ – E♭ – D as phrase endings; moderate-fast tempo; strong dynamic contrasts.

**Intimate introspection (Uşşak)**: descending seyir—begin high, settle low; the neutral second E♭(neutral) – D as the emotional center; slow tempo; the asma karar on A (the fifth) for the primary breathing point.

**Noble warmth (Rast)**: ascending-descending; the neutral third given warmth by holding it slightly longer; moderate tempo with rhythmic clarity; phrases that reach to the upper octave and settle back through the neutral third to the tonic.

**Deep mourning (Hüzzam)**: slow tempo; very free rhythm; the characteristic phrase of Hüzzam (which descends through the neutral second area) as a recurring motif; long silences; end on the tonic with maximum weight.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Ignoring the seyir**: composing in Turkish makam without following the prescribed seyir produces melodies that use the right notes but sound wrong. The seyir is not a suggestion—it is the makam's identity. Uşşak begins high and settles; playing Uşşak starting from the tonic and rising immediately contradicts its structural logic.

**Treating the güçlü as just any note**: the *güçlü* (dominant) is a structural pillar of Turkish makam, not merely a note in the scale. Every composition should arrive at the güçlü deliberately, rest there briefly (asma karar), and then continue. Missing this structural feature makes the composition sound like an improvised melody rather than a makam composition.

**Ending on the wrong note**: every Turkish makam must end on the *karar* (tonic). Ending on any other note—however beautiful the phrase—violates the makam's structural contract with the listener.
