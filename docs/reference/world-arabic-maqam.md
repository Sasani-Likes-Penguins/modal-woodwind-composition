## What Is a Maqam?

The word *maqam* (plural: *maqamat*) means "place" or "position" in Arabic. A maqam is not simply a scale—it is a complete framework for melody: which notes are used, which note feels like home, how the melody should move through those notes, which characteristic phrases define the maqam's personality, and what emotional world the music inhabits [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music))[3](https://splice.com/blog/music-modes-locrian/).

It is sometimes said that each maqam evokes a specific emotion or set of emotions determined by the tone row and the nucleus. A maqam is, in a real sense, a complete musical personality—not just a collection of pitches.

Arabic maqamat are also microtonal: many of their notes fall between the notes of the Western piano, in the "cracks" between the keys. Many maqamat incorporate microtonal intervals, particularly quarter tones—pitches that lie between the notes of the Western chromatic scale. This means that the emotional character of a maqam cannot be fully captured on a fixed-pitch Western instrument, but can be approximated on the bawu (via pitch bending), the kaval (via embouchure adjustment), and the NAF (via half-holing).

## The Building Blocks: Jins (Ajnas)

The fundamental concept of Arabic maqam is the *jins* (plural: *ajnas*)—a short melodic cell of three, four, or five consecutive notes with a characteristic sound and feeling [3](https://splice.com/blog/music-modes-locrian/)[8](https://www.musical-u.com/learn/the-many-moods-of-musical-modes/).

Each maqam scale can be built by chaining together two (or in some cases, three) scale fragments or building blocks called a jins. The maqam gets its intervals, melodic behavior and overall mood from these constituent ajnas.

Think of *ajnas* as melodic DNA. Each *jins* has:
- Its own interval pattern (the specific distances between its notes)
- Its own emotional color
- Its own characteristic phrases—short melodic gestures that signal "we are here"
- A *ghammaz* (pivot point): the last note of the *jins*, from which the melody can jump to a new *jins* and thus a new maqam—this is how modulation works

The most important *ajnas* for a solo wind player to know:

| Jins | Notes (from root) | Emotional character | Key feature |
|------|------------------|--------------------|-----------  |
| **Rast** | root, whole, neutral 3rd, 4th | Noble, warm, calm | The neutral 3rd—between major and minor—is unique to this tradition [6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrygian_mode)[8](https://www.musical-u.com/learn/the-many-moods-of-musical-modes/) |
| **Bayati** | root, whole, neutral 3rd, 4th | Soulful, yearning, the "minor" of Arabic music [4](https://www.pdmusic.org/locrian-mode/) | Shares the neutral 3rd with Rast; darker feeling |
| **Hijaz** | root, half, augmented 2nd, 4th | Exotic, distant desert, dramatic tension [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music)) | The augmented 2nd (one-and-a-half steps) is the signature interval |
| **Kurd** | root, half, whole, 4th | Dark, grounded minor | Resembles Phrygian; commonly heard in folk music [13](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/the-mystical-tritone/) |
| **Nahawand** | root, whole, half, 4th | Minor, familiar to Western ears | Closest to Western natural minor [8](https://www.musical-u.com/learn/the-many-moods-of-musical-modes/) |
| **Saba** | root, neutral 2nd, half, diminished 4th | Profound sadness, pain, depth [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music))[6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrygian_mode) | Unique structure—doesn't complete a normal fourth |
| **Sikah** | neutral 3rd root, whole, neutral 3rd | Love, intimate feeling [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music)) | Unusual: starts on a neutral (microtonal) note |

## The Main Maqam Families

Maqamat are grouped into families based on which *jins* they start with [3](https://splice.com/blog/music-modes-locrian/). The most important families:

### Bayati Family
If Rast is the major scale of Arabic music, Bayati is the minor scale. Maqam Bayati is one of the most common maqamat. Its lower *jins* is Bayati (with the characteristic neutral 2nd and neutral 3rd); its upper *jins* is typically Nahawand or Rast.

**Emotional world**: vitality, joy, femininity (traditional description); deeply expressive, soulful, immediately evocative [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music)). When a Western listener hears "Middle Eastern music," Bayati is often what they are hearing.

**On your instruments**: Bayati on D = D, E-half-flat, F, G, A, B♭, C, D. The E-half-flat (a quarter-tone below E) is the signature note. On the kaval, this can be achieved by slight embouchure pressure. On the bawu, by bending from E. On the NAF, by half-covering the relevant hole.

### Rast Family
Maqam Rast has a calm, noble, and uplifting quality. It is the closest Arabic equivalent to a major feeling, though the neutral 3rd keeps it from being fully Western major.

**Emotional world**: pride, power, soundness of mind [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mode_(music)); open, bright, dignified.

### Hijaz Family
The Hijaz *jins*—root, half-step, augmented 2nd (one and a half steps), half-step—contains the most dramatic interval in Arabic music: the leap of an augmented 2nd. Hiçaz is that stereotypic Arabic sound that Western listeners immediately recognize.

**Emotional world**: distant, yearning, exotic, dramatic. The augmented 2nd creates an immediate sense of "elsewhere"—of distance, of the desert, of something ancient and other.

**On your instruments**: Hijaz on D = D, E♭, F♯, G. The jump from E♭ to F♯ (three half-steps in one leap) is the signature gesture. Available on bawu, recorder, and kaval.

### Saba
Maqam Saba stands alone—it is not part of a family with other maqamat, and its structure is genuinely unusual. The Saba maqam is considered unique and unusual both in terms of its melodic structure and in terms of its texture. Its distinctiveness makes it easy to identify and hard to confuse with any other maqamat.

Saba in Arabic means "yearning" and that correlates to the sound of the *jins*. It is very characteristic, using a quartertone on the second degree, and an augmented second between the diminished 4th and the perfect 5th.

**Emotional world**: the deepest sadness and pain in Arabic music. Saba is played for profound emotional occasions. It is not merely melancholy—it reaches into grief that has no name.

## How to Compose in a Maqam: The Key Principles

### 1. Learn the Characteristic Phrases First

The first thing you must do is forget about "scales." What we have in Middle Eastern music is a range of notes that can be mixed and played in many combinations. Our "scales" are made up of 3 to 5 notes of different intervals called jins.

Before trying to compose in a maqam, find the characteristic phrases—the short 3-5 note melodic gestures that define the maqam's personality. In Hijaz, this might be the leap from the half-step note to the augmented-2nd note. In Bayati, the characteristic settling into the neutral 3rd. These phrases are the maqam's identity.

### 2. The Ghammaz: Where to Modulate

Each *jins* has a pivot note—its last note—from which modulation can occur. The first jins always starts the maqam scale with its tonic at the 1st degree, and the second jins starts at the ghammaz (modulation point) of the first jins.

For solo wind players: rather than modulating to a different key (as in Western music), maqam modulation means shifting emphasis to a different *jins*—different characteristic notes, different emotional color—while staying within the same collection of pitches. This creates enormous expressive variety within a single piece.

### 3. End on the Tonic

A long musical piece can modulate over many maqamat but usually ends with the starting maqam (in rare cases the purpose of the modulation is to actually end with a new maqam).

The piece journeys away from home and returns. This is the fundamental narrative shape of Arabic classical music.

### 4. The Role of Microtones

The neutral 3rd of Rast and Bayati, the specific inflection of Saba's neutral 2nd—these are not approximations to Western notes but precise expressive choices. These emotions are said to be evoked in part through change in the size of an interval during a maqam presentation.

On instruments that allow pitch flexibility (bawu, kaval, NAF), approach microtonal notes by sliding or bending into them rather than landing on them cleanly. The emotional power is in the approach as much as the pitch itself.

## A Composition Exercise: Bayati on the Bawu

**Setup**: D Bayati = D, E-half-flat (bent upward from E♭), F, G, A, B♭, C, D. Slow tempo.

**Rules**:
1. Begin on D. Play the four-note Bayati *jins* (D–E-half-flat–F–G) slowly, feeling each note's weight.
2. Let the E-half-flat be the emotional center—approach it by bending slowly up from E♭, hold it briefly, then step to F or back to D.
3. Write a phrase of 5-6 notes using only the lower *jins* (D–G). End the phrase on G (the pivot note / ghammaz).
4. From G, shift to the upper *jins* (Nahawand on G = G–A–B♭–C). Explore this briefly.
5. Return to the lower *jins* and end on D.
6. The underblown A3 (quiet note below the bawu's main range) may be used as a very quiet phrase opening—a whispered arrival before the melody begins.

**Goal**: music that feels like searching—the characteristic emotional world of Bayati.

- **Fairouz**—recordings in Rast and Hijaz maqam; Lebanese classical style

## Further Reading and Related Topics

- The microtonal system of Arabic music requires extensive ear training to internalize; this guide can only describe it—it cannot replace listening.
- Regional variations (Egyptian, Syrian, Lebanese, Iraqi, Moroccan) mean that the same maqam may be performed differently in different contexts.
- The 72-maqam theoretical system is not a practical performance tool; most musicians work with 15-20 commonly performed maqamat.

## Composing in Arabic Maqam for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### Understanding What Maqam Is Before Composing in It

A maqam is not a scale. This is the single most important thing to understand before attempting to compose in Arabic maqam style.

A scale is a list of notes. A maqam is a complete modal personality: it includes a specific ascending pattern, a specific descending pattern (often different), characteristic phrases (*jins* cells), the most important note (*qarar*—tonic), secondary resting notes, required ornaments, microtonal inflections, and an emotional world. Two maqamat can use the same notes and sound completely different because of how those notes are used.

You cannot compose in maqam by playing a maqam scale. You compose in maqam by internalizing its specific melodic personality—its characteristic phrases, its required ornamental gestures, and its emotional world—and then speaking in that personality.

### The *Jins*: The Building Blocks

Every maqam is built from two overlapping *jins* (plural *ajnas*)—each a tetrachord or pentachord of three to five notes with its own characteristic phrase and emotional fingerprint. The maqam's identity comes from which *ajnas* it combines and where they overlap.

The three most important *ajnas* for beginners:

**Jins Bayati** (the most common lower jins in Arabic music): D – E♭ – F – G (or equivalent)
The characteristic feature: the neutral second (E♭—between E♭ and E♮, slightly flat of the Western E♭). This neutral second is the emotional soul of Bayati—soulful, searching, intimate.

**Jins Hijaz**: D – E♭ – F♯ – G (or equivalent)
The characteristic feature: the augmented second (E♭ → F♯, one-and-a-half steps). This leap is the dramatic, exotic feature of Hijaz—the "Middle Eastern" sound to Western ears.

**Jins Rast**: C – D – E♭(neutral) – F (or equivalent)
The characteristic feature: the neutral third (E♭—between minor and major third). Rast sounds warm, almost major, but with a characteristic color from the neutral third.

### Composing in Maqam Bayati: Step-by-Step for Beginners

**D Bayati**: D – E♭(neutral) – F – G – A – B♭ – C – D

The tonic is D. The most important note is D. The most emotionally charged note is E♭(neutral). The characteristic phrase is: E♭ – D (descending half-step) or F – E♭ – D (three-note descent through the characteristic interval).

**Step-by-step**:

1. **Establish D as home**: play D alone. Four beats. Then A (the fifth). Four beats. Back to D. This two-note rocking (tonic – fifth) is the starting point of almost every maqam improvisation.

2. **Introduce the characteristic interval**: step up to E♭(neutral). On bawu: play the E♭ with a slight upward pitch bend—do not land cleanly on standard E♭; approach it from slightly below and let it settle at its neutral pitch, between E♭ and E♮. The neutral quality is the emotional soul of Bayati.

3. **The characteristic phrase**: F – E♭(neutral) – D. This descending three-note phrase is the most important phrase in Bayati. It defines the maqam. Play it slowly. Let E♭ breathe before it resolves to D. This is the *yearning* of Bayati—the slight pressing of E♭ against D.

4. **Ascending phrase**: D – E♭ – F – G. Pause on G. The upper tetrachord feels more open. From G, descend back through the characteristic phrase: G – F – E♭ – D.

5. **The *ghammaz* (pivot note)**: A is the pivot note of Bayati—the note from which modulation to related maqamat occurs. For a solo composition without modulation, use A as a secondary resting point: phrases that ascend to A and then descend through the characteristic phrase back to D.

6. **Ascending to the upper octave**: D – E♭ – F – G – A – B♭ – C – D (high). Each note in the upper tetrachord (A – B♭ – C – D) should feel slightly more tense than the lower notes. The B♭ is the characteristic note of the upper tetrachord; dwell on it briefly before ascending to C and high D.

7. **The descending return**: from high D, descend slowly: D – C – B♭ – A. Pause on A. Then: A – G – F – E♭ – D. The characteristic phrase again, but after a long journey—it sounds like homecoming now, not just a phrase.

8. **Close**: D alone, then a long held silence.

### Microtonal Inflection: The Heart of Maqam

Without microtonal inflection, maqam becomes just a minor scale. The neutral intervals—the notes that fall between Western half-steps—are not optional colorings; they are the maqam's identity.

**For bawu players**: the bawu's pitch bending allows accurate neutral interval production. For E♭(neutral) in Bayati: play what you would normally call E♭ (standard Western E♭), then bend the pitch slightly upward—approximately 30–50 cents. The result is a pitch that sits between E♭ and E♮. Practice finding this pitch by ear against a recorded Bayati performance.

**For kaval players**: embouchure adjustment allows similar microtonal control. The neutral pitch is approximately 150 cents above D (standard E♭ is 100 cents above D; standard E♮ is 200 cents; the neutral is approximately halfway).

**For recorder and whistle players**: accurate quarter-tones are not achievable with standard technique. Partial hole coverage can approach them. In practical terms: play the nearest standard E♭ and treat it as an approximation. The characteristic phrase (F – E♭ – D) will still communicate Bayati's emotional quality even without precise microtonal accuracy.

### The Seven Maqamat: Compositional Personalities

| Maqam | Key characteristic | Primary emotion | Characteristic phrase |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Bayati | Neutral second above tonic | Soulful yearning, intimacy | F – E♭(neutral) – D |
| Hijaz | Augmented second in lower jins | Dramatic, exotic, distant | E♭ – F♯ – G (upward leap) |
| Rast | Neutral third; "warm major" | Noble warmth, optimism | E♭(neutral) – D – C |
| Saba | Diminished fourth (unusual structure) | Deepest grief, pain | The descent through ♭4 |
| Nahawand | Minor with leading tone | Familiar minor, expressive | G♯ – A (leading-tone arrival) |
| Kurd | Pure Phrygian | Ancient dark, medieval | E♭ – D (Phrygian sigh) |
| Sikah | Neutral starting note | Intimate love | Begins on neutral third (E♭ neutral) |

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Soulful yearning (Bayati)**: slow tempo; the characteristic phrase (F – E♭ – D) on every phrase ending; the neutral E♭ given full time before resolving; long silences between phrases; bawu or kaval rather than fixed-pitch instrument.

**Dramatic tension (Hijaz)**: the augmented second (E♭ → F♯) as an ascending leap on phrase peaks; approach E♭ from below (D → E♭) before leaping to F♯; the drama comes from the preparation and the unexpected brightness of F♯ after the E♭.

**Deepest grief (Saba)**: the most emotionally intense maqam in the Arabic tradition; characterized by a diminished fourth in the scale structure; phrases that descend into the darkest register; very slow; almost no ornament—the scale's structure is distressing enough without embellishment.

**Warm nobility (Rast)**: the neutral third makes it sound "almost major but not quite"; moderate tempo; ascending phrases to the fifth and sixth; the warmth comes from the major-adjacent brightness of Rast's neutral third; appropriate for music that is confident but not harsh.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Treating maqam as a Western scale**: playing all the notes of Bayati uniformly, as if it were a minor scale, produces something that sounds like a Western scale with a wrong note (E♭ instead of E). Playing Bayati requires dwelling on D (home), using E♭(neutral) as the emotional center, and employing the characteristic phrase as a recurring motif.

**Ignoring microtonal inflection**: the neutral intervals are not optional. Without them, Bayati becomes Western minor. Even an approximation of the neutral quality (a slight pitch bend on the bawu, a careful partial hole on the whistle) is far better than playing a clean equal-tempered E♭.

**Playing too many notes**: Arabic maqam improvisation (*taksim*) is characterized by long melodic lines with few notes per phrase but rich ornamentation on each note. Beginners tend to play busily, filling time with notes. Play fewer notes; ornament each one more richly; leave more silence.

**Forgetting to return home**: every maqam improvisation must end on the tonic (D in D Bayati). The entire journey of the taksim—however far it travels—must return to D with a clear, unambiguous arrival. This is not optional; it is the structural grammar of the maqam.
