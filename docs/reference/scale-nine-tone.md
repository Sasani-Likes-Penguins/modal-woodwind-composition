## What Is It?

A nine-tone (nonatonic) scale is any scale containing nine distinct pitch classes within an octave. There is no single "the" nine-tone scale—the term describes a category of dense chromatic pitch collections rather than one specific scale.

The most musically useful and commonly encountered nine-tone scale in the context of this repository is the **blues nonatonic collection**: the combination of the major blues scale and the minor blues scale into a single pitch set. This gives the player access to both the brighter major-blues colors and the darker minor-blues colors simultaneously, with the blue note chromatic passing tones between them.

---

## The Blues Nonatonic Collection

**Minor blues scale**: 1 – ♭3 – 4 – ♭5 – 5 – ♭7 (six notes)

**Major blues scale**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – 3 – 5 – 6 (six notes)

**Combined (A blues nonatonic)**: A – B – C – C♯ – D – E♭ – E – G – (A)

The nine distinct pitches: A, B, C, C♯, D, E♭, E, G

Note that this collection contains:
- Both the minor third (C) and the major third (C♯) of A
- Both the flat fifth (E♭, the blue note) and the perfect fifth (E)
- The major second (B), the perfect fourth (D), and the flat seventh (G)
- Missing: F♯ (major sixth) and B♭ (minor seventh—except the G already represents the ♭7)

The combined collection gives access to the entire expressive vocabulary of blues—the chromatic sliding between major and minor third (C → C♯), the blue note approach to the fifth (E♭ → E), and the open-ended flat seventh (G).

## How It Works in Practice

The nine-tone collection is not intended to be played as a scale—ascending from root to octave using all nine notes. That would sound like a chromatic exercise. Instead, the collection defines the **available pitch palette** from which melody is drawn in an improvisational context:

- **Structural notes** (the harmonic backbone): A, E (root and fifth), D (fourth), G (flat seventh)
- **Characteristic blue notes** (expressive colors): C and C♯ (both thirds, slid between), E♭ (the blue note approach to the fifth)
- **Passing tones** (connecting material): B (major second), between structural notes

The blue note (E♭ in the key of A) is always a *passing* note—it slides through, not rests on. Play it as a bend approaching E from below, never as a sustained pitch. See [Ornamentation Guide](ornamentation-guide.md) for how to execute this on each instrument.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Saturated, chromatically rich | Both major and minor thirds available simultaneously; the full harmonic complexity of blues |
| Searching and bittersweet | The chromatic passing tones create constant motion between brightness and darkness |
| Deeply expressive | The collection contains more expressive resources per octave than almost any seven-note scale |
| Improvisational | The collection is most naturally explored through improvisation, not written melody |

The nine-tone collection does not feel like a scale—it feels like a *language*. Each note in the collection has a specific expressive function (structural, characteristic, passing), and the skill of blues playing is knowing which function each note is serving in each moment.

## Instrument Access

The nine-tone blues collection is accessible on all chromatic instruments and partially accessible on flexible-pitch instruments.

| Instrument | Ease | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Bawu | Excellent | The most natural instrument for blues nonatonic; pitch bending handles C/C♯ sliding and the E♭→E blue note approach organically |
| Alto recorder | Good | All nine notes available via standard and cross fingerings |
| Low D whistle | Moderate | E blues nonatonic is most accessible in the key of D; the blue note (B♭) via half-hole |
| Kaval | Good | Pitch inflection available for blue note approaches |
| NAF | Good partial access | Native pentatonic covers the structural and some characteristic notes; blue note via half-holing |

**The bawu as the blues instrument**: of all instruments in this collection, the bawu's pitch-bending capability most closely matches the expressive vocabulary of blues. The simultaneous C/C♯ sliding (playing between the minor and major third) that is central to blues expression is physically natural on the bawu in a way it is not on recorder or whistle. See [Blues Scales](scale-blues.md) for the full blues treatment.

## Composing with the Nine-Tone Blues Collection

**Think in functions, not scale degrees**: rather than thinking "what note do I play next in the scale," ask: "what is this note doing—is it structural (landing), characteristic (expressive color), or passing (in motion)?"

**Structural notes carry weight**: A, E, D, G in the key of A are the notes that phrases settle on and depart from. They have rhythmic weight—they arrive on strong beats and are held.

**Characteristic notes have color**: C, C♯, E♭ are expressive colors applied to the structural notes. They often neighbor or approach structural notes. C and C♯ are both available—slide between them on the bawu, or choose one based on the emotional moment (C♯ for brightness; C for darkness).

**The E♭ blue note is always in motion**: never sustain E♭ as a resting note. Always approach or leave it. The classic gesture: D (fourth, structural) → E♭ (blue note, in motion) → E (fifth, structural). This is the foundational blues expressive gesture.

**Respond to what you just played**: blues phrasing is fundamentally conversational—question and answer, call and response. A phrase that ends on C (minor, dark) invites a response phrase that uses C♯ (major, bright). The nine-tone collection gives both colors constantly available.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu (A Blues Nonatonic)
**A blues collection**: A, B, C, C♯, D, E♭, E, G (nine notes)

*This exercise practices the fundamental blues gesture and its variations:*

1. **The blue note gesture** (core): D – E♭ – E – A (fall back to root). Play D clearly (structural fourth). Bend slowly upward from E♭ into E (the blue note approach). Settle on E. Then drop to A.
2. **The third gesture**: C (dark, hold) → slide to C♯ (bright). On bawu, this is a slow pitch bend upward from C to C♯. Feel the emotional shift between minor and major.
3. **Combine**: A (root, structural) – C (minor third, dark) – C♯ (bend to major, bright) – D – E♭ – E – A. This is a complete blues phrase in the key of A: root, minor third, bend to major, fourth, blue note, fifth, back home.
4. Play this phrase twice. The third time, change one note—arrive at G (flat seventh) instead of A at the end. The open flat seventh ending creates an implied question: more to come.

**Goal**: fluency with the blues expressive vocabulary—not a melody in the conventional sense, but a language of expressive gesture.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**The ache of the blue note**: the expressive heart of the nine-tone blues collection is the chromatic passing motion through the blue note (E♭ in A blues). To elicit the characteristic blues ache: approach E♭ from D (below) with a slow pitch bend upward, hold at E♭ briefly with a slight downward pressure in the tone (as if the note is sagging under its own weight), then release upward into E (the fifth). This single gesture—approach, hold, release—is the blues vocabulary in miniature.

**Searching restlessness**: the nine-tone collection contains both C (minor third) and C♯ (major third) of A, both available for use. Alternating between them—C for darkness, C♯ for unexpected brightness—creates the characteristic blues mood of searching without finding. Neither the minor nor the major third settles; the music keeps testing both.

**Improvisational freedom**: the nine-tone collection is too large to use systematically—it is a palette. When improvising, trust the feeling of the moment to choose which of the nine pitches serves the phrase. The structural notes (A, E, D, G) provide the framework; the chromatic notes (C♯, E♭, passing B) provide color. Let the improvisation itself determine when each color appears.

### A Beginner's Framework for Blues Improvisation

Beginners approaching blues improvisation often feel overwhelmed by not knowing which note to play next. The nine-tone collection simplifies this by providing a priority system:

**Priority 1 (always safe to land on)**: A (root), E (fifth), D (fourth), G (flat seventh). These are structural notes—any phrase can begin, end, or pause on any of these. They create no dissonance and always feel settled.

**Priority 2 (expressive colors, approach and leave)**: C (minor third), C♯ (major third). Choose based on feeling: C for darkness, C♯ for warmth. These can be sustained briefly but feel more expressive when arrived at with a slight bend or approached from a neighbor note.

**Priority 3 (in motion only)**: E♭ (blue note). Never land on this as a resting pitch. Always approach it and leave it—the gesture is the approach (from below) and the departure (upward to E). The blue note is always traveling.

**The one rule of blues phrasing**: after every phrase, leave space. A phrase, then silence. Then a response phrase. The silence is where the listener fills in the emotional residue. Without the silence, blues becomes a busy wall of notes rather than a conversation.

## Common Beginner Mistakes with the Nine-Tone Collection

**Trying to play all nine notes in every phrase**: the nine-tone blues collection is a *palette*, not a melody. Phrases should draw from 3–5 of the nine notes, not all nine. A phrase that visits every pitch in the collection sounds like a chromatic exercise. Choose the notes that serve the emotional moment of that specific phrase.

**Treating chromatic passing tones as structural notes**: C♯ (the major third), E♭ (the blue note), and B (the major seventh) are chromatic passing tones within the A blues collection. They color the structural notes (A, E, D, G, C) but cannot replace them. Phrases built primarily on the passing tones rather than the structural notes feel harmonically unstable and directionless.

**No silence between phrases**: the nine-tone collection's richness (nine pitches!) tempts beginners to fill every moment with notes. The blues tradition that created this vocabulary is defined by space—a phrase, silence, a response. Without silence, the nine-note richness becomes density rather than expression.
