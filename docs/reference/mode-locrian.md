## What Locrian Is

Locrian is the seventh and last of the seven [diatonic](../glossary.md#diatonic) modes—and the most unusual one. It shares Phrygian's half-step above the [tonic](../glossary.md#tonic), and adds a second destabilizing interval: the fifth above the tonic is also lowered by a half-step, creating a [tritone](../glossary.md#tritone)—the most dissonant interval in Western music.

The result is the only diatonic mode where the chord built on the home note is [diminished](../glossary.md#dissonance)—unstable, restless, unresolved. There is no stable harmonic home in Locrian. There is no place to fully arrive.

This is not a flaw. It is the mode's fundamental nature—and its most powerful expressive tool.

## The Interval Structure

**Step pattern**: Half – Whole – Whole – Half – Whole – Whole – Whole

**B Locrian** (the white-key example):

| Degree | Note | Distance from previous |
|--------|------|----------------------|
| 1 (tonic) | B |—|
| ♭2 | C | Half-step |
| ♭3 | D | Whole step |
| 4 | E | Whole step |
| ♭5 ([tritone](../glossary.md#tritone)) | F | Half-step |
| ♭6 | G | Whole step |
| ♭7 | A | Whole step |
| 8 (octave) | B | Whole step |

**Relationship to Phrygian**: Locrian is [Phrygian](mode-phrygian.md) with one additional change—the fifth degree is lowered by a half-step. Every other note is identical. Think of Locrian as Phrygian with the floor removed: all the dark, pressing quality of Phrygian's half-step-above-tonic, plus the additional sensation that the stable foundation you might have stood on has given way.

**The home chord**: in most modes, the chord built on the tonic is either major (stable, bright) or minor (stable, dark). In Locrian, the tonic chord is B–D–F—a diminished chord, containing the tritone B–F. This chord has no stable fifth. It is inherently restless, always wanting to move somewhere else. This is what makes Locrian fundamentally different from all other diatonic modes.

## Why Locrian Is Difficult

The standard objection to Locrian is that it cannot function as a tonal center because the tonic chord provides no stability. This is true in a harmonic (chord-based) context. A piece with Locrian chords throughout will feel like it never arrives home—which is exactly what happens in most jazz uses of Locrian (the [half-diminished chord](scale-bebop-half-diminished.md) context).

But this objection applies to *harmonic* Locrian—multiple voices or instruments sounding the tonic chord together. For **solo melodic wind playing**, the harmony is implied, not sounded. A single melodic line in Locrian can be deeply beautiful because the listener hears the mode's character through the note choices and their relationships, not through a simultaneously sounding diminished chord.

The key insight: **avoid sounding the ♭5 and the tonic simultaneously.** Keep them melodic—sequential, not concurrent. Then the tritone functions as a point of extraordinary tension in the melody rather than a clashing harmonic nightmare.

## The One Stable Note

The [perfect fourth](../glossary.md#perfect-fourth) above the tonic (the 4th degree—E in B Locrian, G in D Locrian) is the one relatively stable consonance in Locrian. It is the only note that does not form a dissonant relationship with the tonic.

Use the fourth degree as a **resting point**—a temporary plateau in an otherwise restless melodic landscape. Phrases can breathe here before venturing further. The pattern: tonic → tense exploration → arrive at the fourth → rest briefly → continue.

## Emotional Character

Locrian inhabits emotional territory that no other Western diatonic mode can reach:

| Emotion | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Profound, sustained unease | The ♭5 as a passing melodic note; irregular rhythm; sudden silences |
| Resigned darkness | Phrase anchored on the fourth degree; stepwise Locrian melody; sustained soft dynamics |
| Standing at a threshold | Single unaccompanied melodic voice; wide dynamic range; hold the ♭5 in the middle register; let silence follow |
| The unresolvable | End on the ♭2 or ♭5 rather than the tonic; accept the lack of resolution as the emotional statement |
| Profound grief without catharsis | Slow tempo; the ♭2 falling to the tonic (Phrygian's sigh figure, still available in Locrian); but the overall harmonic instability prevents the sigh from fully resolving |

What Locrian expresses is not merely sadness—Aeolian does that better. It is not merely darkness—Phrygian does that with more fire. Locrian expresses the state of something that does not resolve, cannot resolve, and has accepted this. It is the music of the permanently open question.

## How to Make Locrian Sound Beautiful

Beautiful Locrian is possible, demonstrated by John Kirkpatrick's "Dust to Dust"—a folk piece that sounds genuinely lovely while maintaining a Locrian tonal center. His approach provides the clearest model:

**1. Single melodic voice**: no chords, no accompaniment. A single melodic line avoids the harmonic clash of the diminished tonic chord entirely. The listener hears the mode through the note sequence, not through a sounded chord.

**2. Approach tense notes by step, not by leap**: if you leap from the tonic directly to the ♭5, the tritone relationship is stark and harsh. If you step through ♭2, ♭3, 4, ♭5—arriving at the tritone note gradually—it sounds like a discovery, not a collision.

**3. The fourth degree as anchor**: use the fourth (E in B Locrian) generously. It provides rest without committing to the unstable tonic. Phrases can end here, begin here, and return here between more tense moments.

**4. Silence as structural**: Locrian's lack of resolution makes silence more powerful here than in any other mode. A phrase that ends on the ♭5, followed by four beats of silence, creates a moment of suspended tension that is deeply moving. The listener is held in the "wanting" phase—see [Brain Biology and Psychology](brain-biology-psychology.md).

**5. Dynamic restraint**: playing Locrian loudly tends to emphasize the dissonance. Softer dynamics allow the mode's tension to be felt as emotional depth rather than harmonic clash.

**6. Locrian as Phrygian with added color**: many effective Locrian passages are fundamentally Phrygian melodies that occasionally touch the ♭5 as a passing note or a held color—rather than making the ♭5 a structural pillar. The ♭5 appears as a shadow over an otherwise Phrygian landscape.

## Locrian Across Traditions

### Western Art Music

Locrian was recognized in medieval theory but almost never used as a primary mode in Western art music—the unstable tonic chord made it theoretically awkward. This remained true through the common-practice era (1650–1900).

### Jazz

Locrian appears extensively in jazz as the scale used over [half-diminished chords](scale-bebop-half-diminished.md) (also called minor 7♭5 chords). The ii chord in a minor ii-V-i progression is typically half-diminished, and Locrian or Locrian ♮2 is played over it. This is not Locrian as a tonal center but Locrian as a modal color for a specific harmonic function.

Bill Evans and other jazz pianists developed the expressive vocabulary of melodic lines over half-diminished chords—this is the jazz entry point into Locrian thinking.

### Folk Music

John Kirkpatrick's "Dust to Dust" is the clearest example of Locrian used as a beautiful, singable folk mode. His approach—described above—is the practical model for folk and world music applications of Locrian.

### Contemporary and Experimental Music

Some composers since the mid-20th century have used Locrian as a primary modal center, embracing its lack of resolution as a compositional statement rather than a technical problem. When the emotional intention is "the permanently unresolved," Locrian is the correct tool.

### Connection to Other Systems

The tritone relationship in Locrian has parallels in world music: the [Maqam Saba](world-arabic-maqam.md) in Arabic music has an unusual diminished fourth in its structure, giving it similarly deep, unresolved emotional character. The concept of music that does not resolve—that remains in tension—is not foreign to world music traditions that Western classical music avoided.

## Instrument Access

| Instrument | Locrian key | ♭5 available? | Notes |
|-----------|------------|--------------|-------|
| Alto recorder | Any key | Yes | Fully chromatic; the ♭5 requires cross-fingering in most keys |
| Tenor recorder | Any key | Yes | Fully chromatic |
| NAF (Am) | Locrian feel via phrasing | Not as sustained note | Pentatonic scale avoids tritone—actually helpful for melodic Locrian |
| Bawu | D Locrian | Yes (A♭)—check instrument | A♭4 stability varies; approach via pitch bend |
| Low D whistle | B Locrian (via cross-fingering) | F is natural on D whistle | In B Locrian, F (♭5 of B) is available as natural fingering |
| G whistle | E Locrian | B♭ requires half-hole | |
| Moldavian kaval | D Locrian | A♭ available | Approach via microtonal bend; most expressive Locrian access |

**Special note on NAF for Locrian**: the NAF's pentatonic scale naturally avoids the tritone (the ♭5 of Locrian is not a native scale degree). This is actually an advantage for melodic Locrian—the NAF player can evoke Locrian through phrasing, rhythm, and dynamic without sounding the harsh tritone-against-tonic relationship. This is the "Locrian in spirit" approach.

## Composing in Locrian: Practical Techniques

### The Fundamental Question

Before writing in Locrian, ask: does this piece need the *absence of resolution* as its central emotional statement? If yes, Locrian is correct. If the emotion could be expressed by a mode that has a stable resting place, consider whether Locrian is the right choice—its power comes from what it lacks, and that should be what the piece is about.

### Melodic Development Techniques

**Use the fourth as home**: begin phrases on the fourth degree (E in B Locrian, G in D Locrian), end phrases there, return to it. The fourth is not the tonic but it is stable enough to rest on—and the fact that it is not the tonic reinforces the Locrian sense of never fully arriving.

**Touch the ♭5 by approach, not by leap**: step through the scale to arrive at the tritone note. When it arrives by step, it sounds like the melody discovering something—unexpected but not violent.

**Hold the ♭5 in silence**: after reaching the ♭5, stop. Hold the silence. This is Locrian's most powerful compositional gesture. The listener sits in the held tension of the tritone for the duration of the silence. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](brain-biology-psychology.md).

**The ♭2 as a shared Phrygian gesture**: the ♭2 (half-step above the tonic) exists in Locrian as it does in Phrygian. The sigh figure (♭2 → 1) is still available—and in Locrian context, it sounds like brief Phrygian relief within the larger Locrian instability. A moment of almost-arriving before the ♭5 reminds the listener that there is no true home.

**End on the unresolved**: Locrian compositions may end on the ♭2, the ♭5, the fourth, or the ♭7—anywhere except the tonic (which would feel falsely arrived at) or a tritone-against-tonic sonority (which would feel merely dissonant). The fourth degree is the most beautiful ending note: stable enough to rest, but not the tonic, so the resolution is incomplete and accepted.

## Repertoire and Recordings to Study

### Folk and Traditional

- **John Kirkpatrick**—"Dust to Dust" (English folk, Locrian): the primary model for beautiful Locrian in a folk context; demonstrates how single-voice melodic Locrian avoids the harmonic clash; essential listening before composing in this mode

### Jazz

- **Bill Evans**—recordings over half-diminished chords: melodic vocabulary for the jazz Locrian context; the most lyrical available approach to this harmonic territory
- **Miles Davis**—"All Blues": not pure Locrian but demonstrates the expressive vocabulary of the blues scale's ♭5 region
- Any **ii-V-i minor** progression: the ii chord is half-diminished; hear how players navigate the Locrian/half-diminished territory

### Classical (Brief Locrian Use)

- **Beethoven**—String Quartet No. 15 (Op. 132), 3rd movement: Lydian, not Locrian—but demonstrates how a modal choice can make a piece sound ancient and sacred; the contrast with Locrian is instructive
- Contemporary composers exploring atonality and non-resolution often touch Locrian territory without naming it explicitly

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu (D Locrian)  
**Affect**: standing at a threshold—the feeling of something about to happen that cannot be reversed

**D Locrian scale**: D–E♭–F–G–A♭–B♭–C–D  
(Note: A♭ is the ♭5—the tritone; G is the fourth—the stable resting note)

**Steps**:
1. Begin on G (the fourth—the stable note). Soft. Hold for three beats.
2. Step down to F (the third). Step down to E♭ (the ♭2). Step down to D (tonic). You have descended through Locrian to home—but home feels uncertain.
3. From D, step up through the scale slowly: D–E♭–F–G–A♭. Arrive at A♭ (the ♭5). Do not rush to it. When you arrive, sustain it. Use a slight pitch bend—approach the A♭ from slightly below, let it settle. Hold for four beats.
4. Silence for four beats. Do not fill this. The A♭ should still be sounding in the listener's ear.
5. From the silence, step down: A♭–G. Arrive at G and rest. This is the most stable moment of the piece—the fourth after the ♭5, a resting place that is not home.
6. From G, descend back to D through E♭: G–F–E♭–D. End on D, softly. Use the underblown A3 as a final whispered note if it suits the emotional context—the music retreating below its own foundation.

**Goal**: a piece that spends most of its time searching for a place to rest, briefly finds one (the fourth), and returns to a tonic that does not feel like home. The emotional truth of Locrian—accepted rather than resolved.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Locrian

**Perpetual unease**: moderate-slow tempo; phrases that visit the ♭5 (tritone) as a passing note once per phrase—arrived at by step, never by leap; after each ♭5 arrival, silence for 2–3 beats before the next phrase; the music never settles on the tonic or the fourth for more than one beat; the ♭2 (Phrygian half-step) adds a second pressing quality; everything is movement without arrival.

**Resigned acceptance of the unresolvable**: slow tempo; the fourth degree (E in B Locrian, G in D Locrian) as the primary resting point rather than the tonic; long phrases that reach the ♭5 and then step back down to the fourth (not the tonic)—settling on the second-most-stable note rather than home; the final note of the composition on the fourth rather than the tonic; the music has accepted that it cannot come home.

**Profound, still darkness**: very slow; sparse—two or three notes per phrase; only the bottom three degrees of the scale (tonic, ♭2, ♭3) used in the first half of the piece; the ♭5 introduced only in the second half as a single, held note followed by long silence; no dynamic above mezzo-piano; this is darkness that is too complete to be dramatic.

**Threshold and liminality**: the sensation of standing at the edge of something—not fear, but the stillness of a boundary; the ♭5 as the edge itself; phrases that move toward the ♭5 and stop, or approach it and retreat; the music hovering near the ♭5 without fully arriving; the fourth degree as the landing place after each approach.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Locrian

**Trying to make Locrian "work" like other modes**: Locrian is not a mode that resolves or settles in the conventional sense. Beginners often try to make it sound like Phrygian (adding ornaments that imply Phrygian character) or to give it a tonic-feeling arrival. Embrace the instability rather than correcting it. The mode's purpose is to express states that do not resolve.

**Playing the ♭5 too frequently**: the tritone (♭5) is the most dissonant interval in the mode. Used in every phrase, it becomes numbing. Used sparingly—once or twice per composition, as a carefully placed arrival—it retains maximum impact. Plan your ♭5 moments; do not let them happen by accident.

**Ignoring the fourth as the resting point**: because the tonic chord in Locrian is diminished (unstable), the tonic itself is not a comfortable resting point. The fourth degree (E in B Locrian) is the only relatively stable note in the mode. Use it as a resting point. Phrases should arrive at the fourth as their stable point, not the tonic.

**Overly fast tempo**: Locrian's instability requires time to register. At fast tempos, the ♭5 and ♭2 become passing notes rather than expressive events. Slow down. Let each note's relationship to the tonic (which is itself unstable) register fully before moving to the next.

**Avoiding Locrian and writing Phrygian instead**: Locrian and Phrygian differ by one note—the ♭5. Beginners who are uncomfortable with the tritone instability sometimes unconsciously use the natural fifth (which converts Locrian to Phrygian). The natural fifth makes the music more comfortable—and loses the entire point. Use the diminished fifth deliberately.
