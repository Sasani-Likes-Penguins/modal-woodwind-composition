## How to Listen

Before approaching any piece in this list as a compositional model, listen to it multiple times without analysis. The first listening is for feeling. Let the mode's emotional world settle. On later listenings, identify the seed phrases, the characteristic notes, the ornaments, the phrase shapes, the silences.

Ask: where does this melody breathe? What note does it return to most often? Where is the emotional peak? How does it arrive there? What happens after the peak? What ornaments appear on the most important notes? Could you sing this back?

If you can sing it back—even imperfectly—you understand it compositionally. If you cannot, listen again.

## Western Classical: Modal and Expressive Pieces

### Renaissance Modal Polyphony
- **Palestrina**—*Canticum Canticorum* motets (Phrygian): the model of smooth, stepwise modal melody; every note arrives by step; tensions resolve gently; a masterclass in patience
- **Thomas Tallis**—*"Why fum'th in sight"* (G Phrygian): one of the purest Renaissance Phrygian melodies; Vaughan Williams made it famous
- **Orlando di Lasso**—*Prophetiae Sibyllarum* (chromatic modal): Renaissance modal composition pushed toward maximum harmonic ambiguity; useful for understanding how far modal language can be stretched

### Baroque
- **J.S. Bach**—*Toccata and Fugue in D minor, BWV 538* ("The Dorian"): large-scale Baroque Dorian; the characteristic notes of the mode as structural pillars
- **J.S. Bach**—*Chaconne from Partita No. 2 in D minor*: 15 minutes of solo violin in D Aeolian/harmonic minor; the most complete study in how a single mode sustains a large-scale composition
- **Purcell**—*"Dido's Lament"*: Aeolian over a chromatic descending bass; the passus duriusculus as grief foundation; essential study
- **Handel**—*Messiah* arias: major-mode Baroque expression at its most direct; for the contrast with darker modes

### Classical and Romantic
- **Beethoven**—*String Quartet Op. 132*, third movement "Heiliger Dankgesang" (Lydian): the most emotionally significant Lydian piece in the repertoire; sacred and transcendent
- **Beethoven**—*"Moonlight" Sonata*, first movement (C♯ Aeolian/Harmonic Minor): slow, quiet, devastating; model for how stillness carries emotion
- **Schubert**—*"Der Erlkönig"* (D Harmonic Minor): relentless minor momentum; the augmented second appears in the accompaniment; dramatic Harmonic Minor at its peak
- **Brahms**—*Symphony No. 4*, second movement (Phrygian): noble, prayer-like; the mode as something dignified rather than fierce
- **Vaughan Williams**—*Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis* (G Phrygian): the most beautiful orchestral Phrygian piece; long arching Phrygian melody sustained across a vast emotional space

### 20th Century
- **Bartók**—*Romanian Folk Dances*: Romanian Minor and Dorian from folk sources; the most accessible Bartók entry point
- **Bartók**—*String Quartet No. 4*: Balkan modal material in rigorous structure; harder but essential
- **Debussy**—*"Voiles"* (Whole-Tone and Pentatonic): the defining Impressionist modal atmosphere; near-total Whole-Tone for most of the piece
- **Debussy**—*"Syrinx"* (solo flute): the most important solo wind piece in this list; E♭ Phrygian-adjacent modal melody; every solo wind player should know it
- **Arvo Pärt**—*Spiegel im Spiegel* (violin and piano): tintinnabuli technique; silence as structure; essential for understanding sparse modal composition
- **Arvo Pärt**—*Fratres*: tintinnabuli in A Aeolian; multiple arrangements available

## Folk and Traditional: Celtic, Balkan, Eastern European

### Irish and Scottish
- **Planxty**—*"The Jolly Beggar"* (D Mixolydian): the defining recording of Mixolydian in Celtic traditional music; the flat seventh appearing and disappearing through the melody
- **Chieftains**—any album of slow airs: the model for Irish slow air phrasing; breath as structure; ornament as grammar
- **"She Moved Through the Fair"**: D Mixolydian; the most melodically beautiful Mixolydian Irish folk song; recorded by Planxty, Mary Black, and many others
- **"Scarborough Fair"**: D Dorian; the raised sixth defining the mode; Simon and Garfunkel version is most widely known; traditional versions even more direct
- **Tommy Potts**—*"The Liffey Banks"* slow air: extraordinary ornamental Irish uilleann pipe slow air; ornaments as the melody rather than additions to it

### Balkan and Eastern European
- **Taraf de Haïdouks**—*Honourable Brigands, Magic Horses and Evil Eye*: Romanian folk violin; Hungarian Minor and Romanian Minor in their native context; passionate, asymmetric, fierce
- **Muzsikás**—*The Lost Jewish Music of Transylvania*: Hungarian, Romanian, and Jewish folk music from the same region; demonstrates how the same scales appear across traditions
- **Bartók's ethnomusicological recordings** (various releases): Bartók's actual field recordings from 1906–1918; the source material for his compositions; essential for hearing Romanian Minor and Hungarian folk scales in their original context

## Flamenco and Spanish Music

- **Camarón de la Isla**—*La Leyenda del Tiempo* (1979): the most innovative flamenco recording; Phrygian Dominant throughout; *siguiriyas*, *soleá*, *bulería*
- **Paco de Lucía**—any *falseta* recording in E Phrygian: the definitive instrumental Phrygian Dominant vocabulary; clean melodic lines that demonstrate the Andalusian cadence and bimodal mixture
- **Enrique Morente**—*Omega* (with Lagartija Nick): flamenco and rock combined; Phrygian Dominant in an unusual context; useful for hearing the mode's resilience across genre
- **Lole y Manuel**—*Nuevo Día*: new flamenco; Phrygian Dominant in a singer-songwriter setting

## Arabic and Turkish Classical

- **Umm Kulthum**—any pre-1970 recording: the definitive Arabic classical vocal tradition; microtonal maqam inflections; melodic development within a single maqam across 20–40 minute performances; the primary model for how to develop a single modal idea across a long time span
- **Farid al-Atrash**—oud recordings: Arabic maqam on the primary Arabic instrument; the oud's ornamentation is directly applicable to bawu technique
- **Kudsi Erguner**—*ney* recordings in Turkish makam: the instrument acoustically closest to the kaval; *Rumi* (album): Mevlevi sema music in Turkish makam; the primary model for kaval playing in makam
- **Münir Nurettin Selçuk**—Turkish classical vocal recordings: seyir (required melodic direction) audible in the phrasing; how makam ascent and descent differ

---

## Persian Classical

- **Shajarian, Mohammad Reza**—any dastgah-e Shur recording: the greatest living Persian classical vocalist (until his death in 2020); Shur in its full emotional depth; the *forud* (descent to home) as structural and emotional arc
- **Shahram Nazeri**—vocal recordings in dastgah-e Mahur and Shur: lyrical, accessible Persian classical singing
- **Parisa**—female voice in Persian classical tradition; dastgah-e Dashti and Chahargah recordings

## Indian Classical

### Hindustani
- **Hariprasad Chaurasia**—bansuri (bamboo flute) recordings, particularly raga Yaman (similar to Lydian) and raga Bhairavi (similar to Phrygian): the primary model for wind instrument playing in Indian classical music; the *alap* structure in all its patience; meend (slides) as primary expressive vocabulary
- **Ravi Shankar**—sitar recordings, particularly *raga Jog*, *Kafi*, and *Bhairavi*: accessible and widely available; the most recorded Indian classical musician in the Western world
- **Ali Akbar Khan**—sarod recordings: the most complete and technically demanding Indian classical playing available; *raga Bhairav* (similar to Double Harmonic) is essential

### Carnatic
- **M.S. Subbulakshmi**—vocal recordings, particularly *Bhairavi* and *Todi* ragas: the most revered Carnatic vocalist; accessible entry point
- **Lalgudi Jayaraman**—violin recordings: Carnatic violin; the bowing ornaments (gamaka) are closely equivalent to wind ornament techniques

## Jewish Cantorial and Klezmer

- **Yossele Rosenblatt**—cantorial recordings (pre-1930): the definitive Ashkenazi cantor; Ahavah Rabbah (*Freygish* mode) at its most devotionally intense; melismatic vocal ornaments over the Phrygian Dominant scale
- **Joseph Schmidt**—cantorial and operatic recordings: lighter, more lyrical cantorial style
- **Klezmatics**—*"Shvaygn = Toyt"*: modern klezmer; Ahavah Rabbah in contemporary context; the modal vocabulary intact within new arrangements
- **Andy Statman**—klezmer clarinet recordings: the most technically developed instrumental Ahavah Rabbah playing; direct model for wind instrument approach to Jewish modal music

---

## Ethiopian and African

- **Mahmoud Ahmed**—*"Tizita"* and other recordings: the most recorded artist working in Ethiopian kiñit; Tizita (major and minor) as primary mode
- **Mulatu Astatke**—*Éthiopiques* series (particularly Vol. 4): Afro-jazz incorporating Ethiopian kiñit; Tizita, Bati, and Ambassel in a jazz-influenced context; most accessible entry point for Ethiopian music
- **Tilahun Gessesse**—traditional Ethiopian vocal recordings: the most revered Ethiopian singer; kiñit in its most direct form
- **Ali Farka Touré**—*Talking Timbuktu* (with Ry Cooder): the clearest demonstration of West African pentatonic tradition; the connection to American blues made audible

## Jazz: Modal and Blues

- **Miles Davis**—*Kind of Blue* (1959): the founding document of modal jazz; "So What" (D Dorian), "Flamenco Sketches" (various modes), "All Blues" (G Mixolydian-inflected); essential
- **John Coltrane**—*A Love Supreme* (1964): devotional modal jazz; the suite structure as an extended modal journey; comparable in emotional intent to the Indian *alap*
- **Bill Evans**—*Waltz for Debby* (1961): the most lyrical modal jazz piano; sparse, weighted notes with wide silences; closest jazz equivalent to Arvo Pärt's silence-as-structure
- **Herbie Hancock**—*Maiden Voyage* (1965): suspended harmonic world; Lydian Dominant and related scales; the definition of cinematic jazz suspension
- **Charles Lloyd**—*Forest Flower* (1966): jazz flute and saxophone in a modal context; closest to NAF aesthetic of any jazz recording

## Pieces for Solo Wind Instruments

These are the most important pieces for players of the instruments in this collection—models for solo modal melody without accompaniment:

- **Debussy**—*"Syrinx"* (solo flute, 1913): the essential solo wind modal piece; E♭ Phrygian-adjacent; everything this repository teaches is present in this 3-minute piece—seed phrase, development, peak, descent, silence
- **Bach**—*Partita in A minor for solo flute, BWV 1013*: the most structurally demanding solo wind piece in the Baroque repertoire; A Aeolian/Harmonic Minor; motivic development without accompaniment
- **Bach**—*Flute Sonatas* (various): the structure of Baroque two-voice writing adapted for a single melody instrument
- **Traditional Irish slow airs**: any recording by Tommy Potts (uilleann pipes), Paddy Glackin (fiddle), or Matt Molloy (flute); the model for breath-as-structure in solo Celtic melody
- **Hariprasad Chaurasia**—solo bansuri recordings: the most direct model for solo wind alap improvisation in Indian classical tradition
