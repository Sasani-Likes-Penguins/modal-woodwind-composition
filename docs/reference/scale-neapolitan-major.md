## What Is It?

Neapolitan Major is a scale with a Phrygian opening (flat second and flat third) but a bright, nearly major upper range (major sixth and major seventh). It contains what might be called a built-in emotional contradiction: the first three notes suggest darkness and the ancient character of Phrygian, while the upper notes suggest brightness and aspiration.

**Formula**: 1 – ♭2 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – 6 – 7

**C Neapolitan Major**: C – D♭ – E♭ – F – G – A – B – C

It is the fifth mode of the harmonic minor scale starting from the fifth degree—or alternatively understood as a major scale with a flat second and flat third.

---

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from C) | Character |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| 1 (tonic) | C | Home |
| ♭2 | D♭ | Phrygian half-step—pressing, dark opening |
| ♭3 | E♭ | Minor third—darkness confirmed |
| 4 | F | Perfect fourth—neutral, stable |
| 5 | G | Perfect fifth—strength |
| 6 | A | Major sixth—sudden brightness (like Dorian's lift) |
| 7 | B | Leading tone—urgency; pushing toward home |
| 8 | C | Home |

The contrast between the dark lower tetrachord (C–D♭–E♭–F: Phrygian in character) and the bright upper tetrachord (G–A–B–C: nearly major in character) is the defining feature of the scale.

---

## The Bittersweet Paradox

Neapolitan Major creates a melody that passes through two emotional worlds in a single octave. A phrase that ascends the full scale begins in Phrygian darkness (D♭, E♭), passes through neutral territory (F, G), then arrives in near-major brightness (A, B). The return descent goes in the opposite emotional direction: bright → neutral → dark.

This natural emotional arc—dark opening, bright peak, dark return—makes Neapolitan Major a structurally bittersweet scale. Every complete melodic statement contains both the shadow and the light.

| Feeling | Expression |

| Romantic drama | The contrast of the two tetrachords creates natural phrase drama |

## How It Differs from Neapolitan Minor

| Feature | Neapolitan Minor | Neapolitan Major |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| ♭2 | Yes | Yes |
| ♭3 | Yes | Yes |
| 6th degree | Minor (♭6) | Major (natural 6) |
| 7th degree | Raised (leading tone) | Raised (leading tone) |
| Overall feel | Pincer tension; everything dark | Paradoxical; dark below, bright above |

Both scales share the Phrygian half-step opening and the leading tone. The difference is entirely in the sixth degree—Neapolitan Minor's flat sixth adds anguish to the already-dark scale; Neapolitan Major's natural sixth lifts the upper range into brightness.

## Historical and Musical Context

The Neapolitan Major scale is encountered in:

- **Romantic piano music** exploring chromatic harmony—particularly in passages that move from minor to major coloring within a single melody
- **Film music** where a composer wants the ancient or exotic character of the ♭2 alongside the emotional resolution of a major-quality upper range
- **Middle Eastern and Balkan folk music** that shares this combination (♭2 with a bright upper range) as a characteristic scale type; related to some maqam structures
- **Some South Indian ragas**: the melakarta raga *Kokilapriya* (melakarta 11) uses a Neapolitan Major-equivalent scale structure

## Composing with Neapolitan Major

**Play the contrast deliberately**: the most effective Neapolitan Major melodies emphasize the contrast between the Phrygian-dark opening (♭2, ♭3) and the major-bright upper range (6, 7). A phrase that begins low and dark, then ascends to the natural sixth as a moment of unexpected brightness, demonstrates the scale's defining bittersweet quality.

**Use the ♭2 as a lean note**: approach the tonic from the ♭2 (D♭ → C in C Neapolitan Major) for the characteristic Phrygian sigh gesture. This anchors the phrase in the scale's dark character before the melody can ascend to the bright upper notes.

**Slow descents are most expressive**: descending from the leading tone (B → A → G → F → E♭ → D♭ → C in C Neapolitan Major) traverses the full emotional range of the scale—from the aspiring brightness of the leading tone through the neutral middle to the Phrygian darkness of D♭ settling back to C. This descent in a slow, sustained melody is the scale's most expressive compositional gesture.

## Repertoire and Recordings to Study

- Any **klezmer** or **Middle Eastern-influenced** composition that combines ♭2 with a natural 6th and leading tone uses Neapolitan Major territory
- **Rimsky-Korsakov**—passages in *Sheherazade* combine Phrygian openings with major-quality upper ranges
- **Bartók**—some folk-derived melodies from his research combine ♭2 with bright upper notes; *Mikrokosmos* includes scale studies touching this territory

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: alto recorder (C Neapolitan Major)
**C Neapolitan Major**: C – D♭ – E♭ – F – G – A – B – C

1. Begin on C (tonic). Step up to D♭ (lean note—hold for one full beat). Step back to C. This is the Phrygian opening—establishing the dark character from the first gesture.
2. Ascend: C – E♭ – F – G. The E♭ (minor third) and F and G create a neutral, rising quality.
3. Continue ascending: G – A. When A arrives (the natural sixth, unexpectedly bright after the E♭ below), pause briefly. This is the emotional paradox of the scale—the dark scale suddenly brightening.
4. Continue to B (leading tone) – C (high). The leading tone creates urgency toward the octave—aspiration reaching its peak.
5. Descend the full scale: C – B – A – G – F – E♭ – D♭ – C. The descent reverses the emotional arc—from bright aspiration back through neutral territory into the Phrygian dark, landing softly on C.

**Goal**: music that feels like memory—the structure of remembering something beautiful from within something dark.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Bittersweet nostalgia**: slow tempo; a phrase that opens with D♭ → C (the Phrygian sigh—dark) and closes with A → B → C (the major sixth rising to the leading tone then home—bright); the same tonic arrived at through darkness and brightness in sequence.

**Romantic complexity**: moderate tempo; the ♭2 and ♭3 used prominently in the phrase's first half, then the natural sixth and leading tone prominent in the second half; the contrast within a single phrase creates the bittersweet quality.

**Ancient melancholy with aspiration**: the ♭2 and ♭3 establish a Phrygian-adjacent opening; then the sixth (A) and seventh (B) lift the phrase upward in its second half; phrases that rise from Phrygian darkness to near-major brightness and settle on the high tonic.

### The Descending Walk as a Compositional Blueprint

The most characteristic Neapolitan Major gesture for a beginner: play the complete descending scale from high C slowly and deliberately: C – B – A – G – F – E♭ – D♭ – C. Listen carefully to how each note changes the emotional character of the descent:

- **B (leading tone)**: aspiring, bright—the highest energy note
- **A (major sixth)**: warm, open—still bright but more spacious
- **G (fifth)**: stable, neutral—halfway point
- **F (fourth)**: settling, natural—the transition note
- **E♭ (minor third)**: the first shadow—the major-to-minor emotional shift
- **D♭ (flat second)**: the Phrygian press—the second dark note, pressing against the tonic
- **C (tonic)**: home

This descending walk from brightness to darkness *is* the Neapolitan Major scale's emotional story. A composition built around this descent—varied, ornamented, explored from different starting points—has the scale's core emotional logic in it.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Neapolitan Major

**Staying in the dark half of the scale**: the ♭2 and ♭3 of the Neapolitan Major opening (D♭ and E♭ in C Neapolitan Major) sound Phrygian-dark. Beginners sometimes stay in this territory for the whole composition, producing something that sounds like Phrygian with an accidental. The scale's defining quality is the *contrast* between the dark opening and the bright upper range (A and B). Ascend to the natural sixth and seventh—that is where the scale's bittersweet character lives.

**Not using the natural sixth and leading tone prominently**: the A (major sixth) and B (major seventh/leading tone) in C Neapolitan Major are the notes that distinguish it from Phrygian. These bright notes in the upper range must be used with clear intent—on accented beats, at phrase peaks. Without them, the scale's complexity collapses into simple Phrygian.

**Treating the bittersweet quality as incidental**: Neapolitan Major's emotional character—dark opening, bright upper range, complex combination—is not a side effect of the scale's intervals; it is the scale's entire point. Every compositional decision should serve this bittersweet complexity. If a phrase settles comfortably in either pure darkness (Phrygian) or pure brightness (major), it has lost the scale's identity.
