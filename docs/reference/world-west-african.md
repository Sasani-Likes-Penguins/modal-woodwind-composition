## Overview

West African music is rhythmically among the most complex in the world, and melodically it is primarily pentatonic—built on five-note scales that avoid half-steps. But "pentatonic" is a simplification: West African melodic traditions are rich, regionally varied, and deeply intertwined with language, ceremony, and community life [82](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_African_music)[83](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeli)[88].

---

## Key Traditions

### Mande (Griot) Music
The Mande-speaking peoples of Mali, Senegal, Gambia, Guinea, and surrounding regions developed the **griot** (*jeli*) tradition—professional musician-storytellers who preserved oral history through music [82](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_African_music)[87](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flamenco_music).

**The kora** (21-string harp-lute) is the primary griot melodic instrument. Its tuning is pentatonic, and the same five-note collection supports multiple "modes"—different starting points creating different emotional flavors. West African Griot music uses a variety of modes, such as the Dorian mode and Mixolydian mode, in their melodies and harmonies [87](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flamenco_music).

**Characteristic features**:
- **Melodic ostinato**: a short repeating melodic figure (*kumbengo*) over which the singer improvises. This is structurally similar to the cyclic gamelan approach—the foundation loops while expression happens above it
- **Call and response**: the griot's sung melody alternates with instrumental responses
- **Descending melodic movement**: West African scales are often thought of and practiced as descending from top to bottom [81](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizita)

**Emotional world**: Mande music encompasses grief, praise, history, love, and humor—the full human range. The *jaliya* tradition (the griot's craft) is one of dignity and memory.

### Ewe Drumming (Ghana/Togo)
The Ewe people are known for extraordinary rhythmic complexity—multiple interlocking rhythmic patterns creating a whole greater than its parts. Melodically, Ewe music uses pentatonic scales with intervallic structures based on fourths and fifths [81](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizita)[88].

**For wind players**: the concept of an **interlocking melodic pattern**—two short phrases that interleave with each other, neither complete on its own—can be applied to solo wind composition as a structural device: alternate two fragmentary phrases that together create a complete melodic cycle.

### Yoruba Music (Nigeria)
Yoruba music is tonal (the Yoruba language uses tone to distinguish meaning), and Yoruba drumming literally "talks" by imitating the tonal contours of spoken language. Melodically, the Yoruba use pentatonic scales tuned in thirds—somewhat different from the fourth/fifth-based pentatonics of other West African traditions [81](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizita)[88].

## African Pentatonic Characteristics

The African musician hears tones and not semitones—traditional African music avoids the half-step in its melodic vocabulary. Pentatonic and hexatonic scales are very common scales across Africa [81](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizita)[83](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeli).

Key compositional features:
- **No resolution toward leading tones**: unlike Western music, African melody doesn't use half-step approach notes that "lead" to a destination. Instead, resolution comes through return to the tonic from any direction
- **Descending phrases**: many African melodic traditions favor downward movement
- **Oral transmission**: scales are learned by ear and may vary slightly from performer to performer and region to region—there is no "standard" African pentatonic

## The African Origins of Blues

West African pentatonic melody, transported via the Middle Passage to the Americas, became one of the primary sources of the blues tradition [53](https://www.maqamworld.com/en/maqam/bayati.php). The "blue note"—the expressive bent third and seventh—is a direct survival of West African practice of approaching notes from below or above rather than landing on them cleanly.

**For wind players**: the African approach to melody—pentatonic, non-directional, ornamented with slides and bends, phrase-shaped rather than scale-shaped—is exactly what the bawu and kaval do naturally. Playing in a West African-informed style means trusting the pentatonic scale and ornamenting generously.

## Composition Exercise: Griot-Inspired Loop on Low D Whistle

**Setup**: E minor pentatonic on low D whistle (natural fingering, starting on E): E–G–A–B–D–E.

1. Find a 4-note melodic cell that repeats comfortably—e.g., B–A–G–E. This is your *kumbengo*.
2. Play the kumbengo for 8 repetitions. Let it become completely stable.
3. Improvise a longer melodic phrase above it—playing in the same scale but with more motion. The improvisation should always return to the kumbengo.
4. Use whistle ornaments (cuts, rolls, slides) on the kumbengo notes to add texture without changing the underlying pattern.
5. End by slowing the kumbengo and fading on the tonic (E).

**Goal**: music built on a foundation rather than a journey—the griot's craft of stability beneath expression.

## Specific Instruments and Their Melodic Character

### The Kora (Harp-Lute)
The kora's 21 strings span two and a half octaves in a pentatonic tuning. The left and right hands play interlocking patterns—one providing the bass and rhythmic foundation, the other providing melody and ornament above. The result is a self-contained melodic-harmonic texture from a single instrument. Notable kora masters:
- **Toumani Diabaté**—the most internationally recognized kora player; his collaborations with Taj Mahal and Ali Farka Touré demonstrate West African pentatonic in cross-cultural contexts
- **Ballaké Sissoko**—lyrical, meditative kora playing; *Chamber Music* (with Vincent Ségal) places the kora in a classical chamber context
- **Foday Musa Suso**—collaborated with Philip Glass; demonstrates connections between griot tradition and minimalist classical music

### The Balafon (Xylophone)
The balafon is a wooden xylophone with gourd resonators, tuned to regional pentatonic or heptatonic scales that vary by tradition and maker. Its resonance—the gourds buzz and sustain each struck note—creates a shimmer similar to paired gamelan instruments. Balafon scales are not standardized.

### The Ngoni (Lute)
A small plucked lute used in Mande music, often alongside the kora. Its shorter range and buzzing timbre complement the kora's sustained tones. The ngoni is one of the proposed predecessors of the American banjo—another cultural transmission of West African instrumental tradition.

### The Djembe and Talking Drum
While primarily percussion, these instruments demonstrate the integration of rhythmic and tonal elements in West African music. The talking drum (*dùndún* in Yoruba) changes pitch through arm pressure on the strings, allowing it to mimic tonal speech patterns—the closest percussion instrument comes to a melodic function.

## Composing in West African-Influenced Style for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### The Fundamental Difference: Foundation vs. Journey

Western classical composition is a *journey*—music that departs from a starting point, develops through conflict and tension, and arrives at a destination. West African melodic composition is a *foundation*—a stable, repeating pattern beneath which expression occurs.

This difference is the most important thing to understand before composing in this style. You are not writing music that goes somewhere. You are building a stable platform and then speaking from it.

### The Kumbengo: Your Compositional Core

The *kumbengo* is the short repeating melodic figure (typically two to four notes) that forms the foundation of griot and kora music. In West African ensemble music, the kumbengo is played continuously by the accompanying instrument while the vocalist improvises above.

For solo wind, the kumbengo serves a different function: it is the **seed phrase** (this repository's universal term) made explicit and repeating—the fixed element around which variation occurs.

**Finding your kumbengo**:

1. Choose a pentatonic scale—A minor pentatonic (A – C – D – E – G) works naturally on NAF and D whistle
2. Find a two-to-four-note figure that feels settled when repeated. Test: can you play it ten times without feeling like you need to go somewhere else? If yes, it is a potential kumbengo.
3. Examples from A minor pentatonic: E – D – A (three notes, descending, settling); A – C – E – C (four notes, ascending and returning); D – C – A – C (four notes, circling around A)

**The kumbengo test**: play your chosen figure twelve times in a row. If it sounds more settled and natural on the twelfth repetition than on the first—gaining depth rather than becoming tedious—you have found your kumbengo. If it feels strained or boring by the sixth repetition, find a different figure.

### Descending Melodic Direction: The West African Characteristic

West African melodic phrases characteristically descend—they begin relatively high and settle low. This is the opposite of the Western classical tendency toward ascending climax. In West African music, the "conclusion" of a phrase arrives when it has settled downward to rest, not when it has reached a high point.

**Applying this compositionally**:
- Begin each phrase from A high note (not necessarily the highest available, but higher than where you expect to land)
- Let the phrase descend through the scale to a resting point
- The resting point is not always the tonic—it may be the fifth, the fourth, or the third
- The next phrase begins high again from a slightly different starting point and descends again

**Example**: in A minor pentatonic, a West African-style melody might descend: G – E – D – A (high to low); then E – C – A (starting from the fifth, settling to the tonic); then G – E – A (returning to the tonic from the sixth).

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Communal strength and storytelling** (griot style): a pentatonic kumbengo repeated steadily; descending melodic improvisation above the kumbengo; a calling quality in the melody—as if addressing an audience; moderate to moderate-fast tempo; no silences between phrases—the music is continuous, the foundation never stops.

**Meditative presence**: a slower kumbengo; wider silences after each improvised phrase; the descending motion settling very slowly on the tonic; the foundation as a heartbeat rather than a pattern.

**Joyful vitality** (West African dance character): a rhythmically energetic kumbengo with strong beats; the improvised phrases shorter and more rhythmically punchy; major pentatonic (Gong mode) rather than minor for brightness; the bawu's buzzing free-reed resonance suits this register.

**Ancestral solemnity**: the kumbengo in a very low register; very slow; the descending phrases moving from the top of the range all the way to the lowest available note; silence at the tonic before the next kumbengo cycle; the NAF's breath quality or the kaval's dual-voice technique for a resonant, ancient sound.

### Step-by-Step: Writing a Griot-Style Piece for NAF

**A minor pentatonic** (NAF native scale): A – C – D – E – G

1. **Find the kumbengo**: choose E – D – A (three descending notes). This is your foundation. Play it four times. Let it settle into your body.

2. **Begin with the kumbengo alone**: play the kumbengo eight times without variation. This is longer than it feels necessary—but the repetition is not tedium, it is establishment. The listener needs to completely know the pattern before the variation means anything.

3. **First variation: a longer descending phrase above the kumbengo pattern**: between kumbengo repetitions, insert: G – E – D – C – A (a five-note descent from the high G to the tonic). Then immediately back to the kumbengo.

4. **Second variation: start even higher**: G – E – D – A. Wait two kumbengo repetitions. Then: E – C – A (shorter, beginning lower). The phrases are shortening as the music settles.

5. **The call phrase**: play the kumbengo. Then: a phrase that does NOT descend—E – G – E – D – A (ascending first before descending). This single phrase, in a composition of otherwise descending motion, sounds like a question being asked.

6. **The answer**: immediately after the ascending-descending phrase: E – D – C – A – A (settling on A with emphasis, held). The answer is lower and more conclusive than the question.

7. **Return to pure kumbengo**: play the kumbengo six times unvaried to close. The foundation reasserts. The story has been told. The pattern continues because life continues.

### Building on the Foundation: Cross-Traditional Possibilities

The West African kumbengo-and-improvisation structure is structurally equivalent to several other traditions in this repository:

- **Indian alap**: a fixed scale (the raga) provides the foundation; improvisation explores it progressively
- **Gamelan balungan**: the core melodic pattern repeats while elaboration occurs above it
- **Irish ostinato**: some Celtic tunes use a recurring bass-note figure beneath the melody
- **Persian dastgah**: the daramad phrase returns between gushes like a structural anchor

For composers interested in cross-traditional work: building a kumbengo in Phrygian Dominant (the flamenco scale) produces something simultaneously West African in structure and Middle Eastern in scale character. The combination is unusual and rich—neither flamenco nor West African, but drawing from both.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in West African-Influenced Composition

**Building an interesting kumbengo then abandoning it**: the kumbengo is the composition's foundation—it must continue, uninterrupted, beneath the improvised phrases above it. Beginners who establish a good kumbengo and then abandon it to play something more interesting have lost the entire structure. In griot style, the foundation is always present; the expression happens above it, never instead of it.

**Adding too much variety too quickly**: the kumbengo gains depth through repetition, not through variation. Beginners who vary the kumbengo every few repetitions—changing a note here, altering the rhythm there—prevent the foundation from settling into the listener's consciousness. Repeat the kumbengo without variation for a much longer time than seems necessary. The variation, when it comes, will be more striking for the extended establishment.

**Ascending instead of descending**: West African melodic phrases characteristically descend. Beginners with Western classical training tend to ascend toward peaks. In West African-influenced composition, reverse this tendency: begin phrases from a relatively high point and let them settle downward. The resolution of a phrase arrives at its lowest point.

**Treating the music as background**: West African griot music is *storytelling*—it has narrative intent, communal function, and direct address to its audience. Even an instrumental composition in this style should have the quality of direct communication rather than contemplative interiority. Play as if addressing someone.
