## What Is Gamelan?

Gamelan is a traditional ensemble music of Java and Bali (Indonesia), centered on tuned metallophones (instruments with metal bars struck with mallets), gongs, drums, flute (*suling*), and bowed string instruments. Gamelan music is one of the most sophisticated musical traditions in the world, and its tuning system is unlike any Western scale [65](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raga)[68].

The two fundamental Javanese gamelan tuning systems are:
- **Slendro**: a five-note scale with roughly equal spacing
- **Pelog**: a seven-note scale with highly unequal intervals

A full gamelan orchestra includes instruments in both tuning systems, and classically only one tuning is used at a time.

## Why Gamelan Tuning Cannot Be Mapped to Western Notes

This is the most important thing to understand about gamelan scales: **they do not correspond to Western notes, and different gamelans are tuned differently from each other**.

Each Gamelan has a unique spirit poured and hammered from molten bronze, housed across the full body of its instruments. The pitches of the Gamelan are anchored to the gong and overseen by the intuition of a female singer, and the octave in each Gamelan is slightly larger or smaller than its Western counterpart [68].

To copy the pitches of a Gamelan is to insult the spirit housed within it.

In practical terms: the "same" note in two different gamelan orchestras may differ by as much as 40-50 cents (nearly half a semitone). This is not imprecision—it is the intended character of each individual ensemble. Each gamelan has its own personality expressed through its unique tuning.

For a wind player: this means that gamelan scales cannot be simply looked up and played—they must be *heard* and then approximated. The bawu and kaval, with their flexible pitch, are the only instruments in this collection capable of genuinely approximating gamelan tuning; the recorder and whistle can only approximate.

## Slendro: The Five-Note Equal Scale

Slendro divides the octave into **five roughly equal steps** of approximately 240 cents each—almost exactly halfway between a Western whole step and a minor third [63][65](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raga).

This means every note in Slendro is roughly equidistant from its neighbors. There are no half-steps and no large leaps—just a smooth, evenly spaced five-note world.

**Character**: Slendro notation generally produces a light, cheerful atmosphere and feels busier. Serene and smooth due to the roughly equal intervals. However, slendro can also express sadness—in Bali, slendro is the tuning associated with cremation ceremonies, and slendro miring (playing slightly "off" the slendro pitches deliberately) is used for melancholy and longing [65](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raga).

**Pathet**: Slendro has three sub-modes (*pathet*—roughly equivalent to a mood or emotional register) named nem, sanga, and manyura. These appear in order during a traditional *wayang* (shadow puppet) performance [63].

## Pelog: The Seven-Note Unequal Scale

Pelog has **seven notes with highly irregular intervals**—some very large (nearly a minor third), some very small (nearly a half-step) [62][65](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raga).

**Approximate pelog interval pattern** (varies by gamelan): 1–2–3–4–5–6–7 with approximate intervals of: wide – narrow – wide – wide – narrow – wide – (gap back to 1)

Because the intervals are so unequal, pelog has a dramatically different quality depending on which five notes you emphasize. Pelog music typically uses **five-note subsets** of the seven-tone collection.

**The three main pelog modes (for Balinese gamelan)**:
- **Selisir**: the most common mode; considered "the default pelog sound"—the tuning of gamelan gong kebyar, the most internationally known Balinese gamelan
- **Tembung**: a different five-note subset with a darker character
- **Sunaren**: another subset; specific ceremonial associations

**Character**: Pelog notation produces an atmosphere that gives the impression of being manly, regal, and sacred [65](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raga). Its irregular intervals create a more complex, dynamic, and intense sound compared to slendro's smoothness.

## The Emotional World of Gamelan Scales

For experienced participants in gamelan music, the pelog and slendro scales each have a particular feeling, related to the rituals and circumstances in which the scale is used [63].

**Slendro feels**: open, flowing, cheerful in its standard form; deeply melancholy in slendro miring; associated with everyday human drama in wayang

**Pelog feels**: sacred, regal, ceremonial; the "manly" quality comes from its irregular intervals' commanding presence; used for formal and divine occasions in wayang

**The shimmering effect**: In Balinese gamelan, instruments are played in pairs tuned slightly apart, creating interference beating—a shimmering, pulsing quality meant to give the listener a feeling of a god's presence or a stepping stone to a meditative state [62][63]. This is a compositional device: the beating between slightly-out-of-tune octaves creates a texture that feels larger than individual notes.

## Applying Gamelan Concepts to Wind Playing

Since fixed gamelan tuning is not achievable on most wind instruments, the compositional concepts are more applicable than the precise pitches:

**1. Equally-spaced melody (slendro approach)**: compose a five-note melody where all intervals are approximately equal—major 2nds and minor 3rds alternating, creating a smooth, non-directional, flowing quality. No note wants to go anywhere; the melody drifts like water.

**2. The shimmering technique (pelog/Balinese approach)**: on the bawu, slightly detune a sustained note by bending it slowly above or below its center pitch, creating a controlled wavering. The kaval's dual-voice technique (partially covering the back fipple window) creates a similar shimmering texture.

**3. Cyclic form**: gamelan music is structured in cycles—phrases repeat, patterns overlay, there is no strong sense of linear development or climax. Compose a phrase, repeat it, vary it slightly, repeat it again. The accumulation of repeated cycles creates the hypnotic gamelan effect.

**4. The gong as anchor**: in gamelan, the large gong marks the end of each musical cycle—a moment of resolution and return. In wind playing: the lowest note (the "gong note") can serve as the anchor that the melody regularly returns to between cycles of exploration.

## Composition Exercise: Slendro-Inspired Pentatonic Loop on NAF

**Setup**: A minor NAF = A–C–D–E–G. Think of these five notes as approximately equally spaced.

**Rules following slendro cyclic approach**:
1. Choose a short phrase of 4-5 notes (e.g., A–C–D–E–G–A going up, A–G–E–D–C–A going down).
2. Play the phrase. Repeat it exactly. Repeat it again.
3. On the fourth repetition, vary ONE note (e.g., end on E instead of A). Then return to the original.
4. On the eighth repetition, change the rhythm (same notes, different durations).
5. Never stop to "resolve"—let the cycle keep moving. The lack of arrival IS the aesthetic.
6. After many cycles, gradually slow the tempo. Let the final repetitions fade to silence.

**Goal**: music that feels like it has always been playing and will always be playing—the eternal present of gamelan.

## The Spirit of Gamelan: What Cannot Be Transferred

Before the practical sections above, it is worth stating clearly what gamelan is that cannot be transferred to solo wind playing:

Gamelan is a *community instrument*—a set of instruments that belongs to a village, temple, or palace, played collectively. The music is not composed and then performed; it is taught orally within the community and realized collectively in the moment. The shimmer of paired tuning (two instruments for each pitch, tuned slightly apart) cannot be reproduced by one player. The cyclic architecture (building and releasing tension over many repetitions of the gong cycle) requires multiple musicians across multiple registers. The spiritual relationship between the instruments—each gamelan set believed to contain a spirit—is not transferable.

What *can* be transferred to solo wind playing is the compositional *concept* behind gamelan: cyclic structure, shimmer through repetition with variation, and the gong as structural marker. These are borrowed principles, not the thing itself.

## Slendro and Pelog: Further Detail

**Why each gamelan is unique**: the intervals of slendro and pelog are not standardized. Each gamelan is tuned by its maker to specific frequencies that are unique to that instrument set. Two gamelans in the same village may have measurably different tunings. This is not considered a problem—it is considered appropriate; the spirit inhabiting each gamelan requires its own tuning.

The practical consequence for Western musicians: Western transcriptions of gamelan pieces in standard notation are approximate at best. The notes on a page do not capture the actual tuning. Listening is essential; score-reading is misleading.

**Pelog's emotional character by pathet** (modal context):
- *Pathet nem*: used in the opening of a wayang performance (evening); expansive, somewhat neutral
- *Pathet sanga*: midnight section; more intense and focused
- *Pathet manyura*: final section (early morning); the most refined and spiritually elevated

The progression through the three *pathet* over the course of a wayang (shadow puppet) performance mirrors the Persian dastgah concept of a journey through progressively different emotional territories over an extended time period.

## Composing with Gamelan Concepts for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### What You Can Actually Use

Gamelan cannot be fully transferred to solo wind playing. A solo NAF or recorder cannot replicate the shimmering of paired tuned gongs, the interlocking of eight simultaneous melodic lines, or the spiritual community of a village ensemble performing together. Acknowledging this honestly lets you focus on what *can* be borrowed:

1. **Cyclic structure**: organizing music in recurring cycles rather than linear development
2. **The *gong* as structural marker**: using a specific moment—a return to the tonic, a specific note, or a breath—as a structural "gong" that marks the end of each cycle
3. **Shimmer through repetition**: repeating a phrase with slight variations creates a shimmering quality even on a single instrument
4. **Non-directional time**: music that does not "go somewhere" but rather circles, accumulating and releasing without a single climax
5. **The *pathet* emotional arc**: beginning in a lighter emotional register, moving to a more intense one, settling into a refined, elevated register at the close

### The Slendro Approach for NAF and Pentatonic Instruments

Slendro's five roughly equal steps per octave have no Western equivalent, but the closest approximation on equal-tempered instruments is a five-note scale with roughly equal intervals. For the NAF in Am, the native pentatonic (A – C – D – E – G) has approximate intervals of: minor third, whole step, whole step, minor third, whole step. This is not slendro, but it is closer to slendro's approximately equal divisions than any standard diatonic scale.

**Composing in slendro-adjacent style on NAF**:

The key insight is *non-directionality*. Standard Western melody has a direction: it goes somewhere (toward the tonic, toward the climax, toward the resolution). Slendro melody does not—it circulates.

**Step-by-step**:

1. Find a four-note melodic cell—for example, E – G – A – G. This is your *balungan* (core pattern). Play it.
2. Play it again. Identical. The repetition establishes the cycle.
3. Play it a third time, varying the ornamentation slightly: cut the first E, roll the G. The notes are the same; the texture is different. This is gamelan variation—the melody is fixed; the decoration changes.
4. Fourth repetition: same notes, different rhythm. Instead of four equal beats, place the E on beat 1, hold G for two beats, then A and G quickly on beat 4. Same notes, different weight. The melody is the same cycle; time is being distributed differently within it.
5. Add a "gong moment": after four repetitions of the balungan, return to A (the tonic) and hold it for four beats with silence following. This A is your structural gong—the end of the cycle, the breath before beginning again.
6. Begin the second cycle. Same balungan. New ornamentation. The cycle is the same; everything else is in motion.

**The emotional experience of cyclic structure**: the first three repetitions establish the pattern; the fourth or fifth brings a sense of inevitability—this has been happening and will continue happening; the sixth or seventh brings something between hypnosis and depth, a sense of time pooling. This is the gamelan aesthetic: not going somewhere but dwelling somewhere.

### The Pelog Approach for Chromatic Instruments

Pelog's unequal seven-note tuning is used in five-note subsets (*pathet*). The subset most accessible to Western instruments is approximately: C – D♭ – E – G – A♭ (using a chromatic instrument, approximating the unequal intervals with the nearest equal-tempered pitches).

This is essentially a Double Harmonic-like collection—C, D♭ (half-step), E (augmented second), G (minor third), A♭ (half-step). The half-steps and augmented second create a more dramatic, tense quality than slendro.

For recorder or bawu in pelog-inspired style:

1. The half-step D♭ → C (descending) or C → D♭ (ascending) is the characteristic gesture—use it as a structural ornament
2. The augmented second leap (D♭ → E) is the dramatic gesture—use it at phrase peaks
3. Return to C at each "gong moment"
4. The same cyclic structure as slendro: establish the balungan, repeat with variation, mark cycles with returns to C

### Eliciting Specific Emotional States

**Timeless presence** (slendro, slow, non-directional): choose a three-note cell; repeat it very slowly with long silences between cycles; no dynamics louder than mezzo-forte; no climax; the music exists rather than develops.

**Shimmering wonder** (slendro, moderate tempo): a four-note cell at a slightly faster pace; vary ornamentation on each repetition; keep dynamics consistent but let the ornamentation create textural shimmer—cuts and taps in Celtic style, or pitch bends in bawu style, creating surface movement above a fixed melodic core.

**Sacred intensity** (pelog, slow): use the half-step characteristic gesture prominently; the augmented second at phrase peaks; return to the tonic on each gong moment; the bawu or kaval's natural resonance suits pelog's density.

**Deep trance quality**: any cycle at very slow tempo; twelve or more repetitions of the same balungan with gradually accumulating ornamental variation; silence between the last repetition and the gong moment as long as the phrase itself; the music has existed so long in the same mode that the listener has entered it.

### What Gamelan Teaches Composers of Any Tradition

Even for composers who do not primarily work in a gamelan aesthetic, the gamelan approach contains lessons that apply to every mode and scale in this repository:

**The balungan principle**: find the simplest possible melodic cell that contains the emotional essence of your chosen mode. This is your seed phrase, your pakad, your jins—the core from which everything develops. In gamelan, this cell is fixed; in other traditions, it is transformed. But identifying it first is universal.

**Cyclic repetition as emotional accumulation**: repeating a phrase does not dilute it—it deepens it. The third repetition of a phrase carries more weight than the first because the listener now knows it, anticipates it, and has had time to feel it rather than identify it. Every modal tradition uses this principle (Indian raga develops the same phrase for twenty minutes; blues repeats the three-chord pattern for twelve bars; Celtic jigs repeat the A and B sections multiple times). Gamelan makes it most explicit.

**Variation within stasis**: changing ornamentation, rhythm, and dynamics while keeping the pitches fixed is a powerful compositional technique. The listener's attention is held by the changes; the emotional stability is maintained by the sameness. This is the art of variation—not developing new material, but illuminating existing material from different angles.

## Common Beginner Mistakes When Borrowing Gamelan Concepts

**Trying to replicate gamelan sound on a solo wind instrument**: gamelan's defining sonic character—the shimmer of paired tuned gongs, the interlocking of multiple melodic lines, the resonance of struck metal and wood—cannot be replicated by a solo wind instrument. Attempting to do so produces imitation rather than inspiration. Instead, borrow gamelan's *compositional concepts* (cyclic structure, repetition with variation, non-directional time) and apply them to the wind instrument's natural character.

**Abandoning the balungan before the cycle has established itself**: the balungan (core melodic pattern) in gamelan is repeated many times—often 10–20 or more—before variation and ornamentation make it fully present in the listener's experience. Beginners introduce variation after 2–3 repetitions. The pattern has not yet settled; the variation has no context. Repeat the balungan at minimum 6–8 times before introducing variation.

**Forcing a Western climax structure onto cyclic form**: gamelan is not journey music—it does not build toward a single climax. Beginners trained in Western composition instinctively drive a cyclic structure toward a peak and then release. This disrupts the cyclic aesthetic. In gamelan-inspired composition, intensity rises and falls within the cycle rather than building linearly toward one climax. Each cycle is complete in itself.

**Treating all repetitions as identical**: the opposite error—playing the balungan identically for 20 repetitions without any variation or ornamentation. In gamelan, each cycle adds subtle variation in ornamentation while keeping the core melody stable. The variation is what creates the shimmer quality. Perfect identical repetition produces monotony; controlled subtle variation within the cycle produces the gamelan aesthetic.
