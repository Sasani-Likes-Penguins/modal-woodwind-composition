## What Is It?

Super Locrian is the most dissonant seven-note scale within Western equal temperament. It is [Locrian](mode-locrian.md) pushed one step further: while Locrian lowers the second and fifth degrees of natural minor, Super Locrian lowers every degree except the root. Nothing is left at its natural position.

**Formula**: 1 – ♭2 – ♭3 – ♭4 – ♭5 – ♭6 – ♭7

**C Super Locrian**: C – D♭ – E♭ – E♮ (= F♭) – G♭ – A♭ – B♭ – C

Note that the ♭4 (F♭) is enharmonically equivalent to E♮—the note that would normally be the major third of C. Written as F♭, it emphasizes that this is the fourth degree lowered, not a borrowed major third.

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from C) | Distance from previous | Character |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|-----------|
| 1 | C |—| Home |
| ♭2 | D♭ | Half-step | Phrygian press from above |
| ♭3 | E♭ | Whole step | Minor third |
| ♭4 (= 3) | E♮ | Half-step | Diminished fourth / major third |
| ♭5 | G♭ | Whole step | Tritone—the Locrian diminished fifth |
| ♭6 | A♭ | Whole step | Minor sixth |
| ♭7 | B♭ | Whole step | Flat seventh |
| 8 | C | Whole step | Home |

The scale begins with two half-steps in succession (C–D♭–E♭–E♮)—an extremely dense, chromatic opening. Then it opens into whole steps for the remainder. This compression at the bottom and expansion at the top gives the scale its distinctive "squeezed" feeling.

Note the enharmonic ♭4/3: the third and fourth degrees (E♮ and F♭) are the same pitch on an equal-tempered instrument. In jazz notation, the scale is often written as: 1 – ♭9 – ♯9 – 3 – ♭5 – ♭13 – ♭7 (using interval names relative to a dominant chord), making its function clearer.

---

## Why Every Note Is Lowered

Super Locrian does not emerge from Locrian by analogy—it is the seventh mode of melodic minor. Starting from the seventh degree of melodic minor produces a scale where every non-root note is lowered compared to the major scale. The mathematical consequence: the scale contains every possible alteration of a dominant seventh chord (♭9, ♯9, ♭5, ♭13) simultaneously. This is why jazz theory calls it the "altered scale"—it contains all the altered extensions of a V7 chord at once.

For the player, the result is a scale that sounds like maximum tension: everything pressing inward toward the root from every direction.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Maximum instability | Every note is in a tense relationship with the root; there is no comfort anywhere |
| Compressed urgency | The two consecutive half-steps at the opening create intense chromatic pressure |
| Teetering at the edge | The scale sounds like something about to fall—suspended at maximum tension |
| Jazz sophistication | In its jazz context, the tension is deliberate and controlled: the listener knows resolution is coming |

Super Locrian is not beautiful in the way Lydian floats or Phrygian yearns—it is beautiful in the way that extreme tension before release is beautiful. Its emotional function is almost entirely contextual: it means maximum instability *before* resolution.

## As a Mode of Melodic Minor

Super Locrian is built from the seventh degree of melodic minor ascending. In the key of D♭ melodic minor (D♭–E♭–F♭–G♭–A♭–B♭–C), starting from C (the seventh degree) gives: C–D♭–E♭–F♭–G♭–A♭–B♭–C—exactly Super Locrian.

This relationship to melodic minor explains its jazz application: wherever C functions as a dominant seventh chord (C7) resolving to F major or F minor, C Super Locrian (= D♭ melodic minor starting on C) provides a scale that maximizes tension over the C7 chord before the F resolution.

## The Jazz Context: Altered Dominants

In jazz, the most tense harmonic moment in a standard ii–V–I progression is the V7 chord. Super Locrian is the scale that maximizes the dissonance of that moment by altering every possible extension:

- ♭9 (D♭): flatter than the natural ninth—dark, pressing
- ♯9 (E♭ / D♯): sharper than the natural ninth—tense, chromatic
- ♭5 / ♯11 (G♭ / F♯): the tritone substitution pitch
- ♭13 (A♭): flatter than the natural thirteenth—ominous

Using Super Locrian over a V7 chord in jazz creates maximum dissonance that demands and amplifies the resolution to the I chord. Miles Davis, John Coltrane, and virtually all post-bop jazz musicians use the altered scale (Super Locrian) as their default over altered dominant chords.

Key recordings demonstrating altered scale use:
- **John Coltrane**—*"Giant Steps"* (1960): the rapid ii–V–I progressions in multiple keys, each with altered dominant scales; Super Locrian over every V chord
- **Miles Davis**—*"All Blues"* (from *Kind of Blue*, 1959): more subtle altered dominant passages
- **Herbie Hancock**—*"Maiden Voyage"*: jazz harmony where altered scales provide the tension layer
- **Bill Evans**—piano voicings over altered dominants are the model for how to hear Super Locrian in context

## For Solo Wind Players: Practical Use

Without jazz accompaniment, Super Locrian functions differently than in its native jazz context. For solo wind playing, it is most effective as:

**A brief passage of controlled disorientation**: a four-to-eight-bar passage in Super Locrian within an otherwise modal composition creates an extreme moment of tension before returning to the mode. The return makes the original mode sound newly clear.

**The scale of maximum contrast**: used immediately before a resolution to a stable mode (Dorian, Ionian, Phrygian), Super Locrian creates the greatest possible contrast. The listener experiences relief at the resolution proportional to the tension of the Super Locrian passage.

**Tonal palette, not key**: rather than treating Super Locrian as a key to write in, treat it as a palette of chromatic pitches available for a tense transition passage between two more stable modal sections.

---

## Instrument Access

Super Locrian requires fully chromatic instruments—the many lowered degrees need either cross-fingerings or pitch bending.

| Instrument | Ease | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Bawu | Excellent | Pitch bending handles all lowered degrees with maximum expressiveness; the compressed chromatic opening (C–D♭–E♭–E♮) is most vocal on bawu |
| Alto recorder | Good | All notes available via cross-fingering; requires preparation for the chromatic cluster at the opening |
| Low D whistle | Challenging | Most of the lowered degrees need cross-fingering or half-holing; possible but not idiomatic |
| Moldavian kaval | Good | Embouchure flexibility allows the lowered degrees with expressive quality |
| NAF | Very limited | The scale's dense chromaticism is almost entirely outside the NAF's native range |

## Composing with Super Locrian

**Use it as a climactic passage before resolution**: the most effective compositional use of Super Locrian in solo wind writing is as a brief climactic passage at the emotional peak of a piece—a moment of maximum tension before the final return to the home mode.

**The ♭2→1 sigh still works**: despite its extreme character, Super Locrian retains the Phrygian half-step above the root (D♭ above C). The sigh gesture (D♭ → C) is available and provides a moment of familiar Phrygian yearning within the otherwise extreme dissonance.

**Move quickly through the chromatic cluster**: the opening ♭2–♭3–♭4 cluster (D♭–E♭–E♮) moves through three notes that are very close together. Play this section quickly—let it feel like a compression, not a melody. The dissonance is the point; dwell on individual notes later in the scale.

**Resolve clearly and deliberately**: after a Super Locrian passage, the resolution back to a stable mode should be unmistakable—land firmly on the home note of the destination mode, hold it, and let the stability register before continuing.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu
**Context**: Super Locrian as a climactic passage within a D Dorian piece

*D Dorian*: D – E – F – G – A – B – C – D
*D Super Locrian*: D – E♭ – F – G♭ – A♭ – B♭ – C♭ – D

1. Play a sustained D Dorian phrase. Let it settle on A (the fifth).
2. Without preparation, enter D Super Locrian: D – E♭ – F – G♭. The E♭ (♭2) arrives immediately as a pressing half-step above D. Keep the dynamic the same—the dissonance, not the volume, is the tension.
3. Continue: G♭ – A♭ – B♭ – C♭ – D (high). Every note is slightly "wrong" relative to Dorian—but arrived at with intent.
4. From high D: silence for four beats. The tension hangs.
5. Return to D Dorian: D – E – F – G – A – B – C – D. The natural E (instead of E♭), the natural G (instead of G♭)—each note sounds newly right after the Super Locrian passage.

**Goal**: experience the emotional function of Super Locrian as a contrast device—the scale that makes the surrounding modes sound newly clear and stable by comparison.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Maximum instability before resolution**: use Super Locrian as a transitional passage—a brief section of extreme harmonic tension before resolving to a stable mode. The emotional effect is the contrast itself: after the disorientation of Super Locrian's every-note-lowered world, any stable modal arrival sounds like relief. This contrast technique works best when the resolution is to a mode the listener already knows from earlier in the composition (Dorian, Aeolian, Phrygian)—the familiar mode heard with new vividness after the Super Locrian disruption.

**Controlled disorientation**: in a composition that needs a single moment of profound unease—not the gentle unease of Locrian but a more complete harmonic disruption—a four-to-eight bar Super Locrian passage creates an experience of having the ground shift beneath the listener. Follow it with silence, then return.

**Jazz tension over a dominant chord**: if composing in a jazz context with accompaniment, Super Locrian over an altered V7 chord creates the maximally tense moment before the I chord resolution. Each chromatic passing note (♭9, ♯9, ♭5, ♭13) points toward the resolution. Without the accompaniment providing the harmonic context, this function is lost—which is why Super Locrian works better as a solo-wind technique of controlled disorientation rather than as a jazz harmonic tool.

### Step-by-Step: Using Super Locrian as a Compositional Event

Super Locrian is not a scale to compose *in*—it is a scale to compose *with*. Use it as a single dramatic moment within a larger modal piece:

1. **Establish the home mode**: write four to six phrases in your primary mode (Dorian, Aeolian, Phrygian). Let the mode become fully familiar to the listener.

2. **Build to the Super Locrian moment**: the phrase immediately before Super Locrian should end slightly unresolved—on the flat seventh, or on the characteristic note of the mode without fully landing on the tonic. This creates expectation.

3. **Enter Super Locrian**: begin the Super Locrian passage on the same tonic as your home mode. The first note (C in C Super Locrian) is familiar; the second note (D♭) is one half-step away, the same as in Phrygian—familiar; but the third note (E♭, then the ♭4 which is E♮) creates a cluster of three notes very close together. Play these opening three notes deliberately slowly so each note registers before the next.

4. **Navigate the tritone**: the ♭5 (G♭ in C Super Locrian) is the tritone above the tonic—the most dissonant interval in Western music. Sustain G♭ for two beats. This is the heart of the Super Locrian disruption—the tritone above the tonic, held without resolution.

5. **Silence**: after the G♭ (or the full Super Locrian phrase), silence for four to six beats. Let the dissonance hang in the listener's mind.

6. **Return home**: re-enter the original mode clearly, on the tonic, with a melodic gesture that is unambiguously in the home mode. The contrast—the stability of the home mode after the Super Locrian disruption—is the musical point.

### The Key Insight for Beginners

Super Locrian's power is entirely dependent on what surrounds it. Used in isolation (a piece entirely in Super Locrian), it sounds chaotic and unpleasant. Used as a single dramatic event within a modal piece, it sounds like a moment of profound disruption that makes everything around it more vivid.

Think of it as a narrative device: Super Locrian is the moment when the door opens onto something unexpected. The door must be established (the preceding modal passage) and must close again (the return to the home mode) for the opening to have meaning.

## Common Beginner Mistakes with Super Locrian

**Using it as a primary modal language**: Super Locrian is not a key to compose in—it is a tool for controlled disorientation within a modal composition. A piece entirely in Super Locrian sounds chaotic and purposelessly dissonant. It needs the context of a surrounding modal world to function expressively.

**Not resolving after the Super Locrian passage**: the entire point of a Super Locrian disruption is what follows it—the return to a stable mode that sounds newly grounded after the disorientation. Without a clear, deliberate resolution back to a defined mode, the Super Locrian passage just ends rather than landing. Plan the resolution before entering the scale.

**Playing it too fast**: Super Locrian's close intervals (two consecutive half-steps at the opening: C – D♭ – E♭ – E♮) need time to register. Fast passages through Super Locrian produce chromatic noise. Slow, deliberate movement through the scale, especially the opening half-step cluster, allows the dissonance to be heard as expressive tension rather than technical error.
