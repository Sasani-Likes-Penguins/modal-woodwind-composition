## What Is the Double Harmonic Scale?

The Double Harmonic scale (also called the Byzantine scale, Hungarian Major, Arabic scale, or Hijaz Kar in Arabic music) is a major-framework scale with TWO augmented second intervals—one-and-a-half-step leaps [51](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maqam)[52](https://www.maqamworld.com/en/maqam.php)[53](https://www.maqamworld.com/en/maqam/bayati.php). These two dramatic leaps give it the most exotic, intense character of any seven-note scale in common use.

**C Double Harmonic**: C – D♭ – E – F – G – A♭ – B – C

Formula: 1 – ♭2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – 7

The two augmented seconds are:
1. Between ♭2 and 3 (D♭ → E: one-and-a-half steps)
2. Between ♭6 and 7 (A♭ → B: one-and-a-half steps)

The scale has **radial symmetry**—it reads the same from the root going up or from the root going down. This mathematical palindrome gives it an unusual quality of completeness and balance despite its tension [51](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maqam)[57](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_makam).

## The Many Names

The double harmonic scale is simultaneously [53](https://www.maqamworld.com/en/maqam/bayati.php)[57](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_makam)[58](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arel-Ezgi-Uzdilek_system):
- **The Byzantine scale**: used in Byzantine church music and Greek folk music
- **Arabic scale / Hijaz Kar**: the Arabic maqam built from two Hijaz jins
- **Hungarian Major**: Romani musical tradition
- **Bhairav raga**: Indian classical music (see file 21)
- **Mayamalavagowla**: Carnatic melakarta (see file 22)

This convergence demonstrates that different musical cultures independently found this combination of intervals compelling. The emotional power of the two augmented seconds appears to be genuinely cross-cultural.

## The Emotional Territory

The double harmonic scale is *intensely exotic*—the combination of two augmented seconds creates something that immediately signals "not Western European" to most listeners. Its emotional character:

- **Ancient and sacred**: the Byzantine church association means it carries connotations of prayer, incense, and timeless ritual
- **Dramatic and fierce**: the augmented second leaps are commanding, not tentative
- **Middle Eastern, Romani, Balkan**: the scale is the shared heritage of these intersecting musical worlds
- **Film: mystery and otherness**: Hans Zimmer used it in *Dune* for vast alien landscapes [51](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maqam); Saint-Saëns used it for the *Bacchanale* from *Samson et Dalila* [51](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maqam)
- **"Misirlou"** (Dick Dale): surf rock standard built on the double harmonic scale in E [57](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_makam)

## Compositional Approach

The two augmented second leaps are the emotional engines:
- Approach each augmented second as an *expressive event*, not just a scale step
- The leap D♭ → E (ascending through the augmented second) sounds like a sudden brightening within darkness
- The leap A♭ → B sounds like the same gesture an octave higher
- Between the augmented seconds, the scale has relatively stable stepwise motion—these quieter passages make the leaps more vivid by contrast
- The major seventh (B in C Double Harmonic) creates a strong pull toward the octave (home note)—unlike Phrygian, the Double Harmonic has a leading tone, giving it a sense of dramatic arrival

**For the kaval**: the Moldavian kaval's Hungarian-influenced scale already contains intervals similar to the double harmonic. This is the instrument's natural emotional territory.

**Exercise on kaval**: D Double Harmonic = D – E♭ – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C♯ – D. Begin on G (the stable fourth). Descend to F♯ (one step down). Then leap to E♭ (the augmented second—one-and-a-half steps). Then half-step to D. This D–E♭–F♯–G gesture is the scale's characteristic motif.

## Connection to Other Systems

The fourth mode of the Double Harmonic scale = Hungarian Minor (see file 17). The fifth mode ≈ Phrygian Dominant (flamenco mode, see file 28, with flat seventh substituted). These family relationships mean that Double Harmonic, Hungarian Minor, and Phrygian Dominant are all different perspectives on the same underlying pitch collection.

## The Interval Structure in Detail

| Degree | Note (from C) | Distance from previous |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 | C |—|
| ♭2 | D♭ | Half-step |
| 3 | E | Augmented second (1½ steps) ← First augmented second |
| 4 | F | Half-step |
| 5 | G | Whole step |
| ♭6 | A♭ | Half-step |
| 7 | B | Augmented second (1½ steps) ← Second augmented second |
| 8 | C | Half-step |

The symmetry: the lower tetrachord (C–D♭–E–F) and upper tetrachord (G–A♭–B–C) are identical in structure—half-step, augmented second, half-step. This mirror symmetry gives the scale its unusual sense of completeness despite its extreme character.

## Where Double Harmonic Appears

**Folk and popular music**:
- *"Misirlou"* (traditional, popularized by Dick Dale and further by Pulp Fiction): E Double Harmonic; the most widely recognized Double Harmonic melody in popular culture—the descending line through both augmented seconds is the entire melodic identity of the piece
- **Dick Dale**—surf guitar recordings: Double Harmonic as the "exotic" color of 1960s surf music
- **Beethoven**—*Symphony No. 7*, 2nd movement (Allegretto): A Double Harmonic-adjacent in the famous slow march melody

**World music appearance**: The Double Harmonic scale appears under many names across traditions because the same pitch collection has been independently discovered or transmitted across cultures:
- **Arabic music**: Hijaz Kar maqam uses the same pitch collection with microtonal quarter-tone inflections on the ♭2 and ♭6
- **Indian classical**: Bhairav raga (Hindustani) uses the same notes; one of the morning ragas associated with devotion and peace
- **Turkish music**: Hicaz Kar makam; the full eight-note version with both augmented seconds
- **Greek folk music**: the scale appears in traditional Greek music, particularly in dance forms
- **Byzantine chant**: the hard chromatic genus of Byzantine modes closely related to Double Harmonic structure

---

## The Cultural Convergence

The fact that the same scale appears independently in Arabic, Indian, Turkish, Byzantine, and Romani musical traditions—and has been used for centuries in all of them—suggests it corresponds to something fundamental in human musical perception. The two augmented seconds create two "exotic leaps" that immediately signal otherness to listeners whose musical heritage is primarily diatonic. This signal is neurologically potent (augmented seconds activate the amygdala) and culturally loaded with centuries of cross-cultural musical contact.

## Repertoire to Study

- *"Misirlou"*—in any recording, particularly Dick Dale's electric guitar version: the most direct illustration of how the two augmented seconds define the scale's melodic identity
- **Hindustani raga Bhairav**—any classical recording: the same pitch collection in a completely different musical tradition; the contrast demonstrates how pitches and emotional meaning are culturally shaped
- **Arabic Hijaz Kar maqam**—oud or vocal recordings: microtonal version of the same pitch collection

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: Moldavian kaval in D (D Double Harmonic)
**D Double Harmonic**: D – E♭ – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C♯ – D

1. Begin on A (the fifth—stable). Slowly descend: A – G – F♯ – E♭ – D. The F♯ → E♭ (descending augmented second) falls dramatically—from brightness into shadow. E♭ → D is a half-step into home.
2. Ascend: D – E♭ (half-step press) – F♯ (augmented second leap—sudden brightness). Pause on F♯.
3. Continue: F♯ – G – A – B♭ (half-step again) – C♯ (augmented second leap—second dramatic leap) – D (home). The second augmented second (B♭ → C♯) in the upper tetrachord mirrors the first.
4. Use the kaval's dual-voice technique on the held D at the end—partial fipple coverage plus humming—to give the arrival an ancient, resonant quality.

**Goal**: a melodic statement that passes through both augmented seconds in succession, making the scale's symmetrical architecture audible.

## Composing with Double Harmonic Scale for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### What Makes This Scale Unique

The Double Harmonic scale is the only common seven-note scale with two augmented second leaps—one-and-a-half-step jumps occurring twice in the same octave, once in the lower tetrachord and once in the upper. No other scale in this repository produces this kind of dramatic, symmetric intensity.

The scale is also palindromic: the interval structure reads identically ascending and descending. This mathematical symmetry means the same characteristic gestures appear whether the melody is rising or falling, giving Double Harmonic compositions a quality of inevitability in both directions.

### The Two Augmented Seconds as Your Primary Vocabulary

Every compositional decision in Double Harmonic should relate to these two leaps:

**First augmented second** (♭2 → 3): in C Double Harmonic, D♭ → E. From the Phrygian-dark half-step above the tonic, a sudden leap of one-and-a-half steps to the major third. The effect: sudden dramatic brightening within a dark context. The note E arrives with force after the pressed D♭.

**Second augmented second** (♭6 → 7): in C Double Harmonic, A♭ → B. From the anguished flat sixth, a sudden leap to the leading tone. The effect: a second dramatic brightening in the upper tetrachord—the same gesture repeated at a higher octave level.

**Compositional principle**: use these leaps as *expressive events*, not as scale steps to be passed through quickly. When the augmented second arrives, give it space: pause on the upper note (E, then B) briefly before continuing. The note arrived at by leap sounds different from a note approached by step—it sounds *reached for*.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Ancient sacred power**: slow tempo; the two augmented second leaps arrived at deliberately and held; very soft dynamics between the leaps, slightly louder on the leap notes themselves; the full descending scale from high C through both leaps to low C—the scale's symmetry creates a sense of ancient architecture; kaval dual-voice technique at phrase endings.

**Fierce, exotic passion**: moderate tempo with rhythmic drive; the ascending leap D♭ → E as an upward thrust on a strong beat; the parallel gesture A♭ → B slightly later in the phrase; immediate return to the dark notes (D♭, A♭) after each leap—the brightness cannot be sustained; bawu pitch bends into each leap from slightly below for maximum expressiveness.

**Mysterious beauty**: sparse melody at slow tempo; never use both augmented seconds in the same phrase—alternate them (one phrase with the D♭ → E leap, the following phrase with the A♭ → B leap); long silences between phrases; the scale's symmetry felt but not obvious.

**Devotional intensity** (in connection with Bhairav raga character): very slow; the two leaps as points of devotional reaching—the melody reaching upward twice within one octave; the leading tone (B) sustaining briefly with andolan before the final arrival on C; this is the emotional world of Indian raga Bhairav, which uses identical pitch classes.

### Step-by-Step: A Complete Double Harmonic Composition

**C Double Harmonic**: C – D♭ – E – F – G – A♭ – B – C

1. **Establish C as home with the first dark note**: C alone (three beats), then D♭ (the ♭2—half-step pressing from above). Hold D♭ for two beats. Return to C. The Phrygian darkness of the half-step is established before the surprise of the augmented second.

2. **The first leap**: from D♭, leap upward to E (the augmented second—one-and-a-half steps). On bawu: this is a pitch slide, not a clean jump—slide from D♭ continuously up through D to E. The arrival at E should feel like emergence. Hold E for two beats.

3. **Settle after the leap**: from E, step down: E – F – G. These are normal steps, calm after the drama of the leap. Rest briefly on G (the fifth—stable).

4. **Introduce the second dark note**: from G, step to A♭ (the ♭6—one half-step above G, the anguish note). Hold A♭. The same pressing quality as D♭ but an octave higher.

5. **The second leap**: from A♭, leap to B (the second augmented second—same interval relationship as the first leap, now in the upper tetrachord). Hold B for two beats. The second leap should feel like the echo of the first—a gesture repeated at height.

6. **The leading tone arrival**: B is one half-step below C (the leading tone). From B, step to the high C. This final half-step arrival feels conclusive—the dramatic journey through both leaps ended with the simplest possible resolution.

7. **The complete descent**: from high C, descend slowly through the full scale: C – B – A♭ – G – F – E – D♭ – C. Both augmented seconds appear again in the descent (B → A♭ descending, E → D♭ descending)—but going downward they feel like falling rather than leaping. Each is an unexpected drop rather than an upward reach.

8. **Close on C with dual-voice** (kaval): the final low C held with the kaval's partial fipple coverage and humming simultaneously. The ancient resonance of the dual voice suits the Double Harmonic's timeless, sacred quality.

### The Symmetry as a Compositional Tool

Because the scale reads the same ascending and descending, a melody that descends through the scale contains the same characteristic gestures as one that ascends. This allows a specific compositional technique:

**Mirror phrases**: write an ascending phrase that passes through both augmented seconds. Then immediately write its exact mirror—the same phrase descended. The two phrases are structurally identical but emotionally different (ascending = reaching, descending = falling). Placed one after the other, they feel inevitable—as if the music could only have gone this way, and could only return this way.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Rushing through the augmented seconds**: the leaps are the entire emotional vocabulary of this scale. Beginners tend to pass through them quickly to reach the "normal" notes. Slow down on every augmented second. Give each leap's upper note (E and B) full time before continuing.

**Avoiding the dark notes**: the D♭ and A♭ are the notes that make the scale what it is. Beginners sometimes unconsciously avoid them because they feel "wrong." They are not wrong—they are the scale's emotional centers. Return to them after every leap. The brightness of E and B only exists in contrast with the darkness of D♭ and A♭.

**Same dynamic throughout**: a Double Harmonic melody needs dynamic contrast to express the scale's own contrast (dark notes vs. bright leaps). The dark notes (D♭, A♭) should be slightly softer; the leap arrivals (E, B) should be slightly louder. Let the scale's emotional architecture dictate the dynamics.
