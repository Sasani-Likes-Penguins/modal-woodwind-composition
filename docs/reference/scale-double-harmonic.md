## What Is the Double Harmonic Scale?

The Double Harmonic scale (also called the Byzantine scale, Hungarian Major, Arabic scale, or Hijaz Kar in Arabic music) is a major-framework scale with TWO augmented second intervals—one-and-a-half-step leaps [51][52][53]. These two dramatic leaps give it the most exotic, intense character of any seven-note scale in common use.

**C Double Harmonic**: C – D♭ – E – F – G – A♭ – B – C

Formula: 1 – ♭2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – 7

The two augmented seconds are:
1. Between ♭2 and 3 (D♭ → E: one-and-a-half steps)
2. Between ♭6 and 7 (A♭ → B: one-and-a-half steps)

The scale has **radial symmetry**—it reads the same from the root going up or from the root going down. This mathematical palindrome gives it an unusual quality of completeness and balance despite its tension [51][57].

## The Many Names

The double harmonic scale is simultaneously [53][57][58]:
- **The Byzantine scale**: used in Byzantine church music and Greek folk music
- **Arabic scale / Hijaz Kar**: the Arabic maqam built from two Hijaz jins
- **Hungarian Major**: Romani musical tradition
- **Bhairav raga**: Indian classical music (see file 21)
- **Mayamalavagowla**: Carnatic melakarta (see file 22)

This convergence demonstrates that different musical cultures independently found this combination of intervals compelling. The emotional power of the two augmented seconds appears to be genuinely cross-cultural.

## The Emotional Territory

The double harmonic scale is *intensely exotic*—the combination of two augmented seconds creates something that immediately signals "not Western European" to most listeners. Its emotional character:

- **Ancient and sacred**: the Byzantine church association means it carries connotations of prayer, incense, and timeless ritual
- **Dramatic and fierce**: the augmented second leaps are commanding, not tentative
- **Middle Eastern, Romani, Balkan**: the scale is the shared heritage of these intersecting musical worlds
- **Film: mystery and otherness**: Hans Zimmer used it in *Dune* for vast alien landscapes [51]; Saint-Saëns used it for the *Bacchanale* from *Samson et Dalila* [51]
- **"Misirlou"** (Dick Dale): surf rock standard built on the double harmonic scale in E [57]

## Compositional Approach

The two augmented second leaps are the emotional engines:
- Approach each augmented second as an *expressive event*, not just a scale step
- The leap D♭ → E (ascending through the augmented second) sounds like a sudden brightening within darkness
- The leap A♭ → B sounds like the same gesture an octave higher
- Between the augmented seconds, the scale has relatively stable stepwise motion—these quieter passages make the leaps more vivid by contrast
- The major seventh (B in C Double Harmonic) creates a strong pull toward the octave (home note)—unlike Phrygian, the Double Harmonic has a leading tone, giving it a sense of dramatic arrival

**For the kaval**: the Moldavian kaval's Hungarian-influenced scale already contains intervals similar to the double harmonic. This is the instrument's natural emotional territory.

**Exercise on kaval**: D Double Harmonic = D – E♭ – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C♯ – D. Begin on G (the stable fourth). Descend to F♯ (one step down). Then leap to E♭ (the augmented second—one-and-a-half steps). Then half-step to D. This D–E♭–F♯–G gesture is the scale's characteristic motif.

## Connection to Other Systems

The fourth mode of the Double Harmonic scale = Hungarian Minor (see file 17). The fifth mode ≈ Phrygian Dominant (flamenco mode, see file 28, with flat seventh substituted). These family relationships mean that Double Harmonic, Hungarian Minor, and Phrygian Dominant are all different perspectives on the same underlying pitch collection.
