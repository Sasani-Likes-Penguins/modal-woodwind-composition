## What Is Nusach?

*Nusach* (נוסח) is the Hebrew word for the system of musical modes used in Jewish liturgical chant—specifically the improvised and semi-improvised prayer melodies sung by cantors (*hazzanim*) in synagogue services. The word means something like "manner" or "custom," reflecting the fact that the musical style is inseparable from the specific prayer and the specific occasion.

If you took a traditional synagogue-attending Ashkenazi and placed him on a desert island until he had thoroughly lost track of time, then transported him to a service in any Orthodox congregation, within a few minutes he would be able to tell you what day of the week it was and the time of day. Most remarkably, little of this information would come from the words of the prayers. What varies markedly is the musical mode in which that text is sung. [13](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/the-mystical-tritone/)[18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/)

This is not metaphorical. The Jewish prayer modes (called *shtayger* in Yiddish—plural *shtaygers*, meaning approximately "way" or "manner") are functionally equivalent to the Arabic maqamat or Indian ragas: each mode is associated with a specific occasion, a specific emotional register, and a specific set of characteristic melodic motifs that must appear within it.

## The Three Main Shtaygers

### Ahavah Rabbah (אַהֲבָה רַבָּה)—Great Love

The most characteristic and instantly recognizable Jewish musical sound. Named after the blessing that precedes the *Sh'ma* prayer in the morning service (its opening words mean "with great love"). Also called *Freygish* in Yiddish (from "Phrygian").

**Scale**: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E
This is Phrygian Dominant—identical to the flamenco mode and very closely related to Arabic Maqam Hijaz.

The Ahavah Rabbah mode, featuring an augmented second, is rather frequent in synagogue music as well as in folksong and Hasidic melodies; it has also been used for the musical characterization of the Jewish nation by Mussorgsky, Anton Rubinstein, and other composers. [14](https://fastercapital.com/topics/the-role-of-melodic-intervals-in-music-composition.html)

**The key difference from Arabic Hijaz**: while the scale pitches are similar, Ahavah Rabbah strongly favors the third degree (G♯) as its characteristic melodic focus, whereas Hijaz-Nahawand maqam favors the fourth degree. The vocal articulation, ornament patterns, and cadential gestures are also distinctly different—this is Western music practice applied to a Middle Eastern-derived scale, not Middle Eastern music. [16](https://fiveable.me/history-music-1600-1750/unit-13)

**Emotional world**: the paradox at the heart of Ahavah Rabbah is its name. "Great love"—yet the mode sounds intensely yearning, melancholic, searching. This is the emotional world of devotional love that knows it can never be fully satisfied: the relationship between a finite human being and an infinite God. It is used for Shabbat and festival mornings, when the emotional register is simultaneously joyful and awestruck.

**Recognition**: Havah Nagilah ("Let Us Rejoice") began as a Hasidic melody in Ahavah Rabbah. The instantly recognizable "Jewish sound" in Western ears is almost always Ahavah Rabbah. [13](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/the-mystical-tritone/)[20](https://musictales.club/article/phrygian-mode-classic-examples-phrygian-diatonic-scale-through-music-eras)

---

### Magen Avot (מָגֵן אָבוֹת)—Shield of Our Fathers

Named after a paragraph in the *Me'ein Sheva* prayer following the Friday evening Amidah. The simplest and most peaceful of the three main shtaygers.

**Scale**: closely resembles natural minor (Aeolian) in Western terms. A – B – C – D – E – F – G – A

**Emotional world**: tranquility, peaceful Shabbat rest, the freedom of those who are no longer enslaved. It is used in simple davening (prayer), often by means of a single recitation tone, which the cantor uses to cover a large amount of liturgical text in a quickly-flowing style. The simplicity of both the mode and the chanting associated with it is meant to reflect the peaceful atmosphere of Shabbat. [12](https://musiversal.com/blog/learn-dark-chord-progressions)[15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroque_music)

Magen Avot is the mode of quiet acceptance—the opposite of Ahavah Rabbah's yearning intensity. Where Ahavah Rabbah presses upward toward something unreachable, Magen Avot rests in what already is.

---

### Adonai Malakh (אֲדֹנָי מָלַךְ)—God Reigns

Named after Psalm 93:1 ("The Lord reigns"). The mode of majesty, sovereignty, and grandeur.

**Scale**: major scale with a lowered seventh degree—identical to Mixolydian mode in Western terms. C – D – E – F – G – A – B♭ – C

**Emotional world**: majestic, grand, authoritative. It adds flats as it goes higher, and its pitch set is similar to the Persian Dastgāh-e Māhur and Russian Obikhod scale. [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/) Used for Kabbalat Shabbat (Friday evening service), Torah service, Rosh Chodesh, and any moment requiring regal solemnity.

Adonai Malakh is best understood as the mode of proclamation—the musical equivalent of standing upright and speaking with full authority. Where Magen Avot whispers, Adonai Malakh declares.

## The Fourth Mode: Ukrainian Dorian (Mi Sheberakh)

Beyond the three main shtaygers, the **Ukrainian Dorian** mode (also called *Mi Sheberakh* mode, after the blessing sung in this mode) deserves special attention:

**Scale**: D – E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D
This is the natural minor scale with a raised fourth degree (G♯)—creating an augmented second between F and G♯.

The Ukrainian Dorian mode is a combined scale built upon the seventh degree of the Ahavah Rabbah scale. It is closely related to the Magen Avot mode. [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)[12](https://musiversal.com/blog/learn-dark-chord-progressions)

**Emotional world**: intense, searching, somewhat darker than Ahavah Rabbah. Used for grace after meals, High Holiday sections, and moments of emotional intensity. Similar to Arabic Maqam Nikriz.

## The Liturgical Calendar of Modes

One of the most distinctive features of Jewish modal practice: different occasions require different modes. This is not merely tradition—the mode IS the announcement of the occasion's emotional register. [13](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/the-mystical-tritone/)[18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/)

| Occasion | Primary mode | Secondary modes |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Weekday | Magen Avot | Ahavah Rabbah for morning blessings |
| Friday evening (Kabbalat Shabbat) | Adonai Malakh | Magen Avot |
| Shabbat morning | Ahavah Rabbah | Adonai Malakh |
| High Holidays (Rosh Hashana/Yom Kippur) | S'lichah mode | Ahavah Rabbah, Adonai Malakh |
| Three Festivals (Passover, Shavuot, Sukkot) | Ukrainian Dorian | Magen Avot |
| Grace after meals | Mi Sheberakh (Ukrainian Dorian) |—|

## Modulation Between Modes: The Maneuvers

Cantors use specific well-known modulations between modes during a single prayer service, called *maneuvers* [11](https://www.ethanhein.com/wp/2010/scales-and-emotions/)[12](https://musiversal.com/blog/learn-dark-chord-progressions):

**The Yishtabach maneuver**: moves from Magen Avot to Adonai Malakh on the fourth degree of the scale, then passes through Ukrainian Dorian and returns to Magen Avot. Used to highlight the proclamation of God's name.

**The Sim Shalom maneuver**: begins in Ahavah Rabbah, modulates to the major key on the fourth degree of the scale, uses Ukrainian Dorian as a bridge back to Ahavah Rabbah. Used in the closing blessing of the Amidah.

For composition: these maneuvers demonstrate that modal music can modulate expressively between modes within a single continuous piece. The emotional shift of moving from Ahavah Rabbah's yearning to Magen Avot's peace—and then returning—mirrors the emotional arc of prayer itself.

## Sephardi and Mizrahi Practice

The Ashkenazi shtayger system described above is the Eastern European Jewish modal tradition. Sephardi Jews (from Spain/Portugal/Mediterranean) and Mizrahi Jews (from Middle Eastern countries) use different modal systems:

Mizrahi communities such as Syrian Jews use the full maqam system. Syrian Jewish practice assigns a specific maqam to each week's Torah reading (the *Weekly Maqam* system)—the most direct connection between Jewish liturgical practice and the Arabic maqam system described in file 18.

This means the Jewish modal world is itself a bridge between Western/Eastern European musical practice and the Middle Eastern maqam tradition.

## Composition Exercise: Ahavah Rabbah on Recorder

**Setup**: E Ahavah Rabbah (Phrygian Dominant) = E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E. Moderate, searching tempo.

**Rules following cantorial style**:
1. Begin on B (the fifth—the most common recitation tone in cantorial chant). Hold it quietly.
2. Descend: B – A – G♯ – F – E. The G♯ to F (augmented second—one-and-a-half steps downward) is the signature gesture of Ahavah Rabbah. Play it with deliberate weight.
3. From E, ascend: E – F – G♯ – A – B. The F–G♯ ascending augmented second lifts the melody with sudden intensity.
4. At A or B, pause briefly. A temporary resting point before continuing.
5. Return to E via a melismatic phrase (multiple notes on one breath): B – A – G♯ – F – E, ornament the F with a trill or lean note before resolving.
6. Dynamics: quiet and searching throughout, with slight intensification at the augmented second leaps—the emotional charge is in the interval, not the volume.

**Goal**: music that sounds like a question addressed to something vast and silent—the characteristic emotional world of Ahavah Rabbah.

## Composing in Jewish Nusach Style for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### What You Are Imitating

Jewish cantorial composition (*nusach*) is one of the most emotionally specific musical traditions in the world. Each mode is assigned to a specific liturgical occasion, and a trained listener knows not just what scale is being used but *when in the Jewish calendar* and *what time of service* is being sung—before a note of text is sung. You are composing in a tradition where the mode itself makes an announcement.

For wind players, this means: choose your mode, then play as though every phrase is a prayer—not petitionary prayer (asking for something) but the kind of prayer that is simply *being present* before something larger.

### The Three Primary Shtaygers

#### Ahavah Rabbah (Phrygian Dominant / Freygish)
**Scale**: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E (or transposed)
**Occasion**: Shabbat morning, festival mornings, high-holiday services
**Emotional register**: devotional yearning; reaching toward the divine with full awareness of the distance; passionate without being desperate

This is the most emotionally complex shtayger. The augmented second (F → G♯) is the yearning leap—the reaching. The Phrygian half-step (F above E) is the limitation—the distance that cannot be closed. Together they create a mode that is simultaneously passionate and humble.

**Key compositional features**:
- Melodic phrases typically *ascend* through the augmented second (F → G♯) in moments of intensity
- Phrases resolve by descending through F back to E—the yearning releases downward into the tonic
- The G♯ is held longer than surrounding notes; it is the emotional peak of almost every phrase
- Cantorial ornamentation (*melisma*): a single syllable of text is sung across many notes in a flowing, ornamental line. On wind instruments, this becomes: one emotional beat decorated by many ornamental notes, all centering on the same pitch before moving
- Silence between phrases is structural: after the yearning, a moment of stillness before the next reaching

**For wind players**:
- On **bawu**: slide from F upward into G♯ (the augmented second as a pitch bend—most expressive approach); trill or oscillate on G♯ before resolving down through F to E
- On **low D whistle**: E Ahavah Rabbah uses F♮ (first hole, natural) and G♯ (requires cross-fingering); the natural F is what makes this instrument so suited to the mode
- On **recorder**: fully available in any key; the veiled tone of cross-fingered G♯ (in E Ahavah Rabbah) adds a slightly other-worldly quality appropriate to the mode

#### Magen Avot (Natural Minor / Aeolian-adjacent)
**Scale**: D – E – F – G – A – B♭ – C – D (or equivalent natural minor)
**Occasion**: Shabbat evening, Friday night *Kabbalat Shabbat* service
**Emotional register**: peace, rest, the welcoming of Shabbat; a settling after the week; warmth rather than yearning

This is the most restful shtayger—deliberately chosen for the service that welcomes the day of rest. Where Ahavah Rabbah reaches, Magen Avot settles. The minor character is present but without the acute tension of the augmented second or the Phrygian half-step.

**Key compositional features**:
- Phrases are longer and more flowing than Ahavah Rabbah; less fragmented
- The flat seventh (C in D Magen Avot) is used to create open, unresolved phrase endings—the music breathes out rather than pressing
- The flat sixth (B♭) is the most emotionally charged note—it adds the touch of anguish within the peace
- Avoid strong dramatic gestures; this is candlelight music, not fire

#### Adonai Malakh (Mixolydian)
**Scale**: G – A – B – C – D – E – F – G (G Mixolydian)
**Occasion**: Kabbalat Shabbat (Friday evening), *Shacharit* morning service on weekdays
**Emotional register**: regal majesty; God reigning; triumphant yet humble

The major character of Mixolydian gives this shtayger its regal quality. The flat seventh (F in G Mixolydian) prevents it from sounding merely triumphant—there is always an openness, a sense that the majesty is not closed or complete.

**Key compositional features**:
- Ascending major phrases for phrases of declaration
- The flat seventh used at phrase endings for openness—the king has spoken but the subject's response is still to come
- Contrast between phrases beginning in the lower register (humble) and those that rise to the upper (declarative)
- Occasional borrowing from the parallel minor (G Aeolian) for phrases of petition within the larger regal framework

### The Ukrainian Dorian (Mi Sheberakh Mode)
**Scale**: D – E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D (Dorian with raised fourth)
**Occasion**: Torah service; Grace After Meals; blessing of the congregation
**Emotional register**: urgency, blessing with weight; the community gathered before something important

The raised fourth (G♯ in D Mi Sheberakh) creates the augmented second between F and G♯—the same interval as in Ahavah Rabbah, but in a different position (between the third and fourth rather than between the second and third). This gives the mode a slightly more folk-earthy character than Ahavah Rabbah—passionate but grounded.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Devotional yearning (Ahavah Rabbah)**: let the G♯ be the primary destination of every phrase; approach it with a lean (F held briefly before leaping to G♯); use silence after the peak before descending; the phrase arc is always: press upward → arrive at G♯ → release downward through F to E.

**Sacred peace (Magen Avot)**: slow tempo; very legato phrasing; phrases that settle rather than press; the flat sixth (B♭) as a moment of gentle anguish within the peace; avoid all harsh attacks or sudden dynamics.

**Majesty (Adonai Malakh)**: moderate tempo with rhythmic regularity (unlike Ahavah Rabbah which is freely rhythmic); phrases that ascend to declarations; the flat seventh as a characteristic "majesty note" on strong beats.

### Step-by-Step: Writing in Ahavah Rabbah

1. **Establish E as home**: three notes—E, and its neighbors B (above) and D (below). These are the safe notes; everything else presses.
2. **Introduce the press**: step up to F (the Phrygian half-step). One beat. Return to E. This is the beginning of every yearning phrase.
3. **The yearning leap**: from F, leap to G♯ (the augmented second). Sustain G♯ for two to three beats. The yearning is in the sustain—not in the leap itself but in the holding.
4. **The resolution**: step down from G♯ through A, G♯ again briefly, F, E. The descent is slower than the ascent; arriving home is heavier than leaving it.
5. **Second phrase, higher**: begin on A (the fourth) instead of E. Now the augmented second leap lands you on C (two beats), then B, then descend: B – A – G♯ – F – E. Higher register, longer phrase, same emotional arc.
6. **The melismatic peak**: choose one important note (G♯ or A) and ornament it with a flowing line of five to eight notes that all revolve around the same pitch—up a step, back, down a step, back, trill—before moving on. This is the cantorial style: one emotional moment, given maximum time.
7. **Close**: F → E, very softly, perhaps with a trill between them. The yearning does not resolve—it quiets.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Treating Ahavah Rabbah like flamenco**: they share the same scale, but flamenco has fire and rhythm; cantorial music has stillness and elevation. Flamenco phrases are short and rhythmically sharp; cantorial phrases are long and rhythmically free. Do not borrow flamenco's energy here.

**Ignoring the liturgical emotional arc**: the mode makes a specific emotional announcement before you play. Play *to* that announcement—do not subvert it. Magen Avot should sound peaceful; playing it with dramatic surges violates the mode's purpose.

**Too much ornamentation**: cantorial melisma is expressive, not decorative. Each ornamental phrase centers on one important note and illuminates it. Ornamentation that wanders without a center is noise. Every ornamental figure should have a gravitational center.
