## What Is It?

Hungarian Major is a seven-note scale built with an augmented second at the very opening—between the first and second degrees—rather than in the middle of the scale as in [Hungarian Minor](scale-hungarian-minor.md). This creates a dramatically bright, immediately "strange" feeling as the melody rises from its home note.

**Formula**: 1 – ♯2 – 3 – ♯4 – 5 – 6 – ♭7

**C Hungarian Major**: C – D♯ – E – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C

The scale can be understood as the third mode of the harmonic minor scale, or as a Lydian-Mixolydian hybrid with an augmented second opening. The ♯2 (D♯) appearing immediately above the tonic creates an interval of one-and-a-half steps right from the start—the scale announces its unusual nature in its very first step.

---

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from C) | Character |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| 1 (tonic) | C | Home |
| ♯2 | D♯ | Augmented second above tonic—immediate "otherness" |
| 3 | E | Major third—bright |
| ♯4 | F♯ | Raised fourth—Lydian float |
| 5 | G | Perfect fifth—stability |
| 6 | A | Major sixth—Dorian-like lift |
| ♭7 | B♭ | Flat seventh—Mixolydian openness |
| 8 | C | Home |

The scale contains both a raised second (♯2) AND a raised fourth (♯4), giving it two points of "exotic" brightness. The flat seventh (♭7) prevents it from feeling fully triumphant—there is openness at the top despite the dramatic brightness below.

---

## Emotional Character

Hungarian Major is dramatically bright in its opening, formally unusual, and somewhat searching in its lack of a strong leading tone. It sounds simultaneously exotic and grand—more formal and orchestral than Hungarian Minor, less fierce, but equally unusual.

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Dramatic, formal exoticism | The augmented second at the very opening signals "otherness" immediately |
| Bright yet strange | Major third within an unusual scale context |
| Searching | The flat seventh prevents complete resolution |
| Romantic orchestral color | Used by 19th-century composers for harmonic color and exotic effect |

---

## Distinction from Hungarian Minor

| Feature | Hungarian Major | Hungarian Minor |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Opening interval | Augmented second (C → D♯) | Whole step (A → B) |
| Third degree | Major | Minor |
| Overall brightness | Bright-exotic | Dark-fierce |
| Emotional character | Grand, formal, strange | Passionate, fierce, Romani |

Hungarian Minor sounds like a Romani dance; Hungarian Major sounds like an orchestral fantasia. Both contain augmented seconds, but in different positions and emotional contexts.

---

## Where It Appears

- **19th-century Romantic piano music**: composers exploring chromatic and exotic scales for orchestral color used Hungarian Major as a coloring device, particularly in fantasy and rhapsody forms
- **Franz Liszt**—some *Hungarian Rhapsodies* passages touch Hungarian Major territory in their most dramatic ascending figures
- **Harmonic minor modal usage**: jazz musicians working with harmonic minor modes encounter Hungarian Major as the third mode; it appears in more advanced jazz harmony over certain chord types
- **Film music**: used for scenes requiring formal exoticism or ancient grandeur—more dignified than the fiercer Spanish Heptatonic or Hungarian Minor

## Composing with Hungarian Major

**The opening augmented second is the statement**: any Hungarian Major melody should begin with the C → D♯ (or equivalent) augmented second—this is the scale's announcement of its identity. Use it as the first gesture of every phrase.

**Raise then float**: the ♯2 creates a leap upward; the ♯4 (Lydian float) continues the upward energy. A phrase that ascends C → D♯ → E → F♯ traverses both unusual notes in succession—a gesture of theatrical rising that announces the scale's full character.

**The flat seventh as landing**: phrases in Hungarian Major most naturally settle on the flat seventh (B♭) or the fifth (G)—the leading tone is absent, so there is no urgency toward the tonic. This gives Hungarian Major melodies a searching, hovering quality even after all their dramatic ascent.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: alto recorder (C Hungarian Major)
**C Hungarian Major**: C – D♯ – E – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C

1. Begin on C. Step up to D♯—the augmented second. This is larger than a whole step and smaller than a minor third; it arrives with a slight shock of unexpected brightness. Sustain D♯ briefly.
2. Continue: E – F♯ (another raised note—the Lydian float). Pause on F♯. Two unusual notes in succession.
3. Descend from F♯: F♯ – E – D♯ – C. The descent confirms both raised notes.
4. A second phrase from G: G – A – B♭ – C (high). The B♭ (flat seventh) before the high C creates the Mixolydian openness in the upper range—a contrast to the dramatic lower tetrachord.

**Goal**: music that sounds theatrical and slightly formal—the scale announces itself grandly without the folk earthiness of Hungarian Minor.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Dramatic exotic grandeur**: moderate tempo; the opening augmented second (C → D♯) as the first gesture of every phrase—a declaration; the raised fourth (F♯) sustaining briefly after the opening leap; the flat seventh (B♭) as phrase endings—the grandeur never fully arrives at simple resolution.

**Formal theatrical quality**: steady tempo; the two raised notes (D♯ and F♯) as the structural pillars of every phrase—the unusual brightening characteristic of the scale; phrases begin from C (tonic) or G (fifth) and rise through both raised notes; the return descent through B♭ (flat seventh) to C creates the open ending.

**Searching brightness**: slow tempo; phrases that approach D♯ (the augmented second) from slightly below, arriving with some hesitation; the F♯ (Lydian raised fourth) floating after D♯; the scale sounds like something reaching toward light that keeps finding itself in unusual intervals.

### Step-by-Step: Composing in Hungarian Major

**C Hungarian Major**: C – D♯ – E – F♯ – G – A – B♭ – C

The scale announces its character immediately in the first two notes—C to D♯ is an augmented second (one-and-a-half steps). This opening is the scale's identity.

1. **The opening declaration**: play C alone (two beats). Then D♯ (the augmented second—land on it with clear articulation, hold for two beats). The leap of one-and-a-half steps from C to D♯ is the scale's announcement. Do not rush through it.

2. **Continue through the raised notes**: from D♯, step to E (one half-step—small step after the large leap), then to F♯ (the raised fourth—another raised note, one whole step from E). Now you are at the scale's highest raised note. Pause briefly.

3. **The stable mid-section**: from F♯, descend to G (one half-step). G is the perfect fifth—stable and grounding after the raised notes. From G, continue: A (the major sixth—a normal major scale note; warmth). Rest briefly on A.

4. **The open ending**: from A, step to B♭ (the flat seventh—the note that prevents full major resolution). Then B♭ – C (home). The B♭ before C gives the arrival a Mixolydian-open quality—arrived, but not triumphantly.

5. **Second phrase with the augmented second in a different position**: begin on G (the fifth). Ascend: G – A – B♭ – C (high). Descend from high C: C – B♭ – A – G – F♯ – E – D♯ – C. The augmented second now appears in the descent (E → D♯—descending augmented second, a sudden drop). The same interval in both directions has different emotional qualities: ascending leap is dramatic; descending drop is surprising.

### Beginner Guidance

Hungarian Major is most naturally used as a **one-phrase wonder**—a melody that opens with its dramatic augmented second and then explores what lies beyond that opening. The challenge: not letting the unusual opening note (D♯) become isolated. Build the whole phrase through D♯ and beyond, so it becomes part of a melodic arc rather than a single shocking note.

Think of the composition as: unusual opening (C → D♯) → unusual continuation (E → F♯) → stable middle (G → A) → open ending (B♭ → C). Each section has its emotional character; the composition is the sequence of those characters.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Hungarian Major

**Rushing through the augmented second opening**: the C → D♯ opening augmented second (one-and-a-half steps) is the scale's entire identity. Beginners who pass through it quickly as a scale step miss the point. Slow down at D♯. Give it full note value. The scale announces itself in its very first step; let that announcement land.

**Treating it as Lydian Dominant with a different second**: Hungarian Major and Lydian Dominant share the raised fourth (F♯) and flat seventh (B♭), but Hungarian Major's raised second (D♯) changes the scale's opening character completely. The Lydian Dominant starts with two whole steps before the raised fourth; Hungarian Major starts with an augmented second immediately. They sound nothing alike, despite sharing four of seven notes.

**Ignoring the flat seventh as an expressive note**: the B♭ (flat seventh) in C Hungarian Major is what prevents the scale from sounding merely bizarre—it provides the Mixolydian openness that grounds the dramatic raised second and fourth. Phrases ending on B♭ feel searching and open rather than conclusive. Use this deliberately: the dramatic augmented second opening followed by a searching flat seventh ending creates a complete emotional arc.
