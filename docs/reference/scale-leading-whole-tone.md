## What Is It?

The Leading Whole Tone scale is a seven-note scale that begins like a whole-tone scale—moving entirely by whole steps for six of its seven notes—but then inserts a leading tone (a half-step) at the very top, just below the octave. The effect is of something floating and directionless suddenly remembering it must resolve.

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – 3 – ♯4 – ♯5 – ♯6 – 7

**C Leading Whole Tone**: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – B – C

Compare this to the C whole-tone scale: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – C. The only difference is the addition of B (the leading tone, a half-step below C) between A♯ and the octave.

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from C) | Distance from previous |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 | C |—|
| 2 | D | Whole step |
| 3 | E | Whole step |
| ♯4 | F♯ | Whole step |
| ♯5 | G♯ | Whole step |
| ♯6 | A♯ | Whole step |
| 7 | B | Half-step (leading tone) |
| 8 | C | Half-step |

This is also the third mode of the melodic minor scale (built from the third degree of A melodic minor ascending: C–D–E–F♯–G♯–A♯–B).

## The Dream That Must End

The whole-tone scale ([Whole-Tone](scale-whole-tone.md)) is entirely directionless—no note wants to go anywhere, no arrival is expected. The Leading Whole Tone scale is the whole-tone scale that suddenly acquires a destination. After five whole steps of floating, the leading tone arrives: one note that urgently needs to rise to the octave. Everything before it has been formless; this single note has a specific purpose.

Compositionally this creates a two-phase emotional experience:
- **Phase 1** (ascent through whole steps C–D–E–F♯–G♯–A♯): pure floating, no direction, the quality of [Whole-Tone](scale-whole-tone.md) or [Lydian](mode-lydian.md) suspension
- **Phase 2** (arrival at B, the leading tone): sudden urgency; the dream remembers it has a destination

This is the scale's characteristic emotional quality: it tells a story of suspension interrupted by necessity.

---

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Dream interrupted | Floating whole-tone quality suddenly pierced by urgency |
| Expansion then urgency | Rising through the scale builds the suspension; the leading tone breaks it |
| Bittersweet resolution | The leading tone's arrival is a kind of awakening—not unpleasant, but the end of the dream |
| Cinematic sweep | The whole-tone ascent followed by dramatic resolution suits large musical gestures |

The Leading Whole Tone scale is most effective as an ascending passage—the whole steps building suspension, the leading tone arriving as a dramatic climax, then the octave resolving. Descending through it loses much of the structural drama.

## As a Mode of Melodic Minor

The Leading Whole Tone scale is the third mode of melodic minor, built from the third degree of the melodic minor scale. In jazz theory, this mode appears over augmented major seventh chords (Cmaj7♯5)—a chord that already has an augmented fifth (G♯), and the Leading Whole Tone scale provides the full modal context for improvising over it.

This jazz use is specialized but significant: the mode appears in post-bop and contemporary jazz when composers want an extreme "outside" quality over major seventh chords.

---

## Where It Appears

- **Jazz improvisation**: over augmented major seventh chords; post-bop and contemporary jazz
- **Film music**: ascending whole-tone passages that suddenly resolve; used for scenes of building tension or sweep that must arrive somewhere
- **Contemporary classical**: composers working with melodic minor modes use this scale as a dramatic ascending gesture
- **Impressionist-adjacent writing**: Debussy's use of whole-tone scales occasionally touched the leading-tone half-step as a transition out of the whole-tone world

## Instrument Access

The scale requires chromatic capability—the raised fourth (F♯), raised fifth (G♯), and raised sixth (A♯) all require cross-fingerings on recorder and whistle.

| Instrument | Ease | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Bawu | Very good | Pitch bending handles the raised degrees; the dramatic leading-tone arrival is particularly expressive |
| Alto recorder | Moderate | Multiple cross-fingerings required; preparation needed |
| Low D whistle | Challenging | Most natural in D Leading Whole Tone; still requires significant cross-fingering |
| Kaval | Good | Embouchure flexibility helps with the multiple raised degrees |
| NAF | Very limited | Most of the scale's degrees are not native |

## Composing with Leading Whole Tone

**Use it primarily as an ascending gesture**: the scale's drama is almost entirely in the ascent—the whole-tone build followed by the leading-tone arrival. Descending melodies lose the structural logic.

**Make the leading tone land dramatically**: after the five whole steps of floating, the half-step to the leading tone (A♯ → B in C Leading Whole Tone) should feel like an arrival, not a passing note. Give the leading tone weight—a slightly louder dynamic, a slight lengthening—before the final step to the octave.

**Use the whole-tone section for texture, not melody**: the first five notes (C–D–E–F♯–G♯) can function as an atmospheric ascending backdrop—not a melody with melodic interest, but a sweep of rising sound that prepares the ear for the leading tone's arrival.

**Reserve for special moments**: like the [Enigmatic](scale-enigmatic.md) scale, the Leading Whole Tone works best in concentrated doses. Use it for a single dramatic ascending gesture within an otherwise modal composition—the appearance of the unusual scale against a modal context makes it more striking.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu (C Leading Whole Tone)
**C Leading Whole Tone**: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – B – C

This exercise uses the scale as a single dramatic ascending gesture within a Phrygian context:

1. Play a four-bar phrase in E Phrygian (E–F–G–A–B–C–D). Let the phrase settle quietly on E.
2. From E, begin the Leading Whole Tone ascent: E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – B♯ – D – D♯ – E (high). This is E Leading Whole Tone—the same scale structure from E. The whole-tone ascent rises with building intensity; the leading tone (D♯) arrives with urgency; the high E resolves.
3. After the high E: silence for four beats. Then return to the Phrygian melody, very softly.

The dramatic whole-tone sweep interrupts the modal context and then disappears back into it—a moment of cosmic expansion within an otherwise grounded piece.

**Goal**: a compositional texture where the Leading Whole Tone scale functions as a single dramatic event within a larger modal piece rather than as a sustained tonal world.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**A dream that must end**: begin with the whole-tone floating section (ascend through C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯), treating each step as equal and directionless; then when B arrives (the leading tone), play it noticeably differently—slightly louder, with a slight dynamic swell, as if something has suddenly become real; then B → C, the inevitable resolution; the composition is the contrast between the timeless floating and the sudden return of necessity.

**Building tension before a modal arrival**: use the Leading Whole Tone as a *transitional* scale within a primarily modal composition; a passage of whole-tone floating (directionless, floating) followed by the leading tone's sudden urgency before resolving into a clearly defined mode (Phrygian, Dorian, etc.); the modal arrival sounds more grounded and specific after the whole-tone ambiguity.

**Ascending revelation**: treat the whole-tone ascent as an ascent through uncertainty, each step equal and undefined; the leading tone (B in C Leading Whole Tone) as the moment of sudden clarity; the arrival at C as the revelation. This is a micro-narrative in one octave.

### Step-by-Step: Making the Leading Tone Meaningful

The entire emotional content of this scale depends on making the B (leading tone) feel genuinely different from the preceding whole-tone steps. If it doesn't, the scale is simply a chromatic oddity. Here is how to make the leading tone land with full emotional weight:

1. **Ascend through the whole-tone section slowly and evenly**: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯. Each step is exactly a whole step. Play them at the same dynamic, the same tempo, with no special emphasis on any one note. The effect: floating, directionless, each note equal in weight to its neighbor.

2. **When A♯ arrives—pause**: hold A♯ for three full beats. The listener is in the whole-tone world, expecting another whole-step to follow.

3. **B arrives as a half-step—unexpectedly close**: from A♯, the B is only a half-step away (100 cents)—the smallest possible interval. After five whole steps (200 cents each), this tiny step feels like a sudden compression. The B should arrive slightly louder than A♯. The contrast in interval size *is* the musical event.

4. **Hold B**: two beats of B, pressing upward toward C. The leading tone's urgency—after the whole-tone's indifference—is the scale's entire emotional statement.

5. **B → C**: the resolution. Arrive at C cleanly and hold it. After all the whole-tone floating and the leading tone's press, C sounds like arrival in a way it never does at the beginning of a standard major scale.

This five-step sequence—float, pause, compress, press, arrive—is the complete narrative of the Leading Whole Tone scale for a beginning composer.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Leading Whole Tone

**Not differentiating the leading tone from the whole-tone notes**: the B (leading tone) in C Leading Whole Tone must sound different from the preceding A♯. At fast tempos they blur together as two chromatic notes. At slow tempos—which this scale requires—the B should arrive with slightly more emphasis, slightly more dynamic, slightly more weight. The whole-tone notes are equal; the leading tone is special. Mark this distinction clearly.

**Treating it as a transition scale to be moved through quickly**: the Leading Whole Tone's drama depends on the listener experiencing the whole-tone floating before the leading tone's sudden urgency. If the scale is played quickly, the contrast collapses. Treat each note as a compositional event, especially the moment of transition from A♯ to B.

**Not having a destination**: the leading tone (B) points urgently to C—but C must actually arrive, and it must arrive as a clear tonal landing. The entire scale exists to make this arrival meaningful. If C is treated as just another note in a continuing sequence, the leading tone has no purpose. Plan the C arrival as the composition's destination.
