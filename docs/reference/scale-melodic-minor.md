# Melodic Minor: The Scale That Changes Direction

## What Is Melodic Minor?

Melodic minor was developed to solve the melodic awkwardness of harmonic minor's augmented second [36][37][40]. The solution: when ascending, raise both the sixth AND seventh degrees (making them match the major scale); when descending, lower them back (returning to natural minor).

**A Melodic Minor**:
- Ascending: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A
- Descending (classical): A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A (= natural minor)

In jazz, only the ascending form is used in both directions (called the "jazz minor" scale):
- A Jazz Minor: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A (same ascending and descending)

---

## Two Traditions: Classical and Jazz

**Classical melodic minor**: uses different notes ascending and descending. The ascending form (raised sixth and seventh) feels like a major scale briefly arriving—giving upward melodic motion a sense of light. The descending form (natural minor) returns to darkness. The contrast between the two directions is itself expressive: "ascending with hope and descending with melancholy in single melodic gestures" [45].

**Jazz melodic minor**: uses only the ascending form (raised sixth and seventh) in both directions. The result is a scale that is always somewhere between minor and major—never fully one or the other. Jazz musicians value this ambiguity as a source of harmonic richness; the scale supports chord types not found in standard major or minor harmony [41][43][44].

---

## The Emotional Territory

**Classical melodic minor**: the shift between ascending (bright) and descending (dark) gives melodic minor a distinctive quality of double emotional character—the same melody going up sounds different from the same melody going down. This is actually expressively useful for wind players: a phrase that rises with the raised sixth and seventh (hopeful, forward) then falls with the natural minor sixth and seventh (settling, darker) tells an emotional story within a single arc.

**Jazz minor**: smooth, slightly mysterious, sophisticated. The major sixth and seventh over a minor third creates a hybrid feeling—not quite minor, not quite major, but something in between that jazz musicians call "minor-major" [43][44][49]. Think Miles Davis's "Milestones" or the brooding sophistication of late-night jazz.

---

## The Seven Modes of Melodic Minor

The melodic minor scale generates seven modes, several of which are among the most important in jazz [42][44]:

| Mode | Other name | Sound | Use |
|------|-----------|-------|-----|
| 1st (root) | Jazz minor | Minor-major ambiguity | Over minor major 7 chords |
| 2nd | Dorian ♭2 | Phrygian-adjacent | Over suspended chords |
| 3rd | Lydian augmented | Lydian + raised 5th | Expansive and strange |
| 4th | Lydian dominant | Raised 4th + flat 7th | Over dominant 7 chords |
| 5th | Mixolydian ♭6 | Major with flat 6 | Over dominant chords |
| 6th | Aeolian ♭5 / Locrian ♮2 | Locrian with natural 2 | Over half-diminished chords |
| 7th | Super Locrian / Altered | Most dissonant | Over altered dominants |

The 4th mode (Lydian Dominant) and 7th mode (Altered/Super Locrian) are the most commonly used in jazz improvisation [41][42][44].

---

## Instrument Access

Melodic minor is fully available on all chromatic instruments. The ascending form is standard chromatic technique; the descending return to natural minor requires no additional technique.

## Composition Exercise: Melodic Minor Arc on Kaval

**A Melodic Minor** (classical form) on kaval: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A (up) then A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A (down).

1. Ascend slowly: A – C (flat third, establishing minor) – E – F♯ (raised sixth—suddenly brighter) – G♯ – A (leading tone arrival—urgency and resolution).
2. At the high A, hold briefly. *Now descend*: A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A. The G natural and F natural (restored to natural minor) create a sense of settling back into shadow.
3. The whole phrase: an arc that rises into light and descends into dark. This is the classical melodic minor emotional journey in one motion.
