# Melodic Minor: The Scale That Changes Direction

*File 33. ## What Is Melodic Minor?

Melodic minor was developed to solve the melodic awkwardness of harmonic minor's augmented second [36](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bawu)[37](https://organology.net/instrument/bawu/)[40](https://www.tradschool.com/en/irish-music/low-whistle/). The solution: when ascending, raise both the sixth AND seventh degrees (making them match the major scale); when descending, lower them back (returning to natural minor).

**A Melodic Minor**:
- Ascending: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A
- Descending (classical): A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A (= natural minor)

In jazz, only the ascending form is used in both directions (called the "jazz minor" scale):
- A Jazz Minor: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A (same ascending and descending)

---

## Two Traditions: Classical and Jazz

**Classical melodic minor**: uses different notes ascending and descending. The ascending form (raised sixth and seventh) feels like a major scale briefly arriving—giving upward melodic motion a sense of light. The descending form (natural minor) returns to darkness. The contrast between the two directions is itself expressive: "ascending with hope and descending with melancholy in single melodic gestures" [45](https://omeka-s.grinnell.edu/s/MusicalInstruments/item/1532).

**Jazz melodic minor**: uses only the ascending form (raised sixth and seventh) in both directions. The result is a scale that is always somewhere between minor and major—never fully one or the other. Jazz musicians value this ambiguity as a source of harmonic richness; the scale supports chord types not found in standard major or minor harmony [41](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_whistle)[43](https://zhivkovasilev.com/range-and-registers-of-the-bulgarian-kaval/)[44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html).

---

## The Emotional Territory

**Classical melodic minor**: the shift between ascending (bright) and descending (dark) gives melodic minor a distinctive quality of double emotional character—the same melody going up sounds different from the same melody going down. This is actually expressively useful for wind players: a phrase that rises with the raised sixth and seventh (hopeful, forward) then falls with the natural minor sixth and seventh (settling, darker) tells an emotional story within a single arc.

**Jazz minor**: smooth, slightly mysterious, sophisticated. The major sixth and seventh over a minor third creates a hybrid feeling—not quite minor, not quite major, but something in between that jazz musicians call "minor-major" [43](https://zhivkovasilev.com/range-and-registers-of-the-bulgarian-kaval/)[44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html)[49]. Think Miles Davis's "Milestones" or the brooding sophistication of late-night jazz.

## The Seven Modes of Melodic Minor

The melodic minor scale generates seven modes, several of which are among the most important in jazz [42](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaval)[44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html):

| Mode | Other name | Sound | Use |
|------|-----------|-------|-----|
| 1st (root) | Jazz minor | Minor-major ambiguity | Over minor major 7 chords |
| 2nd | Dorian ♭2 | Phrygian-adjacent | Over suspended chords |
| 3rd | Lydian augmented | Lydian + raised 5th | Expansive and strange |
| 4th | Lydian dominant | Raised 4th + flat 7th | Over dominant 7 chords |
| 5th | Mixolydian ♭6 | Major with flat 6 | Over dominant chords |
| 6th | Aeolian ♭5 / Locrian ♮2 | Locrian with natural 2 | Over half-diminished chords |
| 7th | Super Locrian / Altered | Most dissonant | Over altered dominants |

The 4th mode (Lydian Dominant) and 7th mode (Altered/Super Locrian) are the most commonly used in jazz improvisation [41](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_whistle)[42](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaval)[44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html).

## Instrument Access

Melodic minor is fully available on all chromatic instruments. The ascending form is standard chromatic technique; the descending return to natural minor requires no additional technique.

## Composition Exercise: Melodic Minor Arc on Kaval

**A Melodic Minor** (classical form) on kaval: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A (up) then A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A (down).

1. Ascend slowly: A – C (flat third, establishing minor) – E – F♯ (raised sixth—suddenly brighter) – G♯ – A (leading tone arrival—urgency and resolution).
2. At the high A, hold briefly. *Now descend*: A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A. The G natural and F natural (restored to natural minor) create a sense of settling back into shadow.
3. The whole phrase: an arc that rises into light and descends into dark. This is the classical melodic minor emotional journey in one motion.

## Where Melodic Minor Appears

**Classical usage**: melodic minor was developed specifically to smooth the awkward augmented second of harmonic minor in ascending melodic lines.
- **Bach**—virtually all minor-key melodies use the ascending form (raised 6th and 7th) when ascending and natural minor when descending—this is the classical melodic minor practice applied without naming it
- **Mozart**—*Don Giovanni*, many passages: the ascending raised sixth and seventh give upward lines their lift; the descending natural minor their darkness
- **Schubert**—slow movements in minor keys: the ascending melodic minor creates the characteristic Schubert sense of hopeful rising within sadness

**Jazz usage (ascending form only)**:
- The jazz minor scale is the foundation for all melodic minor mode applications in jazz harmony
- **Bill Evans**—voicings and melodies over minor major seventh chords (Cmin maj7) use the jazz minor directly
- **Wayne Shorter**—compositions like "Infant Eyes" create melodic minor harmonic environments

---

## Repertoire to Study

- **Bach**—any minor-key cantata aria: listen specifically for the raised sixth and seventh in ascending passages, natural minor descending
- **Mozart**—*Piano Concerto No. 20 in D minor, K. 466*: classical melodic minor in a concerto context
- **Bill Evans**—*"Peace Piece"* and *Waltz for Debby* (minor sections): jazz minor melodic and harmonic treatment

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: any chromatic instrument (A melodic minor)
**A Melodic Minor (classical)**: ascending A–B–C–D–E–F♯–G♯–A; descending A–G–F–E–D–C–B–A

1. Ascend slowly: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A. The F♯ (raised sixth) arrives: brighter than natural minor. The G♯ (raised seventh): urgency, pressing upward. The high A: resolution.
2. Without pause, descend: A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A. The G natural and F natural return: the shadow reasserts. The mood darkens on the way down.
3. Repeat the ascent, but more expressively—feel the hope in the raised notes, the lift of F♯ after the darker scale below.
4. Jazz version: use only the ascending form in both directions. A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A, then descend: A – G♯ – F♯ – E – D – C – B – A. The sophisticated, slightly major-inflected quality of jazz minor appears in the G♯ and F♯ regardless of direction.

**Goal**: feel the emotional difference between the two forms—classical melodic minor as an arc of hope (ascending) and return to shadow (descending); jazz minor as consistently smooth and sophisticated.

## Composing with Melodic Minor for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### The Two Personalities in One Scale

Melodic minor's defining feature—the ascending form brightened by raised sixth and seventh, the descending form darkened by returning to natural minor—gives every melody written in it a built-in emotional arc. The same notes create two different emotional states depending on the direction of travel:

**Ascending = hope, lift, aspiration**: the raised F♯ and G♯ (in A melodic minor) push the melody upward with increasing brightness. Each ascending step feels like gaining ground.

**Descending = settling into shadow**: the return to F natural and G natural (natural minor) on the way down darkens each descending step. The brightness recedes.

This directional duality is the emotional engine of classical melodic minor. *Jazz minor removes the duality* (using the ascending form in both directions) for a consistently smooth, major-adjacent sound.

### When to Use Classical vs. Jazz Melodic Minor

**Use classical melodic minor when**:
- The composition has a specific emotional arc of hope followed by descent into sadness
- You are working in a Baroque or Romantic compositional style
- You want the asymmetry of ascending brightness and descending darkness as a structural device

**Use jazz melodic minor (ascending form only in both directions) when**:
- You want a minor scale with major-quality upper notes throughout
- You are improvising over minor major seventh chords
- You want a sophisticated, smooth minor sound without the "exotic" quality of harmonic minor's augmented second

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**The arc of hope and shadow** (classical): a phrase that ascends through the raised sixth and seventh (F♯ and G♯ in A classical melodic minor), reaches the upper octave, then descends through F natural and G natural. The ascent feels hopeful; the descent feels resigned. The entire arc is the emotion of something attempted and returned from—tried, but the world is the same as it was.

**Sophisticated, elegant sadness** (jazz minor): the minor third (C in A jazz minor) establishes the minor quality; the major sixth (F♯) and leading tone (G♯) add sophistication; phrases move fluidly without the augmented second harshness of harmonic minor. This is the sound of Debussy's minor passages and Bill Evans's ballads—sad but never crude.

**Momentary brightness within dark writing**: in a primarily Aeolian or Phrygian composition, a single ascending phrase in melodic minor—touching F♯ and G♯ where F and G♮ were expected—creates a sudden brightening within the dark context. Return to natural minor immediately; the contrast illuminates both.

### Step-by-Step: Writing a Classical Melodic Minor Arc

**A Classical Melodic Minor**: up: A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A; down: A – G – F – E – D – C – B – A

1. **The ascending hope phrase**: begin on A. Ascend slowly: A – C (minor third—establishing minor) – D – E – F♯ (the first brightness—raised sixth arriving unexpectedly) – G♯ (the leading tone pressing upward) – A (high tonic—arrival). The ascent should feel like climbing toward light.

2. **The high moment**: hold the high A. Apply a dynamic swell—this is the peak of the arc. Use a [messa di voce](../glossary.md#messa-di-voce) if possible: soft → louder → soft on the single sustained note.

3. **The descending shadow**: from high A, descend using natural minor: A – G (natural seventh—the brightness gone) – F (natural sixth—the shadow falls) – E – D – C – B – A (low tonic). The descent should feel like returning to familiar darkness after a moment of light.

4. **Second arc, starting higher**: begin on E (the fifth). Ascend: E – F♯ – G♯ – A (upper tonic). The same raised sixth and seventh, but starting from the fifth instead of the tonic—a compressed version of the full arc.

5. **The jazz minor contrast phrase**: using only the ascending form in both directions, play: A – C – E – F♯ – G♯ – A (up), then A – G♯ – F♯ – E – C – A (down, still using F♯ and G♯). This phrase sounds smoother and more major-adjacent than the classical version—the shadow of the descending natural minor does not arrive.

6. **Final phrase in classical form**: the full descending scale: A – G♯ – F♯ – E – D – C – B (natural) – A. The raised sixth and seventh appear in the first two descending steps before giving way to natural minor. This creates a gentle version of the descending shadow—the brightness lingers briefly before the dark notes return.

### Instrument-Specific Compositional Tips

**Bawu**: melodic minor's ascending raised sixth and seventh allow the bawu player to use upward pitch bends toward F♯ and G♯ (approaching from F and G natural) as expressive gestures. The ascending "bend into brightness" is particularly natural on this instrument.

**Kaval**: D melodic minor (ascending: D – E – F – G – A – B – C♯ – D) is among the kaval's most natural modal environments. The raised seventh (C♯ in D melodic minor) approaches D from a half-step below—using the kaval's embouchure flexibility for a smooth leading-tone arrival.

**Irish whistle**: E melodic minor (ascending: E – F♯ – G – A – B – C♯ – D♯ – E) uses C♯ and D♯—both requiring cross-fingering on the D whistle. The ascending form is idiomatic; the descending natural minor (C♯ → C♮, D♯ → D♮) requires careful fingering attention.

**Recorder**: fully available in all keys. The classical melodic minor's directional duality is most clearly expressed on the recorder through dynamic shaping—crescendo on the ascending bright form, diminuendo on the descending dark form.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Melodic Minor

**Hope ascending, shadow descending** (classical form): ascend through the raised sixth and seventh to feel optimism and aspiration; descend through the natural minor to feel the return of settled sadness. A phrase that ascends in classical melodic minor and descends in natural minor tells a complete emotional story—the arc of hope attempted and the return to the world as it is.

**Sophisticated minor beauty** (jazz form): the consistently raised sixth and seventh give the jazz minor scale a near-major brightness within a minor framework—more elegant than plain Aeolian, less harsh than harmonic minor. Use for music that is minor in character but refined rather than dark: grief that is articulate, sadness that has aesthetic distance.

**The moment of unexpected brightness within darkness**: in a composition primarily in Aeolian or Dorian, briefly use the raised sixth and seventh (ascending melodic minor form) for a single phrase—the unexpected brightening of those two raised notes makes the phrase feel like a ray of light within the surrounding minor. Return immediately to natural minor.

**Rising aspiration before inevitable return**: a complete composition arc—multiple phrases ascending in melodic minor form (building, brightening) reaching a peak, then descending phrases reverting to natural minor (settling, darkening). The full classical arc of the scale as a compositional narrative.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Melodic Minor

**Not switching back to natural minor when descending** (classical form): the entire expressive point of classical melodic minor is the *difference* between ascending (raised sixth and seventh—bright) and descending (natural minor—dark). Beginners who use the raised notes in both directions are using jazz minor, not classical melodic minor. Check: when the phrase descends, are F♯ and G♯ back to F natural and G natural? If not, you have lost the classical form's defining emotional duality.

**Treating classical and jazz forms as interchangeable**: they produce completely different emotional results. Classical melodic minor has directional duality; jazz minor has consistent smoothness. Choose which you want and use it consistently within a passage. Mixing them accidentally—ascending with raised notes, descending with raised notes in some phrases and natural in others—creates incoherence.

**Playing the jazz minor for everything "minor"**: jazz minor's consistent brightness makes it sound more harmonically sophisticated than Aeolian, which leads some beginners to default to it for all minor writing. But Aeolian's simplicity carries a directness that jazz minor cannot—pure, settled sadness rather than elegant melancholy. Match the scale to the specific emotion, not to a general preference.

**Forgetting the augmented second in the ascending classical form**: the classical ascending form (A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯) has a leading tone (G♯) pressing urgently toward the octave. The G♯ is not optional—it is what makes the ascending phrase feel like it is rising toward light rather than just climbing. Make the G♯ emphatic: slightly louder, slightly held.
