## What Is the Octoechos?

The **Octoechos** (Greek: Ὀκτώηχος, meaning "of the eight sounds/modes") is the eight-mode system used for the composition of religious chant in Eastern Orthodox Christianity [91][98][100]. It was codified in the 8th century by Saint John of Damascus, drawing from earlier Syrian, Greek, and Jewish liturgical traditions.

The Octoechos organizes all Orthodox church hymns into an eight-week cycle—each week uses a different mode (*echos*), rotating continuously through the liturgical year. By rotating modes each week, the Octoechos prevents monotony, allowing hymns to evoke a range of affections from solemnity to jubilation [100].

---

## The Eight Echoi (Tones)

The eight modes are divided into four **authentic** (primary) and four **plagal** (derived) modes [91][92]:

| Mode | Greek name | Character |
|------|-----------|----------|
| 1st Tone | Protos | Hope, strength—the "first voice" of the Church |
| 2nd Tone | Deuteros | Tender, intimate |
| 3rd Tone | Tritos | Festive, bright |
| 4th Tone | Tetartos | Majestic, solemn |
| Plagal 1st | Plagios Protos | Repentant joy—a deepened version of the 1st |
| Plagal 2nd | Plagios Deuteros | Intimate sorrow |
| Grave |—(Plagal of 3rd) | Grave, serious |
| Plagal 4th | Plagios Tetartos | Festivity, celebration |

Each echos carries symbolic attributes—Mode I for hope and strength, Mode V (Plagal First) for repentant joy, Mode VIII (Plagal Fourth) for festivity—mirroring themes of creation, redemption, and divine praise [100].

## Byzantine Scales: Diatonic, Chromatic, Enharmonic

Byzantine music theory uses a more complex tonal system than Western modes. Each echos can be expressed in one of **four scale types** (*genera*) [94][98]:

**Diatonic**: uses standard whole and half steps—closest to Western modes
**Soft Chromatic**: uses augmented seconds (like Hijaz/Hicaz)—the "intensely Byzantine" sound
**Hard Chromatic**: more extreme augmented intervals
**Enharmonic**: uses very small microtonal intervals—the most ancient and difficult to perform

The same echos played in different genera sounds completely different. The soft chromatic version of the 2nd tone (Deuteros), with its augmented second, is one of the most characteristic sounds in all of Byzantine chant.

### The Byzantine Diatonic Scale

Byzantine diatonic tuning is not equal-tempered. The octave is divided into 72 equal commas (a comma is approximately 1/12 of a semitone). Byzantine intervals:

- Whole step = 12 commas (same as Western)
- Half step = 6 commas (smaller than Western)
- Augmented second = 20 commas (larger than Western)

This means Byzantine music has slightly different emotional qualities than the same intervals in Western equal temperament—it is slightly sharper, slightly brighter, than what a piano would play.

## Byzantine Chant in Practice

Byzantine chant is **monophonic**—a single melody line with no harmony, except for the *ison*: a held drone note supporting the melody [93]. This makes it directly applicable to solo wind playing—it was designed for a single voice without accompaniment.

**Rhythmic structure**: Byzantine chant uses three rhythmic styles [93][96]:
- **Heirmologic** (*heirmologica*): quick, one note per syllable—the most common style
- **Sticheraric**: slower, multiple notes per syllable—more elaborate
- **Papadic**: very slow, highly melismatic—used for extended ceremonial hymns

**For wind players**: the heirmologic style is essentially a flowing syllabic melody—each note has weight and intention, but the music moves continuously. The sticheraric style is the most expressive for wind—each syllable (or phrase on a single breath) receives multiple notes, allowing expressive shaping.

## Connection to Middle Eastern Modes

The Byzantine system did not develop in isolation—it emerged from the same Mediterranean musical world as the Arabic maqam and Turkish makam systems [91][93][100]. The relationship is close enough that some modes overlap substantially:

- Byzantine 2nd Tone (soft chromatic) ≈ Maqam Hijaz
- Byzantine Plagal 2nd ≈ Maqam Bayati
- Byzantine 1st Tone (diatonic) ≈ Maqam Rast (slightly)

The kaval, with its gypsy-scale tuning and its use across the Balkans (a historically Byzantine-influenced region), naturally bridges the Byzantine and Middle Eastern worlds.

## Composition Exercise: 2nd Tone Soft Chromatic (Byzantine Hijaz) on Recorder

**Setup**: 2nd Tone soft chromatic from E: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D♯ – E
(Note the augmented seconds: F–G♯ and C–D♯)

1. Begin on B (the "dominant"—the reciting tone of the 2nd tone). Hold it.
2. Descend: B – A – G♯ – F – E. The G♯ after A creates the first augmented-second leap (A down to G♯ = half-step; G♯ down to F = augmented second—one-and-a-half steps). This leap is the signature sound.
3. From E, ascend stepwise: E – F – G♯ – A. The F–G♯ augmented second ascending is the characteristic Byzantine "lift."
4. Return to B (dominant) for a temporary resting point.
5. Use very plain, dignified dynamics—no sudden surges. Byzantine chant has gravity, not drama.
6. End on E (the final/tonic), approached from F (the half-step above)—the Byzantine equivalent of the Phrygian sigh.

**Goal**: music that feels ancient, sacred, and Mediterranean—the sound of prayer across 1500 years of Orthodox tradition.

## Gaps and Limitations

- The full Byzantine modal system requires years of specialized training; this guide provides an entry point only.
- The enharmonic genus (with microtones smaller than Western half-steps) is beyond the scope of most wind instrument playing.
- Byzantine notation (neumes) is a separate and complex system not covered here.
