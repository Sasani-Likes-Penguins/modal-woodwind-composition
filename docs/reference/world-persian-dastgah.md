## What Is a Dastgah?

The word *dastgah* (Persian: دستگاه) translates roughly as "system" or "method." In Persian classical music, a dastgah is not a single scale but a collection of related melodies—each called a *gusheh*—organized into a musical world that a performer journeys through during a performance [21][23](https://synthase.cc/blog/the-phrygian-mode-is-quietly-changing-the-sound-of-loudness/).

A dastgah can be thought of as a family of related modes and motifs, not just a single scale. Each of the seven principal dastgahs contains several melodic segments (gushes) that define its character.

There are **seven principal dastgahs** and five secondary derivative modes (*avaz*):
- **Shur**—the most important, "mother of modes," fire red, meditative
- **Mahur**—bright, joyful, closest to Western major
- **Homayoun**—solemn, grand, complex
- **Segah**—plaintive, introspective, quarter tones
- **Chahargah**—powerful, dramatic, heroic
- **Nava**—haunting, serene, philosophical
- **Rast-Panjgah**—heroic, roughly major-like

## The Radif: Learning by Journey

The complete body of all gushes in all dastgahs is called the **radif** (Persian: ردیف, meaning "order"). The radif is a collection of many old melodic figures preserved through many generations by oral tradition.

Learning Persian classical music means learning the radif. A student memorizes all 300-400 gushes and spends years internalizing their melodic content, emotional character, and performance practice before attempting improvisation.

The first gusheh in each dastgah is always the **daramad** (meaning "opening")—it establishes the tonal center and emotional mood. The subsequent gushes take the performer progressively higher in register, further from home, and sometimes into briefly different modal territories, before the performance circles back to the opening material and closes.

## Microtones: Koron and Sori

Persian music uses two microtonal accidentals not found in Western notation:
- **Koron** (♭): lowers a note by a quarter-tone (halfway between a half-step and nothing)
- **Sori** (♯): raises a note by a quarter-tone

These microtones are not approximations—they are specific pitches with their own emotional functions. Dastgah-e Shur, for example, uses koron notes that give it a particular quality impossible to reproduce on a fixed-pitch Western instrument.

For your instruments:
- **Bawu**: pitch bending allows precise koron and sori inflections
- **Kaval**: embouchure adjustment provides quarter-tone flexibility
- **NAF**: half-holing approximates quarter-tones

## The Seven Dastgahs in Detail

### Dastgah-e Shur (دستگاه شور)
The most important and most performed dastgah. Dastgah-e Shur is one of the most fundamental modes in Persian music and is considered the foundation for several Avazes.

**Scale** (approximate): D – E♭ (koron) – F – G – A♭ (koron) – B♭ – C – D
(Note: the koron inflections make the E♭ and A♭ slightly higher than Western versions)

**Emotional world**: fire, passion, meditation, gnosis; Shur demands concentration on the part of the listener and is good for meditation. Can express both intense emotion and profound calm. It is best played from late morning until noon. Associated with fire.

**For composition**: begin with the daramad—stay in the lower tetrachord (D to G), establishing the koron-inflected second degree as the emotional center. Only gradually move higher. Return home.

**Closest to Phrygian** of all the dastgahs: most gushes in Dastgah-e Shur correspond to the Phrygian mode [21].

### Dastgah-e Mahur (دستگاه ماهور)
**Scale** (approximate): C – D – E – F – G – A – B – C (similar to Western C major, but with a neutral third in some gushes)

**Emotional world**: joy, celebration, brightness. Mahur is often compared to the major scale in Western music, as it shares some similarities in tonality. It is bright, uplifting, and commonly used in festive or celebratory music. Associated with late morning hours.

**Note**: even in Mahur, the radif contains gushes that briefly touch darker modes—including gusheh Delkash, which borrows from Shur. A performance in Mahur will modulate through multiple emotional territories before returning to its bright home.

### Dastgah-e Homayoun (دستگاه همایون)
**Scale** (approximate): C – D – E♭ – F♯ – G – A♭ – B – C

**Emotional world**: solemnity, grandeur, nobility, deep emotion. The scale of Homayoun has a mysterious and sometimes dark quality. A famous gusheh in Dastgah-e Homayoun is "Khayyam," named after the poet Omar Khayyam, whose works are filled with reflections on life and mortality. Associated with early morning, dark green.

### Dastgah-e Segah (دستگاه سه‌گاه)
Named "three-gah" (the third position), Segah has a distinctly quarter-tonal character that makes it unlike any Western mode.

**Emotional world**: plaintive, introspective, passionate. Segah is famous for its ornamental phrases and delicate microtonal nuances. It provides a rich canvas for improvisation and emotional expression. Associated with late afternoon until sunset, dark blue water.

### Dastgah-e Chahargah (دستگاه چهارگاه)
"Four-gah" (the fourth position). Contains augmented seconds (similar to Hijaz/Hicaz) giving it a dramatically exotic character.

**Emotional world**: powerful, dramatic, heroic, action-filled. This mode is full of excitement and action which is mainly caused by the contrast of the intervals between the notes. Festive and heroic; used for epic literature. Associated with morning, yellow/white.

### Dastgah-e Nava (دستگاه نوا)
A soothing, plaintive mode related to Segah.

**Emotional world**: longing, melancholy, serene depth. A more philosophical and subtle dastgah, performed in intimate gatherings and associated with storytelling.

### Dastgah-e Rast-Panjgah (دستگاه راست‌پنجگاه)
Roughly major-like in overall character, heroic.

**Emotional world**: bright, expansive, early morning; lighter tan color.

## The Structure of a Persian Performance

A traditional Persian classical performance follows this architecture [27](https://www.vaia.com/en-us/explanations/music/musical-performance/woodwind-techniques/):

1. **Pishdaramad**: an instrumental prelude setting the tonal and emotional context
2. **Daramad**: the opening gusheh, establishing the home tonal center
3. **Middle gushes**: a journey through related melodic territories, progressively higher in register, sometimes touching borrowed modes (*avaz* and related territories)
4. **Forud**: a descent—returning downward through the scale toward home
5. **Avaz** (singing section): vocal improvisation over the gusheh material
6. **Tasnif**: a composed song in the dastgah
7. **Reng**: a rhythmic closing piece

For a solo wind player composing in Persian style, the essential arc is: **begin low and establish home → journey upward through gushes → descend → arrive home transformed**.

## Gusheh as Seed Phrase

In Persian music, a *gusheh* functions exactly like the "seed phrase" concept described in the guide's composition section—a short melodic figure (often just 4-6 notes) with a specific emotional character that can be elaborated, ornamented, and developed without losing its identity.

The first gusheh in each dastgah or avaz is daramad. And then you go through—and you will be encountering with different sorts of gusheh. Some gushehs are composed based on the modal gushehs. Some gushes are mostly a rhythmic pattern; they present a rhythmic melodic pattern.

**For composition practice**: find a gusheh from the daramad of Shur (Phrygian-like; D–E♭–F–G at the core) and practice playing it until it feels inevitable. Then: ornament it differently each time. Change its rhythm. Play it in a higher register. This is Persian classical music's approach to composition.

## Instrument Access and Practical Approach

The Persian ney (an end-blown reed flute) is the primary melodic instrument of the radif tradition—and it is acoustically very similar to the kaval. The kaval player is therefore in a privileged position to approach Persian music.

| Instrument | Suitability | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| Kaval | Excellent | Natural tonal relationship with ney; microtonality via embouchure; Shur most idiomatic |
| Bawu | Good | Pitch bending for koron/sori inflections; best for Homayoun and Chahargah |
| NAF | Moderate | Pentatonic base approximates some gushes; quarter-tones limited |
| Recorder | Limited | Fixed pitches; Mahur (major-like) most accessible |
| Irish whistle | Limited | Same constraints as recorder |

## Composition Exercise: Shur Daramad on Kaval

**Setup**: D Shur = D, E-koron (between E♭ and D), F, G, A-koron (between A♭ and G), B♭, C, D. Begin low.

**Rules following daramad structure**:
1. Begin softly on D. Play the core four-note cell (D–E-koron–F–G) slowly, dwelling on the E-koron.
2. The E-koron is the emotional heart of Shur—approach it by bending slowly from E♭, letting it settle at its precise pitch. Hold it. Let it breathe.
3. Rise gradually: reach G and pause (the güçlü/ghammaz—the pivot note).
4. Briefly explore the upper tetrachord (A-koron – B♭ – C – D), but do not stay long.
5. Descend: C – B♭ – A-koron – G – F – E-koron – D. The descent is the emotional return.
6. End very softly on D.

**Goal**: music that feels like memory—Persian music's characteristic quality of intimate retrospection.

## Composing in Persian Dastgah for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### The Architecture of a Dastgah Performance

A dastgah performance is a journey. It has a specific architecture that applies whether the performance lasts five minutes or fifty. Understanding this architecture is the key to composing in the Persian classical style:

1. **Daramad** (opening): establishes the tonal center, introduces the characteristic notes of the dastgah, and sets the emotional register. Slow, meditative, unmetered. Never leave the immediate neighborhood of the tonic.

2. **Middle gushes** (ascending development): the melody rises progressively through the middle and upper gushes, each one a different shade of the same dastgah's emotional world. Each gusheh introduces new notes and reaches progressively higher in register.

3. **Forud** (descent to home): after reaching the highest and most intense gusheh, the melody descends back toward the tonic. The forud is characterized by long silences between phrases—the music breathes out, resting at progressively lower points before arriving home.

4. **Final arrival**: the tonic returned to, confirmed, and held.

Even a five-minute solo composition should follow this four-stage arc. The relative time given to each stage can vary, but all four must be present.

### Composing in Dastgah-e Shur: Step-by-Step

Shur is the most important and most performed dastgah in Persian classical music. Its emotional register—meditative fire, devotional depth, ancient yearning—makes it the closest Persian analog to Phrygian.

**D Shur**: D – E♭(koron) – F – G – A – B♭ – C – D

The *koron* on E♭ means the E♭ is slightly raised from standard—approximately 50 cents above D♭ but below a standard E♭. On fixed-pitch instruments, play E♭ and treat it as an approximation. On bawu or kaval, bend slightly upward from standard E♭.

**Stage 1: The Daramad**

1. Begin on D alone. Hold for six beats. This note is not just a starting note—it is an announcement. The entire dastgah is contained in D.
2. Step to E♭(koron): the characteristic note of Shur. On bawu: bend from just below E♭ to its center pitch, then hold. The koron quality—between D♭ and E♭—is the emotional texture of Shur.
3. The primary characteristic phrase of Shur: F – E♭(koron) – D. This three-note descent is the daramad phrase. Play it. Silence for four beats. Play it again, slightly varied (perhaps with a slow trill between E♭ and F before the descent). Silence again.
4. Establish A (the fifth) as a secondary resting point: D – F – G – A. Rest on A briefly. Then: A – G – F – E♭ – D. The characteristic phrase now appears as the ending of a longer phrase rather than the entire phrase.
5. Continue the daramad for four to six phrase pairs, always returning to D and always using the characteristic phrase (F – E♭ – D) at phrase endings.

**Stage 2: Rising Through the Gushes**

6. Begin rising: a phrase that reaches B♭ (the sixth). From B♭, descend through the characteristic phrase back to D. The B♭ is now the highest note—but do not stay there long. The gush of B♭ is touched and then released.
7. Next phrase: ascend to C (the flat seventh). The C is another gusheh tone—slightly darker than B♭. Descend through the characteristic phrase.
8. The climactic gusheh: ascend to the high D (octave) or the high E♭(koron) in the upper register. This is the emotional peak of the composition. Hold the high note—with a slow pitch oscillation (andolan) on bawu—before beginning the descent.

**Stage 3: The Forud**

9. Begin the descent from the high register. The first forud phrase: high D – C – B♭ – A. Pause on A for four beats. Silence for four beats.
10. Second forud phrase: A – G – F – E♭ – D. The characteristic phrase appears now as a resolution after the long journey. Rest on D for eight beats. Silence.
11. Third forud phrase: D – E♭ – F – E♭ – D. A shorter phrase, less far from home. The music is nearly settled.
12. **Close**: D alone. Hold until the sound disappears. Silence that lasts as long as the whole forud.

### The Koron and Sori: Microtonal Color

The *koron* (lowered by approximately a quarter-tone) and *sori* (raised by approximately a quarter-tone) are not decorative—they are the emotional texture of Persian music. Different dastgahs use different koron inflections at different scale degrees, and these inflections change the emotional character of each dastgah as distinctively as a mode change would in Western music.

For beginners on non-flexible instruments: play the nearest standard Western pitch. The characteristic phrase (F – E♭ – D in Shur) communicates the dastgah's identity even without precise microtonal accuracy. But listen extensively to Persian classical recordings to internalize what the correct pitch should eventually sound like.

For bawu and kaval players: develop the koron inflection by sustained listening and practice. Record yourself against a reference recording (Shajarian, Nazeri, or any skilled Persian classical vocalist) and adjust until the koron pitch is correctly placed.

### The Five Other Dastgahs: Brief Compositional Guides

**Dastgah-e Mahur** (the "Persian major"): ascends and descends like Western major; bright, ceremonial, formal. Use for music of celebration or official grandeur. The forud is less searching than Shur's—it arrives home more easily. The characteristic phrase ascends to the fifth and descends cleanly to the tonic.

**Dastgah-e Segah**: built on a neutral third above the tonic (between minor and major third); intimate, devotional, searching. The neutral third is the emotional center. Use for music of intense but quiet devotion. The characteristic phrase circles around the neutral third.

**Dastgah-e Chahargah**: the most intensely dramatic dastgah; two augmented second leaps give it the character of the Double Harmonic scale. Use for music of heroic intensity or passionate grief.

**Dastgah-e Homayoun**: associated with grandeur and solemnity; the opening is often compared to C minor; deeper and more formally serious than Shur.

**Dastgah-e Nava**: the most melancholy and introspective dastgah; associated with longing and loss; the characteristic phrase descends through the scale more heavily than Shur.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Meditative fire (Shur)**: slow and free; the koron E♭ given full emotional weight; the forud as a long, breathing descent with maximum silence between phrases; the bawu or kaval for their continuous resonance.

**Formal grandeur (Mahur)**: moderate tempo with regularity; ascending phrases to the dominant; the bright major character emphasized through tempo and dynamics rather than darkness.

**Devotional yearning (Segah)**: very slow; the neutral third as the most sustained note in every phrase; oscillation (andolan) around the neutral third; more silence than sound.

**Heroic intensity (Chahargah)**: the two augmented second leaps as expressive peaks; strong dynamics; the forud as a dramatic descent rather than a quiet settling.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Skipping the daramad**: beginners rush past the daramad to reach the more dramatic middle gushes. This is a structural error—without the daramad's patient establishment of the tonic and characteristic phrase, the subsequent development has no foundation. Spend at least one-third of the composition's total time in the daramad.

**No forud**: a composition that develops upward and then simply stops has no arc. The forud is not optional—it is the resolution of the journey. Plan the forud from the beginning; know where the descent will begin and how it will settle.

**Ignoring the silence**: the forud in Persian classical music is as much silence as sound. Phrases should have long rests between them during the descent. The silence is the music resting, breathing, approaching home gradually. Fill the forud with notes and you have a descent; fill it with silence and phrases and you have a forud.
