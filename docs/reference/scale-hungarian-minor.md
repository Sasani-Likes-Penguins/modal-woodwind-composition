## What Is Hungarian Minor?

Hungarian Minor is a harmonic minor scale with one note raised: the fourth degree is raised by a half-step, creating an augmented second between the third and raised fourth [61](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dastgah)[62][63].

**A Hungarian Minor**: A – B – C – D♯ – E – F – G♯ – A

Formula: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – ♯4 – 5 – ♭6 – 7

It contains two augmented seconds:
1. Between ♭3 and ♯4 (C → D♯: one-and-a-half steps)
2. Between ♭6 and 7 (F → G♯: one-and-a-half steps)

Hungarian Minor is the **4th mode of the Double Harmonic scale** (file 16)—the same pitch collection rearranged to start from a different note.

---

## Historical Background

The Hungarian Minor scale emerged from Romani musicians performing Hungarian folk and dance music—particularly the *verbunkos* (military recruitment dance) and *csárdás* [62]. Franz Liszt encountered these musicians and used the scale throughout his Hungarian Rhapsodies; Brahms used it in his Hungarian Dances [62].

The scale carries the heritage of Romani migration from northern India (the root of the augmented second intervals) through the Ottoman Empire and into Eastern European folk practice—a living geological record of musical cross-pollination [62].

## The Emotional Territory

Hungarian Minor's two augmented second leaps create a passionate, dramatic quality that is different from the Double Harmonic scale's antiquity and sacred feeling:

- **Passionate and wild**: the Romani *csárdás* character—fiery, dance-like, emotionally unrestrained
- **Dramatic and dark**: the minor third and flat sixth maintain the minor emotional foundation; the leaps add fire
- **Folk authenticity**: this is not an exotic scale in its original context—it IS the voice of a living tradition
- **Bittersweet intensity**: Liszt used it for both wild celebration and aching longing

## The Algerian Scale Connection

The **Algerian scale** is closely related to Hungarian Minor: it has the same step pattern for the first octave, with the second octave using natural minor [63]. The terms are sometimes used interchangeably in scale databases, though the Algerian theoretically has additional notes in its second octave. For practical composition, treat them as equivalent.

## Compositional Approach

The raised fourth (♯4) is the unique note compared to harmonic minor—the note that adds the Romani character:

- The motion from ♭3 to ♯4 (e.g., C → D♯ in A Hungarian Minor) is the "fire" interval—a sudden bright leap within a dark context
- The ♯4 creates a tritone with the root (D♯ and A are six half-steps apart)—an unstable, dramatic relationship
- The raised seventh (G♯) creates a strong pull toward the octave (home note), same as harmonic minor—giving the scale a sense of inevitable, dramatic arrival

**Exercise on bawu**: A Hungarian Minor = A – B – C – D♯ – E – F – G♯ – A. Play A – B – C (pause) – D♯ (the augmented second leap—play this with a slight upward bend into it). Then E – F (another step down into the flat sixth). Then G♯ – A (the leading tone resolution). The emotional arc: dark arrival, sudden bright leap, settling into the flat sixth, then dramatic resolution home.

## The Interval Structure in Detail

| Degree | Note (from A) | Distance from previous |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 | A |—|
| 2 | B | Whole step |
| ♭3 | C | Half-step |
| ♯4 | D♯ | Augmented second (1½ steps) ← The defining leap |
| 5 | E | Half-step |
| ♭6 | F | Half-step |
| 7 | G♯ | Augmented second (1½ steps) ← Second augmented second |
| 8 | A | Half-step |

Like [Double Harmonic](scale-double-harmonic.md), Hungarian Minor contains two augmented seconds. But here they fall in different positions: between ♭3 and ♯4 (C → D♯) and between ♭6 and 7 (F → G♯). The overall framework is minor rather than major, giving it a fiercer, more passionate character than Double Harmonic's ancient grandeur.

## The Romani Musical Connection

The name "Hungarian Minor" points to the music of the Romani people (*Roma*) of Hungary and Central Europe, whose musicians—particularly violin players—used this scale extensively in their performances. Hungarian Romani musicians were the primary performers of café music, dance music, and entertainment music in Hungary from the 18th century onward, and their modal vocabulary—including Hungarian Minor—was what Franz Liszt and Johannes Brahms heard and absorbed.

The scale's combination of passionate minor character with sudden dramatic leaps (the augmented seconds) mirrors the Romani musical aesthetic of intense emotion expressed with theatrical gesture—the musical equivalent of a sharp turn or a sudden flare in a dance.

---

## Liszt, Brahms, and the Hungarian Influence

**Franz Liszt** (1811–1886) grew up in Hungary and was deeply influenced by Romani music. His *Hungarian Rhapsodies* (19 pieces for piano, 1846–1885) use Hungarian Minor extensively. The Rhapsodies alternate between slow, improvisatory *lassan* sections (often in Hungarian Minor) and fast, brilliant *friss* sections (often in major). Liszt's use of the augmented seconds—writing them as bold, dramatic melodic leaps—is the primary classical model for how to use this scale.

**Johannes Brahms** (1833–1897) was also deeply influenced by Hungarian Romani music, encountered through his friendship with the violinist Eduard Reményi. His *Hungarian Dances* (21 pieces, 1869–1880) are among the most played piano duets in the repertoire. Many use Hungarian Minor or closely related scales. Brahms's Hungarian Dances were also arranged for orchestra and became famous in those versions.

## Where Hungarian Minor Appears

- **Liszt**—*Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2*: the most famous Liszt rhapsody; Hungarian Minor in the slow section is the scale at its most theatrical and passionate
- **Liszt**—*Hungarian Rhapsody No. 6*: another central use
- **Brahms**—*Hungarian Dances* (especially Nos. 1, 5, 6): the most widely performed orchestral Hungarian Minor writing
- **Bartók**—collected Hungarian folk songs often in Hungarian Minor and related scales; his *Mikrokosmos* includes studies in these scales
- **Sarasate**—*Zigeunerweisen* (Gypsy Airs) for violin: virtuosic Hungarian Minor violin writing; the most demanding instrumental use of the scale

## Composition Exercise Expanded

**Instrument**: Moldavian kaval in D (D Hungarian Minor)
**D Hungarian Minor**: D – E – F – G♯ – A – B♭ – C♯ – D

The characteristic gesture: F → G♯ (ascending augmented second) and B♭ → C♯ (ascending augmented second in the upper tetrachord).

1. Begin on A (the fifth). Hold.
2. Descend: A – G♯ – F. The G♯ → F (descending augmented second) falls dramatically—fierce, passionate, a sudden drop into the shadow of the flat third.
3. Rise sharply: F – G♯ (ascending augmented second—the leap upward). This is the scale's characteristic gesture: the sudden upward reach from the minor third to the raised fourth. Use the kaval's upper register for G♯—the jump in register adds to the dramatic quality.
4. From G♯: A – B♭ – C♯ – D (high). The second augmented second (B♭ → C♯) arrives in the upper range. Let C♯ → D resolve with the urgency of a leading tone.
5. Repeat but use the kaval's dual-voice technique on the sustained D at the end—partial fipple coverage + humming. The ancient resonance of the dual voice gives the fierce Hungarian Minor drama a deeper, older quality.

**Goal**: music that is fierce, passionate, and slightly theatrical—the emotional world of the Romani violin tradition translated to a Balkan wind instrument.

## Composing with Hungarian Minor for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### The Emotional Core: Fierce Minor with Romani Fire

Hungarian Minor is not gentle. Its combination of minor darkness (flat third) and the sudden, fierce augmented second (♭3 → ♯4) creates music that is passionate, theatrical, and slightly wild. The scale has been central to Romani folk music, Liszt's Hungarian Rhapsodies, and Bartók's folk-derived compositions precisely because it expresses emotions that standard minor scales cannot: grief with fire, darkness with drama, sorrow that does not accept itself quietly.

### The Three Signature Gestures

**The augmented second leap** (C → D♯ in A Hungarian Minor, ♭3 → ♯4): the defining gesture. A sudden one-and-a-half-step leap from the flat minor third to the raised fourth. The flat third is dark; the raised fourth is bright; the leap between them is sudden, dramatic, unexpected. This is the "Romani fire" of the scale.

**The leading-tone arrival** (G♯ → A in A Hungarian Minor): like harmonic minor, Hungarian Minor has a raised seventh that creates a strong pull toward the tonic. After all the drama of the augmented second, the leading-tone arrival (G♯ → A) feels inevitable and conclusive.

**The flat sixth tension** (F in A Hungarian Minor): the flat sixth adds anguish to the scale's already dramatic palette. Between the augmented second leap and the leading tone, the flat sixth provides a moment of dark settling before the dramatic resolution.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Passionate grief** (the Romani lament quality): moderate-slow tempo; phrases beginning in the minor lower range (A – B – C) that suddenly leap through the augmented second (C → D♯) and reach the fifth (E) before descending through the flat sixth (F) and settling at the leading-tone arrival (G♯ → A); the phrase arc is: dark opening → sudden bright leap → dark settling → inevitable resolution.

**Fierce theatrical drama** (Liszt Rhapsody character): faster tempo; the augmented second leap on a strong beat as a declarative gesture; the flat sixth as a moment of sudden shadow after the brightness of the raised fourth; quick, decisive phrase endings through the leading tone.

**Searching, exotic character**: slow tempo; the augmented second used as a question—leap to D♯, then immediately descend back through C to A—the brightness of D♯ touched briefly and retreated from; phrases that circle around the raised fourth without staying there long.

**Folk dance energy** (Czardas character): moderate-fast tempo with rhythmic drive; the augmented second integrated into a flowing phrase rather than emphasized as a dramatic event; the overall effect more vitality than grief; short, punchy phrases ending on the leading-tone arrival.

### Step-by-Step: A Complete Hungarian Minor Composition

**A Hungarian Minor**: A – B – C – D♯ – E – F – G♯ – A

1. **Establish the minor foundation**: begin with A – B – C (tonic → major second → flat third). The flat third (C) establishes the minor character. Settle on C for two beats. This is the ground before the leap.

2. **The defining leap**: from C, leap to D♯ (the augmented second—one-and-a-half steps upward). On bawu: begin the pitch bend from just below D♯ and let it settle at D♯—the approach from below makes the arrival feel like reaching. Hold D♯ for two beats. This is the moment of Romani fire.

3. **The stable fifth**: from D♯, step to E (the fifth—one half-step up). E provides a moment of stability after the tension of D♯. Brief rest on E.

4. **The flat sixth anguish**: from E, step to F (the flat sixth—one half-step—the anguish note of the scale). F drops a half-step from E. This unexpected darkening after the brightness of D♯ and stability of E is the scale's characteristic emotional reversal.

5. **The leading-tone arrival**: from F, leap to G♯ (the leading tone—one-and-a-half steps: the second augmented second of the scale, between ♭6 and 7). G♯ presses urgently upward toward A. Hold G♯ briefly.

6. **Home**: G♯ → A. The most conclusive possible arrival after all the drama. Land strongly. This is the scale's emotional conclusion—not peace, but resolution of a fierce journey.

7. **Descending phrase**: from high A, descend: A – G♯ – F (sudden anguish drop from the leading tone to the flat sixth) – E – D♯ – C – B – A. The augmented seconds appear in descent as drops rather than leaps: G♯ → F (the second augmented second descending) and D♯ → C (the first augmented second descending). Each drop feels like a sudden fall into shadow.

8. **Varied second phrase**: begin on E (the fifth). Ascend: E – F (half-step—one shadow note) – G♯ (leap through the second augmented second—sudden brightness) – A. This is a compressed version of the full drama: dark note, leap, home. Use this compressed phrase as a cadential gesture—the full argument restated in miniature.

### Building Longer Compositions

For longer compositions, organize Hungarian Minor's drama in a journey structure:
- **Opening** (low register, moderate tempo): establish the minor darkness (A – B – C area); touch the augmented second once, quietly
- **Development** (rising through the scale): progressively higher phrases, each one touching the raised fourth (D♯) with more emphasis; tempo increasing
- **Climax** (high register): the augmented second leap in the upper range (D♯ in the upper octave); loudest dynamic; the leading-tone arrival at the highest point
- **Return** (slow descent): the characteristic descent with its falling augmented seconds; tempo slowing; arrive home through the leading tone very quietly—the fire spent

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Treating the raised fourth as a mistake**: the D♯ (raised fourth) looks like an accidental—a mistake in an otherwise conventional minor scale. It is not. It is the scale's entire personality. Every composition in Hungarian Minor must emphasize D♯ deliberately. A piece that accidentally passes through D♯ without giving it weight is not using the scale; it is avoiding it.

**Ignoring the flat sixth**: the F (flat sixth) is the note between the two dramatic gestures—the augmented second leap (ending at D♯ or E) and the leading-tone arrival (G♯ → A). This middle-anguish note provides the scale's emotional continuity between drama and resolution. Landing on F and giving it a slight dynamic swell before rising to G♯ makes the leading-tone arrival more powerful.

**Playing too fast**: Hungarian Minor's two augmented seconds require time to register. At fast tempos, the leaps become indistinguishable from passing notes. Slow enough that each augmented second is an event. Romantic and folk music in this scale is passionate, but passion is not the same as speed.
