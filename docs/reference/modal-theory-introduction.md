## What Is a Mode?

A [mode](../glossary.md#mode) is a scale—a set of notes organized in a specific sequence of distances—that has its own emotional character, its own gravitational centers, and its own paths through the musical landscape.

What makes a mode more than just a scale is that it implies a specific way of moving through its notes. A mode has a home note (the [tonic](../glossary.md#tonic)), notes that feel stable or restful, notes that carry tension, and notes that are so characteristic they define the mode's entire personality.

Think of a mode as a landscape rather than a list. The major scale is an open, sunlit plain—easy to travel, clear in all directions. Phrygian is a narrow mountain pass in shadow, one wall pressing close. Lydian is a plateau where the horizon is always just a little further away than expected. Each landscape has its own walking speed, its own emotional weather, its own silence.

## How Modes Are Built

Every mode is built from a sequence of [whole steps](../glossary.md#whole-step) (W) and [half-steps](../glossary.md#half-step) (H). A whole step skips one note; a half-step is the smallest interval available in standard Western tuning.

The pattern of these steps determines the mode's character. The most important step relationship is the one between the home note and the note immediately above it:

- A **whole step** above the tonic: sounds open, natural, resolved. Major-feeling scales.
- A **half-step** above the tonic: sounds pressed, tense, leaning. The defining feature of Phrygian and Locrian—and of the Arabic [Bayati maqam](../glossary.md#bayati), the Jewish [Ahavah Rabbah](../glossary.md#ahavah-rabbah), and the flamenco [Phrygian Dominant](../glossary.md#phrygian-dominant).

The position and size of half-steps throughout the scale determines everything else: where the melody rests comfortably, where it feels tension, and what emotional world the mode inhabits.

## The Seven Diatonic Modes

The seven [diatonic](../glossary.md#diatonic) modes are built from the same seven notes—but each one starts from a different note and therefore has a different sequence of steps. Using the white keys of a piano as an example:

| Mode | Start note | Step pattern | Quick character |
|------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| [Ionian](mode-ionian.md) | C | W–W–H–W–W–W–H | Bright, resolved, major |
| [Dorian](mode-dorian.md) | D | W–H–W–W–W–H–W | Resilient minor, slight brightness |
| [Phrygian](mode-phrygian.md) | E | H–W–W–W–H–W–W | Half-step above tonic, ancient dark |
| [Lydian](mode-lydian.md) | F | W–W–W–H–W–W–H | Raised fourth, floating, dreamlike |
| [Mixolydian](mode-mixolydian.md) | G | W–W–H–W–W–H–W | Flat seventh, open major |
| [Aeolian](mode-aeolian.md) | A | W–H–W–W–H–W–W | Natural minor, resigned sadness |
| [Locrian](mode-locrian.md) | B | H–W–W–H–W–W–W | Diminished fifth, perpetual unease |

Each mode is explored in full detail in its own document (links above). Each document covers: interval structure, emotional character, historical use, instrument access, repertoire to study, and a composition exercise.

## The Characteristic Note

Every mode has one note that defines its personality more than any other—often called the **characteristic note** or, in this repository, the **defining note**. This is the note that immediately signals the mode to a trained listener, even in a short melodic phrase.

| Mode | Characteristic note | Why it matters |
|------|--------------------|-|
| Ionian | Major 7th (leading tone) | Half-step pull toward the octave—urgency and resolution |
| Dorian | Major 6th | Brightness within a minor context—the "lift" |
| Phrygian | Minor 2nd (half-step above tonic) | Presses against home from above—ancient tension |
| Lydian | Augmented 4th (raised fourth) | Floats above the normal fourth—suspension |
| Mixolydian | Minor 7th (flat seventh) | Removes the leading tone—openness without urgency |
| Aeolian | Minor 6th | Adds anguish to the minor foundation |
| Locrian | Diminished 5th ([tritone](../glossary.md#tritone)) | Destabilizes the tonic chord completely |

When composing in a mode, return to the characteristic note deliberately. It is the emotional center of the mode—the note that holds the most charge, carries the most weight, and communicates the most to the listener's nervous system even on first hearing.

## Non-Western Modal Systems

Western modes are only one approach to organizing pitch. Across the world, dozens of modal systems exist with their own theoretical frameworks, emotional vocabularies, and compositional rules.

Crucially, non-Western modal systems are generally richer than a single scale—they specify how to move through the notes, which notes are most important, how to ornament them, and what emotional world they inhabit. In this sense they are closer to the full concept of a mode (personality + movement + feeling) than a simple list of notes.

## How Modes Feel: Emotional Character

The emotional character of a mode comes from specific physical properties of its intervals, not from convention alone. Several of these properties have measurable neurological effects:

**The half-step above the tonic** (Phrygian, Locrian, Neapolitan Minor, and several non-Western scales): a minor second interval—one half-step—appears more often in human infant crying than any other interval. The brain's emotional response to this interval may be partly biological rather than learned. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](brain-biology-psychology.md) for the full research summary.

**The tritone** (Locrian, several jazz scales): six half-steps—exactly halfway between two octaves. Activates the amygdala, the brain's emotional and threat-detection center. Historically called *Diabolus in musica* ("the devil in music") in medieval Europe.

**The augmented second** (Double Harmonic, Hungarian Minor, Harmonic Minor, Hijaz, Ahavah Rabbah): three half-steps—one and a half steps. Rare enough in most listeners' musical experience to reliably spike attention and activate the amygdala when it appears unexpectedly.

**Major versus minor thirds**: major thirds (four half-steps) are consistently associated with brighter, more positive emotional responses across cultures; minor thirds (three half-steps) with darker, sadder responses.

The full emotional map—interval by interval—is covered in [Emotional Affect](emotional-affect.md).

## Modes and Instruments

Not every mode is equally accessible on every instrument. Some instruments are diatonic (can only play a fixed scale without special technique); some are pentatonic (five notes only); some are fully chromatic (every note available). The table below shows the basic access each instrument in this collection has to the seven diatonic modes:

| Instrument | Full chromatic access? | Most natural modes |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Alto recorder | Yes | All seven modes in all keys |
| Tenor recorder | Yes | All seven modes in all keys |
| NAF (Am) | Pentatonic minor native | Aeolian and Dorian feel; Phrygian via grace notes only |
| NAF (F♯m) | Pentatonic minor native | Same as Am NAF, different pitch |
| NAF (B♭m) | Pentatonic minor native | Same as Am NAF, different pitch |
| Bawu | Chromatic + pitch bend | All modes; bending adds microtonal depth |
| Low D whistle | Diatonic D major; chromatic with cross-fingering | E Phrygian, E Dorian, E Aeolian, D Mixolydian natural |
| G whistle | Diatonic G major; chromatic with cross-fingering | A Phrygian, A Dorian, A Aeolian, G Mixolydian natural |
| Moldavian kaval in D | Diatonic with Hungarian-scale semitones + chromatic | D Phrygian, Double Harmonic, Hungarian Minor natural |

For detailed instrument information see [Instrument Capabilities](instrument-capabilities.md).

## Microtones and Modal Colour

Standard Western notation uses only twelve fixed pitches per octave, separated by equal half-steps. Most world music traditions use notes *between* those fixed pitches—[microtones](../glossary.md#microtone)—that have their own names, notation symbols, and emotional functions.

These are not "out of tune" notes. They are precisely placed pitches with specific expressive roles in their traditions. The [neutral third](../glossary.md#neutral-interval) of Arabic Bayati maqam—sitting between the Western minor and major third—cannot be approximated by either; it has its own distinct identity. The [koron](../glossary.md#koron) inflection in Persian music is not a mistake; it is a specific pitch carrying the emotional character of the dastgah.

**Notation systems for microtones**:
- Arabic/Turkish quarter-tones: special arrow-head symbols added to standard ♭ and ♯ signs
- Persian [koron](../glossary.md#koron) (♭): lowers by approximately a quarter-tone
- Persian [sori](../glossary.md#sori) (♯): raises by approximately a quarter-tone
- Turkish [koma](../glossary.md#koma): 1/53 of an octave; specialized Turkish notation exists
- Indian shruti: 22 microtonal divisions of the octave; not typically notated in Western staff notation

For wind players: the bawu (via pitch bending), the kaval (via embouchure adjustment), and the NAF (via half-hole covering) can all produce microtonal pitches. The recorder and whistle can approximate some microtones via cross-fingerings but cannot produce the full range of quarter-tone inflections.

The full treatment of microtonal systems is in [Microtonal Notation and Theory](microtonal-notation-and-theory.md).

## Historical Overview

**Ancient Greece**: The word "mode" comes from the Greek *harmonia* (plural *harmoniai*)—patterns of intervals used in ancient Greek music and theory. The Greek modes (Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, etc.) had specific emotional associations (*ethos*): Dorian for bravery, Phrygian for passion, Lydian for grief. These were not exactly the same as the modern modes that bear the same names.

**Medieval Europe**: The Catholic Church organized its chant repertoire into eight ecclesiastical modes (*octoechos*)—four authentic and four plagal. These modes used the same note names as ancient Greek theory but with different structures. The eight medieval modes are the direct ancestors of the seven modern diatonic modes.

**Renaissance (1400s–1600s)**: Theorists including Glarean (*Dodecachordon*, 1547) proposed expanding the eight modes to twelve, eventually settling on the modern seven. Renaissance composers—Palestrina, Tallis, Schütz, Josquin—wrote extensively in modal rather than tonal (major-minor) harmony.

**Common practice era (1650–1900)**: Major and minor keys dominated; modal writing largely disappeared from mainstream Western composition, though it survived in folk music throughout Europe and the Middle East.

**20th century revival**: Bartók's folk music research, Debussy's impressionism, modal jazz (Miles Davis, John Coltrane), and the minimalism of Arvo Pärt all brought modal thinking back to Western art music. Non-Western modal systems became widely studied by Western musicians and theorists.

**Today**: Modal composition is central to world music studies, jazz education, film scoring, folk revival, and the growing field of ethnomusicology. The full spectrum of modal possibilities—from the seven diatonic modes to the 72 Carnatic melakarta to the dozens of Arabic maqamat—is more accessible than at any previous point in history.
