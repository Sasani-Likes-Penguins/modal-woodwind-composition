## Bebop Scales: Eight Notes for Jazz Clarity

*Sources: [71][72][73][74][75][76][77][78][79][80]*

Bebop scales are not exotic constructions—they are standard major, minor, and dominant scales with one chromatic passing tone added, making them eight-note scales [74][75][80]. The addition of the eighth note serves a specific purpose: when a melody descends in even eighth notes, the chord tones all land on the strong beats (downbeats) rather than alternating between beats [75][80].

Bebop scales were not invented by a theorist—they were named retroactively for what Charlie Parker, Dizzy Gillespie, and other bebop musicians naturally did when improvising [74][80].

### Bebop Dominant Scale

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – 6 – ♭7 – 7

Mixolydian + a natural seventh as passing tone between the flat seventh and the octave [72][75][77][79].

**How to use it**: descend from the octave in eighth notes, starting on a chord tone. The natural seventh (7) slides chromatically into the flat seventh (♭7) in passing—the chord tones (root, third, fifth, flat seventh) land on strong beats.

**Emotional character**: swinging, confident, jazz fluency. The bebop sound.

**For wind players**: most idiomatically used as a descending melodic pattern—start high, flow downward. The natural seventh appears only in passing, never as a structural note.

### Bebop Major Scale

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – 6 – 7

Major scale + a chromatic passing tone (♭6) between the 5th and 6th [75][76][80].

**How to use it**: same principle as bebop dominant—descend in eighth notes, chord tones on downbeats.

### Bebop Minor Scale (Dorian Bebop)

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – 3 – 4 – 5 – 6 – ♭7

Dorian (or natural minor) + a major third passing tone between the minor third and the fourth [74][75][76].

The major third (3) appears in passing within a minor context—briefly suggesting major before resolving back to minor. This chromatic passing tone gives bebop minor lines their characteristic tension.

### Bebop Half-Diminished Scale

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – 4 – ♭5 – ♭6 – ♭7 – ♭8

Used over half-diminished (minor 7♭5) chords—the vi chord of a minor ii-V-i progression [71][73][75].

**How to use it**: start from the flat sixth (♭6) of the chord and descend using the bebop scale. Bill Evans's use of bebop scales over half-diminished chords is the primary model [71].

### Bebop Scales on Wind Instruments

The bebop scales are fundamentally *melodic patterns* rather than harmonic scales. For solo wind players without jazz accompaniment:

- Use bebop scales as ornamentation patterns within otherwise modal improvisation—the eight-note descent through a bebop scale over a sustained home note gives a jazz character to a predominantly modal piece
- The passing tone (the added eighth note) functions like an expressive ornament—a chromatic grace note that makes the surrounding chord tones more vivid by comparison
- The bawu's ability to bend into and out of the passing tone chromatically makes bebop-style lines most idiomatic on that instrument
- On the recorder: use clear articulation (tonguing) on the chord tones, lighter articulation on the passing tones—this rhythmically emphasizes the chord tones and makes the passing tone sound like an ornament even at speed
