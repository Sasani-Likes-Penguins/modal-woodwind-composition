## What Is It?

Romanian Minor is a seven-note scale that combines the resilience of [Dorian](mode-dorian.md) (minor with a raised sixth) with the floating quality of [Lydian](mode-lydian.md) (raised fourth). The result is a distinctly folk-inflected minor scale with an unexpected brightness at the fourth degree—earthy and grounded, yet with a moment of surprising openness within the minor context.

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – ♯4 – 5 – 6 – ♭7

**D Romanian Minor**: D – E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D

The scale appears in Romanian, Hungarian, and wider Balkan folk music traditions, and was systematically documented and used by Béla Bartók in compositions derived from his ethnomusicological field recordings. It is also the fourth mode of the melodic minor scale (built from the fourth degree of A melodic minor ascending).

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from D) | Character |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| 1 (tonic) | D | Home |
| 2 | E | Whole step—natural, open |
| ♭3 | F | Minor third—darkness established |
| ♯4 | G♯ | Raised fourth—unexpected brightness; augmented second from F |
| 5 | A | Perfect fifth—stability |
| 6 | B | Major sixth—Dorian's lift (same as Dorian's raised sixth) |
| ♭7 | C | Flat seventh—open ending, folk quality |
| 8 | D | Home |

The scale contains **two augmented seconds**: F → G♯ (one-and-a-half steps, the raised fourth leap) and implicitly the relationship between ♭3 and ♯4. These are the scale's most distinctive intervals.

---

## The Raised Fourth: The Defining Note

The raised fourth (G♯ in D Romanian Minor) is what distinguishes this scale from standard Dorian. Where Dorian has a natural fourth (G in D Dorian), Romanian Minor raises it—creating a Lydian-like floating quality within a minor context.

**The augmented second F → G♯**: this leap (one-and-a-half steps) is the scale's most characteristic gesture. It appears also in [Double Harmonic](scale-double-harmonic.md), [Hungarian Minor](scale-hungarian-minor.md), [Harmonic Minor](scale-harmonic-minor.md), and several maqam *jins* cells. In Romanian Minor the augmented second occurs between the flat third and raised fourth (rather than between the flat second and major third as in [Spanish Heptatonic](scale-spanish-heptatonic.md))—giving it a slightly more interior, folk-earthy quality rather than the fierce outer expressiveness of Hijaz or Phrygian Dominant.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Folk-earthy minor | Minor foundation with an unusual brightness—like Dorian but with a different kind of lift |
| Slightly exotic but grounded | More accessible than Double Harmonic or Spanish Heptatonic; the exoticism is understated |
| Searching and open | The raised fourth and flat seventh both contribute to an unresolved, forward-moving quality |
| Eastern European pastoral | The sound of Romanian, Hungarian, and Balkan folk melody |

Romanian Minor sits between the accessible darkness of Dorian and the fiercer exoticism of Double Harmonic. It is the "folk exotic"—immediately recognizable as unusual but not aggressively foreign.

## Cultural Origins and Names

The scale appears across Eastern European folk traditions under different names and with different theoretical frameworks:

**Romanian folk music**: the scale is native to Romanian folk melody, particularly in Transylvanian and Moldavian regional traditions. Romanian folk fiddlers, lutanists (*cobzari*), and wind players use this scale as a primary melodic vocabulary without necessarily having a theoretical name for it.

**Hungarian folk music**: Bartók documented the scale extensively in his Hungarian field recordings (1905–1918). He considered it one of the characteristic scales of the Carpathian Basin region.

**Jewish music (Ukrainian Dorian / Mi Sheberakh)**: in Ashkenazi Jewish liturgical practice, a scale with an identical or very similar structure is called the *Ukrainian Dorian* or *Mi Sheberakh* mode (named after the blessing "Mi sheberakh," which is sung in this mode). See [Jewish Nusach](world-jewish-nusach.md) for the full liturgical context.

**Jazz theory**: jazz musicians sometimes call this the "Lydian ♭3" or "acoustic scale (4th mode)"—the fourth mode of the melodic minor scale built from the fourth degree.

---

## The Jewish Nusach Connection

The *Mi Sheberakh* mode used in Ashkenazi Jewish prayer is virtually identical to Romanian Minor: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – ♯4 – 5 – 6 – ♭7. It is used for the synagogue blessing sung over those called to the Torah reading, and for Grace After Meals. The connection between the Jewish *Mi Sheberakh* mode and Romanian folk music reflects the shared Ashkenazi-Eastern European cultural history—Jewish communities in Romania, Hungary, and Ukraine absorbed the folk modal vocabulary of the surrounding cultures while giving it its own liturgical function and ornamental style.

## Bartók and Romanian Folk Music

Béla Bartók (1881–1945) was the most important composer to document and use Romanian Minor systematically. His ethnomusicological research (1905–1918) took him to Romanian, Hungarian, Slovakian, Bulgarian, and Turkish villages to record folk music. He transcribed thousands of folk melodies and found the raised-fourth minor scale recurring throughout the Carpathian Basin region.

**Bartók's compositional use**:
- **Romanian Folk Dances** (1915): six dances arranged from Bartók's field recordings; several use Romanian Minor scales directly from the source material
- **Romanian Christmas Songs** (*Colinde*, 1915): folk melodies in Romanian Minor and related scales
- **Mikrokosmos** (piano pedagogical series): includes explicit pieces exploring Eastern European modal scales including Romanian Minor
- **String Quartet No. 2** (1917): incorporates Romanian folk modal material including raised-fourth minor
- **Music for Strings, Percussion, and Celesta** (1936): Hungarian and Romanian folk modal influences throughout

Bartók's technique was to use folk melodies and folk scales as a starting point, then develop them using Western compositional techniques (fugue, variation, polytonality). Romanian Minor in his hands retains its folk character while being given rigorous structural treatment.

| NAF | Limited | Raised fourth not native to the pentatonic scale |

**Kaval as home instrument**: Romanian folk music is part of the Moldavian kaval's native cultural tradition. Playing D Romanian Minor on the kaval is not an academic exercise—it is the instrument in its most natural modal environment.

## Composing with Romanian Minor

**Lead with the augmented second**: the F → G♯ leap (♭3 → ♯4) is the scale's signature gesture. Use it as an opening melodic statement, as a recurring motif, and as the ornamented note at phrase peaks.

**The folk melodic cell**: in Romanian and Hungarian folk music built on this scale, characteristic melodic cells often circle around the raised fourth (G♯ in D Romanian Minor), approaching it from below (F → G♯ ascending) or from above (A → G♯ descending). Find your own characteristic cell for the scale before composing longer material.

**The flat seventh's open ending**: like [Dorian](mode-dorian.md) and [Mixolydian](mode-mixolydian.md), Romanian Minor has a flat seventh that prevents the urgency of a standard leading-tone resolution. Phrases can end on the flat seventh (C in D Romanian Minor) for an open, folk-like quality that implies continuation rather than conclusion.

**Asymmetric rhythms**: Romanian and Balkan folk music uses asymmetric rhythmic cycles (7/8, 9/8, 5/8) that give the music an inherent rolling unpredictability. The raised fourth of Romanian Minor amplifies the effect of these rhythms—the scale's unusual note falls at different positions within each rhythmic cycle. The kaval tradition is particularly well-suited to these rhythms.

## Repertoire and Recordings to Study

- **Béla Bartók**—*Romanian Folk Dances*: the most accessible introduction to Romanian Minor in a classical arrangement; the folk scales appear directly from Bartók's field recordings
- **Béla Bartók**—*Mikrokosmos*, Bulgarian Dances section: explicit scale studies in Eastern European folk modes
- **Béla Bartók**—*String Quartet No. 4*: more complex treatment of Balkan modal material
- **Taraf de Haïdouks**—Romanian folk ensemble: authentic Romanian traditional music using Romanian Minor in its original folk context; unfiltered and essential listening
- **Gheorghe Zamfir**—Romanian pan flute recordings: folk melodies in Romanian Minor on a wind instrument; melodic phrasing closely related to what is available on kaval
- **Jewish liturgical recordings**—Mi Sheberakh mode: the same scale in a completely different emotional and ornamental context; demonstrates how identical pitches sound different across traditions

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: Moldavian kaval (D Romanian Minor)
**D Romanian Minor**: D – E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D

1. Begin on A (the fifth—open and stable). Descend: A – G♯ – F. The G♯ → F (descending augmented second) falls suddenly—from the bright raised fourth into the minor third. This is the scale's most characteristic descending gesture.
2. Continue descending: F – E – D. Land quietly on D.
3. Ascending phrase: D – E – F – G♯ (ascending augmented second—the folk leap). Pause on G♯ briefly. Apply the kaval's *patter* (rhythmic pulsing) to the held G♯.
4. Continue: A – B – C – D (high). The B (major sixth, Dorian-like lift) followed by C (flat seventh, open) and D (high tonic) creates the folk-earthy Dorian quality in the upper range.
5. Descend again from D through the full scale. Use asymmetric phrasing—the second phrase longer than the first by two beats.

**Goal**: music that sounds like it belongs to a specific landscape—the Eastern European valley, the mountain village, the folk violin player at a wedding. Grounded, slightly exotic, vital.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Romanian Minor

**Folk-earthy exotic minor**: moderate tempo; the augmented second (F → G♯ in D Romanian Minor) as the characteristic gesture—upward leap from the flat third to the raised fourth; the Dorian raised sixth (B in D Romanian Minor) as a secondary lift; phrases that combine the folk-earthy character of Dorian with the sudden exotic leap of the augmented second; the kaval's native voice in this scale.

**Passionate Balkan folk energy**: moderate-fast tempo with rhythmic drive; the augmented second leap on strong beats; short punchy phrases ending on the fifth (A in D Romanian Minor) or the tonic; asymmetric meter (7/8 or 9/8) if available—the raised fourth fits naturally into asymmetric rhythmic structures because it creates rhythmic surprise as well as harmonic surprise.

**Searching, bittersweet folk minor**: slow tempo; the scale treated as a Dorian melody with an unexpected raised fourth appearing in certain phrases; the augmented second not always arriving—sometimes the fourth (G natural) appears instead of G♯, creating the bimodal fluctuation characteristic of Romanian folk music; the raised sixth (B) for the moments of Dorian lift.

**Bartók-style folk derivation**: moderate tempo with clear rhythmic structure; the augmented second as a structural gesture at the climax of each phrase; the flat seventh (C in D Romanian Minor) as phrase endings; the overall texture earthy and folk-derived but with classical phrase architecture.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Romanian Minor

**Treating the raised fourth as an accidental**: the G♯ (raised fourth in D Romanian Minor) looks like a chromatic accident—an error in what would otherwise be D Dorian. It is not. It is the scale's defining characteristic. Every composition in Romanian Minor should make G♯ structurally prominent—on a strong beat, in the peak phrase, at least once per musical statement.

**Playing only the Dorian notes**: because Romanian Minor differs from Dorian by only one note (the raised fourth), beginners often revert to Dorian's G natural when they should use G♯. These are different scales with different emotional characters. Romanian Minor has folk-exotic fire; Dorian has resilient minor. Make the choice deliberately each time the fourth degree appears.

**Ignoring the augmented second as an expressive gesture**: the leap from F to G♯ (one-and-a-half steps in D Romanian Minor) is the most characteristic interval in the scale. If this leap is not felt as an expressive event—if it is just a scale step—the composition has missed Romanian Minor's essential quality. Slow down at the augmented second. Give it a slight dynamic emphasis. Let it be the gesture it is.
