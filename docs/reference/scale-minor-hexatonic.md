## What Is It?

The Minor Hexatonic scale is natural minor with the flat sixth removed, plus the major second added. This creates a six-note (hexatonic) scale that sits between the lightness of the five-note pentatonic minor and the full darkness of natural minor.

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – ♭7

**A Minor Hexatonic**: A – B – C – D – E – G – A

Compare to A natural minor (Aeolian): A – B – C – D – E – F – G – A

The only difference: F (the flat sixth) is removed. The resulting six-note collection avoids the flat sixth's anguish while retaining the minor third, fourth, fifth, and flat seventh—giving the scale a minor quality without the heaviest note of natural minor.

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from A) | Distance from previous |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 | A |—|
| 2 | B | Whole step |
| ♭3 | C | Half-step |
| 4 | D | Whole step |
| 5 | E | Whole step |
| ♭7 | G | Minor third (3 half-steps) |
| 8 | A | Whole step |

The gap between the fifth (E) and flat seventh (G) is a minor third—the largest interval in the scale and the most distinctive. This minor third "jump" over the flat sixth gives the scale its characteristic folk-airy quality.

## Why Six Notes?

Pentatonic scales (five notes) avoid all half-steps—every note is a comfortable whole step or minor third from its neighbors. This gives pentatonic scales their open, unobstructed quality. Seven-note scales include half-steps that create the push-pull tension of tonal music. Six-note hexatonic scales sit between these two worlds—they have one or two half-steps but fewer than the full diatonic scale.

The Minor Hexatonic's one half-step (B–C, the major second falling to the minor third) is enough to establish the minor character without the additional half-step friction of the flat sixth (E–F) that Aeolian has. The scale breathes more freely than full natural minor while having more melodic richness than the pentatonic.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Minor but airy | Darker than major pentatonic, lighter than natural minor |
| Folk-like and open | The missing flat sixth removes the heaviest minor note |
| Grounded but not heavy | Minor foundation without anguish |
| Improvisational and natural | Sits easily in a pentatonic-based player's vocabulary |

The Minor Hexatonic is the scale you might naturally arrive at if you were a pentatonic player who started adding one note. It extends the pentatonic's vocabulary without crossing into the full complexity of heptatonic modes.

## The Added Major Second: What It Contributes

The major second (B in A Minor Hexatonic) is the note added beyond the standard minor pentatonic (A–C–D–E–G). Its presence:

- Fills the gap between the root (A) and the minor third (C) with a step (B)—making stepwise motion possible in the opening of the scale
- Allows the "questioning" melodic gesture B → C (major second falling to minor third): a characteristic folk-minor inflection
- Gives the scale a slightly Dorian-adjacent quality—Dorian also has a major second above the tonic, and the Minor Hexatonic's upper portion (B–C–D–E–G) resembles Dorian in character
- Provides one more structural note for melodic development

## Folk and Instrument Connections

**Irish whistle in E minor hexatonic**: the low D whistle playing E Minor Hexatonic (E–F♯–G–A–B–D) uses only natural fingerings—every note is a standard position on the D whistle with no cross-fingerings required. This makes the scale one of the most idiomatic folk-minor scales for the D whistle.

**NAF**: the NAF's native minor pentatonic (A–C–D–E–G in A minor) is the Minor Hexatonic minus the major second (B). Adding B via cross-fingering or a partial hole gives access to the full hexatonic. Many NAF players naturally drift toward this extended pentatonic in longer improvisations.

**Celtic folk**: Celtic minor tunes that use only six notes are often Minor Hexatonic. The scale appears naturally in tunes that want minor character without the complete seven-note Dorian or Aeolian vocabulary.

**African-American folk**: many spiritual and early blues melodies use a pentatonic or hexatonic minor scale—the addition of the major second to the standard pentatonic minor is a natural first step in expanding the vocabulary.

## Composing with Minor Hexatonic

**The pentatonic player's expansion**: if you are comfortable with minor pentatonic (the NAF's native scale), treat the Minor Hexatonic as a pentatonic scale with one new note—B (the major second). Introduce B as an expressive approach to C (the minor third) or as a decorative neighbor to A (the root). Let the pentatonic skeleton remain the structural foundation while B adds color.

**The B→C gesture**: the major second falling to the minor third (B → C in A Minor Hexatonic) is the scale's most characteristic melodic motion. It is a gentle version of the half-step tension—not as pressing as a Phrygian half-step above the tonic, but still a direction with feeling. Use it as a phrase-opening gesture: begin on B, step down to C, settle.

**The minor third gap at the top**: the leap from E to G (a minor third) in the upper range of the scale is the largest interval—and the one that most distinctly differs from natural minor (which would have E → F → G, filling the gap). Use this leap for expressive effect: an ascending E → G is an open, airy gesture; a descending G → E is a warm settling.

**Avoiding the flat sixth deliberately**: since the flat sixth (F in A Minor Hexatonic) is the note the scale omits, any time a melody might naturally include F, consider what effect the omission creates. The absence of F keeps the scale lighter than full natural minor—this is the scale's definition. If you find yourself wanting the darker, heavier quality that F would bring, move to [Aeolian](mode-aeolian.md) instead.

## Repertoire

Many folk melodies that feel "minor but not too dark" are in fact Minor Hexatonic—they simply never include the flat sixth:

- Many **Irish slow airs** use the Minor Hexatonic instinctively—the airs feel minor but airy, without the heaviness of full natural minor
- **Appalachian folk songs** in minor keys often use a hexatonic collection
- **Early blues** spirituals and field hollers use pentatonic and hexatonic minor collections
- Any **Celtic fiddle tune** in minor that seems to avoid the sixth degree is probably Minor Hexatonic

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: low D whistle (E Minor Hexatonic)
**E Minor Hexatonic**: E – F♯ – G – A – B – D – E (all natural fingering on D whistle)

1. Begin on E (tonic). Hold for two beats.
2. Rise: E – F♯ – G. The F♯ (major second) → G (minor third) is the scale's characteristic opening gesture—a whole step and then a half-step, arriving at the minor color.
3. Continue: G – A – B – D (high). The B → D leap (minor third, the scale's largest) is an airy folk gesture—an open skip rather than a heavy step.
4. From D, descend: D – B – A – G – F♯ – E. The descent is naturally smooth—no large leaps, each step is a whole step or half-step.
5. Second phrase: begin on B (the second degree). Descend: B – A – G – E. Simple, stepwise, settling on the tonic without touching the F♯. This phrase uses only four notes from the hexatonic—the scale's pentatonic core. Then: add F♯ as an ornamental grace note before the final E.

**Goal**: music that feels minor but not heavy—the emotional territory between darkness and openness, between Aeolian's resignation and Dorian's resilience.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Minor Hexatonic

**Minor but airy** (the core affect): moderate tempo; phrases that use all six notes fluidly; the flat seventh (G in A minor hexatonic) as phrase endings—open, folk-like, not arrived; the minor third (C) as the dark note touched briefly in passing; the overall atmosphere: minor but breathing, sad but not heavy.

**Folk lament with forward motion**: slow to moderate tempo with rhythmic pulse; the major second (B in A minor hexatonic) as the note that gives the scale its folk energy—the second is the stepping stone out of the tonic and into the scale's territory; phrases that ascend B → C (major second to minor third—a half-step descent into minor darkness) as a characteristic gesture.

**Meditative minor openness**: slower tempo; sparse; the flat seventh (G) as a sustained note—the most harmonically open note in the scale (a minor seventh above the tonic); phrases that settle on G rather than the tonic, leaving the composition permanently slightly unresolved; the pentatonic core (A – C – D – E – G) used for most phrases with B appearing as an occasional expressive color.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Minor Hexatonic

**Never using the major second**: the B (major second in A minor hexatonic) is the note that lifts the scale above pure pentatonic. A melody that never uses B is pentatonic minor, not minor hexatonic. Place B on accented beats at least once per phrase—particularly as an approach to C (the minor third) from below.

**Treating the flat seventh as a leading tone**: G in A minor hexatonic is a flat seventh (whole step below the tonic), not a leading tone. It does not press urgently toward the octave. Phrases that end on G feel open and folk-like, not tense. This openness is the minor hexatonic's greatest expressive feature—let phrases settle on G without treating it as unresolved tension.

**Staying in the lower half of the scale**: all six notes of A minor hexatonic sit within an octave, but beginning composers often stay in the lower half (A – B – C – D) and rarely visit the upper half (E – G – A). The minor third descent B → C and the flat seventh G – A together make the scale most itself. Use the full range.
