## What Is Harmonic Minor?

Harmonic minor is natural minor with one change: the seventh degree is raised by a half-step, creating a leading tone [31](https://themusictheoryprofessor.com/analyzing-famous-compositions-with-notable-deceptive-cadences/)[32](https://www.flutopedia.com/refs/Iriarte_2012_PlayingModesOnTheNAF_FP.pdf)[36](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bawu)[37](https://organology.net/instrument/bawu/)[40](https://www.tradschool.com/en/irish-music/low-whistle/). This gives the scale a strong pull toward the home note from below—the dramatic, inevitable resolution that natural minor lacks.

**A Harmonic Minor**: A – B – C – D – E – F – G♯ – A

The G♯ (raised seventh) is a half-step below A—like a finger pressing urgently toward the door of home. The result is a scale with:
- The darkness of natural minor (flat third and sixth)
- The drama of a leading tone (urgent half-step approach to home)
- An augmented second between the flat sixth and raised seventh (F → G♯ = one-and-a-half steps)—the "exotic" or "Middle Eastern" quality

---

## Why It Was Created

Baroque composers needed a strong resolution from the fifth chord (dominant) back to the tonic chord in minor keys. Natural minor's flat seventh made the dominant chord minor rather than major—which felt weak. By raising the seventh, they got a major dominant chord with a proper leading tone [36](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bawu)[40](https://www.tradschool.com/en/irish-music/low-whistle/). The scale was designed *for harmony*, not melody—hence the name "harmonic minor."

The augmented second between the flat sixth and raised seventh was considered awkward for melodic writing [33](https://chantingdroneflutes.com/exploring-the-pentatonic-minor-scale-and-the-seven-hole-native-american-style-flute/)[36](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bawu)[40](https://www.tradschool.com/en/irish-music/low-whistle/)—which is why melodic minor was developed to smooth it out. But for expressive composition, that augmented second IS the harmonic minor sound.

---

## The Emotional Territory

Harmonic minor occupies the space between natural minor and the exotic/classical:

- **Dark with drama**: the leading tone creates urgency that natural minor lacks
- **Classical and Baroque**: the raised seventh is the sound of Bach, Handel, and every classical period composer in minor keys
- **Middle Eastern/exotic**: the augmented second (F → G♯) connects harmonic minor to maqam Hijaz, flamenco, and Romani music
- **Intense and resolving**: where Phrygian's half-step sits above the tonic (pressing from above), harmonic minor's sits below (pressing from below)—both are intense, but in different emotional directions

**Famous harmonic minor examples**: virtually all Baroque and Classical minor-key music; metal guitar solos; Klezmer music; Arabic-influenced film scores.

## The Augmented Second

The augmented second between the flat sixth and raised seventh (F → G♯ in A harmonic minor, one-and-a-half steps) is the scale's most distinctive sound [31](https://themusictheoryprofessor.com/analyzing-famous-compositions-with-notable-deceptive-cadences/)[35](https://atflutes.com/information/learning/native-american-style-flute-pentatonic-scales)[39](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tin_whistle):

- Ascending (F → G♯): a sudden brightening leap from flat-sixth shadow into leading-tone urgency
- Descending (G♯ → F): an abrupt drop from tension into shadow—like a dramatic fall

This augmented second appears in the same position as the augmented second in Phrygian Dominant, maqam Hijaz, and the Double Harmonic scale—connecting harmonic minor's "classical" context to these Middle Eastern and folk traditions through shared interval structure.

---

## Instrument Access

All instruments in this collection can play harmonic minor—the raised seventh requires no unusual technique. The augmented second (flat sixth to raised seventh) is available on all fully chromatic instruments, and approximated on kaval and bawu via embouchure and pitch bend.

## Composition Exercise on Recorder

**A Harmonic Minor**: A – B – C – D – E – F – G♯ – A.

1. Establish the home note (A) and the minor character (C = flat third) in the first phrase.
2. Build toward a cadence: E – F – G♯ – A. The augmented second (F → G♯) just before arrival on A makes the arrival feel urgent and inevitable.
3. Ornament G♯ with a lean note (approach it via F, hold it, then resolve to A)—the leading tone at maximum expressiveness.
4. Use the augmented second leap (F → G♯) as a recurring expressive gesture throughout the piece, not just at cadences.

## The Augmented Second as Expressive Tool

The augmented second between the flat sixth and raised seventh (F → G♯ in A harmonic minor) is the scale's most distinctive melodic feature—the one that gives it its "exotic" or Middle Eastern sound to Western ears. This interval appears also in:
- [Spanish Heptatonic / Phrygian Dominant](scale-spanish-heptatonic.md)—the flamenco/Ahavah Rabbah sound
- [Double Harmonic Scale](scale-double-harmonic.md)—two augmented seconds
- [Hungarian Minor](scale-hungarian-minor.md)—augmented second in a different position
- [Arabic Maqam Hijaz](world-arabic-maqam.md)—the same interval in maqam context

The augmented second is not melodically awkward—it is dramatically charged. Use it as a highlighted expressive gesture: approach the raised seventh from below via the flat sixth, making the one-and-a-half-step leap a deliberate melodic statement.

## Where Harmonic Minor Appears

**Classical era**: Harmonic minor is the foundational minor scale of Western classical composition from approximately 1600 to 1900.
- **Mozart**—*Piano Sonata in A minor, K. 310*: A harmonic minor; the leading tone creates the dramatic resolution throughout
- **Bach**—*Well-Tempered Clavier* fugues in minor keys: harmonic minor is the structural basis for most of the minor-key fugues
- **Beethoven**—*Moonlight Sonata* (C♯ minor): harmonic minor as the atmosphere of late-night introspection
- **Vivaldi**—*"Summer"* from *The Four Seasons*: harmonic minor in the furious storm sequence

**Guitar and metal**: harmonic minor became a defining scale in heavy metal and progressive rock from the 1980s onward, precisely because its augmented second creates "exotic" tension that the blues scale does not.
- **Yngwie Malmsteen**—virtually his entire output is in harmonic minor; he called it the "neo-classical" sound
- **Ritchie Blackmore**—*Rainbow* recordings: harmonic minor in a rock context
- **Jason Becker**—classical-metal fusion built on harmonic minor

**Middle Eastern connection**: the augmented second of harmonic minor sounds "Middle Eastern" to Western ears because Arabic Maqam Hijaz uses the same interval relationship. Harmonic minor is the Western scale most closely related to Hijaz maqam territory.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: alto recorder (A harmonic minor)
**A Harmonic Minor**: A – B – C – D – E – F – G♯ – A

1. Begin on E (the fifth—structural). Descend: E – D – C – B – A. The descent through the clean lower range of harmonic minor establishes the minor character without drama.
2. Ascend: A – B – C – D – E – F. Pause on F (the flat sixth). From F, leap to G♯ (the raised seventh—the augmented second: F → G♯). This one-and-a-half-step leap is the scale's defining gesture. Give G♯ full weight before stepping to A.
3. G♯ → A (home). The leading-tone resolution feels like inevitability—the only possible arrival after the tension.
4. Second phrase: place the augmented second earlier. A – C – D – F – G♯ – A. The F → G♯ appears in the middle of the phrase, not at the end—a surprise rather than a conclusion.

**Goal**: experience the augmented second both as climactic resolution (gesture 2) and as mid-phrase surprise (gesture 4).

## Composing with Harmonic Minor for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### What Harmonic Minor Does That Other Scales Cannot

Harmonic minor occupies a specific emotional territory: the tension of something approaching its inevitable conclusion. The leading tone (G♯ in A harmonic minor, a half-step below the octave) creates the most directed pull in Western music—the feeling of being drawn toward a specific point with no possibility of stopping. Combine this with the anguish of the flat sixth (F in A harmonic minor) and the exotic urgency of the augmented second (F → G♯) and you have a scale built for dramatic inevitability.

Use harmonic minor when the emotion requires: arrival that cannot be avoided; grief with a classical formal quality; the feeling of something building toward a predetermined conclusion; or the "Middle Eastern" augmented second sound within a Western minor context.

### The Three Core Gestures

Every effective harmonic minor melody uses these three gestures as its primary emotional vocabulary:

**Gesture 1: The leading-tone arrival** (G♯ → A in A harmonic minor)
The half-step below the octave pressing upward to home. This is the most conclusive melodic motion available—even more directed than the Phrygian half-step (which falls down to the tonic) because this one *rises* urgently. Use at phrase endings of maximum conclusive weight.

**Gesture 2: The flat-sixth anguish** (F in A harmonic minor)
The flat sixth (F) is the most emotionally charged note in harmonic minor—its flat quality creates anguish within the surrounding minor framework. Land on it briefly, with a dynamic swell, before moving to G♯ or E. The combination of F's anguish and G♯'s urgency creates harmonic minor's characteristic emotional charge.

**Gesture 3: The augmented-second leap** (F → G♯: one-and-a-half steps upward)
The single interval that gives harmonic minor its "exotic" quality. In ascending context: from the flat sixth, leap to the leading tone—a one-and-a-half-step upward jump that sounds both dramatic and inevitable. In descending context: G♯ → F is a sudden drop from urgency into shadow.

### Step-by-Step: Composing a Harmonic Minor Piece

**A Harmonic Minor**: A – B – C – D – E – F – G♯ – A

**Step-by-step**:

1. **Establish the minor character**: first phrase should establish A (tonic), C (minor third—the darkness), and E (fifth—stability). A – C – E – C – A. Pure minor; no harmonic minor character yet.

2. **Introduce the flat sixth**: second phrase ascends: A – C – D – E – F. Land on F (the flat sixth) with a slight dynamic emphasis. Hold briefly. This single note transforms the phrase from generic minor into harmonic minor's specific anguish.

3. **The augmented second**: from F, leap to G♯ (the leading tone: the one-and-a-half-step upward leap). On bawu: make this a pitch bend—slide from F continuously up through F♯ to G♯. The continuous slide amplifies the drama of the leap. Hold G♯ for two beats.

4. **The leading-tone arrival**: G♯ → A (home). The most conclusive step in the scale. This should feel like a door closing, a lock turning, an inevitability fulfilled. Arrive loudly (relative to the surrounding dynamics), then immediately soften.

5. **Second phrase, more complex**: A – E – F (emphasis) – G♯ (emphasis) – A. Now both the flat sixth and the leading tone appear in the same short phrase. The emotional intensity is compressed—anguish and urgency in immediate succession.

6. **The descending version of the augmented second**: descend from high A: A – G♯ – F. The G♯ → F downward (one-and-a-half steps descending) is a dramatic fall—from urgency into shadow. This descending augmented second creates grief rather than the upward version's drama.

7. **Close with the complete descending scale**: A – G♯ – F – E – D – C – B – A. The G♯ → F in the descent; the final B – A arrival (a major second falling to the tonic, less urgent than the leading tone but still directed). End softly.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Classical drama** (Baroque and Classical style): slow to moderate tempo; the augmented second (F → G♯) as a deliberate, emphasized gesture at phrase peaks; the leading-tone arrival (G♯ → A) always on a downbeat; terraced dynamics (sudden loud/soft changes rather than gradual swells); no rubato.

**Romantic grief** (Romantic style): more rubato; the flat sixth (F) sustained longer, with a dynamic swell; the augmented second played as a vocal, expressive gesture rather than an architectural one; the leading-tone arrival softer rather than loud—arriving home with exhaustion rather than triumph.

**Exotic fire** (Middle Eastern influence): the augmented second as the primary melodic gesture—repeat it, ornament it, approach it from above and below; the Phrygian character of the flat second (A harmonic minor also contains A♭ → B♭ → C if you begin from the seventh degree, but in standard A harmonic minor the exotic quality comes from the augmented second alone); moderate to fast tempo; strong rhythmic pulse.

**Gothic drama**: slow, sparse, registrally extreme—only the lowest and highest notes of the range used; silence between phrases as long as the phrases themselves; the augmented second approached very slowly, the half-step landing on G♯ before the final arrival.

### Instrument-Specific Tips

**Recorder**: the leading-tone resolution (G♯ → A) in A harmonic minor places G♯ as a cross-fingering with a slightly different tone than the surrounding notes. Use this tonal difference expressively—the leading tone's slightly "veiled" quality makes it sound even more urgent than it would on a keyboard instrument.

**Bawu**: the augmented second (F → G♯) as a continuous pitch bend (slide from F upward through G to G♯) is the most expressive possible approach to this interval. This is the bawu's greatest contribution to harmonic minor expression.

**Low D whistle in A harmonic minor**: A harmonic minor is available with D as the tonic (D harmonic minor); G♯ (the raised seventh of A harmonic minor) becomes C♯ in D harmonic minor—accessible via standard cross-fingering.

**Kaval**: D harmonic minor is among the kaval's most natural modal environments; the augmented second (B♭ → C♯ in D harmonic minor) is available across the full range.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Harmonic Minor

**Using the scale for every phrase**: harmonic minor's augmented second is dramatic—but drama loses impact through repetition. If every phrase contains the F → G♯ leap, the listener normalizes it. Reserve the augmented second for phrases of maximum emotional weight; let other phrases use only the lower part of the scale (A – B – C – D – E) without touching F or G♯.

**Confusing harmonic minor with natural minor**: the only difference between A harmonic minor and A natural minor (Aeolian) is the raised seventh (G♯ vs. G natural). Beginners sometimes unconsciously revert to G natural, producing Aeolian instead. Check each phrase: every time the seventh degree appears, is it G♯ (harmonic minor) or G natural (Aeolian)? Choose deliberately—both are valid, but they produce different emotions.

**Leading tone on every cadence**: using G♯ → A at every single phrase ending creates mechanical predictability—every phrase clicks shut identically. Vary the phrase endings: some through the leading tone (G♯ → A—decisive), some from above (B → A—settling), some on the fifth (E—left open). The leading tone arrival should feel like a decisive event, not a reflex.
