## What Is Lydian?

Lydian is the fourth of the seven [diatonic](../glossary.md#diatonic) modes—and the brightest. It is the only diatonic mode that raises a note compared to the major scale, making it "super-major": a scale with the full brightness of major and one note that extends beyond it into something more expansive, floating, and dreamlike.

The Greek philosopher Pythagoras considered the Lydian mode to be the most optimistic, making it an ideal scale for promoting human goodness and uplifting the spirit.

Songs written in the Lydian mode generally have a bright, happy sound due to the scale's super-major nature. But Lydian's particular character is not merely happiness—it is wonder. The floating, unresolved quality of its raised fourth creates the sensation of suspension, of being lifted above ordinary expectation, of hovering at the threshold of something vast.

## The Interval Structure

**Step pattern**: Whole – Whole – Whole – Half – Whole – Whole – Half

**F Lydian** (the white-key example):

| Degree | Note | Distance from previous |
|--------|------|----------------------|
| 1 (tonic) | F |—|
| 2 | G | Whole step |
| 3 | A | Whole step |
| ♯4 (raised fourth) | B | Whole step |
| 5 | C | Half-step |
| 6 | D | Whole step |
| 7 | E | Whole step |
| 8 (octave) | F | Half-step |

**Relationship to major**: Lydian is the major scale with one change—the fourth degree is raised by a half-step. In F Lydian, this means B natural replaces the B♭ that F major would have. The Lydian scale can be described as a major scale with the fourth scale degree raised a semitone, making it an augmented fourth above the tonic.

The [augmented fourth](../glossary.md#augmented-interval) between the tonic and the raised fourth (F to B in F Lydian) is a [tritone](../glossary.md#tritone)—the most dissonant interval in Western music. In Lydian, this tritone sits above the tonic rather than pointing urgently toward resolution, creating the characteristic floating sensation.

## The Characteristic Note

The raised fourth (♯4)—B in F Lydian, F♯ in C Lydian—is Lydian's defining note and the source of all its emotional character.

**What the raised fourth does**: in a standard major scale, the fourth degree (B♭ in F major) sits a [perfect fourth](../glossary.md#perfect-fourth) above the tonic—a stable, grounded interval that closes the scale downward. Lydian replaces this with an augmented fourth, which leans *upward* rather than settling downward. The note seems to want to ascend rather than resolve, creating the characteristic "floating" quality.

It took the already bright major scale and made it sound even more expansive, hopeful, floaty, and just a tad bit mysterious.

**The tritone paradox**: the tritone in most contexts creates tension that urgently wants to resolve. In Lydian, the tritone is between the tonic and the raised fourth—not between the seventh and fourth (as in standard major), where it drives the leading tone upward and the fourth downward. Because neither note of the Lydian tritone is the tonic, the tension floats rather than pulling toward a specific resolution. This is the structural reason why Lydian sounds suspended rather than tense.

## Why Lydian Sounds the Way It Does

In Lydian mode, the tonic, dominant, and supertonic triads are all major. The subdominant is diminished. This means the chord on the raised fourth—which in standard major would be a major IV chord—becomes a diminished chord in Lydian. The practical effect: the most natural harmonic motion in Lydian is I → II (tonic to the chord on the second degree), not I → IV as in major. This II major chord motion creates an unusual, hovering harmonic feeling with no clear sense of direction.

George Russell's *Lydian Chromatic Concept of Tonal Organization* argued that Lydian was actually more fundamental than major as a tonal center—that the natural tendency of harmonic series acoustics led toward Lydian rather than Ionian as the "most resolved" scale. This theory directly inspired the modal jazz revolution. In practical terms it should be said that few rock songs that use modes such as the Phrygian, Lydian, or Locrian actually maintain a harmony rigorously fixed on them—Lydian often appears as a momentary color, not a sustained tonal environment.

## Emotional Character

| Emotion | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Wonder and awe | The floating raised fourth creates suspension—the listener is lifted and held |
| Magical or mystical | The mode consistently evokes fantasy, dreams, and the otherworldly |
| Transcendent joy | Brighter than major—joy without limitation, hope without ceiling |
| Sacred and timeless | Medieval hymnody used Lydian for festive and elevated occasions |
| Bittersweet nostalgia | The floating quality can express yearning for something beautiful |

Lydian is the mode of John Williams's most awe-inspiring film moments—the sound of impossibility made possible. It is also the mode of Beethoven's prayer of thanksgiving after surviving near-death: joy that has passed through the shadow.

## Lydian Across History

### Ancient Greece and Medieval

The name Lydian refers to the ancient kingdom of Lydia in Anatolia. In Greek music theory there was a Lydian scale—though scholars note it may not have corresponded precisely to the modern Lydian mode.

In medieval European church music, Lydian was Mode V (the authentic mode on F), theoretically using B♮ but in practice more commonly featuring B♭—which would have made such pieces in the modern F major scale. The use of B♭ versus B♮ was a central point of debate, and the natural B (Lydian) was favored in festive, elevated contexts. The Lydian mode was particularly favored in religious songs around the festive Advent, Christmas, and Easter seasons.

Medieval Lydian examples:
- **Hymn to St. Magnus** (13th century, F Lydian): one of the oldest surviving examples of Lydian mode in notation; a solemn, bright sacred hymn
- **Motet** *Nato nobis Salvatore* (Renaissance, F♯ Lydian, performed by Early Music New York): demonstrates the Lydian brightness in Renaissance polyphony

Bach's famous chorale *"Es ist genug"* from Cantata 60 (BWV 60) opens with the first four notes of the Lydian scale. Bach makes sense of this in terms of diatonic harmony, but this does not prevent it having the strange and transporting effect described above. The magical feel of this chorale led the 20th-century composer Alban Berg to incorporate it into the last movement of his Violin Concerto.

## Lydian in Classical Music

**Beethoven**—*String Quartet No. 15 in A minor, Op. 132*, 3rd movement (1825, "Heiliger Dankgesang", F Lydian):
Perhaps the most famous and deeply personal use of Lydian in all of classical music. After recovering from a serious intestinal illness that he feared would prove fatal, Beethoven wrote this movement as a prayer of thanksgiving. He inscribed it: "Heiliger Dankgesang eines Genesenen an die Gottheit, in der lydischen Tonart" (Sacred Song of Thanksgiving from a Convalescent to the Deity, in the Lydian Mode). The opening chords, played without vibrato, are like chords played on an organ. The Lydian mode sounds almost like a major scale, but off just enough to impart an antique air to Beethoven's sublime chorale. Widely considered one of the most significant movements Beethoven wrote, this piece demonstrates Lydian's capacity for sacred, transcendent joy.

The movement alternates with a section marked "Neue Kraft fühlend" (Feeling new strength) in an Andante 3/8 with all the decorative trills and delights that are excluded from the utter simplicity of the Lydian hymn—a structural demonstration of the contrast between Lydian floating timelessness and active major-key vitality.

**Other classical examples**:
- **Beethoven**—*Symphony No. 5*, last movement: unexpectedly uses a Lydian F♯ over a C pedal when moving towards the dominant key. The result is like a jolt of electricity, catapulting us forward 200 years to the lush sounds of contemporary film music
- **Charles-Valentin Alkan**—*Allegro Barbaro* from *12 Etudes in All the Minor Keys*: the most intriguing piece in Lydian mode for piano in the Romantic period
- **George Enescu**—*Decet for Winds, Op. 14* (1906): includes Lydian-mode passages in the second and third movements
- **Carlos Chávez**—*Symphony No. 3* (1951–54), scherzo movement: opens with a fugue subject in C Lydian
- **Bartók**—*Romanian Dance No. 6*: begins in D Lydian, moves to C Lydian; demonstrates Lydian's folk connection and its mixture with Mixolydian

**Polish folk music**: many Polish folksongs, including the mazurka, are in the Lydian mode; the first six notes of this mode were sometimes known as the "Polish mode."

## Lydian in Film Music

Lydian is the dominant mode of film scoring for scenes of wonder, magic, discovery, and transcendence. It is the mode that tells the listener: something impossible is possible; something beautiful lies ahead.

**John Williams**—the master of cinematic Lydian:
- *Star Wars*: "The Force Theme" and much of the score uses Lydian for the mystical and transcendent moments
- *E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial*: "Flying Theme" in Lydian for maximum emotional lift—the feeling of flight, literally and emotionally
- *Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull*: the skull motif uses Lydian for mysterious, ancient-alien grandeur
- *Close Encounters of the Third Kind*: the alien communication theme and the wonder of first contact

**Michael Giacchino**—*LOST* (John Locke's theme): right from the start you can hear the Lydian scale—the raised fourth signals mystery and otherness.

**Danny Elfman**—*The Simpsons* theme: one of the most globally recognized pieces of Lydian writing. While many people consider The Simpsons theme song to be composed in Lydian mode, the 12-note motif is more Lydian dominant than true Lydian, thanks to the quirky opening arrangement.

**Debussy**—*L'isle joyeuse (The Joyous Island)*: Debussy uses the Lydian scale to create a bridge between whole-tone and diatonic scales, using A Lydian to create a floating, shimmering effect. In L'isle Joyeuse, Debussy uses the Lydian scale to create a bridge between whole-tone and diatonic scales.

## Lydian in Jazz, Folk, and Pop

**Jazz**: George Russell's *Lydian Chromatic Concept of Tonal Organization* (1953) placed Lydian at the center of a new harmonic theory, influencing Miles Davis, John Coltrane, Ornette Coleman, and Woody Shaw. The raised fourth (♯11) of Lydian is fundamental to jazz improvisation over major seventh chords. Joe Satriani's "Flying in a Blue Dream" exemplifies Lydian in instrumental rock fusion—an indescribably floaty feeling.

**Classical crossover**:
- **Steve Porcaro / Michael Jackson**—*"Human Nature"*: the verse melody uses Lydian-adjacent harmony

## Instrument Access

| Instrument | Natural Lydian key | Raised fourth access |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Alto recorder | F Lydian | B natural is standard fingering in F Lydian context |
| Tenor recorder | C Lydian | F♯ requires cross-fingering but is readily available |
| Low D whistle | D Lydian | G♯ requires cross-fingering |
| G whistle | G Lydian | C♯ requires cross-fingering |
| Bawu | C or D Lydian | Fully chromatic; raised fourth accessible throughout |
| NAF | Limited | Raised fourth usually requires 7th-hole extension if available |
| Moldavian kaval | D Lydian | Available |

**Note for recorder players**: the raised fourth in F Lydian (B natural) is already a natural fingering in most recorder scales—there is no cross-fingering required. This makes F Lydian one of the most idiomatic Lydian keys for the recorder, and it is worth noting that this key was historically favored for Lydian composition (hence the "Polish mode" being the first six notes of the F Lydian scale).

## Composing in Lydian: Practical Techniques

### Let the Raised Fourth Float

The raised fourth's power comes from not resolving it immediately. Where Phrygian's characteristic note wants to fall, and Dorian's raised sixth wants to lift to a peak and settle, Lydian's raised fourth wants to hover. Sustain it. Let it sit in the air. The longer it floats without resolving, the stronger the sense of suspension.

### Approach the Raised Fourth from Below

The most expressive path to the raised fourth is from below—ascending through the major third to the raised fourth: 3 – ♯4 – 5 (A – B – C in F Lydian). This upward sweep through the augmented fourth position feels like lifting off. The note arrives at the raised fourth as a moment of expansion.

### The I–II Motion as Harmonic Color

Since the tonic chord (I) and the chord on the second degree (II) are both major in Lydian, moving between them creates an unusually open harmonic motion—two major chords a whole step apart with no sense of the normal tonal "tension-resolution" pull. This motion (F major → G major in F Lydian) is Lydian's characteristic harmonic color.

### Use Silence to Let the Mode Float

Lydian's floating quality is amplified by silence. After a phrase that arrives at the raised fourth, allow silence before the next phrase. The suspension hangs in the silence, and the listener is held in the wondering state. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](brain-biology-psychology.md) for why silence after a suspended note is neurologically powerful.

### Avoid the Perfect Fourth

The perfect fourth (B♭ in F Lydian—the note that would be in F major) is the one note that "corrects" Lydian back to standard major. Use it deliberately when you want to ground the mode briefly, or avoid it entirely to maintain the consistent floating quality throughout.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: alto recorder (F Lydian)
**F Lydian**: F – G – A – B – C – D – E – F
**Affect**: wonder at something vast and beautiful

1. Begin very quietly on C (the fifth—stable). Hold for four beats.
2. Descend to F through A: C – A – G – F. Major and bright; Lydian feels almost major here.
3. Ascend slowly: F – G – A – B. When B arrives (the raised fourth), sustain it. Do not rush to resolve. Let it float for three full beats.
4. Very slowly step from B to C. This resolution—raised fourth to fifth—should feel like a gentle landing, not a decisive arrival.
5. From C, continue upward: C – D – E – F (octave). The ascending major scale in the upper register, following the floating raised fourth, creates a sense of arrival at the top without heaviness.
6. Descend back to low F very quietly. End pianissimo.

**Goal**: music that suspends the listener in wonder—the modal equivalent of looking at something very large from a great distance and feeling the scale of it.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Lydian

**Pure wonder and awe**: moderate-slow tempo; the raised fourth (B in F Lydian) as the emotional center—arrived at from below (A → B), held for 3–4 beats without resolving, then continuing upward (B → C) or descending (B → A → G → F); the phrase lingers at B rather than pressing through it; dynamic swell on B (the messa di voce applied to the raised fourth); long silences after each B arrival; high register for the peak phrase.

**Cinematic vastness** (film music Lydian): moderate tempo; ascending phrases to the raised fourth; the II chord motion (in Lydian: tonic → chord on the second degree, both major—F major → G major in F Lydian) felt as a melodic ascent from F through G; the raised fourth as the point where the melody "opens up" into the sky; the flat seventh (when used to become Lydian Dominant) adds ominous depth to the vastness.

**Floating dream**: very slow; the raised fourth held so long it becomes the de facto tonal center rather than the tonic; phrases that begin and end away from F (the tonic), spending most of their time on B (raised fourth), E (major seventh), or C (fifth); the tonic F felt as a brief touch-and-leave rather than a resting point; the music has no gravity.

**Playful brightness** (lighter Lydian): moderate tempo with rhythmic clarity; the raised fourth as a surprising note that arrives and continues upward rather than floating; short phrases; the mode's "super-major" brightness emphasized through tempo and articulation; the flat seventh avoided (to keep the Mixolydian earthiness out and maintain the floating brightness).

**Sacred timelessness**: slow to very slow; the raised fourth as a note of pure elevation—not wonder at something new, but recognition of something eternal; the mode's ancient use for festive church occasions present in the sparseness and gravity; low ornament; each note weighted equally.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Lydian

**Using the perfect fourth by accident**: the perfect fourth (B♭ in F Lydian—the note that would be in F major) is the one note that "corrects" Lydian back to standard major and eliminates all of the mode's floating quality. Beginners who are not vigilant slip into B♭ by habit (it is the "correct" fourth in F major). Every time you need to play what would be the fourth degree, play it sharp (B natural in F Lydian). This is the entire Lydian mode in one discipline.

**Not dwelling on the raised fourth**: the raised fourth is most powerful when it floats—held, suspended, without immediate resolution. Beginners pass through it quickly as a melodic step rather than treating it as the mode's emotional center. Slow down when it arrives. Give it time. The entire floating quality of Lydian lives in how long the raised fourth is held before continuing.

**Treating Lydian as "just happy major"**: Lydian is not simply a brighter major scale. Its emotional range includes wonder, awe, mystery, timelessness, and even longing—none of which is simple brightness. Playing Lydian at a fast, jolly tempo with quick ornaments produces cheerful music, not Lydian music. The mode's characteristic emotion is expansive and suspended, not bouncy.

**Always resolving the raised fourth upward**: the raised fourth (B in F Lydian) can resolve upward to C or descend back to A. Beginners often resolve it upward every time because that feels "correct" (it is a half-step below C—the leading-tone relationship). But descending from B through A back to G or F creates a different emotional quality—the floating note falling slowly back into the major world below. Vary the direction.
