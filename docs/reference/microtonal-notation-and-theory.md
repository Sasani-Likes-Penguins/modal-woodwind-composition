## What Is a Microtone?

A[microtone](../glossary.md#microtone) is any musical interval smaller than a standard Western half-step ([semitone](../glossary.md#half-step)). In equal temperament—the standard Western tuning system—the octave is divided into 12 equal half-steps of exactly 100[cents](../glossary.md#cent) each. A microtone is any interval smaller than 100 cents.

Microtones are not "out of tune" notes. They are precisely placed pitches with specific expressive and structural roles in the traditions that use them. The[neutral third](../glossary.md#neutral-interval) of Arabic[Maqam Bayati](world-arabic-maqam.md)—approximately 150 cents, sitting exactly between a minor third (100 cents) and a major third (200 cents)—is not an imprecise approximation of either. It is its own pitch, with its own emotional identity, that neither the minor nor major third can replace.

Understanding microtones is essential for working with:
-[Arabic Maqam](world-arabic-maqam.md)—quarter-tone inflections
-[Turkish Makam](world-turkish-makam.md)—53-comma koma system
-[Persian Dastgah](world-persian-dastgah.md)—koron and sori accidentals
-[Hindustani Raga](world-hindustani-raga.md)—22-shruti system (in theory) and gamaka inflections
-[Carnatic Melakarta](world-carnatic-melakarta.md)—gamaka and shruti
-[Byzantine Modes](world-byzantine-modes.md)—soft chromatic and enharmonic genera
-[Gamelan](world-gamelan.md)—non-equal-tempered tuning with no Western equivalent

## Why Microtones Matter for This Repository

The instruments in this collection—bawu, kaval, NAF, and (with technique) whistle—are capable of producing microtones. The recorder and whistle in standard fingering are essentially fixed to the 12 equal pitches of Western equal temperament, but cross-fingerings and partial hole coverage open a range of microtonal possibilities.

For any player working with Arabic maqam, Turkish makam, Persian dastgah, or Indian raga, the ability to produce the characteristic microtonal inflections of those systems is not optional—it is what makes the music sound like itself rather than like a Western approximation of it.

The[neutral second](../glossary.md#neutral-interval) of Bayati maqam, the[koron](../glossary.md#koron)-inflected notes of Persian Shur, the specific shruti placement of a Carnatic gamaka—these are the emotional heart of the music they appear in. Approximating them with the nearest standard Western pitch produces a different, less expressive result.

## Units of Microtonal Measurement

### The Cent

The **cent** is the standard unit for measuring small pitch intervals in Western music theory. One cent equals one hundredth of a[half-step](../glossary.md#half-step) (semitone). There are 100 cents in one semitone and 1,200 cents in one octave.

Cents allow precise comparison of intervals from different tuning systems:

| Interval | Cents |
|----------|-------|
| Unison (same note) | 0 |
| Quarter-tone (half a half-step) | 50 |
| Half-step (semitone) | 100 |
| Whole step (major second) | 200 |
| Minor third | 300 |
| Major third | 400 |
| Perfect fourth | 500 |
| Tritone | 600 |
| Perfect fifth | 700 |
| Octave | 1200 |

Microtones are typically described in cents: a "neutral second" is approximately 150 cents; a "slightly flat major third" might be 380 cents; the Turkish koma is approximately 22.6 cents.

### The Savart

A less common unit used in some French and older European music theory. One savart = approximately 3.99 cents. Rarely encountered in practice.

### The Comma (General)

In music theory, a "comma" refers to small pitch differences that arise when comparing different tuning systems. Several different commas are named:

- **Pythagorean comma**: the difference between 12 perfect fifths and 7 octaves—approximately 23.5 cents
- **Syntonic comma**: the difference between a pure major third (from the harmonic series) and the Pythagorean major third—approximately 21.5 cents
- **Turkish koma**: specifically 1/53 of an octave—approximately 22.6 cents (see below)

## Western Microtonal Notation Systems

Western classical music notation, developed for equal temperament, has no standard symbols for microtones. Several notation systems have been proposed, with varying adoption:

### Quarter-Tone Notation (Most Common)

Quarter-tones—intervals of approximately 50 cents, halfway between standard half-steps—are the most commonly notated microtonal intervals. Several symbol sets exist:

**Arrows on accidentals** (most widely used):
- ↑ or ↑♯ before a note: raise by one quarter-tone (approximately 50 cents)
- ↓ or ↓♭ before a note: lower by one quarter-tone (approximately 50 cents)
- These symbols are placed before the note like standard accidentals

**Half-sharp and half-flat** (alternative system):
- ♮⬆ or **d** (half-sharp): raise by a quarter-tone
- ♭⬇ or **𝄳** (half-flat): lower by a quarter-tone

**Practice note**: there is no single universally agreed-upon Western notation system for quarter-tones. Different publishers and composers use different symbols. When writing music that includes quarter-tones for Western-trained performers, always include a legend explaining the symbols used.

### Three-Quarter Tones and Other Subdivisions

For intervals other than exact quarter-tones:
- Three-quarter sharp: raise by three quarter-tones (150 cents)
- Three-quarter flat: lower by three quarter-tones (150 cents)

These are rarer in practice. Most microtonal Western notation focuses on quarter-tones as the primary subdivision.

### Extended Accidentals (Hába, Carrillo, others)

20th-century microtonal composers developed their own notation systems:

- **Alois Hába** (Czech, 1893–1973): notation for sixth-tones and twelfth-tones
- **Julián Carrillo** (Mexican, 1875–1965): numeric notation for his "Sonido 13" system (up to 96 equal divisions)
- **Harry Partch** (American, 1901–1974): just intonation system with custom notation for 43-tone scale

These systems are not relevant for the instruments in this collection but demonstrate that Western notation for microtones is an evolving and non-standardized field.

## Persian Notation: Koron and Sori

Persian classical music uses two specific microtonal accidentals that lower or raise notes by approximately a quarter-tone:

### Koron (♭ in Persian notation: کُرُن)

**What it does**: lowers a note by approximately 50 cents (a quarter-tone).

**Symbol**: in Persian notation, the koron is written as a curved-line symbol resembling a backwards flat (♭) or a specific Persian-tradition symbol. When transcribed into Western staff notation for Western musicians, it is typically indicated with a downward arrow before the relevant accidental or with the half-flat symbol.

**Where it appears**: most commonly on the second degree of Dastgah-e Shur (making it between a standard minor second and no accidental at all), on the third degree of Shur (making a neutral third rather than a minor third), and at various positions in other dastgahs.

**Emotional function**: the koron inflection is not merely a pitch—it is an expressive identity. The koron-inflected second degree of Shur (approximately E-half-flat in D Shur) is the defining note of that dastgah's character. It cannot be substituted with either E♭ (too low) or E♮ (too high) without fundamentally changing the dastgah's emotional world.

**On wind instruments**: the bawu (via pitch bending) and the kaval (via embouchure adjustment) can produce koron inflections accurately. The recorder and whistle can only approximate—partial hole coverage raises or lowers pitch slightly, but the degree of control is limited.

### Sori (♯ in Persian notation: سُری)

**What it does**: raises a note by approximately 50 cents (a quarter-tone).

**Symbol**: the sori uses a different Persian-tradition symbol—often written with an upward arrow before an accidental or a half-sharp in Western transcription.

**Where it appears**: less common than koron, but used in specific dastgahs and gushes where a note is raised by a quarter-tone from its standard position.

**Practical note**: koron and sori are defined in Persian music theory as precise intervals, but in practice they are performed with some flexibility—the "correct" koron pitch for Shur may differ slightly from the "correct" koron pitch for Segah, because each dastgah has its own intonation identity.

## Arabic and Turkish Notation

### Arabic Quarter-Tone System

Arabic music theory divides the octave into 24 equal quarter-tones (compared to Western music's 12 equal half-tones). The characteristic[neutral](../glossary.md#neutral-interval) intervals of Arabic maqam—particularly the neutral second and neutral third—are produced by the quarter-tone divisions between the standard Western half-steps.

**Standard Arabic notation**: Western staff notation with additional symbols:
- A downward arrow (↓) before a note: lower by a quarter-tone
- An upward arrow (↑) before a note: raise by a quarter-tone

**In practice**: many Arabic musicians and composers use the approximation of a half-flat (♭ with a superscript half) or half-sharp for quarter-tones when notating for mixed ensembles.

**The neutral second**: approximately 150 cents—halfway between a half-step (100 cents) and a whole step (200 cents). This is the interval heard in the second degree of Maqam Bayati, the characteristic "searching, yearning" note of that maqam. On a piano, it falls exactly between E♭ and E♮ in the context of D Bayati.

**The neutral third**: approximately 350 cents—between a minor third (300 cents) and a major third (400 cents). Heard in Maqam Rast, which has a "warm major" feeling due to this neutral third being somewhat brighter than a minor third but not as bright as a major third.

### Turkish Koma-Based Notation

Turkish makam theory uses a more precise system than Arabic quarter-tone notation. The octave is divided into 53 equal parts called komas (or komas).

**The koma**: 1/53 of an octave = approximately 22.6 cents

**Why 53?**: The number 53 is mathematically special because 53 equal divisions of the octave provides extremely close approximations to both just perfect fifths (702 cents ≈ 31 komas × 22.6) and just major thirds (386 cents ≈ 17 komas × 22.6). The 53-koma system is one of the most acoustically accurate equal-division systems.

**Standard Turkish intervals** (approximate cent equivalents):

| Turkish interval name | Komas | Approximate cents | Western closest |
|----------------------|-------|-------------------|----------------|
| Koma | 1 | 22.6 |—(between notes) |
| Küçük mücennep | 4 | 90.6 | Minor half-step |
| Büyük mücennep | 5 | 113 | Major half-step |
| Tanini | 9 | 204 | Whole step |
| Küçük üçlü | 12 | 271 | Minor third (Western: 300) |
| Büyük üçlü | 17 | 384 | Major third |
| Dörtlü | 22 | 498 | Perfect fourth |

**Turkish makam notation**: in standard Turkish classical music notation, accidentals are written with specialized symbols indicating koma-based alterations—typically with a superscript number indicating the number of komas of alteration. In practice, these are often approximated to the nearest quarter-tone for performers from outside the Turkish tradition.

## Indian Shruti: The 22-Division System

Indian classical music theory describes 22 *shrutis* (Sanskrit: श्रुति, literally "what is heard")—the smallest perceptible pitch divisions within an octave recognized in the traditional theoretical system.

**Important caveat**: the shruti system is an ancient theoretical model and does not map precisely onto modern acoustical measurement. Different scholars and traditions assign different cent values to the 22 shrutis. The system is descriptive of perceptible pitch distinctions, not a prescriptive performance tuning system.

**The seven notes (swaras) and their shrutis**:

| Swara | Name | Number of shrutis from Sa | Approximate Western note |
|-------|------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| Sa | Shadja | 0 | C (fixed) |
| Re | Rishabha | 3 or 4 | D (with variants) |
| Ga | Gandhara | 2 more | E (with variants) |
| Ma | Madhyama | 4 more | F (with raised variant) |
| Pa | Panchama | 4 more | G (fixed) |
| Dha | Dhaivata | 3 or 4 | A (with variants) |
| Ni | Nishada | 2 more | B (with variants) |

Each of the notes Re, Ga, Ma, Dha, and Ni has two or three variant positions—the different shrutis within each swara's region give the 22-division total.

**In practice**: modern Carnatic and Hindustani musicians do not consciously calculate shrutis during performance. The shruti system is a theoretical framework that describes the pitch territory available within the octave—the[gamaka](../glossary.md#gamaka) ornamentations of Indian classical music move freely through these pitch territories as an expressive vocabulary rather than landing on fixed discrete points.

The practical implications for wind players: the[meend](../glossary.md#meend) (slide) of Hindustani music naturally traverses the shruti territory between standard Western pitches. The bawu's continuous pitch bending and the kaval's embouchure flexibility both allow this kind of fluid microtonal movement without requiring the player to consciously target specific shrutis.

## The Neutral Interval: Between Major and Minor

The "neutral" interval—sitting acoustically between the minor and major version of a given interval—is one of the most important microtonal concepts for players of these instruments.

### Neutral Second (approximately 150 cents)

Halfway between a minor second (100 cents) and a major second (200 cents). Heard in:
- Arabic Maqam Bayati (second degree), Maqam Sikah (starting note)
- Turkish Makam Uşşak (second degree—approximately 12 komas above the tonic)
- Persian Dastgah-e Shur (koron-inflected second degree)

**Sound character**: searching, bending, yearning—neither a half-step pressing against home nor a whole step stepping away comfortably. A note that occupies its own emotional middle ground.

### Neutral Third (approximately 350 cents)

Halfway between a minor third (300 cents) and a major third (400 cents). Heard in:
- Arabic Maqam Rast (third degree—gives Rast its "warm major" quality)
- Turkish Makam Rast (same position)
- Persian Dastgah-e Rast-Panjgah (third degree)

**Sound character**: somewhere between the darkness of minor and the brightness of major. Heard as "exotic" or "warm" by Western listeners because it falls between the two categories they recognize.

### Neutral Sixth (approximately 850 cents)

Between a minor sixth (800 cents) and a major sixth (900 cents). Less commonly discussed but present in some maqam scales.

## Microtones and Equal Temperament

Equal temperament—the standard Western tuning—is a deliberate compromise. By making all 12 half-steps exactly equal (100 cents each), equal temperament allows instruments to play in any key without retuning, but slightly misrepresents most pure harmonic intervals:

| Pure harmonic interval | Just intonation (cents) | Equal temperament (cents) | Difference |
|-----------------------|------------------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Perfect fifth | 702 | 700 | 2 cents flat |
| Major third | 386 | 400 | 14 cents sharp |
| Minor third | 316 | 300 | 16 cents flat |
| Perfect fourth | 498 | 500 | 2 cents sharp |

For most Western music, these differences are imperceptible or acceptable. But for world music traditions built on pure intonation (Indian raga, for example, where the drone makes small intonation differences audible), or for traditions with microtonal intervals that fall between equal-tempered pitches (Arabic maqam), equal temperament is simply the wrong tuning system.

**The implication for wind players**: bowed-string players, vocalists, and flexible-pitch wind players (bawu, kaval, NAF) naturally adjust toward pure intonation in ensemble contexts. This is not a problem to be solved—it is correct musical behavior. A bawu player performing Maqam Bayati with an *oud* or a *ney* should tune the neutral second to the acoustically correct approximately 150 cents, not to the nearest equal-tempered pitch.

## Producing Microtones on Each Instrument

### Bawu

The bawu's[free-reed](../glossary.md#free-reed) mechanism allows continuous pitch variation through embouchure (lip pressure) and airspeed. This is the most flexible microtonal instrument in this collection.

**How to raise pitch**: increase air pressure or tighten embouchure slightly
**How to lower pitch**: decrease air pressure or relax embouchure
**Range of variation**: approximately ±50 cents from the base pitch with standard technique; somewhat more with extreme embouchure adjustment

**Practical microtonal technique**:
- For koron-type inflections (lower by a quarter-tone): relax embouchure slightly while maintaining steady airflow; find the pitch by ear against a drone or reference pitch
- For neutral seconds and thirds: adjust toward the target pitch while sustaining—the bawu's continuous airflow allows searching for the pitch dynamically
- For sori-type inflections (raise by a quarter-tone): tighten embouchure slightly

**Important note**: the exact pitch produced depends on the specific reed, instrument, and player. Develop your microtonal accuracy by tuning against a reference pitch (drone, recording, or electronic tuner set to the target frequency in Hz).

### Moldavian Kaval in D

The kaval's open-bore design and embouchure-dependent tone production allow significant microtonal flexibility, especially in the lower and middle registers.

**How to raise pitch**: increase air pressure and/or change embouchure angle
**How to lower pitch**: relax embouchure and/or reduce air pressure
**Range of variation**: ±30–50 cents depending on register; more in the lower register

**Practical technique**:
- For Arabic/Turkish characteristic neutral intervals: approach the target pitch from below (from the nearest lower standard pitch) and find the neutral position by ear
- The kaval's dual-voice technique (partially covering the back fipple window) can be used to create a slight timbral change alongside the pitch inflection—reinforcing the emotional character of the microtonal note
- Circular breathing (when used) allows sustained microtonal notes without pitch drift from breath interruption

### Native American Flute

Partial hole coverage (half-holing) is the primary microtonal technique on the NAF.

**Half-holing**: covering a hole only partially—anywhere from 10% to 90% coverage—produces pitches between the fully open and fully closed positions. The relationship is not linear; the pitch change accelerates as coverage approaches 50%, then levels off.

**Practical range**: approximately ±30–50 cents on most notes via half-holing

**Limitation**: the NAF's pentatonic native scale already omits the most common microtonal positions (the minor second above the tonic, for example, is not a standard hole position). Half-holing is most useful for subtle inflections and for the NAF's traditional non-tempered playing style rather than for systematic microtonal scale construction.

**Breath pressure variation** also shifts pitch slightly on the NAF—useful for vibrato and subtle inflections.

### Irish Whistle (Low D and G)

The whistle is the least flexible for microtonal production, but several techniques exist:

**Partial hole coverage**: slowly covering or uncovering a hole produces a gradual pitch change. The most useful microtonal technique on whistle—produces the characteristic Irish slide ornament as a by-product.

**End-hole partial coverage**: partially covering the open end of the whistle (the bottom, below the last hole) raises the pitch slightly—approximately 10–30 cents depending on coverage. Can be used to fine-tune intonation or create slight inflections.

**Breath pressure**: slight increases in breath pressure raise the pitch; decreases lower it. Range is limited (approximately ±10–15 cents) but sufficient for intonation adjustment.

**Practical assessment**: the Irish whistle is not well-suited to systematic microtonal composition. It can produce the slight pitch inflections that characterize idiomatic Irish playing style, but the neutral intervals of Arabic maqam or the koron inflections of Persian music are difficult to produce accurately and consistently on a whistle.

### Alto and Tenor Recorder

The recorder's open-hole design allows several microtonal techniques:

**Partial hole coverage**: covering a hole partly produces intermediate pitches. On the recorder, half-holing is used routinely for notes that require cross-fingering but can be refined to specific microtonal positions.

**Cross-fingerings**: many cross-fingerings on the recorder produce notes slightly flat or sharp of equal temperament—these small deviations can be used expressively as microtonal colors.

**Breath pressure (limited)**: the recorder's fipple mechanism means breath pressure adjustments have a smaller effect on pitch than on open-embouchure instruments. Some pitch variation is possible but range is limited.

**Practical assessment**: the recorder can produce approximate microtones for coloristic purposes, but it is not precise enough for systematic maqam or dastgah performance requiring consistent neutral intervals. Use the recorder for modal music where the exact microtonal pitches are not structurally essential.

## Practical Notation for Composers

When writing music that includes microtonal inflections for the instruments in this collection, the following approaches are practical:

**For bawu and kaval (flexible intonation)**: use standard Western notation with a symbol or text indicating the microtonal quality:
- A downward arrow (↓) before a note: lower by approximately a quarter-tone
- An upward arrow (↑) before a note: raise by approximately a quarter-tone
- The Persian symbols koron and sori where the performer knows the tradition
- A performance note in the score: "The second degree of this maqam is a neutral second—approximately 150 cents above the tonic. Find this pitch by ear."

**For recorder and whistle (limited flexibility)**: indicate the nearest standard pitch with a performance note explaining the intended microtonal quality: "Play this note slightly flat of E♭—approaching the neutral second of Bayati maqam." The performer adjusts within their instrument's capability.

**For NAF**: performance direction rather than notation is most practical: "Play the second note of this phrase with a slight downward inflection—a breath-gentle lowering that leaves the pitch between the two standard holes." The NAF tradition is oral and improvisational; detailed microtonal notation is inconsistent with the instrument's practice tradition.

**For scores meant for general reference** (not instrument-specific): use the Arabic arrow notation (↑↓ before accidentals) as the most internationally recognized system, with a glossary in the score.
