## The Central Idea: Music as Emotional Rhetoric

Baroque music (roughly 1600–1750) was governed by a powerful idea: music should express specific emotions with the same precision and intention that a great speaker uses to move an audience [18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/). Composers thought of themselves as orators—making arguments in sound rather than words, but with the same goal of producing a specific feeling in the listener.

The formal name for this idea was the *Doctrine of Affections* (German: *Affektenlehre*)—from the Latin word *affectus*, meaning "feeling" or "passion" [15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroque_music)[18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/). In practice, it meant:

1. Before writing anything, decide exactly what emotion this piece expresses.
2. Every note, ornament, rhythm, and volume choice serves that emotion.
3. If you can't connect a decision to the emotional goal, it doesn't belong.

This principle is the most useful thing Baroque music can offer a composer working today, regardless of style. Choose the feeling first. Everything else follows.

## Choosing One Feeling

Early Baroque composers typically wrote entire movements expressing a single emotion—the same sustained feeling from first note to last [18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/). Bach, who came later, was celebrated for being able to move between different emotional states within a single piece—but this was considered a mark of unusual mastery, not the default [18](https://louis.pressbooks.pub/exploringarts/chapter/568/).

For a solo wind player composing in Phrygian or Locrian: **start by trying the single-affect approach**. Choose grief, or sacred awe, or fierce passion, and commit to it entirely for one piece. This discipline is easier than it sounds and produces astonishingly focused, emotionally coherent music.

## Ornaments: The Emotional Vocabulary

In Baroque music, ornaments—trills, lean notes, slides, turns—were not decorations added after the important work was done. They *were* the important work. Performers were expected to improvise them freely from what the composer wrote [15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroque_music)[16](https://fiveable.me/history-music-1600-1750/unit-13).

The French tradition of Baroque music notated ornaments in elaborate detail; the Italian tradition largely left their execution to the performer [17](https://tafelmusik.org/explore-baroque/baroque-101/). Between these extremes, the shared understanding was: ornaments are how specific emotions are expressed with precision.

**The main ornaments and what they express**:

**The lean note** (Italian: *appoggiatura*): Instead of landing cleanly on the home note, land first on the one-up note—on a stressed beat, holding it with weight—then step down to home. The one-up note "leans" against home before resolving. This is the most emotionally expressive way to use Phrygian's defining note. The tension is held open for a beat before it releases.

**The trill**: Rapid alternation between a note and the note just above it. On the one-up note in Phrygian, this creates sustained, shimmering yearning—the note that wants to resolve but keeps being pulled back. Use on long notes, especially at phrase endings.

**The turn**: A four-note ornament that swirls around a main note (above, main, below, main). Applied to the one-up note, it makes the tension shimmer rather than just land.

**The mordent**: A very quick "dip"—the written note, quickly the note below, back to the written note. Adds a flash of direction without sustained tension.

**The sigh** (Latin: *suspiratio*): A short note, then a rest, then the next phrase begins. The rest *is* the sigh—the breath held after the emotion. Use in grief passages.

**The lament descent** (Latin: *passus duriusculus*): Moving the melody downward in pure half-steps—for example E → D♯ → D → C♯ → C. Baroque composers used this as the "grief figure"—the sound of inevitable, descending sorrow [15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroque_music). Even three or four half-steps creates this effect.

## Tone Color on Baroque Woodwinds

All Baroque woodwind instruments (recorder, baroque flute, baroque oboe) required non-standard fingerings to play notes that weren't in the instrument's natural scale [17](https://tafelmusik.org/explore-baroque/baroque-101/). These non-standard fingerings produce a slightly different tone quality—slightly quieter, slightly more "veiled" or indirect.

Baroque composers knew this and used it expressively [17](https://tafelmusik.org/explore-baroque/baroque-101/). The one-up note in Phrygian, being outside the home key's natural notes, will often be one of these "veiled" notes on recorder. Rather than trying to match the tone of the surrounding notes, treat that slight timbral difference as part of the note's emotional identity—a note that sounds slightly "other," slightly searching.

## Volume in Baroque Style: Sharp Contrasts

Baroque music typically changed volume in clear steps rather than gradual swells—loud, then quiet, rather than gradually getting louder or softer [26](https://www.musicalinstrumenthub.com/archives/21844). This came partly from the harpsichord (the keyboard instrument of the era), which couldn't vary volume based on touch. The aesthetic was contrast, not gradient.

For a solo wind instrument in Baroque style:
- Use clear loud and soft sections rather than continuous shaping
- The moment of switching from loud to soft (or vice versa) is itself expressive—a sudden drop to quiet can feel like an intake of breath, a moment of sudden private grief
- Playing the same phrase first loud then soft (or first soft then loud) was a standard Baroque way of varying a repeated phrase

That said: **Baroque flute players did shade volume within individual phrases**—breath pressure naturally varies as emotion varies. The "no gradual dynamics" rule is a structural principle for sections, not a ban on nuance within a phrase [19](https://www.oldflutes.com/baroq.htm).

## Phrase Structure: Music as Speech

Baroque composers thought of musical phrases the way we think of sentences—with questions, answers, exclamations, and pauses. Named figures from ancient Greek rhetoric were applied to music:

**The question phrase** (*antecedent*): a phrase that ends unresolved—on the one-up note, or on the note four steps up, or somewhere that clearly hasn't arrived home. It asks.

**The answer phrase** (*consequent*): a phrase that follows and resolves—arriving at the home note. It answers.

This question-and-answer pair is the smallest complete emotional exchange in Baroque music. You can build entire pieces from it, varying whether the question gets answered, delayed, or answered unexpectedly.

**Phrase length**: Baroque phrases were often four bars long, but composers regularly stretched or shortened phrases for expressive purposes [28](https://www.thejazzpianosite.com/jazz-piano-lessons/jazz-reharmonization/composition-and-melodic-development/). A phrase that "should" end at bar four but continues to bar six creates accumulating tension. A phrase that ends at bar three instead of four creates breathlessness.

## Form: The Shape of a Whole Piece

Most Baroque wind music used one of these forms:

**Binary (AB)**: two sections, each usually repeated. Section A establishes the home note and main emotional character; Section B develops the material and explores different territory, then closes. For Phrygian, the B section might briefly touch a different mode (plain minor, or a slightly brighter color) before returning to Phrygian at the close.

**Da capo (ABA)**: A section, then a contrasting B section, then the A section returns. The return of A sounds transformed by what came between—the familiar material arriving with new meaning.

**Through-composed**: no repeated sections; the music develops continuously from first note to last. Well-suited to Locrian, where a fixed point of return (harmonic "home") is unavailable anyway.

## Applying This to Your Instruments

**Recorder**: The recorder is the most naturally Baroque of your instruments. Use it for music that demands structural clarity, precise ornamental language, and the specific tender-to-grief emotional range that Baroque composers associated with it. The French Baroque writer François Raguenet described the recorder (and transverse flute) as suited to making music "groan" and "sigh" in mournful and tender airs [19](https://www.oldflutes.com/baroq.htm).

**Moldavian kaval**: While not a Baroque instrument, the Baroque approach of choosing a single affect, structuring phrases as questions and answers, and using the lean-note ornament to emphasize the one-up note translates entirely to the kaval. The result is not "Baroque kaval music"—it's Baroque thinking applied to a Balkan folk instrument, which produces something genuinely new.

**Irish whistle**: The Irish slow air is structurally Baroque in the best sense—a single long melody, deeply felt, with ornamental elaboration and phrase shapes that mirror vocal speech [40](https://www.tradschool.com/en/irish-music/low-whistle/). The "whistle" tradition's ornamentation (cuts, rolls, slides) is simply a different dialect of the same ornamental language Baroque composers used.

**NAF**: The Baroque idea of choosing one emotion and playing from it entirely—what the NAF tradition calls playing from the heart, what some players describe as a "medicine song"—is the same impulse. Different cultural frame, identical artistic logic.

## Baroque Repertoire for Wind Players to Study

The following pieces demonstrate Baroque compositional principles in context, and are directly applicable to the instruments in this collection:

### Solo Wind Pieces
- **J.S. Bach**—*Partita in A minor for solo flute, BWV 1013*: the most structurally complete solo wind piece in the Baroque repertoire; A Aeolian/Harmonic Minor; the allemande movement is a masterclass in motivic development without accompaniment
- **J.S. Bach**—*Sonata in E minor for flute and continuo, BWV 1034*: the slow movements demonstrate the lean note (*appoggiatura*) and *passus duriusculus* in their most characteristic forms
- **Telemann**—*Fantasias for solo flute* (12 fantasias, TWV 40:2–13): the most comprehensive set of Baroque solo wind pieces; each explores a different affect in a different key; many are in minor modes related to those in this repository
- **Telemann**—*Concerto in E minor for flute, recorder, and strings*: demonstrates how two instruments with different timbres articulate the same modal melody differently

### Keyboard Pieces (Transferable to Wind)
- **Bach**—*Well-Tempered Clavier*, Fugue in C♯ minor (Book 1): the *passus duriusculus* appears in the subject; grief rendered in counterpoint
- **Bach**—*Well-Tempered Clavier*, Prelude in E minor (Book 1): stepwise Aeolian melody demonstrating how a simple scale-based melody carries deep affect through phrasing alone
- **Handel**—*Suite No. 4 in D minor, HWV 437* (Sarabande): the melody used in Kubrick's *Barry Lyndon*; one of the most expressive Baroque sarabande melodies; D Aeolian with Harmonic Minor elements

### Sacred Vocal Music (For Understanding the Affect System)
- **Bach**—*Cantata BWV 82 "Ich habe genug"* (bass and oboe): the oboe melody in the opening aria is the single most expressive Baroque wind melody for grief affect; study how the *suspiratio* (sigh figure) structures each phrase
- **Purcell**—*"Dido's Lament"* from *Dido and Aeneas*: lament bass (*passus duriusculus*) over which the melody makes its final statement; the most structurally clear example of Baroque grief techniques

## Connecting Baroque Principles to Other Traditions

The Doctrine of Affections—choose one feeling, express it completely—appears across every tradition in this repository under different names:

| Tradition | Name for the principle | Application |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------|
| Baroque | Affektenlehre | Choose one affect; every note serves it |
| Indian classical | Rasa | Nine emotional essences; each raga has one primary rasa |
| Persian classical | Emotional world of each dastgah | Each dastgah has a specific emotional character |
| Jewish nusach | Shtayger for each occasion | The mode announces the emotional register before a note is played |
| Irish slow air | Playing from the heart | Interpret the text's emotion; everything serves it |
| Arabic maqam | Maqam's emotional color | Each maqam has a specific emotional world |

Every tradition independently reached the same insight: music is most effective when it knows what it is about emotionally before the first note is played.

## Composing in Baroque Style for Solo Wind: Step-by-Step

### The Single-Affect Composition: The Most Important Exercise

The most useful thing you can learn from Baroque music is the single-affect approach—composing an entire piece expressing one specific emotion from first note to last. This is harder than it sounds. Modern composition instinct tends toward emotional variety: we want contrast, development, different feelings. Baroque single-affect composition resists this and produces something different: depth within a single emotional world.

**How to do it**:

1. **Choose an affect before touching the instrument**. Write it down: "The dignified grief of something that cannot return." Not "sadness"—that is too general. Something specific, with texture.

2. **Choose a mode that serves the affect**. Dignified grief: Phrygian (the half-step pressing, ancient) or Aeolian (quiet resignation). Fierce passion: Phrygian Dominant or the Spanish Heptatonic scale. Sacred wonder: Lydian or a Dorian mode played very slowly.

3. **Choose one or two ornaments that express the affect**. Grief: the appoggiatura (lean note) on the characteristic note, and the sigh figure (short note + rest). Passion: the trill on the characteristic note. Wonder: the turn (*gruppetto*) on the peak note.

4. **Write phrases as questions and answers**. The question phrase ends somewhere unresolved (on the characteristic note, or the fourth, or the seventh). The answer phrase responds and lands somewhere more settled—not necessarily the tonic, but a step closer to rest.

5. **Use binary form**: two sections. Section A establishes the affect and mode in the lower-to-middle register. Section B develops the material in the upper register, returning home for the final phrase. Each section may be repeated.

6. **Never change the affect**. If you feel the urge to write a "lighter" phrase because the music has been serious for a while—resist it. Stay in the chosen emotional world. The depth comes from sustained commitment.

### The Rhetoric Structure of a Single Phrase

Baroque phrases have a rhetorical structure—like sentences in a speech. Understanding this structure gives you a template for every phrase you write:

**Opening gesture** (propositio): the first two or three notes that state the phrase's character. In Phrygian: E – F (the half-step press) states the mode immediately. This is the subject of the sentence.

**Development** (confutatio or thesis): the middle of the phrase, where the melodic idea is developed—moved to a higher note, flipped, ornamented. In Phrygian: from F, ascend to A or B, ornamenting as appropriate. This is the elaboration of the subject.

**Arrival** (confirmatio): the phrase ends at a note that either completes a thought (the tonic—a period) or leaves it open (the characteristic note or fourth—a comma or question mark). Choose deliberately.

**A complete single phrase contains all three**: opening gesture, development, arrival. A composition is a sequence of such phrases, each building on the previous, with a final arrival that closes the rhetorical argument.

### Applying the Doctrine of Affections to Beginners

If you have never composed before, the single most accessible Baroque principle is: **decide what the music is about before writing anything, and make every decision serve that decision.**

This is not just a Baroque principle—it is the foundation of all composition in every tradition in this repository. The Baroque codified it explicitly; Indian rasa theory formalized it differently; Persian dastgah assigns it by tradition; Jewish nusach encodes it in the choice of shtayger. The principle is universal.

**Practical exercise for beginners**:

1. Choose a single emotion. Write it in one sentence on paper.
2. Choose a mode from this repository that matches it.
3. Find your seed phrase (two to four notes)—the minimal melodic idea that feels like it contains the emotion.
4. Write four phrases:
   - Phrase 1: the seed phrase stated quietly
   - Phrase 2: the seed phrase restated, slightly modified—perhaps inverted, or at a different pitch level
   - Phrase 3: the seed phrase developed—reaches the highest note of the composition
   - Phrase 4: the seed phrase in its simplest, quietest form—landing on the tonic
5. Add one ornament on the most important note of phrase 3. Just one.

This four-phrase structure with one ornament is your first Baroque composition. It is small but complete. It has the emotional arc, the question-answer structure, the single affect, and one properly placed ornament.

### The Named Rhetorical Figures: A Practical Toolkit

The Baroque *musica poetica* tradition named specific melodic gestures after their emotional effects. These are not metaphors—they are specific note patterns used to create specific emotional responses:

| Figure | Notes | Emotional effect | How to use it |
|--------|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| *Suspiratio* (sigh) | Short note + rest | Grief, restraint | End a phrase with a short note, then rest before continuing |
| *Passus duriusculus* (hard step) | Descending half-steps | Inevitable grief, descent | Descend chromatically 3–6 half-steps |
| *Anabasis* (ascent) | Rising phrase | Hope, aspiration, joy | Rising phrase ascending to a peak note |
| *Katabasis* (descent) | Falling phrase | Sorrow, descent, conclusion | Falling phrase returning to the tonic |
| *Circulatio* (circle) | Circular motion around a note | Turning, uncertainty | Return repeatedly to the same note from above and below |
| *Saltus duriusculus* | Augmented interval leap | Shock, extreme emotion | Augmented second or fourth appearing unexpectedly |
| *Exclamatio* | Upward sixth or octave leap | Outcry, proclamation | A sudden upward leap of a sixth or more |

Use these as a compositional toolkit. A composition in Phrygian might use: *suspiratio* (sigh figure) at phrase endings; *passus duriusculus* (chromatic descent) for a passage of grief; *anabasis* (ascending phrase) before the peak; *katabasis* (descending phrase) for the close.
