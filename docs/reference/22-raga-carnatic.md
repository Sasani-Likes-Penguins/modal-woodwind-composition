## What Is Carnatic Music?

Carnatic music is the classical music of South India—older than Hindustani in its current form, more structured, and more composition-oriented. Where Hindustani music prizes individual improvisation and emotional exploration, Carnatic music prizes faithful interpretation of composed works alongside improvisation within strict structural rules [31][49].

The raga concept is shared between both systems—but how ragas are treated differs significantly.

---

## The Melakarta System: 72 Parent Scales

The most distinctive feature of Carnatic music theory is the **melakarta** system—a mathematically complete set of 72 "parent" scales, from which all other ragas can be derived [44][47].

A melakarta raga is a scale that contains all seven notes in both ascending and descending form—no notes skipped, no different notes on the way up versus down. From these 72 parents, hundreds of derived (*janya*) ragas are created by omitting notes, using different notes on ascent and descent, or adding characteristic motifs.

**The mathematical logic**: the 72 melakarta scales are constructed systematically from the 12 semitones of the octave:
- Sa (root) and Pa (fifth) are always fixed
- Ma (fourth) has two variants: natural or sharp
- The remaining four notes (Ri, Ga, Dha, Ni) each have three variants
- This gives: 2 × 6 × 6 = 72 possible combinations

The 72 melakartas are divided into 12 groups (*chakras*) of 6 ragas each [44]. The 36 melakartas in the first half use a natural fourth; the 36 in the second half use a sharp fourth.

**What this means for composition**: any combination of seven notes you can construct exists within this system somewhere. Carnatic theory gives you a map of all possible seven-note scales.

## Gamaka: Where Carnatic and Hindustani Differ

While both traditions use ornamentation, Carnatic *gamakas* are more highly codified and are considered **inseparable from the raga's identity**—to an even greater degree than in Hindustani music [41][45].

In Carnatic music, the same scale played without its characteristic gamakas is not the raga—it is merely a scale. The ornamentation IS the raga. Singing or playing a raga entails attention to subtle tonal shades just as embellishments (gamaka) as heard in a proper concert performance.

Key Carnatic gamakas:
- **Gamaka** (oscillation): a rapid oscillation between two notes—the defining sound of Carnatic melody
- **Kampita**: sustained oscillation—like a trill but smoother
- **Meend**: slide between notes—same as in Hindustani
- **Jaru**: a glide that begins from outside the main pitch

The bawu's natural pitch flexibility makes it the most Carnatic-capable instrument in this collection—the constant subtle oscillations of Carnatic melody are achievable through breath modulation.

## Vadi and Samvadi in Carnatic Music

Every raga has a **vadi** (the most prominent and frequently used note) and a **samvadi** (the second most prominent). These two notes are always harmonically related—typically a fourth or fifth apart [42][44][49].

The vadi is the note the melody returns to again and again; the samvadi is the note that provides balance and resolution to the vadi. Together they anchor the raga's emotional world even more strongly than the full scale does.

**For composition**: identify the vadi and samvadi of the raga you're working in. Make your vadi the note you return to after every expressive gesture. Make your samvadi the note where phrases most naturally rest before continuing.

## Key Melakarta Ragas for Wind Players

**Shankarabharanam** (melakarta 29): identical to Western major scale. Used for majestic, confident, dignified music. The Carnatic equivalent of composing in a major mode—but the gamaka vocabulary transforms it completely.

**Kharaharapriya** (melakarta 22): similar to natural minor (Dorian). Balanced, expressive, versatile—one of the most commonly performed melakartas.

**Kalyani** (melakarta 65): uses a raised fourth (♯Ma)—the Carnatic equivalent of Lydian. Bright, expansive, sometimes associated with heroism.

**Natabhairavi** (melakarta 20): uses flat third, flat sixth, flat seventh—the Carnatic "darkest" common scale. Used for deep pathos and karuna (sorrow) rasa.

**Mayamalavagowla** (melakarta 15): uses flat second AND major third—similar to the Double Harmonic / Byzantine scale. A very characteristic Carnatic sound—used for raga alapana teaching.

## The Composition Approach

Carnatic composition begins with a **kriti**—a three-part composed piece with:
- **Pallavi**: the main theme (like a refrain)
- **Anupallavi**: a contrasting middle section
- **Charanam**: the closing section, often more complex

After the kriti is established, improvisation unfolds through:
- **Raga alapana**: unmetered exploration of the raga (equivalent to Hindustani alap)
- **Neraval**: variation on a specific line of the composition
- **Kalpana swaras**: rhythmic improvisation on solfège syllables

**For a solo wind player**: start with the raga alapana—the unmetered, free exploration of the raga's character. Follow the same principles as the Hindustani alap (see file 21): begin low, introduce notes gradually, dwell on vadi, ascend slowly, descend. Add characteristic Carnatic gamakas (oscillations) to every sustained note.

## Composition Exercise: Mayamalavagowla Alapana on Recorder

**Setup**: C Mayamalavagowla = C, D♭, E, F, G, A♭, B, C. Slow. Vadi = G (fifth).

1. Begin on C. Add a slow oscillation (kampita gamaka) between C and D♭—the half-step above home, echoing the Phrygian one-up note.
2. Introduce E (the major third—the bright note that contrasts with D♭'s darkness). The contrast between D♭ and E within this scale is the raga's defining tension.
3. Rise to G (vadi)—rest here. The G feels like a natural plateau after the tension below.
4. Continue to A♭—another flat note, another moment of darkness.
5. Arrive at high C. Descend slowly: B – A♭ – G – F – E – D♭ – C.
6. Apply gamaka (oscillation) to every sustained note—no note should be perfectly static.

**Goal**: the alternation of brightness (E, B) and darkness (D♭, A♭) within the same scale creates a uniquely bittersweet Carnatic emotional quality.

## Gaps and Limitations

- Carnatic music's rhythmic complexity (*tala* cycles, particularly sophisticated ones like Khanda Chapu) is not covered here.
- The 72-melakarta system is a theoretical framework; in practice, only about 20-30 melakartas are regularly used in performance.
- Regional variations (Kerala, Karnataka, Andhra) exist within the Carnatic tradition.
