## Why Listen to Specific Pieces

Reading about composition technique is useful. Hearing it in masterful practice is essential. Each piece listed here demonstrates a specific principle from this guide—and in many cases demonstrates it more clearly than any description can.

---

## Baroque: Phrygian in Practice

### Heinrich Schütz—*St. John Passion* (1666)
**Mode**: Phrygian [6]
**What to listen for**: One of the longest and most sustained uses of Phrygian writing from the Baroque era. The vocal melody's characteristic gesture—leaning on the one-up note, then resolving to the home note—appears throughout. Notice how the mode creates a quality of ancient grief and solemnity without ever feeling gimmicky or exotic. This is Phrygian as a primary language, not a color.

### J.S. Bach—Cantatas preserving Phrygian church melodies (e.g., BWV 76)
**Mode**: Phrygian [6]
**What to listen for**: Bach took old church melodies in Phrygian mode and set them within his elaborate harmonic language. Notice how the Phrygian character of the original melody persists even within complex surrounding harmonies—the one-up note is still there, still doing its characteristic work. A lesson in how modal identity survives harmonic context.

### Thomas Tallis—*Why fum'th in sight* (Psalm 2)
**Mode**: G Phrygian [6][20]
**What to listen for**: Renaissance choral Phrygian at its most direct. The melodic lines move stepwise, with great dignity. Notice how the mode creates a sense of weight and age without any dramatic gesture. Each note feels chosen; the simplicity is itself expressive. Vaughan Williams was so moved by this music that he built a major orchestral work (*Fantasia on a Theme of Thomas Tallis*) from it a century later.

### Georg Philipp Telemann—Concerto for Flute and Recorder in E minor [49]
**What to listen for**: direct Baroque writing for the family of instruments you play. E minor is not Phrygian, but it's closely related, and Telemann's phrase shapes, ornament placements, and melodic contours are directly applicable. Listen especially to how the two instruments share and echo material—a model for how motives can be developed across a piece.

## Renaissance: Pure Modal Melody

### Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina—*Canticum Canticorum* (Five Phrygian motets) [20]
**What to listen for**: Palestrina represents the peak of Renaissance vocal polyphony. His Phrygian melodies are characterized by smooth, predominantly stepwise motion—very few large leaps, and those few are carefully prepared. A lesson in melodic grace: how to be expressive without being dramatic, and how to make every note feel inevitable.

### Tallis—*Lamentations of Jeremiah*
**What to listen for**: deeply modal, deeply expressive, and deeply simple. The melodic language shows how to build a powerful emotional statement with very limited materials. Everything comes from a few characteristic gestures repeated and developed.

## Romantic and Post-Romantic: Phrygian for Beauty

### Vaughan Williams—*Fantasia on a Theme of Thomas Tallis* (1910), G Phrygian [20]
**What to listen for**: one of the most beautiful orchestral uses of Phrygian ever written. Study Vaughan Williams' melodic arcs—the patient, gradual rise, the luminous peak, the quiet resolution. The Phrygian mode here sounds not dark or menacing but ancient, noble, and luminous. This is bittersweet Phrygian beauty at its fullest. Apply the shape of these long arcs to solo wind writing.

### Camille Saint-Saëns—*Bacchanale* from *Samson et Dalila* (1877), A Phrygian [20]
**What to listen for**: fierce, passionate Phrygian—the opposite of the Vaughan Williams. The one-up note is used as a driving, rhythmic force rather than a point of tender yearning. A lesson in how the same mode can produce completely different emotional characters depending on tempo, rhythm, and articulation.

### Claude Debussy—*Syrinx* (1913) for solo flute [50]
**What to listen for**: **the essential study piece for solo woodwind modal playing**. A single flute line, no accompaniment. About three minutes long. Creates an entire emotional world through dynamic shaping, modal ambiguity, and the strategic use of silence. Every player of melodic wind instruments should know this piece. The recorder, low whistle, and bawu are all natural vehicles for the spirit of this music.

## Folk and World: Phrygian in Living Practice

### John Kirkpatrick—"Dust to Dust" (English folk, Locrian) [3][48]
**What to listen for**: **the primary model for beautiful Locrian melody**. Locrian is rare in folk music—this is one of the very few examples that sounds genuinely lovely. Kirkpatrick avoids the worst dissonance by keeping the melody in a single voice, approaching the shrunken fifth (the tritone above home) by step rather than by leap, and never sounding it directly against the home note. This is your compositional model for Locrian.

### Flamenco solo guitar—*Soleares* and *Siguiriyas* forms
**What to listen for**: Phrygian as living tradition. Listen for the characteristic cadential gesture (the one-up note falling home, over and over, with rhythmic drive) and for the *falseta* sections (solo melodic passages). The melodic cells built from the one-up note and home are directly transferable to any wind instrument. Notice how the same half-step relationship can feel both ancient and fiercely immediate.

### Middle Eastern ney (*nay*) improvisations—maqam Bayati
**What to listen for**: the ney (an end-blown reed flute) is the closest acoustic relative of the kaval in this musical tradition. Maqam Bayati is essentially Phrygian. Ney improvisations in Bayati demonstrate sustained, breathing, single-voice Phrygian melody built from characteristic cells—and show how long a player can dwell within the mode's emotional territory without it becoming monotonous. Seek recordings by Turkish or Persian ney masters.

### Irish low whistle—Finbar Furey, Davy Spillane, Michael McGoldrick [41]
**What to listen for**: the inventors and early masters of the modern low whistle tradition. Their slow airs demonstrate how the low D whistle's haunting tone is used for maximum expressive effect in the air form—long, sustained phrases, subtle dynamic shaping, and a relationship with silence that makes each note feel essential. The whistle is not playing a tune; it is speaking.

### Bulgarian and Macedonian kaval recordings—players like Stoyan Velichkov
**What to listen for**: the kaval in its native folk tradition. Listen for the *patter* technique (gentle pulsing of held notes), the movement between registers, and the scale's characteristic half-step intervals in action. The folk melodic vocabulary—short characteristic phrases repeated and varied—is directly applicable to Phrygian composition on your Moldavian instrument.

## Locrian—What Little Exists

Genuinely beautiful Locrian melody in solo wind music is rare. Your primary reference points:

- **Kirkpatrick "Dust to Dust"**: see above—the single most important model [3]
- **Jazz and contemporary music** occasionally approaches Locrian through the half-diminished scale (a closely related collection of notes). Listen for pieces over half-diminished chords in jazz to hear how skilled players navigate similar territory melodically.
- **Your own ears and hands**: for Locrian on these specific instruments, you are largely in unexplored territory. The principles in this guide (single voice, stepwise approach to tense notes, silence as a structural element) are your best guides. What you discover may be genuinely new.

## Gaps and Limitations

- Most of the cited recordings are in the classical and folk Western traditions. Contemporary world music using Phrygian on these specific instruments may exist but is not well-documented in English-language sources.
- NAF recordings in specifically modal traditions are limited; the NAF tradition is primarily oral and improvisational, with little formally documented repertoire.
