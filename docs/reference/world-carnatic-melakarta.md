## What Is Carnatic Music?

Carnatic music is the classical music of South India—older than Hindustani in its current form, more structured, and more composition-oriented. Where Hindustani music prizes individual improvisation and emotional exploration, Carnatic music prizes faithful interpretation of composed works alongside improvisation within strict structural rules [31](https://themusictheoryprofessor.com/analyzing-famous-compositions-with-notable-deceptive-cadences/)[49].

The raga concept is shared between both systems—but how ragas are treated differs significantly.

---

## The Melakarta System: 72 Parent Scales

The most distinctive feature of Carnatic music theory is the **melakarta** system—a mathematically complete set of 72 "parent" scales, from which all other ragas can be derived [44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html)[47].

A melakarta raga is a scale that contains all seven notes in both ascending and descending form—no notes skipped, no different notes on the way up versus down. From these 72 parents, hundreds of derived (*janya*) ragas are created by omitting notes, using different notes on ascent and descent, or adding characteristic motifs.

**The mathematical logic**: the 72 melakarta scales are constructed systematically from the 12 semitones of the octave:
- Sa (root) and Pa (fifth) are always fixed
- Ma (fourth) has two variants: natural or sharp
- The remaining four notes (Ri, Ga, Dha, Ni) each have three variants
- This gives: 2 × 6 × 6 = 72 possible combinations

The 72 melakartas are divided into 12 groups (*chakras*) of 6 ragas each [44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html). The 36 melakartas in the first half use a natural fourth; the 36 in the second half use a sharp fourth.

**What this means for composition**: any combination of seven notes you can construct exists within this system somewhere. Carnatic theory gives you a map of all possible seven-note scales.

## Gamaka: Where Carnatic and Hindustani Differ

While both traditions use ornamentation, Carnatic *gamakas* are more highly codified and are considered **inseparable from the raga's identity**—to an even greater degree than in Hindustani music [41](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_whistle)[45](https://omeka-s.grinnell.edu/s/MusicalInstruments/item/1532).

In Carnatic music, the same scale played without its characteristic gamakas is not the raga—it is merely a scale. The ornamentation IS the raga. Singing or playing a raga entails attention to subtle tonal shades just as embellishments (gamaka) as heard in a proper concert performance.

Key Carnatic gamakas:
- **Gamaka** (oscillation): a rapid oscillation between two notes—the defining sound of Carnatic melody
- **Kampita**: sustained oscillation—like a trill but smoother
- **Meend**: slide between notes—same as in Hindustani
- **Jaru**: a glide that begins from outside the main pitch

The bawu's natural pitch flexibility makes it the most Carnatic-capable instrument in this collection—the constant subtle oscillations of Carnatic melody are achievable through breath modulation.

## Vadi and Samvadi in Carnatic Music

Every raga has a **vadi** (the most prominent and frequently used note) and a **samvadi** (the second most prominent). These two notes are always harmonically related—typically a fourth or fifth apart [42](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaval)[44](https://www.robertsnider.com/kavals/kavalbeginning.html)[49].

The vadi is the note the melody returns to again and again; the samvadi is the note that provides balance and resolution to the vadi. Together they anchor the raga's emotional world even more strongly than the full scale does.

**For composition**: identify the vadi and samvadi of the raga you're working in. Make your vadi the note you return to after every expressive gesture. Make your samvadi the note where phrases most naturally rest before continuing.

## Key Melakarta Ragas for Wind Players

**Shankarabharanam** (melakarta 29): identical to Western major scale. Used for majestic, confident, dignified music. The Carnatic equivalent of composing in a major mode—but the gamaka vocabulary transforms it completely.

**Kharaharapriya** (melakarta 22): similar to natural minor (Dorian). Balanced, expressive, versatile—one of the most commonly performed melakartas.

**Kalyani** (melakarta 65): uses a raised fourth (♯Ma)—the Carnatic equivalent of Lydian. Bright, expansive, sometimes associated with heroism.

**Natabhairavi** (melakarta 20): uses flat third, flat sixth, flat seventh—the Carnatic "darkest" common scale. Used for deep pathos and karuna (sorrow) rasa.

**Mayamalavagowla** (melakarta 15): uses flat second AND major third—similar to the Double Harmonic / Byzantine scale. A very characteristic Carnatic sound—used for raga alapana teaching.

## The Composition Approach

Carnatic composition begins with a **kriti**—a three-part composed piece with:
- **Pallavi**: the main theme (like a refrain)
- **Anupallavi**: a contrasting middle section
- **Charanam**: the closing section, often more complex

After the kriti is established, improvisation unfolds through:
- **Raga alapana**: unmetered exploration of the raga (equivalent to Hindustani alap)
- **Neraval**: variation on a specific line of the composition
- **Kalpana swaras**: rhythmic improvisation on solfège syllables

**For a solo wind player**: start with the raga alapana—the unmetered, free exploration of the raga's character. Follow the same principles as the Hindustani alap (see file 21): begin low, introduce notes gradually, dwell on vadi, ascend slowly, descend. Add characteristic Carnatic gamakas (oscillations) to every sustained note.

## Composition Exercise: Mayamalavagowla Alapana on Recorder

**Setup**: C Mayamalavagowla = C, D♭, E, F, G, A♭, B, C. Slow. Vadi = G (fifth).

1. Begin on C. Add a slow oscillation (kampita gamaka) between C and D♭—the half-step above home, echoing the Phrygian one-up note.
2. Introduce E (the major third—the bright note that contrasts with D♭'s darkness). The contrast between D♭ and E within this scale is the raga's defining tension.
3. Rise to G (vadi)—rest here. The G feels like a natural plateau after the tension below.
4. Continue to A♭—another flat note, another moment of darkness.
5. Arrive at high C. Descend slowly: B – A♭ – G – F – E – D♭ – C.
6. Apply gamaka (oscillation) to every sustained note—no note should be perfectly static.

**Goal**: the alternation of brightness (E, B) and darkness (D♭, A♭) within the same scale creates a uniquely bittersweet Carnatic emotional quality.

## Specific Melakarta Ragas for Wind Players: Expanded

Beyond Mayamalavagowla, several melakarta ragas are particularly well-suited to the instruments in this collection:

### Natabhairavi (Melakarta 20)
**Scale**: C – D – E♭ – F – G – A♭ – B♭ (equivalent to natural minor / Aeolian)
The Western natural minor scale appears as a melakarta raga—demonstrating the structural completeness of the 72-scale system. In Carnatic performance, Natabhairavi has gamaka and characteristic phrases completely different from Western Aeolian, despite sharing the same pitch set.

### Harikambhoji (Melakarta 28)
**Scale**: C – D – E – F – G – A – B♭ (equivalent to Mixolydian)
The Western Mixolydian scale appears here as a Carnatic melakarta. The raga built on this scale is associated with an affectionate, sweet quality—very different from the folk-outdoor quality of Western Mixolydian, demonstrating how the same pitches carry different emotional frameworks.

### Kalyani (Melakarta 65)
**Scale**: C – D – E – F♯ – G – A – B (equivalent to Lydian)
One of the most important Carnatic ragas. The raised fourth (F♯) gives Kalyani a bright, expansive quality equivalent to Lydian's floating character in Western theory. Often performed in the evening; associated with peace, serenity, and devotion.

### Shankarabharanam (Melakarta 29)
**Scale**: C – D – E – F – G – A – B (equivalent to Ionian / major)
The Western major scale as a Carnatic melakarta. Called "the king of ragas" by some—the most harmonically complete and stable raga. Demonstrates that even the most familiar Western scale becomes something different when performed with Carnatic gamaka vocabulary.

## Composing in Carnatic Style for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### How Carnatic Composition Differs from Hindustani

Both Hindustani and Carnatic are Indian classical music—but their approaches to composition differ significantly:

- **Hindustani**: more improvisational; the alap is the core creative act; the composed piece (*gat*) is a framework for improvisation
- **Carnatic**: more composition-oriented; the *kriti* (composed devotional song) is the central form; improvisation (*manodharma*) occurs within highly structured formats

For solo wind players without accompaniment, Carnatic compositional logic translates most naturally as: **compose the melody completely first, then learn to ornament it according to the raga's gamaka vocabulary.**

### Gamaka Is the Melody

The single most important principle of Carnatic music for composers:

In Carnatic music, *gamaka is not added to the melody—gamaka IS the melody.*

A sequence of written notes in a Carnatic raga is merely a skeleton. The actual melody exists only when those notes are played with their raga's characteristic gamaka. The same written sequence in two different ragas will sound completely different because the gamaka differs.

**What this means practically**: you cannot compose in Carnatic style by writing a melody and then adding ornaments. You must begin with the gamaka vocabulary of the raga and build the melody from that. Study recordings of the raga's characteristic phrases—not as notes, but as complete gamaka gestures—and learn those gestures first.

### Gamaka Types in Carnatic Practice (Applied to Wind)

**Kampita**: a sustained oscillation around the main note. On bawu: pitch-bend above and below the center pitch in a regular, slow oscillation. On kaval: embouchure oscillation. The rate and width of the oscillation varies by raga and context—some ragas require wide, slow kampita; others a tight, fast trembling.

**Jaru**: a glide that begins outside the normal scale pitch and arrives at the main note. On bawu: begin the pitch bend from slightly above or below the target note, sliding to its center. This is the Carnatic equivalent of *meend* in Hindustani and the *slide* in Celtic music—the note arrived at rather than landed on.

**Pratyahata**: a repeated downward deflection of a note, briefly touching the note below before returning. On bawu: bend the pitch slightly downward from the center, return, repeat—a rapid repeated downward dip. On whistle: a rapid *tap* ornament repeated.

**Nokku**: a quick touch of the lower neighboring note before the main note. On any instrument: a brief grace note from below. Similar to the appoggiatura but from below rather than above.

**Odukkal**: a glide upward from a note while changing the vowel sound (in vocal music)—translated to wind: a simultaneous pitch-bend and breath-pressure increase creating a rising inflection on the note without changing fingering.

### Composing in Raga Mayamalavagowla: Step-by-Step

Mayamalavagowla is the first raga taught in Carnatic music—it contains the Double Harmonic scale structure and has a clear, open character that makes its gamaka vocabulary accessible to beginners.

**Scale (from C as Sa)**: C – D♭ – E – F – G – A♭ – B – C

The two augmented seconds: D♭ → E (between second and third) and A♭ → B (between sixth and seventh). These two leaps are the structural signature of the raga.

**The arohana and avarohana**:
- Ascending (*arohana*): C – D♭ – E – F – G – A♭ – B – C (all notes, ascending)
- Descending (*avarohana*): C – B – A♭ – G – F – E – D♭ – C (all notes, descending)
- In Mayamalavagowla the arohana and avarohana are the same—all seven notes in both directions. This is unusual among Carnatic ragas and makes it beginner-accessible.

**Step-by-step alap (Carnatic *alapana*)**:

1. **Establish Sa (C)**: play C alone. The establishment of Sa is the same in Carnatic as in Hindustani—an announcement of the tonal world.

2. **The characteristic gesture of the lower tetrachord**: C – D♭ – E – F – E – D♭ – C. This phrase moves through the augmented second (D♭ → E) and returns. The jaru approach to D♭ (from below): begin slightly below D♭ and arrive at it; then leap to E cleanly.

3. **The phrase with kampita**: hold E (Ga) with a slow kampita—gentle oscillation. In Mayamalavagowla, E is not as dramatically emphasized as in Bhairavi, but it should breathe with its own quality.

4. **The middle range phrase**: F – G – A♭ – B – C (high). The second augmented second (A♭ → B) appears in this ascent. Approach A♭ with a jaru from below; leap to B clearly. The B is the leading tone—it presses upward toward high C.

5. **Descending with ornamentation**: from high C: C – B – A♭ (kampita on A♭ briefly) – G – F – E – D♭ – C. The descent should be slower than the ascent. Let each note settle before moving.

6. **Characteristic Carnatic ornamental phrase**: in Mayamalavagowla, a characteristic phrase involves a series of nokku figures (brief lower-neighbor grace notes) on successive scale degrees: each of D♭, E, F, G, A♭, B is approached with a brief lower-grace before landing. This creates the flowing, melismatic quality of Carnatic melody.

7. **Close**: C – D♭ – C (the Sa approached from Re♭—the Phrygian quality of Mayamalavagowla present even at the close). End on Sa.

### Eliciting Emotion in Carnatic Style

**Sacred devotion**: Carnatic music is explicitly devotional—originally performed as an offering to deities, not as entertainment. Approach composition in Carnatic style with this intention: not "I am composing a beautiful melody" but "I am offering this sound." The difference in intention produces a difference in the music.

**The quality of space**: Carnatic composition uses silence as deliberately as Indian classical in general. Between each alapana phrase, a silence equal to the phrase's length allows the gamaka to resonate and the listener to receive it.

**Speed as emotional expression**: the transition from alap (no pulse) to jor (emerging pulse) to jhala (fast, climactic) represents an emotional arc from depth to intensity. For solo wind compositions that do not need to follow the concert structure, still include this arc: begin without rhythm, develop with increasing pulse, arrive at a rhythmically energetic peak, then slow and settle for the close.

### Common Beginner Mistakes in Carnatic Style

**Playing gamaka as if it were added afterward**: the most fundamental error. Gamaka must be part of the melodic conception from the start. Before playing a single phrase, know which gamaka each note in the phrase receives.

**Uniform note duration**: in Western classical music, note values are indicated in the score. In Carnatic alap, the same note may be held for different durations in different phrases depending on its emotional weight in that phrase. The *vadi* receives more time than any other note; the non-characteristic notes receive less. Vary duration according to emotional importance, not according to a fixed rhythmic value.

**Treating all seven notes equally**: the vadi and samvadi are structurally privileged. Other notes are connective tissue. A correctly proportioned Carnatic phrase spends the most time on the vadi, uses the other notes primarily to approach and leave the vadi, and treats the non-characteristic notes as passing material.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Carnatic Style

**Devotional depth** (*bhakti* rasa): slow tempo; the vadi (most important note) dwelt upon for long periods—held, oscillated (kampita), approached from below (jaru), and released slowly; much silence between phrases; the alap's patience maintained throughout the composition; the emotional register is not intense devotion but quiet, sustained presence before something larger.

**Wonder** (*adbhuta* rasa): the scale's unusual intervals (augmented seconds in Mayamalavagowla, unusual tetrachords in other ragas) as the expressive vehicle; the leap of the augmented second arrived at slowly from below and held; the neutral or unusual intervals that give the raga its specific character emphasized rather than smoothed over; a sense of musical discovery.

**Sorrow** (*karuna* rasa): in ragas like Bhairavi (scale equivalent to Phrygian): slow; the vadi of Bhairavi (E♭—the flat third from C) as the emotional center; phrases that return to E♭ repeatedly; the gamaka on E♭ as andolan (slow oscillation); descending phrases that settle on the tonic after touching each sorrow-inflected note.

**Heroic strength** (*veera* rasa): in ragas with strong ascending patterns; moderate-fast tempo for the jor section; phrases that ascend through the scale decisively, with clear rhythmic articulation; the gamaka becomes gamak (forceful, wide oscillation) rather than the gentle andolan of devotion; arrival on the tonic is strong and declarative.

**Serene peace** (*shanta* rasa): very slow; extremely sparse; the tonic held for extended periods as the entire first phrase; new notes introduced one by one with great patience; the emotional register is stillness rather than contemplation—the music has reached a state beyond seeking.
