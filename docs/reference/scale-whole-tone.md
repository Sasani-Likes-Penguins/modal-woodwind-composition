## Six-Tone Symmetrical Scale (Whole-Tone Scale)

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – 3 – ♯4 – ♯5 – ♯6 (all whole steps)

**C Whole-Tone**: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – C

The whole-tone scale has no half-steps—every interval is exactly a whole step [83][89]. This perfect symmetry means:
- All notes are harmonically equal—there is no "home" note, no leading tone, no anchor
- Any note can serve as the starting point—the scale sounds identical from any position
- The tritone is everywhere (every pair of notes three steps apart is a tritone)

**Emotional character**: completely ambiguous, floating, disorienting, dreamlike. Debussy used it in "Voiles" and "Le vent dans la plaine" for exactly this quality of frictionless drift [83][89]. There is no gravity in the whole-tone scale—no note wants to go anywhere.

**Compositional use**: the whole-tone scale is a color, not a key. Use brief whole-tone passages within modal compositions for moments of maximum ambiguity—the tonal anchor disappears temporarily. The return from whole-tone to a modal scale (Phrygian, Dorian) is dramatic precisely because the mode's structure is so vivid after the formlessness.

**Exercise on recorder**: C whole-tone. Ascend slowly: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – C. At no point does the melody feel like it's arrived anywhere. This is the scale's entire personality. Then play one E Phrygian note (E) immediately after. The contrast is jarring—suddenly there is gravity again.
