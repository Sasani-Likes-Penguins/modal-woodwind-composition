## What Is It?

The whole-tone scale is a six-note scale in which every consecutive interval is exactly a whole step—no half-steps anywhere in the scale. This perfect symmetry produces a scale with no leading tone, no stable tonic-dominant relationship, no note that "wants" to go anywhere. The result is a scale of total ambiguity: floating, directionless, and profoundly atmospheric.

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – 3 – ♯4 – ♯5 – ♯6

**C Whole-Tone**: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – C

There are only two whole-tone scales in Western equal temperament:
- Scale 1: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ (and C again)
- Scale 2: C♯ – D♯ – F – G – A – B (and C♯ again)

Every note is in one of these two scales. Because the scale is perfectly symmetrical, it sounds identical from any starting point—C whole-tone and D whole-tone use the same six notes; only the labeled starting point changes.

## The Interval Structure

All intervals are equal: Whole – Whole – Whole – Whole – Whole – (Whole back to octave)

| Note (from C) | Interval above C |
|--------------|-----------------|
| D | Major second (2 half-steps) |
| E | Major third (4 half-steps) |
| F♯ | Augmented fourth / tritone (6 half-steps) |
| G♯ | Augmented fifth (8 half-steps) |
| A♯ | Augmented sixth / minor seventh (10 half-steps) |
| C (octave) | Octave (12 half-steps) |

The scale contains: one tritone (C–F♯), one augmented fifth (C–G♯), and multiple augmented seconds and thirds. All of these are dissonant intervals, which is why the whole-tone scale has no stable home note—every note is in an equally unstable relationship with every other note.

---

## Mathematical Properties: The Symmetric Scale

The whole-tone scale has a special mathematical property: it is one of the most symmetrical pitch collections possible. Because every interval is equal, the scale has six-fold rotational symmetry—rotating it by any number of whole steps produces the same collection. This symmetry is the structural basis of its directionlessness.

Compare this to the major scale, which has no such symmetry: its pattern (W–W–H–W–W–W–H) creates a strong hierarchy of notes (tonic, dominant, subdominant, etc.). The whole-tone scale's pattern (W–W–W–W–W–W) creates complete equality among all notes—which means no note is more "home" than any other.

## Why It Sounds Directionless

In virtually all Western music, certain structural features create the sense of direction—the feeling that the music is "going somewhere":

- **The leading tone**: a half-step below the octave that urgently wants to rise. The whole-tone scale has no leading tone.
- **The perfect fifth**: the most stable interval after the octave, giving music a harmonic anchor. The whole-tone scale's fifth is augmented (G♯ above C rather than G)—unstable.
- **The perfect fourth**: another stable interval. The whole-tone scale's fourth is augmented (F♯ above C)—a tritone.
- **Half-steps**: in any standard scale, half-steps create push and pull between adjacent notes. The whole-tone scale has no half-steps.

Strip away all of these features and what remains is a scale that simply *is*—without wanting to go anywhere or come from anywhere. This is the whole-tone scale.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Dreamlike floating | The absence of harmonic direction creates the quality of drifting through sound |
| Impressionistic shimmer | The tritones and augmented intervals create a shimmering, iridescent effect |
| Disorientation (gentle) | The listener finds no landmarks; the experience is pleasantly lost rather than anxiously lost |
| Underwater or in fog | Debussy's characteristic metaphors for the whole-tone sound: fluid, visual, atmospheric |
| Ancient magic | Used in film and classical music for supernatural, mystical, or timeless scenes |

The whole-tone scale is one of the few scales in this repository that does not primarily evoke an emotion—it evokes a *state of consciousness*. It is the sound of hovering between waking and sleep, between here and somewhere else.

## Debussy and the Whole-Tone Scale

Claude Debussy (1862–1918) is the composer most associated with the whole-tone scale, and his use of it is the primary model for composers working with it today. He did not invent it—it appears in Glinka, Berlioz, and Liszt before him—but he developed it into a systematic compositional language.

**Key Debussy whole-tone works**:

- ***"Voiles"*** (Sails, Preludes for Piano Book 1, No. 2, 1910): the most purely whole-tone piece Debussy wrote; almost entirely in whole-tone from beginning to end, with a single brief contrasting section in pentatonic. The piece evokes sails reflected in still water—motionless movement, shimmer without destination. Essential listening.

- ***"Le vent dans la plaine"*** (The Wind in the Plain, Preludes Book 1, No. 3): uses whole-tone scales for the sweeping, formless quality of wind across flat landscape

- ***La Mer*** (The Sea, 1905): uses whole-tone passages to evoke the unpredictable, shimmering surface of the sea; the third movement "Dialogue of the Wind and the Sea" contains extensive whole-tone writing

- ***Pelléas et Mélisande*** (opera, 1902): whole-tone passages for supernatural or dreamlike dramatic moments

- ***"Nuages"*** (Clouds, from Nocturnes for Orchestra, 1899): whole-tone scales used for the indefinite, slowly shifting character of clouds

Debussy's technique was to use the whole-tone scale not as a "key" but as a *texture*—a brief atmospheric passage before returning to modal or tonal material. The contrast between the whole-tone and the surrounding modal or diatonic passages is itself expressive: the whole-tone is an opening into an other world, and the return from it makes the modal world feel newly substantial.

---

## Other Composers and Uses

- **Liszt**—*Faust Symphony* (1857): brief whole-tone passages in the Mephisto section for supernatural character; one of the earliest classical uses
- **Rimsky-Korsakov**—*The Golden Cockerel* (1907): whole-tone scales for the magical Shemakha Queen character
- **Ravel**—uses whole-tone scales more sparingly than Debussy but with similar atmospheric intent
- **Bartók**—whole-tone scales appear in some of his more dissonant passages and in the chromatic language of his string quartets
- **Film music**: the whole-tone scale has become a standard film-music shorthand for magical, supernatural, or dreamlike scenes; used by Bernard Herrmann, John Williams, and countless others in specific "dream" or "flashback" sequence moments
- **Video game music**: whole-tone passages signal unreality, magic, or mystery in RPG and fantasy game soundtracks

## Instrument Access

The whole-tone scale requires chromatic capability—all the raised degrees (F♯, G♯, A♯) need cross-fingerings on recorder and whistle.

| Instrument | Ease | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Bawu | Excellent | The floating, shimmering quality of the whole-tone scale is particularly natural on bawu; pitch bending between whole-tone notes creates a particularly liquid sound |
| Alto recorder | Good | All notes available; multiple cross-fingerings but all standard |
| Low D whistle | Moderate | D whole-tone (D–E–F♯–G♯–A♯–C): C natural is easy; F♯ and G♯ require cross-fingering |
| Kaval | Good | Available; the kaval's tone suits whole-tone passages well |
| NAF | Limited | F♯ (raised fourth) is the limit of what most NAFs can reach via cross-fingering; G♯ and A♯ generally unavailable |

## Composing with the Whole-Tone Scale

**Use it as a parenthesis, not a key**: the whole-tone scale works best when it appears briefly within a piece otherwise in a defined mode—Phrygian, Dorian, a maqam, etc. The contrast between the modal world and the whole-tone "opening" makes both more vivid.

**Slow, smooth movement**: because there is no direction in the whole-tone scale, fast passages through it sound agitated rather than floating. Move slowly, allow each note to sound fully before the next arrives.

**Return to something concrete**: after a whole-tone passage, returning to a modal scale (with its half-steps and tonal hierarchy) creates a dramatic sense of landing—the listener returns from the floating world with a heightened awareness of the modal world's structure.

**Parallel motion**: Debussy often moved entire melodic figures in parallel whole-tone steps—the same four-note pattern starting a whole step higher, then higher again. This parallel motion reinforces the scale's symmetrical quality and creates the shimmering effect he associated with water and light.

**Silence before and after**: the whole-tone passage is most effective when approached from silence and released into silence before the next phrase begins. The suspension of the scale needs space—before, during, and after.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu (C Whole-Tone)
**C Whole-Tone**: C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – C

*This exercise places the whole-tone scale within a Phrygian context—using it as a brief "other world" opening:*

1. Play a short E Phrygian phrase: E – F – G – A – B. Settle quietly on E.
2. *Enter the whole-tone world:* from E, begin the whole-tone scale from the nearest whole-tone starting point: E – F♯ – G♯ – A♯ – C – D – E. Play this ascending very slowly—one note per two beats, each note held for its full value. Do not rush.
3. At the high E: silence for four beats. The whole-tone world dissolves into silence.
4. Return: E Phrygian phrase again, but softer. The mode has the same notes but feels different after the whole-tone passage—more substantial, more grounded.

**Goal**: music in which the whole-tone scale functions as a window into another state of consciousness—opened briefly, then closed, leaving the modal world feeling newly real.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in the Whole-Tone Scale

**Frictionless floating** (the core affect): slow tempo; phrases that ascend through the scale evenly, giving each note equal time; no note treated as more important than any other—all are equal; silence between phrases of equal length to the phrases themselves; the music has no direction, no gravity, no urgency; pure hovering.

**Dreaming**: treat the whole-tone scale as an ambient texture rather than a melody—a wash of sound rather than a statement; use sustained notes rather than moving phrases; each phrase a different note held for many beats; the intervals between sustained notes always the same (a whole step), making every movement feel equally weightless.

**Supernatural, magical atmosphere**: the whole-tone scale as a brief interlude within a modal composition; the moment the stable mode disappears and the music enters a timeless, directionless space; the return to the modal world making the mode sound sharply defined by contrast.

**Impressionist shimmer**: moderate tempo; rapid parallel motion—the same phrase starting a whole step higher, then another whole step higher, cascading upward; Debussy's technique of shimmer through parallel whole-tone movement; the bawu's continuous resonance or the recorder's sustained tone suits this character.

---

## Common Beginner Mistakes in the Whole-Tone Scale

**Treating it as a key to compose in**: the whole-tone scale has no home note, no leading tone, no stable intervals. Composing an entire piece "in" the whole-tone scale produces music with no sense of arrival, departure, or return—which may be the intention for very brief atmospheric passages, but makes longer compositions feel adrift. Use it as a color or transition rather than a home.

**Playing it at the same pace as normal scales**: the whole-tone scale's directionlessness becomes most apparent when each note is given equal time and equal dynamic. Playing it quickly in passing as a scale run removes the floating quality and replaces it with chromaticism. Slow down—give the equality of the intervals time to register.

**Not having a plan for when to leave it**: entering the whole-tone scale is easy; knowing when and how to return to a modal world is the compositional challenge. Plan the exit before the entry. The whole-tone passage should resolve to a specific modal note with clear intent—the modal note arriving as a reestablishment of gravity after the weightlessness.
