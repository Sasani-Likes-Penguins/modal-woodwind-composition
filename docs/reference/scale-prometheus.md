## What Is It?

The Prometheus scale is a six-note scale (hexatonic) associated with the late compositions of Alexander Scriabin (1872–1915), particularly his orchestral work *Prometheus: The Poem of Fire* (1910). Scriabin used a specific six-note chord—the "mystic chord"—as the harmonic basis for his late works, and the Prometheus scale is essentially that chord arranged as a scale.

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – 3 – ♯4 – 6 – ♭7 (six notes)

**C Prometheus**: C – D – E – F♯ – A – B♭ – C

Note: the perfect fifth (G) is omitted. This absence of the fifth is one of the scale's most distinctive features—without the fifth, there is no stable harmonic anchor. The scale floats without the grounding that the perfect fifth normally provides.

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from C) | Distance from previous |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 | C |—|
| 2 | D | Whole step |
| 3 | E | Whole step |
| ♯4 | F♯ | Whole step (tritone above C) |
|—| G | *Omitted* |
| 6 | A | Minor third |
| ♭7 | B♭ | Whole step |
| 8 | C | Whole step |

The first four notes (C–D–E–F♯) are a whole-tone sequence—identical to the beginning of the [Whole-Tone](scale-whole-tone.md) scale. Then a minor third leap to A, then B♭. The scale moves through whole-tone ambiguity and then suddenly arrives at a note (A) that is outside the whole-tone series, creating a moment of unexpected color.

## The Mystic Chord Connection

Scriabin's "mystic chord" (also called the "Prometheus chord") is: C – F♯ – B♭ – E – A – D

Stacked in thirds from the bottom, the notes are C, E, F♯, A, B♭, D—identical to the Prometheus scale. Scriabin used this chord as a substitute for the traditional dominant seventh chord, creating harmonic motion without conventional resolution. The chord and scale are two expressions of the same pitch collection.

Scriabin's mystic chord consists of notes derived from the overtone series. He believed this chord represented a spiritual rather than physical harmonic foundation—a sound that pointed "upward" toward transcendence rather than grounding itself in earthly harmonic function.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Mystical, cosmic suspension | No perfect fifth means no conventional grounding; the scale hovers |
| Spiritual transcendence | Scriabin's explicit intention: music pointing beyond ordinary consciousness |
| Unresolved wonder | Similar to Lydian's floating quality but with even less harmonic anchor |
| Slightly ominous beauty | The tritone (C–F♯) and the absence of G create a beautiful instability |

The Prometheus scale sounds like standing at the threshold of something vast and formless—not threatening, but utterly beyond ordinary musical categories. It is the most deliberately transcendent scale in this repository.

## Scriabin: The Composer and the Vision

Alexander Scriabin began his career as a Chopin-influenced Romantic pianist-composer, then underwent a dramatic transformation in the 1900s, developing a unique late style that abandoned traditional tonality in favor of a language built around the mystic chord. He was deeply influenced by Theosophy (a spiritual movement that sought a universal synthesis of science, philosophy, and religion) and believed music could be a vehicle for spiritual evolution.

His late works use the Prometheus scale and related pitch collections throughout:

- **Piano Sonata No. 7** (*White Mass*, 1911): mystic chord and Prometheus scale as primary pitch material
- **Piano Sonata No. 9** (*Black Mass*, 1913): similar pitch language, darker affect
- **Piano Sonata No. 10** (1913): the scale used for shimmering, crystalline passages
- ***Prometheus: The Poem of Fire*** (Op. 60, 1910): the orchestral work that gives the scale its name; originally scored for orchestra, piano, organ, chorus, and a "clavier à lumières" (light keyboard) that was supposed to project colors corresponding to the harmonies onto the concert hall walls; Scriabin believed sound and light corresponded systematically and that a "total artwork" should address multiple senses simultaneously
- ***Le Poème de l'extase*** (Poem of Ecstasy, 1908): a transitional work between Scriabin's Romantic and late periods

Scriabin died at 43, before completing his planned magnum opus—the *Mysterium*, a seven-day "gesamtkunstwerk" (total artwork) that he believed would transform human consciousness and end the current cycle of human history. The Prometheus scale was intended as the musical foundation of this unrealized work.

## Instrument Access

The Prometheus scale is relatively accessible on chromatic instruments—only six notes, and the ♯4 (F♯) is a standard chromatic note.

| Instrument | Ease | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Alto recorder | Good | All six notes available; F♯ via cross-fingering in C Prometheus |
| Bawu | Very good | The absence of G means one fewer note to worry about; pitch bending adds Scriabin-like inflection |
| Low D whistle | Moderate | D Prometheus (D–E–F♯–G♯–B–C): G♯ requires cross-fingering |
| Kaval | Good | Available throughout the range |
| NAF | Limited | ♯4 not native; B♭ also non-standard |

## Composing with the Prometheus Scale

**Embrace the lack of the fifth**: the absence of G (in C Prometheus) is the most important compositional feature. Do not try to imply G by surrounding it; instead, use the scale's notes exactly as given. The hovering quality comes precisely from the absence of the grounding fifth.

**The tritone as a floating point**: C and F♯ form a tritone—the most unstable interval in Western music. But in the Prometheus scale, this tritone is not between the fourth and seventh (where it would urgently want to resolve) but between the first and fourth—between the home note and the raised fourth. The tritone floats rather than resolves, echoing the [Lydian](mode-lydian.md) raised-fourth quality.

**The A as a surprise**: after the whole-tone sequence C–D–E–F♯ (four notes a whole step apart), the leap of a minor third to A is the one "unexpected" move in the scale. Use A as the scale's emotional turning point—the note that breaks the whole-tone pattern and gives the scale its specific identity.

**Sparse, widely spaced melody**: Scriabin's own piano textures were often highly ornamented and dense, but for solo wind instruments, the Prometheus scale works best as a spare, meditative approach—few notes, much silence, each note given space to sound before the next.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu (C Prometheus)
**C Prometheus**: C – D – E – F♯ – A – B♭ – C

1. Begin on C. Hold for four beats. Silence.
2. Ascend through the whole-tone portion: C – D – E – F♯. Each step is equal—no direction emerges. Float.
3. After F♯, silence for two beats. Then: A. The leap of a minor third from F♯ to A is the scale's emotional pivot—unexpected after the whole-tone equality. Hold A.
4. Step down: A – B♭. The flat seventh arrives. Then: B♭ – C (home).
5. Second phrase: begin on F♯ (the tritone above C). Descend: F♯ – E – D – C. The descent through the whole-tone portion arrives home, but home feels different after the floating journey.

**Goal**: music that creates the impression of existing outside of time—the Prometheus scale has no strong directional pull, so phrases seem to hover rather than move.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in the Prometheus Scale

**Mystical cosmic suspension**: the scale's absence of a perfect fifth means the music has no conventional harmonic anchor—it hovers. To elicit this specifically: begin with the whole-tone ascent (C – D – E – F♯), holding each note for 2–3 beats with silence between; when A arrives (the note that breaks the whole-tone pattern), hold it even longer—this is the scale's one moment of harmonic color; the flat seventh (B♭) as a phrase ending that opens rather than closes.

**Spiritual transcendence** (Scriabin's intention): slow tempo; each note treated as a separate contemplative event rather than part of a melodic line; the raised fourth (F♯) as the center of gravity—the note the music keeps returning to; the whole-tone character of most of the scale creating frictionless motion toward the F♯.

**The mystic chord in melodic form**: Scriabin's mystic chord (C – F♯ – B♭ – E – A – D) can be arpeggio'd melodically as a Prometheus scale passage—ascending through the notes in order. This translates the chord's harmonic hovering into melodic time.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in the Prometheus Scale

**Missing the sixth note (A)**: the Prometheus scale's structural interest depends on the A—the single note that breaks the whole-tone sequence and gives the scale its identity. A Prometheus melody that stays only in the whole-tone notes (C – D – E – F♯ – [skip A] – B♭) is just a whole-tone melody with an added flat seventh. The A is essential; use it as the scale's emotional pivot.

**Treating it as a substitute for Lydian Dominant**: Lydian Dominant (C – D – E – F♯ – G – A – B♭) contains a perfect fifth (G); Prometheus (C – D – E – F♯ – A – B♭) does not. This absence of G is the entire difference in emotional character. Prometheus floats more completely because there is nothing stable in the fifth-degree position. Do not accidentally use G in a Prometheus passage.

**Using it too long**: like the Enigmatic scale, the Prometheus scale works best as a concentrated event—a passage of cosmic suspension within a longer modal composition. Extended use loses the hovering quality as the scale's sound becomes normalized.
