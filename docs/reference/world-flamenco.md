## What Makes Flamenco a Modal System?

Flamenco is not merely music that uses the Phrygian mode. It is a complete musical language with its own modal theory, its own characteristic scales, its own approach to time, and its own way of expressing emotion that is distinct from every other tradition in this guide.

Flamenco has preserved the Dorian or Phrygian mode in ways that Western classical music did not. Three elements define whether something is genuinely flamenco: a flamenco modal tonality, the *compás* (rhythm), and the performer's identity within the tradition [1][9]. For a composer without that cultural background, the first element—the modal language—is what can be most fruitfully studied and applied.

---

## The Flamenco Mode: Phrygian Dominant

The primary flamenco scale is not pure Phrygian. It is what theorists call **Phrygian Dominant**—Phrygian with a raised third degree [1][8][4]:

**E Phrygian Dominant**: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E

Compare to pure E Phrygian: E – F – G – A – B – C – D – E

The G♯ (raised third) is what gives flamenco its distinctive fire. The flamenco version of the Phrygian mode contains two frequent alterations in the 7th and more often the 3rd degree of the scale: if the scale is played in E Phrygian, G and D can be sharp.

The result is a scale with:
- The half-step tension of Phrygian (F immediately above E)
- The brightness and passion of a major third (G♯)
- The augmented second between F and G♯ (one-and-a-half steps)—the same interval that appears in Hijaz/Hicaz maqam

This scale is used liberally in flamenco music. It is also colloquially called the "Hava Nagila scale," due to its use in that well-known song. The connection between flamenco and Jewish music through shared Andalusian history is real and direct.

## The Andalusian Cadence: The Engine of Flamenco

The most recognizable harmonic gesture in flamenco is the **Andalusian cadence**—a descending four-chord sequence that defines the harmonic feel of most traditional flamenco forms [7][5]:

**In E Phrygian**: Am – G – F – E

Each chord descends one step. The bass moves: A – G – F – E. The final chord (E major, or E with a flat ninth: E–F) is the home chord—a major chord built on the Phrygian tonic, which creates the characteristic flamenco tension between major brightness and Phrygian darkness.

The Andalusian cadence is a descending four-chord progression central to flamenco music, structured as iv–III–II–I with respect to the Phrygian mode. It creates a poignant, stepwise descent in the bass line evoking emotional depth and tension resolution that defines many traditional Spanish musical forms.

**For solo wind players**: the Andalusian cadence as a melodic gesture means descending through the scale—A–G–F–E—with the E arriving as a point of power and rest. This descending tetrachord (four-note falling line) is the backbone of flamenco melody.

## The Palos: Flamenco Forms as Modal Contexts

The *palos* are the different styles or forms of flamenco, each with its own compás (rhythmic cycle), emotional character, and modal association [4][6][9]:

| Palo | Mode | Compás | Character |
|------|------|--------|-----------|
| Soleá | E Phrygian | 12-beat, accents on 3,6,8,10,12 | Deep, serious, the "mother form" |
| Bulerías | A Phrygian | 12-beat, fast | Fierce, passionate, rhythmically complex |
| Siguiriyas | A Phrygian | 12-beat variant | The deepest grief in flamenco—*cante jondo* |
| Alegrías | Major (E major) | 12-beat | Bright, celebratory |
| Farruca | Minor | 4/4 | Dark, controlled, masculine |
| Granaina | B Phrygian | Free rhythm | High, introspective |
| Taranta | F♯ Phrygian | Free rhythm | Melancholic, from mining regions |
| Fandango | Bimodal | 3/4 | Opens Phrygian, sings in major |

The choice of key affects the entire character of the palos. Bulerías: usually in A Phrygian. Soleá: typically in E Phrygian. Granaina: played in B Phrygian, creating a higher, brighter sound. Seguiriyas: often in A Phrygian, with a distinctive deep, tragic character.

## Cante Jondo: The Deep Song

*Cante jondo* ("deep song") is the name for the most serious and emotionally profound flamenco forms—particularly siguiriyas, soleá, and related styles. The Andalusian cadence emerged through its integration into cante jondo, the raw and emotive "deep song" style that emphasized themes of sorrow and longing.

For composition: *cante jondo* represents Phrygian at its most unadorned and powerful. No flourish for its own sake; every note carrying emotional weight. The melodic line often descends, returns to the tonic, and descends again—like someone returning again and again to a grief that does not resolve.

## The Falseta: The Melody Unit

A *falseta* is a short melodic phrase—the instrumental unit of flamenco composition [9]. A flamenco guitar recital consists of many falsetas strung together, each complete in itself, separated by rhythmic strumming patterns (*rasgueado*).

For solo wind players: the falseta is the exact equivalent of the seed phrase (motive) discussed throughout this guide. Each falseta:
- Is 4–8 bars long
- Contains the characteristic Phrygian cadence gesture (descending toward E)
- Has its own emotional arc
- Is repeated, varied, or followed by a contrasting falseta

**Composing flamenco-style on wind**: write a series of falsetas in E Phrygian Dominant. Each begins differently but ends with the same descending approach to E (the Andalusian cadence in the melody). Between falsetas, breathe—a moment of stillness before the next phrase.

## The Bimodal Character: Major/Minor Mixture

One of flamenco's most characteristic and emotionally complex features is its **bimodal** mixing of Phrygian and major [4][6]:

Fandangos and derivative palos like malagueñas, tarantulas and cartageneras are bimodal: guitar introductions are in Phrygian mode whereas the singing develops in a major key, modulating to Phrygian at the end of the text.

For melodic composition: begin a phrase in Phrygian (F natural, the half-step above E), then briefly shift to major (F♯, G♯)—the same notes but suddenly brighter—then return to Phrygian darkness. This major/minor mixture is the source of flamenco's characteristic quality of simultaneous joy and grief.

## Historical Roots: Moorish, Jewish, Romani, Byzantine

Flamenco did not emerge from one tradition. The Jews were an important group in al-Andalus, able to maintain their own traditions under a culture of religious tolerance fostered by the Moorish rulers. Certain flamenco palos like the Peteneras and saetas have been attributed a direct Jewish origin.

Manuel de Falla's theory links the melismatic forms and the Phrygian mode in flamenco to the Visigothic Mozarabic rite, which was strongly influenced by Byzantium.

This means flamenco's modal language connects directly to the Byzantine modes (file 27), the Arabic maqam (file 18), the Turkish makam (file 19), and the Jewish modal system (file 29). The Phrygian Dominant scale is the convergence point of all these traditions in Andalusia.

## Composition Exercise: Soleá Falseta on Low D Whistle

**Setup**: E Phrygian Dominant on low D whistle. Slow, heavy, serious tempo.

**Rules following soleá character**:
1. Begin on A (the note four steps above E—the "subdominant"). Slow, deliberate.
2. Descend the Andalusian cadence melodically: A – G – F – E. The F is the characteristic Phrygian half-step; let it sound its full weight before landing on E.
3. E arrives as a major-quality moment—strong, final, but not light.
4. From E, construct a second phrase that ascends: E – G♯ – A – B. The G♯ (raised third) creates the flamenco brightness momentarily.
5. From B, descend back through F to E again—Phrygian gravity reasserts itself.
6. Use a roll on F (the one-up note) before each resolution to E.

**Goal**: a melody that alternates between the brightness of the raised third and the darkness of the half-step—the emotional bimodality that defines flamenco.

## Repertoire to Study

- **Camarón de la Isla**—the defining voice of cante jondo; siguiriyas and soleá recordings are essential
- **Paco de Lucía**—guitar; the most complete flamenco melodic vocabulary on record
- **Enrique Morente**—vocalist; bridging traditional and experimental flamenco
- **La Leyenda del Tiempo** (Camarón/Morente)—the album that opened flamenco to experimental modal mixture
- **Tomatito**—guitar; clear falseta structure in traditional palos

## Further Reading and Related Topics

- Authentic flamenco requires the *compás* (rhythmic cycle) and the cultural context of the tradition; this guide covers only the modal and melodic dimension.
- The Romani (Gitano) contribution to flamenco—its most characteristic emotional quality—is an oral tradition that resists theoretical description.
- Regional styles (Seville, Cádiz, Granada, Almería) each have distinct modal and melodic vocabulary not fully covered here.
