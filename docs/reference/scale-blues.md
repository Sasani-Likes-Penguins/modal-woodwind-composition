## What Are They?

The blues scales are a family of hexatonic (six-note) scales that emerged from West African pentatonic tradition meeting Western diatonic harmony in the American South. They are not a theoretical construction—they were identified by theorists after the fact, naming patterns that already existed in the living tradition of blues, gospel, jazz, and soul music.

There are two primary blues scales:

**Minor Blues**: natural minor pentatonic + one added note (the ♭5, the "blue note")
**Major Blues**: major pentatonic + one added note (the ♭3, a chromatic passing tone)

Both scales can be understood as pentatonic scales with a single chromatic passing tone added—one note that does not function as a resting pitch but as a voice in motion, always sliding toward or away from a stable note.

---

## Historical Origins

The blues scale's origins lie in the encounter between West African musical traditions and European harmonic systems in the American South from the 17th century onward. West African music, brought by enslaved people, used pentatonic scales, melismatic ornamentation, and microtonal pitch inflections—notes that deliberately hovered between the fixed pitches of European equal temperament.

The major and minor scales of European music gave blues musicians a harmonic context (particularly the dominant seventh chord, whose structure aligns with Mixolydian and blues scales). The African tradition contributed the pentatonic foundation and the characteristic inflection of the third, fifth, and seventh degrees—bending them slightly flat of their equal-tempered positions to create the expressive blue notes.

The result was a new scale vocabulary that sounded simultaneously familiar (pentatonic) and expressive in a way European scales could not quite achieve. This vocabulary became the foundation of blues, jazz, gospel, soul, rock and roll, funk, and every genre descended from them.

See [West African Music](world-west-african.md) for the full cultural history.

## The Minor Blues Scale

**Formula**: 1 – ♭3 – 4 – ♭5 – 5 – ♭7

**A Minor Blues**: A – C – D – E♭ – E – G – A

This is the minor pentatonic scale (A–C–D–E–G) with the flat fifth (E♭) added between the fourth (D) and the fifth (E).

### The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from A) | Character |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| 1 | A | Root—home |
| ♭3 | C | Minor third—darkness |
| 4 | D | Perfect fourth—stable secondary anchor |
| ♭5 | E♭ | Blue note—maximum tension; never rests here |
| 5 | E | Perfect fifth—resolution from the blue note |
| ♭7 | G | Flat seventh—open, unresolved |
| 8 | A | Home |

### Emotional Character

Longing, searching, bittersweet, urgent. The minor blues scale is the most emotionally direct scale in this repository for expressing a particular human experience: wanting something that is just out of reach, grief that has fire in it, love that has already been through loss.

The scale's emotional directness comes from its simplicity—five notes of the pentatonic, plus the blue note. The blue note (E♭) carries almost all the expressive weight: it is the note that aches.

## The Blue Note: The Soul of Blues Expression

The blue note (♭5, also written as ♯4) is the most distinctive and emotionally powerful note in the blues tradition. In A minor blues, this is E♭—a half-step below the perfect fifth (E) and a whole step above the fourth (D).

**What makes it special**: the blue note is a chromatic passing tone—it is never a resting note. It always appears in motion: approaching the fifth from below (E♭ → E) or departing from the fourth above (D → E♭). When held briefly, it creates an acute tension that immediately resolves to the fifth.

**The historical blue note**: in the original oral blues tradition, the "blue notes" were not exactly E♭ but slightly flat versions of the third, fifth, and seventh degrees—pitches that fell between the keys of a piano, impossible to notate precisely in standard notation. The systematization of the blue note as E♭ (exactly halfway between D and E) is a later theoretical approximation of what blues singers and instrumentalists did by ear.

**On instruments with pitch flexibility**: the bawu, kaval, NAF, and Irish whistle all allow the blue note to be played as a continuous pitch bend approaching E from below, passing through the approximate quarter-tone territory of the original oral blue note. This is far more expressive than landing cleanly on E♭ and then stepping to E. The slide IS the blue note.

**The blue note across traditions**: the concept of a slightly flattened pitch that creates expressive tension appears in multiple world music traditions:
- Indian meend (glide) approaching the vadi of a raga
- Arabic maqam neutral second and third (notes between standard pitches)
- Persian koron inflections (quarter-tone lowering)
- The Turkish koma system

These are not the same thing as the blues blue note, but they share a common insight: a note slightly below its expected pitch creates expressive yearning that a fixed, equal-tempered pitch cannot.

## The Major Blues Scale

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – 3 – 5 – 6

**C Major Blues**: C – D – E♭ – E – G – A – C

This is the major pentatonic scale (C–D–E–G–A) with a flat third (E♭) added as a chromatic passing tone between the second (D) and the major third (E).

### Emotional Character

Brighter than minor blues—the major pentatonic foundation gives it a warm, positive energy. But the ♭3 (E♭) adds a soulful "grain"—a brief shadow within the brightness. The characteristic gesture is the E♭ → E slide: from darkness to light, from the shadow note to the bright one.

Major blues is the sound of gospel music, country soul, and the brighter side of blues where the sun is out but the feeling still runs deep.

### The E♭ → E Slide

Like the minor blues blue note, the major blues ♭3 is most expressively performed as a pitch bend rather than a clean note. Slide from E♭ up to E—the transition between them is the expressive event, not either note in isolation.

In C major blues: D (major second, clear landing) → E♭ (passing note, in motion) → E (major third, arrival). This three-note gesture is the major blues signature.

## Blues Scales Across Musical Traditions

The blues scale vocabulary has spread far beyond its American origins:

**Blues-influenced jazz**: the minor blues scale underlies jazz improvisation in minor keys. Every jazz musician learns it early. Charlie Parker used blues scales extensively even in bebop contexts—the blues foundation persists beneath the harmonic sophistication.

**Rock and metal**: virtually all rock guitar improvisation and many rock vocal melodies use minor blues scale. Eric Clapton, Jimi Hendrix, Jimmy Page, Carlos Santana, B.B. King—all built their melodic vocabulary primarily on the minor blues scale.

**Celtic connection**: the Irish minor pentatonic (the NAF's native scale without additions) shares five of the six notes of A minor blues (A–C–D–E–G). Adding the blue note (E♭) to Celtic minor pentatonic creates a Celtic-blues hybrid that is surprisingly natural on the Irish whistle.

**Indian connection**: raga Malkauns (Hindol in Carnatic) uses the notes of the minor blues scale (1–♭3–4–♭5–♭7)—the same pitch collection, completely different approach, ornaments, and emotional framework. The convergence of pitch collections across completely separate traditions points toward these intervals' fundamental expressiveness.

**African connection**: see [West African Music](world-west-african.md). The minor pentatonic at the blues scale's core is directly descended from West African pentatonic traditions.

## Famous Recordings to Study

### Minor Blues—Essential Recordings
- **Robert Johnson**—*"Cross Road Blues"* (1936, A minor blues): the foundational Delta blues recording; the blue note appears in the vocal slides throughout
- **B.B. King**—*"The Thrill Is Gone"* (1969, B minor blues): the defining electric blues minor blues statement; King's guitar bending demonstrates the blue note's expressive range
- **John Lee Hooker**—*"Boogie Chillen"* (1948, E minor blues): raw, hypnotic minor blues; minimal ornamentation, maximum emotional directness
- **Muddy Waters**—*"Hoochie Coochie Man"* (1954, A minor blues): electric Chicago blues; the blue note in electric context
- **Miles Davis**—*"All Blues"* (*Kind of Blue*, 1959, G minor blues-inflected): jazz treatment of the minor blues vocabulary

### Major Blues—Essential Recordings
- **Louis Armstrong**—*"St. James Infirmary"* (major blues-adjacent): early jazz major blues tradition
- **Ray Charles**—*"What'd I Say"* (C major blues): soul-gospel major blues at its most joyful
- **Chuck Berry**—*"Johnny B. Goode"* (B♭ major blues): rock and roll built from major blues guitar riffs
- **Stevie Ray Vaughan**—*"Pride and Joy"* (E major blues): modern major blues; the ♭3→3 slide in the riff

### Blues on Wind Instruments
- **Sonny Rollins**—any minor blues recording on tenor saxophone: the most direct model for wind blues expression
- **Charlie Parker**—blues recordings (*"Now's the Time"*, 1945, F minor blues): bebop blues synthesis; chromatic ornaments over blues scale foundation
- **Junior Wells**—blues harmonica recordings: the harmonica's pitch bending makes it the most direct wind instrument parallel to the bawu for blues; the blue note as a bent pitch

|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Bawu | Excellent | Excellent | Pitch bending is the most natural vehicle for the blue note; this is the most blues-capable instrument in the collection |
| Low D whistle | Good | Good | Blue note (♭5) via half-hole; ♭3 in major blues via partial covering; E minor blues most natural |
| Alto recorder | Good | Good | All notes available; pitch bending limited but chromatic passing tones accessible |
| NAF (Am) | Excellent (5 of 6 notes native) | Good | Native minor pentatonic is the blues scale minus the blue note; add ♭5 via half-holing |

**The bawu as the blues instrument**: of all instruments in this collection, the bawu most naturally expresses the blues vocabulary. Its free-reed mechanism allows pitch to be continuously bent—the blue note as a sliding pitch approach, the ♭3→3 transition as a smooth vocal glide. Playing blues on the bawu is the instrument at its most natural.

## Composing with Blues Scales

**The blue note is always passing**: never land on the ♭5 (minor blues) or ♭3 (major blues) as a resting pitch. Always approach it from below or above and continue moving. The expressive charge of the blue note comes from its instability—held, it loses its power.

**Call and response**: blues melody is inherently conversational. A phrase of three or four notes that arrives somewhere (question); a responding phrase that answers from a different part of the scale (answer). The question often ends on the blue note or the flat seventh; the answer resolves more firmly.

**The flat seventh as openness**: the flat seventh (G in A minor blues) is the scale's most "unresolved" stable note—it settles but does not arrive home. Phrases that end on the flat seventh feel open, implying continuation. Use this for phrases that are not final—the music has more to say.

**Silence as grammar**: blues phrasing is famous for its use of space—a phrase, then silence, then a response. The silence is not empty; the listener fills it with the emotional residue of the phrase. This technique is described in detail in [Expressive Playing](expressive-playing.md) and [Brain Biology and Psychology](brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Mix major and minor blues**: both scales can appear in the same piece in the same key, alternating as the emotional moment requires. A passage of minor blues (searching, dark) followed by a phrase of major blues (warming, brightening) creates an arc from shadow to light within a single modal language.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: bawu (A minor blues)
**A Minor Blues**: A – C – D – E♭ – E – G – A

The fundamental blues phrase structure—three lines of four bars each (AAB form in classic blues):

**Line 1 (question)**:
1. Begin on A (root). Hold for two beats.
2. Rise: A – C – D. Pause on D (the fourth—an open, questioning resting point).
3. Bend from D upward through E♭ (blue note—in motion) into E (fifth—brief stability).
4. Descend: E – C – A. End quietly on A.

**Line 2 (repetition/deepening)**:
5. Repeat a variation of Line 1. This time, hold the E♭ (blue note) slightly longer before resolving to E—let the tension accumulate.
6. End on G (the flat seventh) rather than A. The flat seventh ending makes the line feel unfinished, reaching.

**Line 3 (resolution)**:
7. From G, descend: G – E – D – C – A. The descent bypasses the blue note entirely—after the tension of Lines 1 and 2, the resolution through the clean pentatonic notes feels like release.
8. End on A (root), softly. Silence.

**Goal**: experience the emotional arc of the blues phrase structure—question, deepening, resolution—as a complete compositional unit that can be repeated and varied.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Blues Scales

**The blues ache** (the core affect of minor blues): the blue note (E♭ in A minor blues) must always be in motion—approaching the fifth (E) from below or departing from it. Never rest on the blue note. The emotional effect: something urgently wanting but never quite reaching. Sustain the approach (bend from D upward through E♭) rather than the arrival.

**Call and response longing**: a short phrase (the call) that ends on the blue note or the flat seventh, followed by silence, followed by a responding phrase (the answer) that resolves more firmly—but still not completely. The answer should settle on the fourth (D) or the fifth (E), not the tonic (A)—the response is still searching.

**Joyful soul** (major blues affect): the flat third passing note (E♭ in C major blues) sliding immediately upward to the major third (E)—the characteristic major blues gesture; the brightness of the major pentatonic underneath making the flat third feel like a shade added to sunlight rather than a shadow; moderate-fast tempo with rhythmic energy.

**Deep, private grief** (slow blues): very slow; the blue note as a long, sustained, slightly bent pitch—held in the middle of its bend rather than at either the E♭ below or the E above; minor third (C in A minor blues) as the phrase anchor; the flat seventh (G) as phrase endings; much silence between phrases; low register throughout.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Blues Scales

**Landing on the blue note and staying there**: the blue note (E♭ in A minor blues) has no stability—its power comes entirely from its motion. Holding it for more than a beat without moving reduces it from an expressive passing tone to an out-of-tune resting pitch. The rule: approach, touch, depart. Never anchor.

**Playing the full scale linearly**: the blues scale is not meant to be played C – D♭ – D – E♭ – E – G from bottom to top as a scale exercise. It is a collection of notes for melodic improvisation. Choose from them based on feeling, not position. A phrase that skips from A to E to G to D to E♭ (not in scale order) sounds like blues; a phrase that plays A – C – D – E♭ – E – G in order sounds like a scale demonstration.

**No space**: blues phrasing is defined by space—a phrase, then silence, then a response. Beginners who fill every moment with notes lose the call-and-response structure that gives the blues its conversational quality. The silence is where the blues lives. Let the phrase end, let the room hold the note for a moment, then respond.

**Same dynamic throughout**: the blues emotional arc within a phrase requires dynamic shaping. The blue note (E♭) should arrive at a slightly louder dynamic than its approach—the moment of expressive peak—then the resolution to E should soften slightly. This micro-shaping makes the blue note feel weighted rather than incidental.
