## Blues Scales (Major and Minor)

*Sources: [91][92][93][94][95][96][97][100]*

### Minor Blues Scale (Hexatonic Minor Blues)

**Formula**: 1 – ♭3 – 4 – ♭5 – 5 – ♭7

**A Minor Blues**: A – C – D – E♭ – E – G – A

Natural minor pentatonic with one added note: the ♭5, called the "blue note" [91][92][96]. The ♭5 (E♭ in A minor blues) is a chromatic passing tone between the fourth (D) and fifth (E)—it creates a momentary tension that is immediately released by the perfect fifth.

**Emotional character**: longing, searching, bittersweet, urgent. The blue note is simultaneously the most expressive and least stable note in the scale—it doesn't rest, it slides. On instruments with pitch flexibility (bawu, kaval), the blue note is played as a bent pitch approaching the fifth from below, creating a sliding, vocal quality that is the soul of blues expression.

**For wind players**: the blues scale is essentially the NAF's native scale with one added chromatic note. The bawu's pitch bending makes the blue note most idiomatic—slide from E♭ to E, letting the tension dissolve into the fifth. The low D whistle can approximate the blue note via a half-hole.

**Historical context**: the minor blues scale emerged from West African pentatonic tradition meeting Western harmony in the American South—the same cultural convergence that created jazz, gospel, and rock. The blue note (slightly flat third, fifth, and seventh in the original oral tradition, systematized into the ♭5) is the sound of that meeting [91][92][93].

### Major Blues Scale

**Formula**: 1 – 2 – ♭3 – 3 – 5 – 6

**C Major Blues**: C – D – E♭ – E – G – A – C

Major pentatonic with an added flat third as a chromatic passing note [91][92][96]. The ♭3 (E♭) slides into the major third (E)—creating a brief shadow within the brighter major context.

**Emotional character**: brighter than minor blues, but with a soulful "grain" added by the passing flat third. Used in country, gospel, and soul music for a sound that is major-bright but not sterile. The flat third gives it warmth and humanity.

**Exercise on G whistle**: A major blues = A – B – C – C♯ – E – F♯ – A. Begin on A. Step to B. Then C (the flat third—a brief shadow) sliding immediately to C♯ (the major third—the brightness returns). The C → C♯ slide IS the major blues characteristic gesture. On a whistle, use a slide or half-hole technique to approximate this chromatic passing motion.
