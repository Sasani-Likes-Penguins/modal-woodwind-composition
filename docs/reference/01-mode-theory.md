*Covers dimension 1 (mode theory). See ../citations.md for source details.*

## What Is a Mode?

A **mode** is simply a scale—a set of notes in a particular order—with a distinctive personality. You can think of modes as different emotional flavors of the musical alphabet. Major scales sound bright and resolved; natural minor sounds darker and sadder; Phrygian and Locrian have their own specific characters.

What gives each mode its character is the pattern of distances between its notes. Those distances are either a **whole step** (two piano keys apart, counting the black key between them) or a **half step** (one piano key apart, no gap). The specific sequence of whole steps and half steps is what makes each mode sound the way it does.

## Phrygian

### The Pattern of Steps

Phrygian follows this sequence from home note to home note one octave up:

**Half – Whole – Whole – Whole – Half – Whole – Whole**

The crucial thing is that first distance: a **half step**. In almost every other common scale, the first step up from the home note is a whole step. In Phrygian, it's only a half step—the smallest possible distance. That single note, pressed tight against the home note, is the source of everything emotionally distinctive about Phrygian.

### The Notes in E Phrygian

| Step from home | Note | Distance from previous |
|---------------|------|----------------------|
| Home | E |—|
| One-up note | F | Half step (one piano key) |
| Two-up | G | Whole step |
| Three-up | A | Whole step |
| Four-up | B | Whole step |
| Five-up | C | Half step |
| Six-up | D | Whole step |
| Octave (home again) | E | Whole step |

The **one-up note (F in E Phrygian)** is the defining note of the mode [1][7]. It creates a powerful tension—always leaning against the home note, always wanting to fall back down to it. Research has shown that this half-step interval appears more often in human infant crying than any other interval [10], suggesting the emotional response to it may be built into us rather than learned.

### How Phrygian Relates to Other Scales

Phrygian is essentially a natural minor scale with one change: the note one step above home is lowered by a half step [1][7]. If you know what natural minor sounds like—that familiar dark, sad sound—Phrygian takes that and makes it more tense and more ancient-sounding by tightening that first step.

### The Characteristic Gesture

The most recognizable Phrygian move is stepping up to the one-up note and then stepping back down to home. In E Phrygian that's F → E; in D Phrygian it's E♭ → D. This gesture—a half-step lean and release—is the musical equivalent of a sigh. It recurs in flamenco guitar, Renaissance church music, Baroque compositions, and heavy metal, always with a similar emotional weight: dark, tending downward, resigned, or passionate [95].

### Where Phrygian Has Been Used

| Era / Tradition | Examples |
|----------------|---------|
| Ancient Greece | Associated with passion and wildness [6] |
| Medieval church music | "Mode III"—used for plainchant |
| Renaissance (1400s–1600s) | Palestrina's sacred motets; Tallis; Heinrich Schütz's St. John Passion [6][46] |
| Baroque (1600s–1750s) | J.S. Bach preserved old Phrygian church melodies in his cantatas [6] |
| Romantic (1800s) | Brahms used it for a prayer-like slow movement; rare but present [98] |
| Impressionist (early 1900s) | Debussy used it for ambiguity and shimmer [22] |
| Folk revival (1900s) | Vaughan Williams—*Fantasia on a Theme of Thomas Tallis*, in G Phrygian [20] |
| Flamenco / Spanish music | The core harmonic language of flamenco [95] |
| Middle Eastern music | Called *Bayati maqam* in Arabic/Turkish music [20] |
| Indian classical music | Called *Bhairavi thaat* [20] |
| Metal and rock | Common in heavy metal since the 1980s |

## Locrian

### The Pattern of Steps

Locrian follows this sequence:

**Half – Whole – Whole – Half – Whole – Whole – Whole**

Locrian shares Phrygian's tight first step (half step to the one-up note). It adds one more unusual compression: the note five steps up from home is also lowered by a half step, creating what musicians call a **tritone**—an interval that Western music has historically called "the devil's interval" because of its extreme tension and instability [12].

### The Notes in B Locrian

| Step from home | Note | Distance from previous |
|---------------|------|----------------------|
| Home | B |—|
| One-up note | C | Half step |
| Two-up | D | Whole step |
| Three-up | E | Whole step |
| Four-up | F | Half step |
| Shrunken five-up | F (same as four-up!) |—wait—|

Let's do it correctly:

| Step from home | Note |
|---------------|------|
| Home | B |
| One-up | C |
| Two-up | D |
| Three-up | E |
| Four-up (stable landing point) | E |

Actually the clearest way to hear it: **B Locrian = B C D E F G A B**. The home chord (B + D + F) is a diminished chord—it contains that tight tritone interval (B to F), which is exactly six half-steps, exactly halfway between two octaves. There is no stable resting point in this chord. There is no "home" in the harmonic sense.

### Why Locrian Is Hard But Worth It

Because the home chord is unstable, Locrian as a harmonic language (for chords and harmony) is genuinely difficult. But as a **melodic language**—a single voice, no chords—it can be deeply beautiful [3][5].

The secret is that the instability only becomes a problem when you sound the tense notes simultaneously with the home note. A solo wind player naturally plays one note at a time, which sidesteps most of the problem. The folk musician John Kirkpatrick demonstrated this: by keeping the melody in a single voice and approaching the tense notes by step rather than by leap, he created genuinely beautiful Locrian music [3].

**The one stable note in Locrian**: the note four steps up from home (E in B Locrian; G in D Locrian) is the one landing spot that doesn't clash severely with the home note. Phrases can rest here before venturing further into tense territory [4].

### The Difference Between Phrygian and Locrian

Think of Locrian as Phrygian with an additional twist of the knife. Phrygian is dark and tense but has a stable foundation—the note five steps up (B in E Phrygian) is solid. Locrian removes that stability. The floor falls out.

**When to choose each**: Phrygian for grief, passion, awe—emotions that have a center, even if it's a dark one. Locrian for unease, instability, a feeling that something is fundamentally not-right—emotions that have no resolution.

## What These Modes Mean for Each Instrument

Because several of these instruments have fixed scales or limited chromatic access, "playing in Phrygian" means different things on different instruments:

| Instrument | What's possible | Practical approach |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Alto recorder | Fully chromatic—any mode in any key | A Phrygian and D Phrygian most natural-sounding [15]; one-up note via cross-fingering has a veiled tone [17] |
| Tenor recorder | Fully chromatic | D Phrygian most natural; same veiled one-up note |
| NAF in A minor | Five-note scale—no one-up note by default | One-up note as quick grace note via cross-fingering; evoke Phrygian through phrasing rather than specific pitch [35] |
| NAF in F♯ minor | Same | Same approach |
| NAF in B♭ minor | Same | Same approach |
| Bawu | Chromatic with pitch bending | D Phrygian works well; slide into the one-up note rather than landing cleanly [36][37] |
| Low D whistle | Naturally plays D major scale; E Phrygian uses all natural holes | E Phrygian is the most idiomatic key—the one-up note (F) is the next natural hole [39] |
| G whistle | Naturally plays G major; A Phrygian uses natural holes | A Phrygian is most natural; B♭ (one-up note) requires half-hole [39] |
| Kaval in D | Scale includes the half-step intervals Phrygian needs | D Phrygian is the most natural key; one-up note (E♭) is built into the instrument's tuning [62] |

## Gaps and Limitations

- The microtonal shadings of Middle Eastern Phrygian (maqam *Bayati*)—where the one-up note is slightly flatter than the Western equal-tempered version—are not fully covered here. Players of kaval and NAF interested in Middle Eastern-adjacent sounds may want to explore this further.
- Locrian as a primary scale in Western art music before the 20th century is essentially absent from the historical record; all the beautiful uses of it come from folk music, jazz, or contemporary composition.
