# Gamelan: Pelog and Slendro—Scales Without Western Equivalents

## What Is Gamelan?

Gamelan is a traditional ensemble music of Java and Bali (Indonesia), centered on tuned metallophones (instruments with metal bars struck with mallets), gongs, drums, flute (*suling*), and bowed string instruments. Gamelan music is one of the most sophisticated musical traditions in the world, and its tuning system is unlike any Western scale [65][68].

The two fundamental Javanese gamelan tuning systems are:
- **Slendro**: a five-note scale with roughly equal spacing
- **Pelog**: a seven-note scale with highly unequal intervals

A full gamelan orchestra includes instruments in both tuning systems, and classically only one tuning is used at a time.

---

## Why Gamelan Tuning Cannot Be Mapped to Western Notes

This is the most important thing to understand about gamelan scales: **they do not correspond to Western notes, and different gamelans are tuned differently from each other**.

Each Gamelan has a unique spirit poured and hammered from molten bronze, housed across the full body of its instruments. The pitches of the Gamelan are anchored to the gong and overseen by the intuition of a female singer, and the octave in each Gamelan is slightly larger or smaller than its Western counterpart [68].

To copy the pitches of a Gamelan is to insult the spirit housed within it.

In practical terms: the "same" note in two different gamelan orchestras may differ by as much as 40-50 cents (nearly half a semitone). This is not imprecision—it is the intended character of each individual ensemble. Each gamelan has its own personality expressed through its unique tuning.

For a wind player: this means that gamelan scales cannot be simply looked up and played—they must be *heard* and then approximated. The bawu and kaval, with their flexible pitch, are the only instruments in this collection capable of genuinely approximating gamelan tuning; the recorder and whistle can only approximate.

## Slendro: The Five-Note Equal Scale

Slendro divides the octave into **five roughly equal steps** of approximately 240 cents each—almost exactly halfway between a Western whole step and a minor third [63][65].

This means every note in Slendro is roughly equidistant from its neighbors. There are no half-steps and no large leaps—just a smooth, evenly spaced five-note world.

**Character**: Slendro notation generally produces a light, cheerful atmosphere and feels busier. Serene and smooth due to the roughly equal intervals. However, slendro can also express sadness—in Bali, slendro is the tuning associated with cremation ceremonies, and slendro miring (playing slightly "off" the slendro pitches deliberately) is used for melancholy and longing [65].

**Pathet**: Slendro has three sub-modes (*pathet*—roughly equivalent to a mood or emotional register) named nem, sanga, and manyura. These appear in order during a traditional *wayang* (shadow puppet) performance [63].

## Pelog: The Seven-Note Unequal Scale

Pelog has **seven notes with highly irregular intervals**—some very large (nearly a minor third), some very small (nearly a half-step) [62][65].

**Approximate pelog interval pattern** (varies by gamelan): 1–2–3–4–5–6–7 with approximate intervals of: wide – narrow – wide – wide – narrow – wide – (gap back to 1)

Because the intervals are so unequal, pelog has a dramatically different quality depending on which five notes you emphasize. Pelog music typically uses **five-note subsets** of the seven-tone collection.

**The three main pelog modes (for Balinese gamelan)**:
- **Selisir**: the most common mode; considered "the default pelog sound"—the tuning of gamelan gong kebyar, the most internationally known Balinese gamelan
- **Tembung**: a different five-note subset with a darker character
- **Sunaren**: another subset; specific ceremonial associations

**Character**: Pelog notation produces an atmosphere that gives the impression of being manly, regal, and sacred [65]. Its irregular intervals create a more complex, dynamic, and intense sound compared to slendro's smoothness.

## The Emotional World of Gamelan Scales

For experienced participants in gamelan music, the pelog and slendro scales each have a particular feeling, related to the rituals and circumstances in which the scale is used [63].

**Slendro feels**: open, flowing, cheerful in its standard form; deeply melancholy in slendro miring; associated with everyday human drama in wayang

**Pelog feels**: sacred, regal, ceremonial; the "manly" quality comes from its irregular intervals' commanding presence; used for formal and divine occasions in wayang

**The shimmering effect**: In Balinese gamelan, instruments are played in pairs tuned slightly apart, creating interference beating—a shimmering, pulsing quality meant to give the listener a feeling of a god's presence or a stepping stone to a meditative state [62][63]. This is a compositional device: the beating between slightly-out-of-tune octaves creates a texture that feels larger than individual notes.

## Applying Gamelan Concepts to Wind Playing

Since fixed gamelan tuning is not achievable on most wind instruments, the compositional concepts are more applicable than the precise pitches:

**1. Equally-spaced melody (slendro approach)**: compose a five-note melody where all intervals are approximately equal—major 2nds and minor 3rds alternating, creating a smooth, non-directional, flowing quality. No note wants to go anywhere; the melody drifts like water.

**2. The shimmering technique (pelog/Balinese approach)**: on the bawu, slightly detune a sustained note by bending it slowly above or below its center pitch, creating a controlled wavering. The kaval's dual-voice technique (partially covering the back fipple window) creates a similar shimmering texture.

**3. Cyclic form**: gamelan music is structured in cycles—phrases repeat, patterns overlay, there is no strong sense of linear development or climax. Compose a phrase, repeat it, vary it slightly, repeat it again. The accumulation of repeated cycles creates the hypnotic gamelan effect.

**4. The gong as anchor**: in gamelan, the large gong marks the end of each musical cycle—a moment of resolution and return. In wind playing: the lowest note (the "gong note") can serve as the anchor that the melody regularly returns to between cycles of exploration.

## Composition Exercise: Slendro-Inspired Pentatonic Loop on NAF

**Setup**: A minor NAF = A–C–D–E–G. Think of these five notes as approximately equally spaced.

**Rules following slendro cyclic approach**:
1. Choose a short phrase of 4-5 notes (e.g., A–C–D–E–G–A going up, A–G–E–D–C–A going down).
2. Play the phrase. Repeat it exactly. Repeat it again.
3. On the fourth repetition, vary ONE note (e.g., end on E instead of A). Then return to the original.
4. On the eighth repetition, change the rhythm (same notes, different durations).
5. Never stop to "resolve"—let the cycle keep moving. The lack of arrival IS the aesthetic.
6. After many cycles, gradually slow the tempo. Let the final repetitions fade to silence.

**Goal**: music that feels like it has always been playing and will always be playing—the eternal present of gamelan.

## Repertoire to Study

- **Any Javanese gamelan recording**—especially Central Javanese court gamelan (*gending*) for pelog
- **Balinese gamelan gong kebyar**—for the shimmering selisir pelog sound
- **Wayang kulit (shadow puppet) recordings**—to hear how slendro and pelog alternate and contrast in narrative context
- **Lou Harrison's "Double Music"**—Western composer who created for American gamelan; bridge between traditions

## Gaps and Limitations

- Gamelan tuning varies enormously; no two gamelan sound alike. This guide describes general principles.
- The melodic instrument of gamelan (suling flute) has its own technique developed specifically for gamelan context; full study requires engaging with gamelan tradition directly.
- Balinese and Javanese traditions are distinct in important ways not fully detailed here.
