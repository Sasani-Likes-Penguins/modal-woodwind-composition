## What Is Dorian?

Dorian is the second of the seven [diatonic](../glossary.md#diatonic) modes—and one of the most beloved and widely used in music across every era and tradition. It is a minor-feeling scale with one striking exception: the sixth degree is raised by a half-step compared to natural minor, giving Dorian a touch of brightness within its darkness.

One writer described this as "bittersweet melancholy with a twist of hope." The darkness is real and present; so is the lift. The combination makes Dorian one of the most emotionally nuanced modes available.

Dorian also has a mathematical distinction: it is the only diatonic mode that is perfectly [symmetrical](../glossary.md#interval)—it uses the same sequence of intervals going up and down.

---

## The Interval Structure

**Step pattern**: Whole – Half – Whole – Whole – Whole – Half – Whole

**D Dorian** (the white-key example):

| Degree | Note | Distance from previous |
|--------|------|----------------------|
| 1 (tonic) | D |—|
| 2 | E | Whole step |
| ♭3 | F | Half-step |
| 4 | G | Whole step |
| 5 | A | Whole step |
| 6 | B | Whole step (the raised sixth) |
| ♭7 | C | Half-step |
| 8 (octave) | D | Whole step |

**Relationship to other scales**: Dorian is natural minor ([Aeolian](mode-aeolian.md)) with one change—the sixth degree is raised by a half-step. Everything else is identical. In D Dorian, B natural replaces the B♭ of D natural minor. That single note is the entire difference between Dorian's resilient character and Aeolian's settled sadness.

Dorian can also be understood as [Mixolydian](mode-mixolydian.md) with a lowered third—sharing Mixolydian's flat seventh while adding a minor third above the tonic.

---

## The Characteristic Note

The raised sixth—B natural in D Dorian, F♯ in E Dorian—is Dorian's defining note. Its melody hovers around the major sixth in such a gut-wrenching, dramatic way that even listeners unfamiliar with modal theory feel the distinction immediately. The minor third (F in D Dorian) establishes the minor foundation; the major sixth (B) lifts it unexpectedly. The tension between these two notes—one dark, one bright—is the emotional engine of the mode.

**Compositional technique**: to make a melody sound clearly Dorian rather than merely minor, emphasize the raised sixth. Use it as a melodic peak, ornament it, resolve to it, return to it. Without deliberate emphasis on the raised sixth, a Dorian melody risks sounding like any minor melody.

---

## Why Dorian Sounds the Way It Does

Dorian sits precisely between [Aeolian](mode-aeolian.md) (natural minor) and [Mixolydian](mode-mixolydian.md). From Aeolian's point of view, Dorian has had its sixth degree brightened. From Mixolydian's point of view, Dorian has had its third degree darkened. It is a hybrid—neither purely sad nor bright, but containing both simultaneously.

The raised sixth creates a chord on the fourth degree that is major (in D Dorian, G major) rather than minor (as in D natural minor, G minor). This major IV chord gives Dorian its characteristic harmonic openness—the iv–I motion of natural minor, which is somber and enclosed, becomes IV–I in Dorian, which is open and slightly triumphant even within a minor context.

Dorian mode is unique among the diatonic modes for being symmetrical, meaning that it uses the same sequence of intervals going up and down. When you write Dorian on the chromatic circle, it's left-right symmetrical. This symmetry contributes to its balanced character—it is not pulling urgently in any one direction.

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Resilient grief | The minor third establishes sadness; the raised sixth prevents collapse. Music that mourns but does not yield. |
| Noble darkness | The mode associated historically with heroism, bravery, and the dignity of sorrow |
| Soulful vitality | The raised sixth over minor harmony creates jazz, funk, and soul's characteristic "major-chord-over-minor" energy |
| Medieval timelessness | The mode of Gregorian chant, sea shanties, and ancient folk melody |
| Searching introspection | Music that looks inward but has not given up |

Dorian is particularly effective when the emotion requires darkness that is *active* rather than resigned—Aeolian is the mode of acceptance; Dorian is the mode of endurance.

## Dorian Across History

### Ancient and Medieval

In ancient Greek theory, the Dorian *harmonia* was associated with courage, bravery, and manly virtue—Plato considered it the ideal mode for soldiers. This is distinct from the modern Dorian mode but shares the emotional association with strength and dignity.

In medieval European church music, Dorian was Mode I (the first and most important authentic mode), used extensively for Gregorian chant. Here is the Dies irae sequence, a 13th century Gregorian chant in Dorian mode. The *Dies irae* (Day of Wrath)—perhaps the most famous medieval chant—is in D Dorian, demonstrating the mode's capacity for both solemnity and intensity.

Other medieval Dorian examples:
- **Cantigas de Santa Maria** (Alfonso X, 13th century): the vast collection contains 420 poems with musical notation, including several pieces in medieval D Dorian mode. *Santa Maria, strela do dia* is one of the most widely performed of the Cantigas
- **Buxtehude**—*Chorale Prelude, BuxWV 180: Christ, unser Herr zum Jordan kam* (Dorian mode)

### Baroque

J.S. Bach wrote his famous *Toccata and Fugue in D minor, BWV 538* in Dorian—it is informally known as "the Dorian Toccata" to distinguish it from the more familiar BWV 565, because it bears no key signature (Dorian was traditionally written without a key signature in this period). Bach also used Dorian in his *German Organ Mass* (Clavier-Übung III, 1739) including a chorale prelude and fugue on "Dies sind die heilgen zehn Gebot" (The Ten Commandments) in G Dorian.

Beethoven used Dorian or Dorian-adjacent passages in his later works. Written in accordance with medieval music theory, Beethoven's Dorian passages could include a B♭ as well as a B natural. The *Et incarnatus est* from the Credo movement of the *Missa Solemnis* uses Dorian mode. Jean Sibelius's Symphony No. 6 contains large portions in Dorian.

### Romantic and 20th Century Classical

- **Saint-Saëns**—*"Royal March of the Lions"* from *Carnival of the Animals*: uses Dorian mode to evoke a "Persian style"
- **Saint-Saëns**—*"La Brise"* from *Mélodies Persanes, Op. 26*: E Dorian
- **Béla Bartók**—*Mikrokosmos* (piano pedagogical pieces): includes explicit Dorian mode pieces; Bartók's folk research led him to use Dorian as a primary compositional mode in many works
- **Sibelius**—Symphony No. 6: substantial Dorian sections throughout

## Dorian in Folk and World Music

Dorian is arguably the most natural mode for folk melody. Its combination of minor character with a major sixth makes it perfectly suited to melodies that are both dark and vital—the folk singer's combination of sadness and energy.

**Irish traditional music**: most Celtic minor-key tunes are in Dorian rather than natural minor. The raised sixth gives Irish and Scottish minor tunes their characteristic resilience—they grieve without losing forward motion. Famous Dorian Irish tunes include "The Rocky Road to Dublin" and many reels and jigs in minor keys.

**English folk**: *Scarborough Fair*, an English folk song popularized by Simon and Garfunkel, is a textbook example of the Dorian mode. Its melody hovers around the major sixth in such a gut-wrenching, dramatic way. The song feels somber, without feeling overdramatic.

**Sea shanties**: sea shanties have been around since at least the 15th century. Dorian mode suits the content and intent of sea shanties particularly well due to its dark but not necessarily morose nature. "What Shall We Do with the Drunken Sailor" is composed in D Dorian.

**Indian connection**: although Indian music adds quarter tones to scales, whereas Western music stops with half tones, listeners can still hear the Dorian scale in this classic Indian piece. Ustad Ali Akbar Khan and sitarist Ravi Shankar perform Zila Kafi, a raga written in a Dorian-equivalent scale. The Hindustani raga Kafi and Carnatic raga Kharaharapriya both use the Dorian scale structure.

**Gregorian chant**: the first church mode (*Primus tonus*) corresponds to modern D Dorian. The entire repertoire of Catholic Gregorian chant in Mode I is effectively Dorian.

## Dorian in Jazz

The archetypal Dorian mode jazz tune is Miles Davis' "So What." It switches between D Dorian and E-flat Dorian. John Coltrane played on this recording, and it evidently made a strong impact on him. He wrote many tunes in Dorian, and arranged several folk and traditional songs that use it too. Here's his version of "Greensleeves", mainly in D Dorian. And here's "Spiritual" in C Dorian.

Dorian is the default scale for minor chord improvisation in jazz—when a jazz musician encounters a minor seventh chord, Dorian is the first scale they reach for. This is because Dorian's raised sixth fits naturally over the minor seventh chord's harmonic context, providing a brighter color than natural minor.

George Russell's *Lydian Chromatic Concept* (1953), the theoretical basis for modal jazz, placed major importance on the Dorian mode as a near-equivalent to Lydian in expressing tonal gravity. Miles Davis absorbed Russell's thinking directly while recording *Kind of Blue* (1959).

Key jazz Dorian repertoire:
- **Miles Davis**—*"So What"* (*Kind of Blue*, 1959): the founding document of modal jazz; D Dorian and E♭ Dorian
- **Miles Davis**—*"Milestones"* (1958): D Dorian and G Dorian
- **John Coltrane**—*"Impressions"*: based on "So What," D Dorian
- **Horace Silver**—*"Song for My Father"*: F Dorian
- **Bill Evans**—*"Re: Person I Knew"*: Dorian-inflected modal jazz

## Dorian in Rock, Pop, and Soul

The raised sixth of Dorian fits naturally over the dominant seventh harmony that underlies blues and rock, making Dorian one of the most pervasive modes in popular music—often without composers consciously knowing they are using it.

**Classic rock and folk-rock**:
- *Mad World* by Tears for Fears (F♯ Dorian; F Dorian in Gary Jules' cover): hear the Dorian mode most distinctly in the pre-chorus: "I find it kinda funny, I find it kinda sad."
- **Van Morrison**—*"Moondance"* (A Dorian): Dorian's jazz-inflected minor quality at its most lyrical
- **Simon and Garfunkel**—*"Scarborough Fair"* (A Dorian): ancient folk quality in a folk-rock context
- **Pink Floyd**—*"Another Brick in the Wall"*: one of the most famous examples of Dorian in rock music

**Funk and soul**:
- **Santana**—*"Oye Como Va"* (A Dorian): the bass riff is a pure Dorian groove, and the raised sixth appears prominently in the melody
- **Michael Jackson**—*"Thriller"* (Dorian modal inflections throughout): not only Dorian in the melodic pitches but also in the harmony
- **Stevie Wonder**—*"I Wish"* (A♭ Dorian): the alternation between two chords creates harmonic stasis over a Dorian scale
- **Lauryn Hill**—*"Doo Wop (That Thing)"*: the parallel moving B♭ Am Gm chords really enhance the Dorian mode feel
- **Daft Punk**—*"Get Lucky"* (B Dorian)

**Video game and film music**:
- The theme of the Halo video game series, taking significant inspiration from medieval Gregorian chants to effect an "ancient and mysterious" sound, is written in E Dorian.
- Numerous RPG soundtracks use Dorian for dungeon and adventure themes precisely because of its medieval-nobility association

| Instrument | Most natural Dorian key | Ease |
|-----------|------------------------|------|
| Low D whistle | D Dorian | Completely natural—all standard fingering |
| G whistle | G Dorian | Completely natural |
| Alto recorder | A Dorian | Natural; raised sixth (F♯) standard fingering |
| Tenor recorder | D Dorian | Natural |
| Bawu | D Dorian | Fully available; raised sixth via standard fingering |
| NAF (Am) | A Dorian feel | The raised sixth (F♯) can be produced by cross-fingering; otherwise evoke through phrasing |
| Moldavian kaval | D Dorian | Available throughout the full range |

**Special note on Irish whistle in D Dorian**: D Dorian on the low D whistle uses completely natural fingering. The characteristic raised sixth (B natural) is a standard hole position. This makes D Dorian one of the most idiomatic modes for this instrument—and explains why so many traditional Irish minor tunes sound naturally "at home" on the D whistle.

## Composing in Dorian: Practical Techniques

### Establish the Tonic and the Minor Third First

Begin any Dorian composition by establishing the minor character before introducing the raised sixth. Start on the tonic (D), move through the minor third (F in D Dorian), and settle into the lower part of the scale. Only then introduce the raised sixth—so that when it arrives, the listener feels the lift against an established minor foundation.

### Use the Raised Sixth as a Peak or Ornament

The most expressive uses of Dorian place the raised sixth at a melodic high point—the note the phrase ascends toward. In D Dorian, a phrase that climbs through F and G and arrives at B natural before descending back to D demonstrates the mode's full emotional range: darkness in the minor third, openness in the raised sixth, return to the grounded tonic.

A trill or [roll](ornamentation-guide.md) on the raised sixth—if the instrument supports it—gives the bright note maximum expressiveness before it resolves.

### The Major IV Chord as Melodic Territory

Because the fourth degree chord in Dorian is major (G major in D Dorian rather than G minor in D Aeolian), melodic phrases that sit on the fourth degree have an open, almost major character even within the minor mode. A phrase that rests on the fourth and its immediate neighbors (F♯–G–A in D Dorian) sounds unexpectedly bright—a momentary stepping into the light before returning to the minor world below.

### Dorian and Aeolian Mixture

One of the most expressive techniques in Dorian writing is to occasionally lower the sixth back to its natural minor position—momentarily borrowing Aeolian—before returning to Dorian's raised version. The contrast makes both notes more vivid: the flat sixth sounds darker by contrast with the raised sixth; the raised sixth sounds brighter by contrast with the flat. This mixture is common in Celtic music, where the same tune often uses both sixth degrees in different phrases.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: low D whistle (D Dorian)
**D Dorian**: D – E – F – G – A – B – C – D
**Affect**: the grief of something endured, not collapsed into

1. Begin on A (the fifth—open, stable). Descend: A – G – F – E – D. The F (minor third) arrives on the way down—darkness acknowledged.
2. Ascend from D: D – E – F – G – A – B. When B arrives (the raised sixth), apply a [roll](ornamentation-guide.md). Let it ring. This is the emotional lift of Dorian—the unexpected brightness within the minor world.
3. From B, descend back: B – A – G – F – E – D. End softly on D.
4. The second phrase: D – F – G – A – B – C – B – A. The C (flat seventh) followed by the return to B (raised sixth) creates a momentary shadow that makes the B sound even brighter when it returns.
5. Vary the phrase length of the second phrase—make it one beat longer than the first. The asymmetry creates forward motion.

**Goal**: music that demonstrates Dorian's defining quality—minor in its foundation, but lit from within by the raised sixth.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in Dorian

**Resilient sadness** (the Celtic slow air quality): slow tempo; the raised sixth (C♯ in E Dorian) as the emotional peak of each phrase—arrived at from below (A → B → C♯), sustained briefly, then descending back through the minor foundation (C♯ → B → A → G → F♯ → E); the phrase arc is darkness → brief lift → return to darkness; the raised sixth sounds like a moment of remembered light within an otherwise minor world.

**Medieval nobility**: moderate tempo; phrases built around the fifth (B in E Dorian) as a secondary resting point; the raised sixth as color on ascending phrases rather than a climactic peak; clear phrase endings on the tonic; the overall dynamic steady rather than strongly shaped; this is the Dorian of Gregorian chant and Celtic slow airs—dignified, not anguished.

**Jazz-inflected minor** (Miles Davis *Kind of Blue* character): moderate tempo with rhythmic freedom; the raised sixth (C♯) as a characteristic color note on unaccented beats rather than a dramatic peak; phrase endings on the flat seventh (D in E Dorian)—open, questioning; the fourth (A) used as a secondary landing; no strong tonic arrivals—the music floats within the Dorian space without urgency.

**Soulful, restless**: the raised sixth arriving unexpectedly in the middle of what seemed like a natural minor phrase—the listener was expecting C natural and got C♯; the surprise of the raised sixth creates the soul-music characteristic tension; use this once per phrase, then resolve naturally through the rest of the scale.

**Dark folk energy**: faster tempo with rhythmic pulse; the Dorian raised sixth integrated into the rhythmic flow rather than held expressively; short punchy phrases; Celtic ornamental vocabulary (rolls, cuts, slides) applied to the characteristic notes; the music feels minor but vital, dark but not still.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Dorian

**Never emphasizing the raised sixth**: the single note that makes Dorian distinctively Dorian is the raised sixth (C♯ in E Dorian, F♯ in A Dorian). A composition that passes through this note without emphasis sounds like natural minor with an accidental. Place the raised sixth on at least one strong beat per phrase. Use a roll or trill ornament on it. Let the listener hear it.

**Accidentally using the natural sixth instead**: the natural minor (Aeolian) sixth (C natural in E Dorian context) produces a completely different emotional effect than the Dorian raised sixth (C♯). Beginners often revert to C natural by habit because it feels "more minor." It is also correct—but using C natural consistently converts your Dorian into Aeolian. Choose deliberately between C natural (for a darker, more resigned phrase) and C♯ (for the Dorian resilience).

**Playing Dorian as if it were minor pentatonic**: removing both the raised sixth and the second degree (leaving only A – C – D – E – G in A Dorian) produces minor pentatonic—which has no Dorian character at all. Both the second and the raised sixth must be present and meaningful. Use all seven notes.

**No dynamic arc**: Dorian's emotional range from dark minor to resilient brightness needs dynamic shape to be expressed. Phrases that approach the raised sixth should build slightly; phrases that descend from it should soften. Without this shaping, the raised sixth sounds like a wrong note rather than an expressive peak.
