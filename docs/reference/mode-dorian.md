## What Is Dorian?

Dorian is a minor-feeling scale with one surprising note: the sixth degree is raised by a half-step compared to natural minor [1][2][3]. Natural minor has a flat sixth that adds heaviness and sadness; Dorian has a major sixth that adds a touch of brightness—the musical equivalent of a shadow with a silver edge.

**D Dorian**: D – E – F – G – A – B – C – D

That B natural (instead of B♭ that natural minor would have) is the entire personality of Dorian. Without it, the scale would be pure natural minor—sad and resigned. With it, Dorian has what one writer described as "bittersweet melancholy with a twist of hope" [1]. The minor third establishes darkness; the major sixth lifts it slightly.

**Step pattern**: Whole – Half – Whole – Whole – Whole – Half – Whole

## The Emotional Territory

Dorian sits between natural minor's resignation and the brightness of major. Musicians across centuries have reached for it when they want:

- **Complexity without extremity**: not purely sad, not purely hopeful—something more human
- **Resilient darkness**: grief that holds its head up, darkness that has vitality
- **Soulful introspection**: the raised sixth gives Dorian a lift that makes it work in jazz, funk, and soul where pure minor would feel too heavy [4][7]

Plato reportedly wanted soldiers to listen only to Dorian and Phrygian modes because they "elicit strong, powerful vibes"—contrasting with Lydian and Ionian, which felt too dreamy and innocent [15]. This ancient association of Dorian with strength-within-darkness persists.

**Famous Dorian melodies**: "Moondance" (Van Morrison, A Dorian); "Oye Como Va" (Santana, A Dorian); "Mad World" (Tears for Fears); "Get Lucky" (Daft Punk, B Dorian); most of what we think of as "Celtic minor" [3][5][8]

## The Characteristic Note: The Raised Sixth

The raised sixth is the flavor note of Dorian [2]. Emphasizing it is what makes a melody sound distinctly Dorian rather than just minor:

- In **D Dorian**: the raised sixth is B natural. Emphasize it, resolve to it, ornament it—let it be heard.
- The major sixth creates a surprising openness within minor surroundings. It sounds like sunlight through a gap in clouds.
- The mode oscillates back and forth between the dark minor third (F in D Dorian) and the bright major sixth (B). That contrast IS the Dorian emotion.

**Compositional technique**: let a phrase dwell on F (darkness), then briefly touch B (light), then return to F. The contrast makes both notes more vivid.

## Dorian Across Styles

**Medieval and Celtic**: Dorian is the predominant minor mode in Celtic music—most Irish and Scottish minor-key tunes are Dorian, not natural minor [30]. Its lift makes the music feel vital rather than defeated. "Scarborough Fair" is Dorian; many Irish reels and jigs in minor keys are Dorian.

**Jazz**: Dorian is the scale of choice for minor chord improvisation in jazz. Miles Davis's *Kind of Blue* (1959) famously uses D Dorian as its primary scale—an entire album essentially improvised in one mode [3].

**Rock and funk**: The raised sixth works perfectly over dominant seventh chords, which is why Dorian saturates blues-influenced rock and funk. The bass riff of "Oye Como Va" is a classic Dorian groove.

**Folk**: Dorian's ancient roots in medieval European music mean it appears naturally in folk traditions from Ireland to the Balkans to Central Asia.

## Composition Exercise: Dorian Air on Low D Whistle

**Setup**: D Dorian = D – E – F – G – A – B – C – D. Moderate, breathing tempo.

1. Begin on A (the fifth—a natural starting note for Dorian airs). Descend to D through G: A – G – F – E – D.
2. The F (flat third) establishes the minor character. Dwell here.
3. Now ascend: D – E – F – G – A – B. When B arrives (the raised sixth—the bright note), apply a roll or cut on it. Let it shine briefly.
4. Descend back: B – A – G – F – E – D. The return from B to F (bright to dark) is the Dorian emotional journey.
5. A phrase of 6–8 notes that moves through both the dark third and the bright sixth, returning to the tonic.

**Goal**: music that feels resilient rather than defeated—minor but not without light.

## Further Reading and Related Topics

The term "Dorian" has been applied inconsistently across history; the Greek Dorian harmonia was very different from the medieval church mode Dorian, which is again different from the modern Dorian described here.
