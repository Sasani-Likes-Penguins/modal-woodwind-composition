## What Is It?

The Spanish Heptatonic scale—also called Phrygian Dominant—is a seven-note scale built on the [Phrygian](mode-phrygian.md) foundation (with a half-step between the first and second degrees) but with the third degree raised to a major third rather than the minor third of pure Phrygian. This creates an [augmented second](../glossary.md#augmented-second)—a leap of one-and-a-half steps—between the flat second and the major third.

The formula: **1 – ♭2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – ♭7**

**E Spanish Heptatonic (Phrygian Dominant)**: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E

This single scale has been independently central to three living musical traditions for centuries: flamenco, Arabic and Turkish classical music, and Jewish synagogue cantorial music. The convergence of three traditions on the same pitch collection—with completely different names, different ornamental vocabularies, and different emotional frameworks—makes this one of the most emotionally potent and culturally resonant scales in world music.

## The Interval Structure

| Degree | Note (from E) | Distance from previous |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 (tonic) | E |—|
| ♭2 | F | Half-step |
| 3 (major third) | G♯ | Augmented second (one-and-a-half steps) |
| 4 | A | Half-step |
| 5 | B | Whole step |
| ♭6 | C | Half-step |
| ♭7 | D | Whole step |
| 8 (octave) | E | Whole step |

The scale shares the [half-step above the tonic](../glossary.md#half-step) from [Phrygian](mode-phrygian.md)—F pressing against E—and adds the dramatic leap of an augmented second from F to G♯. This combination of the Phrygian half-step tension *and* the augmented-second leap creates an immediate intensity unlike any other scale.

## The Augmented Second: The Defining Leap

The augmented second—F to G♯, one-and-a-half steps—is the most recognizable interval of this scale. It is the leap that every listener immediately associates with the "Middle Eastern," "Spanish," or "Jewish" sound. Neurologically, augmented seconds are rare enough in most listeners' musical experience that they reliably spike attention and activate the [amygdala](../glossary.md#amygdala)—the brain's emotional center. See [Brain Biology and Psychology](brain-biology-psychology.md).

**Ascending**: F → G♯ (upward augmented second): sudden brightening within darkness; the fierce leap that characterizes flamenco passion and cantorial intensity

**Descending**: G♯ → F (downward augmented second): a falling into shadow; grief arriving after a moment of brightness

This interval is also the defining gesture of:
- [Arabic Maqam Hijaz](world-arabic-maqam.md)—the lower *jins* of Hijaz uses the same F–G♯ (or equivalent) leap
- [Turkish Makam Hicaz](world-turkish-makam.md)—the same structure in Turkish theory
- [Jewish Ahavah Rabbah shtayger](world-jewish-nusach.md)—the devotional yearning of Shabbat morning prayer

## Emotional Character

| Feeling | Expression |
|---------|-----------|
| Fierce passion | The augmented second leap upward; rapid ornamentation on the half-step; sudden dynamic surges |
| Devotional yearning | The half-step above the tonic pressing downward; the augmented second as a reaching toward the divine |
| Ancient and exotic | The combination of Phrygian darkness with the augmented second creates immediate "otherness" |
| Grief with fire | Unlike pure Phrygian's more resigned grief, this scale has an intensity that makes grief feel active |
| Sacred solemnity | In liturgical contexts, the scale carries centuries of devotional association |

The Spanish Heptatonic scale is not merely "dark"—it contains fire within its darkness. The augmented second lifts the third degree above what pure Phrygian would allow, creating a brightness that makes the surrounding darkness more vivid by contrast.

## Where This Scale Appears

### Flamenco
In flamenco, this is simply "the Phrygian mode"—but with the raised third so ubiquitous it is inseparable from the tradition. The [Andalusian cadence](world-flamenco.md) (♭II → I, here F major → E minor or E major) is the engine of flamenco, and the Spanish Heptatonic scale is its melodic vocabulary. Palos including *soleá*, *bulerías*, and *siguiriyas* are built on this scale. See [Flamenco](world-flamenco.md) for the full treatment.

**Recordings**: Camarón de la Isla (*siguiriyas* recordings), Paco de Lucía (*falsetas* in E Phrygian Dominant), Enrique Morente.

### Jewish Cantorial Music (Ahavah Rabbah)
In Jewish liturgy, this is the [Ahavah Rabbah](world-jewish-nusach.md) shtayger—named after the morning prayer blessing. It is the mode of Shabbat and festival morning services. The cantorial tradition developed an elaborate ornamental vocabulary built on this scale. *Hava Nagila* began as a Hasidic niggun (wordless melody) in this scale and was later set to text.

**Recordings**: Yossele Rosenblatt (the definitive Ashkenazi cantor; Ahavah Rabbah recordings), klezmer ensembles playing in *Freygish* mode.

### Arabic and Turkish Classical Music
In Arabic music, [Maqam Hijaz](world-arabic-maqam.md) uses the Hijaz *jins* (D♭–E–F in Arabic notation from D, or equivalent in other transpositions)—the same augmented second interval relationship. In Turkish music, [Makam Hicaz](world-turkish-makam.md) follows similar principles with the 53-[koma](../glossary.md#koma) microtonal system. In both traditions, the augmented second appears in the lower tetrachord, just as in the Spanish Heptatonic scale.

### Western Classical
- **Rimsky-Korsakov**—*Sheherazade* (1888): the "Scheherazade theme" is in E Phrygian Dominant; the scale is used to evoke the Arabian Nights setting
- **Saint-Saëns**—*"Bacchanale"* from *Samson et Dalila*: A Phrygian Dominant; fierce and exotic
- **Bizet**—*Carmen*: the Habanera and other themes use Phrygian Dominant for their "Spanish" character
- **Verdi**—*Aida*: Egyptian exoticism conveyed in part through Phrygian Dominant passages
- **Mahler**—Symphony No. 2, passages suggesting ancient or Eastern origins

### Popular Music
- **"Hava Nagila"**: the most internationally known melody in this scale; E Ahavah Rabbah / Phrygian Dominant
- **Metallica**—*"Wherever I May Roam"*: the guitar riff uses the augmented second
- **The Doors**—various Jim Morrison improvisations used Phrygian Dominant for "exotic" passages

## How It Differs Across Traditions

The same pitch collection is experienced very differently depending on the tradition:

| Tradition | Name | Key feature | Ornaments |
|-----------|------|-------------|----------|
| Flamenco | Flamenco Phrygian / Spanish scale | Andalusian cadence (♭II→I); *compás* rhythm | *Adornos*; rapid runs; *rasgueado* |
| Jewish (Ashkenazi) | Ahavah Rabbah / Freygish | Cantorial melisma; liturgical occasion | Melismatic vocal ornaments; *portamento* |
| Arabic | Hijaz maqam | *Jins* cell structure; quarter-tone inflections | *Zamzama* trill; approach figures |
| Turkish | Hicaz makam | *Seyir* (ascending-descending direction); *koma* system | Specific Turkish ornamental patterns |
| Western classical | Phrygian Dominant / "Spanish scale" | Harmonic exoticism; orchestral color | Standard Western ornaments |

Understanding which tradition's emotional framework you are working within shapes every choice of ornament, tempo, rhythm, and phrasing—even when the pitches are identical.

## Composing with This Scale

**Choose your tradition first**: the same pitches sound completely different depending on whether you approach them with a flamenco *compás*, a cantorial melisma, or an Arabic *jins* phrase. Choose a tradition to work within before composing—or consciously blend them if that is your intention.

**The augmented second as gesture**: approach the augmented second as an expressive event, not merely a scale step. Ascending (F → G♯ in E Phrygian Dominant): use it for moments of sudden brightening or fierce arrival. Descending (G♯ → F): use it for a falling into shadow, grief arriving unexpectedly.

**The half-step above the tonic**: like [Phrygian](mode-phrygian.md), the half-step between the tonic and its neighbor (E–F) is always pressing. Use the F as a lean note (arriving on F on a strong beat before resolving to E) or as an ornamental trill (rapid alternation E–F) for maximum Phrygian Dominant expressiveness. See [Ornamentation Guide](ornamentation-guide.md).

**The major tonic chord**: unlike pure Phrygian (which has a minor tonic triad), Phrygian Dominant has a major tonic triad (E–G♯–B in E Phrygian Dominant). This gives the mode a powerful, declarative quality at moments of arrival—a major chord preceded by the Phrygian half-step creates the characteristic flamenco and cantorial sense of intensity resolving into authority.

## Composition Exercise

**Instrument**: low D whistle (E Phrygian Dominant)
**E Phrygian Dominant**: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E

1. Begin on B (the fifth—open, stable). Descend slowly: B – A – G♯ – F – E. The G♯ → F (descending augmented second) is the emotional center of the descent—give it full weight. F should arrive as a shadow after the brightness of G♯.
2. From E, begin an ascending phrase: E – F (lean on F, the half-step above tonic, for one full beat) – G♯ (the augmented second leap upward—sudden brightening) – A – B. The phrase rises through the characteristic gesture of the scale.
3. From B, ornament: trill E–F rapidly, then land on E firmly. The trill on the Phrygian half-step above the tonic is the most characteristic ornamental gesture of this scale across all three traditions.
4. A second phrase: descend A – G♯ – F – E. The augmented second descending (G♯ → F) this time feels like resolution into darkness rather than sudden shadow—familiar now, accepted.
5. End on E, very softly.

**Goal**: music that contains both fire and grief simultaneously—the characteristic quality that three living traditions have independently found in these pitches.

## Eliciting Specific Emotions in the Spanish Heptatonic Scale

**Flamenco fire** (the primary affect): see [Flamenco](world-flamenco.md) for the complete guide. In brief: moderate-fast tempo; the augmented second (F → G♯ in E Phrygian Dominant) as a fierce upward leap on accented beats; the Phrygian sigh (F → E) as cadential phrase endings; dynamic surges; the bimodal character (E major tonic chord vs. Phrygian surroundings) creating brightness within darkness.

**Cantorial devotional yearning** (Ahavah Rabbah affect): see [Jewish Nusach](world-jewish-nusach.md) for the complete guide. Slow tempo; the G♯ as the sustained peak note of each phrase—held with andolan (gentle oscillation); the descending phrase G♯ → F → E as the characteristic yearning gesture; long melismatic phrases; no rhythmic pulse.

**Ancient Middle Eastern character**: moderate tempo; the augmented second (F → G♯) as the scale's first identity statement; the Phrygian darkness of the ♭2 (F pressing against E) adding antiquity; the scale's major third (G♯) giving fierce brightness; phrases that move freely through all seven notes but always return through F → E for the Phrygian cadence.

**Exotic, dramatic orchestral color**: the scale used as a single striking color within a composition otherwise in a simpler mode; the augmented second appearing once per composition as a deliberate surprise; the surrounding Phrygian framework giving it historical weight.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Spanish Heptatonic

**Confusing the three traditions that use this scale**: flamenco, Jewish cantorial (Ahavah Rabbah), and Arabic Hijaz maqam all use the same or very similar pitch sets, but their ornamental vocabularies, tempos, and emotional frameworks are completely different. Choose which tradition you are working in before composing—the same notes played with flamenco rhythm sound completely different from the same notes played with cantorial melisma.

**Neglecting the ♭2**: the Phrygian half-step (F in E Phrygian Dominant) is as important as the augmented second (F → G♯). The combination—the half-step above E pressing down, combined with the sudden leap from F to G♯—is the scale's complete emotional identity. A composition that only uses the augmented second without the ♭2 is missing half the scale's character.

**Not establishing E as the tonic**: the scale's E major tonic (E – G♯ – B) sits within a Phrygian framework (E as home, F as the pressing half-step above). Some beginners, finding G♯ so prominent, drift toward G♯ as a tonal center (which would make the scale a different mode). Return to E consistently. Every phrase should ultimately anchor to E.
