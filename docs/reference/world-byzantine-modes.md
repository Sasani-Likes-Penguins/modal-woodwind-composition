## What Is the Octoechos?

The **Octoechos** (Greek: Ὀκτώηχος, meaning "of the eight sounds/modes") is the eight-mode system used for the composition of religious chant in Eastern Orthodox Christianity [91][98](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quarter_tone)[100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koron_(music)). It was codified in the 8th century by Saint John of Damascus, drawing from earlier Syrian, Greek, and Jewish liturgical traditions.

The Octoechos organizes all Orthodox church hymns into an eight-week cycle—each week uses a different mode (*echos*), rotating continuously through the liturgical year. By rotating modes each week, the Octoechos prevents monotony, allowing hymns to evoke a range of affections from solemnity to jubilation [100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koron_(music)).

---

## The Eight Echoi (Tones)

The eight modes are divided into four **authentic** (primary) and four **plagal** (derived) modes [91][92]:

| Mode | Greek name | Character |
|------|-----------|----------|
| 1st Tone | Protos | Hope, strength—the "first voice" of the Church |
| 2nd Tone | Deuteros | Tender, intimate |
| 3rd Tone | Tritos | Festive, bright |
| 4th Tone | Tetartos | Majestic, solemn |
| Plagal 1st | Plagios Protos | Repentant joy—a deepened version of the 1st |
| Plagal 2nd | Plagios Deuteros | Intimate sorrow |
| Grave |—(Plagal of 3rd) | Grave, serious |
| Plagal 4th | Plagios Tetartos | Festivity, celebration |

Each echos carries symbolic attributes—Mode I for hope and strength, Mode V (Plagal First) for repentant joy, Mode VIII (Plagal Fourth) for festivity—mirroring themes of creation, redemption, and divine praise [100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koron_(music)).

## Byzantine Scales: Diatonic, Chromatic, Enharmonic

Byzantine music theory uses a more complex tonal system than Western modes. Each echos can be expressed in one of **four scale types** (*genera*) [94][98](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quarter_tone):

**Diatonic**: uses standard whole and half steps—closest to Western modes
**Soft Chromatic**: uses augmented seconds (like Hijaz/Hicaz)—the "intensely Byzantine" sound
**Hard Chromatic**: more extreme augmented intervals
**Enharmonic**: uses very small microtonal intervals—the most ancient and difficult to perform

The same echos played in different genera sounds completely different. The soft chromatic version of the 2nd tone (Deuteros), with its augmented second, is one of the most characteristic sounds in all of Byzantine chant.

### The Byzantine Diatonic Scale

Byzantine diatonic tuning is not equal-tempered. The octave is divided into 72 equal commas (a comma is approximately 1/12 of a semitone). Byzantine intervals:

- Whole step = 12 commas (same as Western)
- Half step = 6 commas (smaller than Western)
- Augmented second = 20 commas (larger than Western)

This means Byzantine music has slightly different emotional qualities than the same intervals in Western equal temperament—it is slightly sharper, slightly brighter, than what a piano would play.

## Byzantine Chant in Practice

Byzantine chant is **monophonic**—a single melody line with no harmony, except for the *ison*: a held drone note supporting the melody [93]. This makes it directly applicable to solo wind playing—it was designed for a single voice without accompaniment.

**Rhythmic structure**: Byzantine chant uses three rhythmic styles [93][96]:
- **Heirmologic** (*heirmologica*): quick, one note per syllable—the most common style
- **Sticheraric**: slower, multiple notes per syllable—more elaborate
- **Papadic**: very slow, highly melismatic—used for extended ceremonial hymns

**For wind players**: the heirmologic style is essentially a flowing syllabic melody—each note has weight and intention, but the music moves continuously. The sticheraric style is the most expressive for wind—each syllable (or phrase on a single breath) receives multiple notes, allowing expressive shaping.

## Connection to Middle Eastern Modes

The Byzantine system did not develop in isolation—it emerged from the same Mediterranean musical world as the Arabic maqam and Turkish makam systems [91][93][100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koron_(music)). The relationship is close enough that some modes overlap substantially:

- Byzantine 2nd Tone (soft chromatic) ≈ Maqam Hijaz
- Byzantine Plagal 2nd ≈ Maqam Bayati
- Byzantine 1st Tone (diatonic) ≈ Maqam Rast (slightly)

The kaval, with its Hungarian-scale tuning and its use across the Balkans (a historically Byzantine-influenced region), naturally bridges the Byzantine and Middle Eastern worlds.

## Composition Exercise: 2nd Tone Soft Chromatic (Byzantine Hijaz) on Recorder

**Setup**: 2nd Tone soft chromatic from E: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D♯ – E
(Note the augmented seconds: F–G♯ and C–D♯)

1. Begin on B (the "dominant"—the reciting tone of the 2nd tone). Hold it.
2. Descend: B – A – G♯ – F – E. The G♯ after A creates the first augmented-second leap (A down to G♯ = half-step; G♯ down to F = augmented second—one-and-a-half steps). This leap is the signature sound.
3. From E, ascend stepwise: E – F – G♯ – A. The F–G♯ augmented second ascending is the characteristic Byzantine "lift."
4. Return to B (dominant) for a temporary resting point.
5. Use very plain, dignified dynamics—no sudden surges. Byzantine chant has gravity, not drama.
6. End on E (the final/tonic), approached from F (the half-step above)—the Byzantine equivalent of the Phrygian sigh.

**Goal**: music that feels ancient, sacred, and Mediterranean—the sound of prayer across 1500 years of Orthodox tradition.

## The Genera in Detail: A Practical Guide

The four Byzantine genera (scale types) are not simply different scales—they are different ways of filling in the same tetrachordal framework. Understanding them helps with the Double Harmonic scale, Arabic maqam, and Persian dastgah, all of which use similar distinctions.

### Diatonic Genus
Whole step – Whole step – Half step (within a fourth)
The most familiar genus to Western ears; corresponds to the standard diatonic scale's tetrachord structure. Byzantine Tone 1 (First Echos) uses the diatonic genus.

### Soft Chromatic Genus
Three-quarter tone – Three-quarter tone – One-and-a-half step (within a fourth)
The most characteristic Byzantine sound to Western ears. The two small intervals at the bottom give chromatic Byzantine chant its specific tense, searching quality. This genus is closely related to Arabic maqam Hijaz and Turkish Hicaz—the same structural relationship. Byzantine Tone 2 (Second Echos) uses the soft chromatic.

### Hard Chromatic Genus
Half step – Half step – One-and-a-half step (within a fourth)
More tense than soft chromatic. The two consecutive half-steps create a feeling of extreme compression before the wide augmented second leap.

### Enharmonic Genus
Quarter tone – Quarter tone – Two whole steps (within a fourth)
The most microtonal genus; requires [microtonal](../glossary.md#microtone) intervals not available on standard Western instruments. On flexible-pitch instruments (bawu, kaval), the quarter-tone intervals can be approximated. The enharmonic genus is considered the most ancient and spiritually profound in Byzantine theory.

## Byzantine Chant and Orthodox Liturgy

Byzantine chant is the liturgical music of the Eastern Orthodox Christian churches—Greek Orthodox, Russian Orthodox, Serbian Orthodox, Bulgarian Orthodox, Antiochian Orthodox, and others. The Octoechos cycle means each Sunday is in a different echos, rotating through all eight over two months; each echos has its own character and its own hymns.

**Ison (drone)**: Byzantine chant in congregational practice uses an *ison*—a sustained drone note held by part of the choir while the melody moves above it. This drone creates acoustic beating with the melody notes, similar to Indian tanpura practice. On a solo wind instrument, playing over a drone recording creates the same effect.

**The neumatic notation**: Byzantine chant uses its own notation system based on neumes (Greek: signs), not Western staff notation. Each neume indicates a melodic gesture rather than a specific pitch. Learning to read Byzantine neumes opens access to a vast historical repertoire not available in Western staff notation.

## Composing in Byzantine Modal Style for Solo Wind: A Complete Guide

### The Starting Point: Understand the Liturgical Purpose

Byzantine music was created for a specific function: the worship service of the Eastern Orthodox Church. Every compositional choice—mode selection, phrase length, tempo, dynamics—serves that purpose. When composing *in the style of* Byzantine music for solo wind, you are borrowing a vocabulary designed for prayer. The appropriate emotional register is always elevated, reverential, and contemplative.

This does not mean solemn or slow in a simple sense. Byzantine chant spans the full range from the quiet meditation of Orthros (morning office) to the jubilant Paschal (Easter) proclamations. But even the most joyful Byzantine music maintains a quality of elevation—it is not casual.

### Choosing an Echos

The eight *echoi* (tones) of the Octoechos cycle are assigned to specific occasions. For compositional purposes, choose based on emotional intent:

**Tone 1 (First Echos, diatonic)**: bright and grounded; approximately D Dorian character. For music that is noble and forward-moving without darkness.

**Tone 2 (Second Echos, soft chromatic)**: the most characteristic Byzantine sound to Western ears. The two small intervals (three-quarter tone + three-quarter tone) followed by the large step create an immediately "Byzantine" quality. For music of searching devotion—neither triumphant nor dark, but reaching.

**Tone 3 (Third Echos, diatonic)**: a higher, more transparent brightness. For elevated praise.

**Tone 4 (Fourth Echos, soft chromatic or enharmonic)**: the most sacred and solemn; reserved for the gravest occasions of the liturgical year.

**Tones 5–8 (Plagal echoi)**: "lower" versions of tones 1–4; slightly less tense, more settled. The plagal Tone 4 (the "grave tone") is used for burial services and is the darkest of the eight.

### Composing in Second Tone (Soft Chromatic): Step-by-Step

Second Tone is the most distinctive and most approachable for beginners. Its soft chromatic genus (approximately three-quarter tone – three-quarter tone – minor third within a fourth) sounds unmistakably Byzantine.

**The practical scale for wind instruments** (approximating Second Tone in an accessible way):
- Use E as the tonal center
- The lower tetrachord: E – F (half-step) – G♯ (augmented second leap) – A (this approximates the chromatic genus with a more accessible interval set)
- This is essentially E Phrygian Dominant / Spanish Heptatonic—the closest available approximation on equal-tempered instruments

**Full approximated scale**: E – F – G♯ – A – B – C – D – E

For full microtonal Second Tone (with three-quarter tones), the bawu or kaval can produce the characteristic inflections—the F should be slightly sharper than standard (approaching F♯ but not reaching it), creating the "between" quality of the soft chromatic genus.

**Step-by-step composition**:

1. **Establish the center**: play E alone, slowly. Then A (the fourth above). Then back to E. The fourth relationship (E–A) is the structural anchor of Byzantine melody.

2. **The chromatic characteristic phrase**: from A, descend: A – G♯ – F – E. The G♯ → F (the augmented second) is the defining gesture. Take the full value of each note; do not rush through the G♯. The augmented second is not a passing note—it is the mode's identity.

3. **The ascending phrase**: from E, ascend: E – F – G♯ – A. The F arrives first (half-step above E—the pressing quality); the G♯ follows (the upward resolution of the chromatic step). This ascending figure is the counterpart to the descending phrase in step 2.

4. **Second phrase from the fifth**: begin on B (the fifth). Descend: B – A – G♯ – F – E. The same characteristic phrase but starting from the fifth—longer, more formal.

5. **A phrase from the upper range**: ascend to D or high E. From there, descend through C – B – A – G♯ – F – E. The full scale descent, slowly, arriving home through the chromatic characteristic phrase.

6. **Close**: E alone. Perhaps with a very slow trill between E and F (the Phrygian half-step) as a final devotional gesture—the half-step pressing and releasing.

### The *Ison* Effect for Solo Players

Byzantine chant typically uses an *ison* (drone)—a sustained note held by part of the choir while the melody moves above. For solo wind players, the drone effect can be approximated:

- **Bawu**: play a sustained melody note in the middle of the range, then return to E (the tonic) at phrase endings. The tonal memory of E as a constant reference point creates a drone-like psychological effect even without sounding it continuously.
- **Kaval**: the dual-voice technique (partial fipple coverage + humming) can produce a simultaneous E drone beneath a melody that moves above it. This is the closest any instrument in this collection comes to the actual ison effect.
- **Any instrument**: play a phrase, return to E, play the next phrase, return to E. The structural return to the drone note creates a sense of the ison being present even when it is not sounding.

### Eliciting Specific Emotions

**Sacred contemplation**: slow tempo; sparse ornamentation; every phrase beginning and ending with E; the soft chromatic descending phrase (A – G♯ – F – E) as the primary melodic gesture; dynamics never louder than mezzo-forte; long silences between phrases.

**Joyful elevation** (Paschal character): moderate tempo with clear rhythmic pulse; ascending phrases dominant; the fourth relationship (E – A) as a leaping gesture; brighter dynamic; the diatonic tones (1, 5) rather than the chromatic tones (2, 6).

**Penitential gravity**: very slow; the enharmonic genus if the instrument can produce it (three-quarter tone steps); the plagal fourth tone character (lower, more settled); dynamics beginning at piano and never rising above mf; the chromatic genus as a sound of ancient prayer.

### Connection to Maqam and Dastgah for Composition

Byzantine composition shares the same interval structure as several maqam and dastgah systems. This convergence opens compositional possibilities:

- A melody composed in Second Tone (Byzantine) can be developed using Arabic *taksim* principles (free improvisation exploring the modal territory)
- Persian *forud* (descent to home) is structurally identical to Byzantine phrase endings that settle back to the tonic through the chromatic phrase
- The Byzantine concept of "melodic formulas" (specific phrases assigned to each tone) is equivalent to the Persian *gusheh* system

When composing in Byzantine mode, feel free to borrow the structural logic of Persian or Arabic improvisation—the emotional languages are related.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Byzantine Modal Composition

**Treating Byzantine modes as "exotic major and minor"**: Byzantine echoi are not simply major or minor scales with occasional chromatic notes. Each echos has a specific melodic direction, specific characteristic phrases (called *theseis*), and a liturgical function. Composing "Byzantine-style" by playing Phrygian with a few extra chromatic notes misses the tradition's depth. Study at least one echos's characteristic phrases before composing in it.

**Ignoring the ison**: Byzantine chant is built around a drone (*ison*)—a sustained note held against the melody. Even in solo wind performance, the melody should be conceived in relation to an implied drone. Every phrase should feel its relationship to the tonic. Phrases that wander harmonically without reference to the implied drone sound more like free improvisation than Byzantine style.

**Mistaking the soft chromatic genus for Phrygian Dominant**: the soft chromatic genus (approximately D♭ – E – F in the lower tetrachord from C) is similar to Phrygian Dominant but uses different microtonal inflections. On flexible-pitch instruments, the D♭ in Byzantine soft chromatic should be slightly higher than standard—approaching a three-quarter tone rather than a full half-step. This microtonal difference is the entire distinction between the Byzantine echos and the flamenco/Jewish Ahavah Rabbah mode.

**Playing too fast**: Byzantine chant is meditative and liturgical. Even instrumental compositions in Byzantine style should maintain a meditative tempo that gives each phrase and its ornaments room to breathe. The tradition's emotional depth comes from patient unfolding, not from melody-per-second.
